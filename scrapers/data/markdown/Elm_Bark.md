# Elm Bark

## Overview

Elm bark is the bark of the elm (Ulmus minor) tree. This tree is native to central Asia. It is used in medicine ( 101817 ). KEY HIGHLIGHTS

- Traditionally used for digestive problems, female infertility, and wound healing, but there is no strong evidence to support any use.
- Safety or possible side effects are unknown.
- No known major interactions.

## History

Elm bark is used in Persian traditional medicine to increase female fertility ( 101817 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, elm bark is used for digestive disorders, severe diarrhea, and female infertility. Sometimes it is used as a diuretic and astringent. Topically, elm bark is used for cleaning open and/or festering wounds.

#### General

No adverse effects have been reported. However, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of elm bark.

#### Adult
Oral:

General : People have used one cup of tea orally 2-3 times daily. The tea is prepared by simmering 2 teaspoons of bark in 150 mL of boiling water for 10-15 minutes and straining. Alternately, 2-5 grams of powdered root daily has been used ( 18 ).

Topical:

General : A 20% tea diluted 1:1 with water has been used ( 18 ).

#### General

Elm bark contains aliphatic hydrocarbons and triterpenes ( 101817 ).

#### Fertility effects

Elm bark is traditionally used to increase female fertility. In an animal model of polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), elm bark reduced the high levels of testosterone and the number of follicles ( 101817 ).
