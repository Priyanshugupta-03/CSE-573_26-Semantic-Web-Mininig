## Overview

Nasal irrigation has been used traditionally to help facilitate drainage from the nose and sinuses ( 93990 ). The process involves flushing out the nasal cavity with saline by instilling it in one nostril and allowing it to drain out of the other. Some people use a small neti pot, which looks like a teapot. Others use a bottle, a bulb syringe, or a spray ( 16135 , 17039 , 93988 , 93993 ). The saline used in nasal irrigation might be normal or hypertonic. Hypertonic solutions are usually between 1.25% and 3.5% saline ( 16135 , 16136 , 93992 ). KEY HIGHLIGHTS

- Some evidence for use in allergic rhinitis (hay fever), rhinosinusitis, and recovery from intranasal surgery.
- Likely safe when used appropriately. There is a risk of infection if performed improperly. Water for homemade nasal irrigation solutions should be boiled, bottled, or distilled. Neti pots and rinse bottles should be washed with hot soapy water after every use and not shared with other people.
- Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) : There is interest in using nasal irrigation to reduce the risk for, or severity of, COVID-19 by physically reducing the viral load. But there is no strong evidence to support this practice. Ensure that patients follow healthy lifestyle choices and proven prevention methods as well. In order to reduce the risk for transmission, recommend that patients with suspected or confirmed COVID-19 wash their hands and disinfect the rinse bottle and surrounding surfaces after nasal irrigation.

## History

Nasal irrigation has been used for centuries to clean the airways ( 93990 ). In yoga and Ayurveda, it is traditionally called "Jala neti" ( 16137 , 88344 , 93995 ).

## People Use This For

Nasal irrigation is used to clear out nasal mucus, moisturize the nasal cavity, and improve nasal hygiene. It is also used for sinus conditions such as rhinosinusitis, allergic rhinitis, the common cold, post-nasal drip, rhinitis of pregnancy, asthma, bronchiolitis, and others. Nasal irrigation is also used in the postoperative care of surgical patients following intranasal surgery.

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Nasal irrigation has been safely used in several clinical trials ( 16135 , 16136 , 16137 , 17039 , 88344 , 88345 , 88347 )  ( 88348 , 88349 , 88351 , 88352 , 88353 , 88354 , 88355 , 88357 ) ( 93991 , 93993 , 97146 ). There is some concern about chronic use causing infection. Maintaining good hygiene is important for safe use of nasal irrigation. To maintain hygiene, boiled, bottled, or distilled water should be used in the neti pots or rinse bottles for irrigating. Pots and rinse bottles should also be washed with hot soapy water after every use and should never be shared with other people ( 93993 ). Rinse bottles should also be switched to a new bottle monthly.

CHILDREN: Likely Safe when used appropriately. Nasal irrigation has been safely used in clinical trials in children ( 16139 , 16140 , 16141 , 88358 , 93986 , 93990 , 93992 , 97145 , 97150 , 97152 )( 97153 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Likely Safe when used appropriately ( 17039 , 93985 ). Saline solution is not harmful to the fetus or newborn ( 17039 ).

#### General

Nasal irrigation can cause mild side effects including nasal burning, pain, stinging, discharge, and irritation, and ear fullness, ringing, and pain. Irrigation solutions with a higher concentration of salt increase these side effects ( 16135 , 17039 , 88354 , 93984 , 93986 , 93988 , 93993 , 93994 , 93996 ). In rare cases, epistaxis, nausea, coughing, and headaches have occurred ( 17039 , 88354 , 93986 , 93994 ). There is some concern that chronic use of nasal irrigation might actually increase the risk of sinus infection if water, bottles, or tools are contaminated ( 17040 ). Also, there have been reports of amebic brain infection in people using tap water in neti pots. The ameba enters the body via the nasal passages ( 17911 ). To use nasal irrigation safely, good hygiene is necessary.

#### Gastrointestinal

Rarely, nausea has occured ( 93986 ).

#### Immunologic

There is some concern that chronic use of nasal irrigation might actually increase the risk of sinus infection recurrence in patients with chronic rhinosinusitis. In a preliminary study, chronic users of nasal irrigation had a reduced risk of rhinosinusitis recurrence after discontinuing nasal irrigation compared to those who continued using nasal irrigation ( 17040 ). It is unclear if this is due to nasal irrigation itself or improper use of irrigation such as using contaminated rinse bottles. Some evidence suggests that 67% of bottles used for nasal irrigation are contaminated with Staphylococcus aureus while 86% to 97% demonstrate bacterial growth of some sort ( 93987 , 93993 ). Use of tap water or boiling water to clean bottles was not effective. Cleaning with detergent, Milton's solution, and/or microwave sterilization was more effective ( 93987 ). Use of a one-way valve on the bottle to prevent re-contamination does not appear to be effective ( 93993 ). There have been two reports of amebic brain infection in people using neti pots for nasal irrigation. In these reports, patients were using tap water in Louisiana. Amebic infection is more common with freshwater from warm climate lakes and rivers. The ameba enters the body via the nasal passages. Tap water should not be used for nasal irrigation ( 17911 ). There is also one case report of a patient developing an abscess around a cochlear implant and into the mastoid bone following nasal irrigation for an acute sinusitis episode. The patient experienced fever. The cochlear implant was removed, testing positive for Group A streptococcus and Propionibacterium acnes ( 93982 ). To use nasal irrigation safely, good hygiene is necessary. To maintain good hygiene, boiled, bottled, or distilled water should be used in the neti pots or rinse bottles for irrigating. Pots and rinse bottles should also be washed with hot soapy water after every use and should never be shared with other people. Rinse bottles should also be switched to a new bottle monthly.

#### Neurologic/CNS

Rarely, nasal irrigation can cause side effects such as headache ( 88345 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

Nasal irrigation can cause side effects including ear fullness, ringing, popping, and pain. Irrigation solutions with a higher concentration of salt might increase this side effect ( 16135 , 17039 , 93984 , 93986 , 93993 , 93994 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

Nasal irrigation can cause side effects including nasal burning, pain, stinging, discharge, and irritation. Irrigation solutions with a higher concentration of salt increase these side effects ( 16135 , 17039 , 88354 , 93984 , 93986 , 93988 , 93992 , 93993 , 93994 , 105856 )( 105857 ). In rare cases, epistaxis ( 17039 , 88354 , 93994 ) and coughing ( 93986 ) have occurred. Nasal irrigation does not seem to negatively impact the sense of smell ( 93989 ).

Possibly Effective
Allergic rhinitis (hay fever).

Most research shows that nasal irrigation reduces symptoms of allergic rhinitis in both children and adults for up to 3 months ( 16139 , 16140 , 88348 , 88351 , 88352 , 88353 , 93984 , 93985 , 93990 , 93991 )( 97143 , 103083 , 103084 , 103085 , 103086 ). Meta-analyses of clinical research in adults and children with allergic rhinitis show that using nasal irrigation 1-7 times daily for up to 3 months improves symptoms such as itching, sneezing, and secretion ( 93985 , 103083 ). Nasal irrigation also reduces the need for conventional medicines such as nasal corticosteroids ( 93985 , 97143 ). However, it is not yet clear whether adding nasal irrigation to intranasal corticosteroids further improves symptoms of allergic rhinitis. Although one small uncontrolled study found some symptomatic benefit after adding nasal irrigation to intranasal corticosteroids when compared to baseline, a smaller controlled study did not find benefit when the combination treatment was compared to intranasal corticosteroids alone ( 93991 , 103083 ).

Some non-traditional types of nasal irrigation have also been investigated in adults. Clinical research suggests that a 1% ephedrine saline nasal wash improves allergy symptoms and nasal inspiratory flow rate in adults with allergic rhinitis ( 88349 ). Preliminary clinical research also shows that inhaling heated water vapor through the nose may improve nasal airflow and allergic symptoms for up to 6 hours compared to nasal saline irrigation in some patients; however, the improvement may be due to the heat of the vapors, and subjects received treatments at weekly intervals rather than daily ( 88350 ). Researchers have investigated whether some types of solutions are better than others for nasal irrigation. There is not enough evidence to determine whether saline sprays or saline rinses are more effective ( 93985 ). Some research shows that hypertonic saline solutions are more effective than isotonic saline solutions ( 93990 , 93992 , 97150 ), but not all research agrees ( 93985 , 105857 ). Also, while hypertonic saline shows efficacy in mild-to-moderate allergic rhinitis, it appears to be less effective compared to triamcinolone nasal spray and may not be appropriate for patients with moderate-to-severe symptoms ( 88348 ). Whether buffering a solution impacts its efficacy is not clear. Clinical research involving a small number of participants suggests that buffering isotonic to mild alkalinity (pH 7.2-7.4) reduces nasal symptoms of allergic rhinitis when compared with baseline; however, an unbuffered solution or buffering to pH 8.2-8.4 does not reduce symptoms. Buffering to mild alkalinity also increases patient preference ( 93984 ). Buffering a hypertonic saline solution also seems to have better outcomes compared to a non-buffered normal saline solution. Some clinical research shows that nasal irrigation with a hypertonic saline solution buffered to a pH of 7-7.4 is modestly better for improving rhinorrhea, itching, and sneezing compared to unbuffered isotonic saline in children with allergic rhinitis ( 97150 ).

Intranasal surgery.

Observational research has found that nasal irrigation with normal saline 1-3 times daily for up to 2 months is associated with lower risk of sinus surgery in children with refractory chronic sinusitis ( 97145 , 97151 ). There is also some evidence that nasal irrigation might benefit patients when used after intranasal surgery. Some clinical research shows that treatment with nasal irrigation following nasal surgery helps decrease hospital recovery days, physician visits, and patient complaints ( 16136 , 88347 ). A small clinical study in patients undergoing endonasal endoscopic surgery shows that nasal irrigation with 60 mL of solution containing xylitol 1 gram in each nostril three times daily for 30 days, starting the second day after surgery, reduces pain and nasal obstruction when compared with saline nasal irrigation ( 103889 ). Some research also shows that a saline stream, such as from a neti pot, is more effective than saline instillation ( 16136 ). Other clinical research suggests that nasal irrigation with alkaline solution might improve nasal patency in patients following septal or turbinate surgery ( 88346 ). However, irrigation with lactated Ringer's solution after sinus surgery seems to be superior to saline solutions. One clinical study in patients post sinus surgery for chronic rhinosinusitis shows that irrigation with lactated Ringer's solution three times daily for 6 weeks improves congestion, pain, and nasal discharge when compared to irrigation with isotonic and hypertonic saline ( 97149 ).

Rhinosinusitis.

Nasal irrigation techniques might be beneficial for both acute and chronic rhinosinusitis, although most research has investigated chronic rhinosinusitis. Clinical research shows that administering nasal saline irrigation significantly reduces symptoms of chronic rhinosinusitis, improves quality of life, and reduces the need for antibiotics when compared with baseline, no treatment, or placebo ( 16136 , 16137 , 16142 , 88353 , 88354 , 88355 , 93994 , 97151 ).

Different solutions such as hypertonic saline, hypochlorous solution, and steam have been tried for rhinosinusitis. Hypertonic saline nasal irrigation seems to be better than steam inhalation for improving chronic rhinosinusitis symptoms ( 97148 ). Hypertonic saline solution also seems to be better than normal saline. Several meta-analyses of clinical research shows that nasal irrigation with hypertonic saline is modestly to significantly better than isotonic saline for improving symptoms in patients with chronic rhinosinusitis ( 97146 , 105856 ). Some preliminary clinical research also shows that irrigation with hypertonic Dead Sea salt solution might improve symptom relief and quality of life when compared with a standard hypertonic saline solution ( 88357 ). Additional preliminary clinical research shows that, in children with rhinosinusitis, hypertonic saline irrigation may improve cough and radiology scores more than isotonic saline irrigation, although both treatments seems to improve nasal secretions equally ( 88358 ). Hypochlorous solution also seems to work better than isotonic saline in rhinosinusitis. Some research shows that nasal irrigation with low concentration of hypochlorous solution twice daily for 4 weeks seems to improve symptoms to a greater degree than irrigation with isotonic saline in children and adults with chronic rhinosinusitis ( 97157 ). There is some concern that chronic use of nasal irrigation might actually increase the risk of sinus infection recurrence. In a preliminary study, chronic users of nasal irrigation had a reduced risk of rhinosinusitis recurrence after discontinuing nasal irrigation when compared with those who continued using nasal irrigation ( 17040 ). It is unclear if this is due to nasal irrigation itself or improper use of irrigation such as using contaminated rinse bottles. Research evaluating nasal irrigation for acute rhinosinusitis is limited. Preliminary clinical research shows that, in addition to standard treatment, nasal irrigation with a 1.25% buffered hypertonic saline or a non-buffered isotonic saline solution one to three times daily for up to 3 weeks, improves congestion, cough, headache, and nasal discharge in children with or without atopy and acute rhinosinusitis ( 93993 , 97155 ). Furthermore, other preliminary clinical research shows that using nasal irrigation alone in children with acute rhinosinusitis results in a slower but similar resolution of symptoms compared to using nasal irrigation with an antibiotic. In children with acute rhinosinusitis, giving nasal irrigation with amoxicillin 80-100 mg/kg daily for up to 14 days leads to a more rapid improvement in certain clinical symptoms when compared with nasal irrigation alone. However there was no clinical difference in outcomes between groups at 14 days or later ( 97147 , 97152 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Asthma.

A small clinical study in children with asthma and allergic rhinitis shows that adding isotonic saline nasal irrigation 60-150 mL twice daily to standard asthma therapy for 12 weeks reduces bronchial hyperresponsiveness when compared with baseline, but not when compared with control ( 103084 ).

Bronchiolitis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that using a single dose of hypertonic or isotonic saline nasal irrigation 1 mL, in addition to standard treatment, improves oxygen saturation in infants with bronchiolitis compared to standard treatment alone. There were no differences between groups in symptoms such as respiratory rate and wheezing ( 97153 ).

Respiratory tract infections.

Although there is some evidence that nasal irrigation reduces symptoms of various respiratory tract infections, most studies have been small and have a high risk of bias.

A meta-analysis of four small clinical studies in children and infants with upper respiratory tract infections shows that saline irrigation modestly improves nasal symptoms, but not respiratory symptoms or health status, when compared with control ( 103082 ). One large study, included in the meta-analysis, also shows that isotonic saline nasal irrigation reduces use of conventional treatments including antipyretics, decongestants, mucolytics, and antibiotics ( 16141 ). However, there was no conclusive benefit when studies with children and adults were analyzed together. Meta-analyses of up to 2 clinical trials show that nasal irrigation does not reduce symptoms, shorten the duration of illness, or reduce the need for conventional treatments when compared with routine care ( 17910 , 93988 ). Reasons for the discrepancies are unclear, but may be due to differences in patient populations, small study size, and high risk of bias. Nasal irrigation might also reduce symptoms caused by infection with some coronaviruses. A small post-hoc study in adults infected with alpha and beta-coronavirus has found that using nasal irrigation with saline 2.6% reduces certain symptoms such as nasal blockage and hoarseness when compared with control ( 103087 ). It is unknown if these benefits apply to patients infected with severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2). Some experts state that nasal irrigation especially with hypertonic saline or povidone solution might physically reduce SARS-CoV-2 viral load. However there is also risk that nasal irrigation in a person with SARS-CoV-2 infection can contaminate the nasal solution, rinse bottle, and surrounding surfaces, potentially increasing viral transmission ( 103653 ). Recommend that patients with suspected or confirmed SARS-CoV-2 practice good hand hygiene and disinfect surrounding surfaces after nasal irrigation. Limited research has also evaluated nasal irrigation for PREVENTION of respiratory tract infections. A small clinical study in healthy adults shows that daily use of isotonic saline nasal irrigation for 10 weeks at least 60% of the time reduces common cold symptoms, including nasal secretions and nasal blockage, when compared with not using nasal irrigation ( 88360 ).

More evidence is needed to rate nasal irrigation for these uses.

Adult

Nasal:

General : Boiled, bottled, or distilled water should be used to maintain hygiene during nasal irrigation. Pots should be washed with hot soapy water after every use and should never be shared with other people. Rinse bottles should be switched to a new bottle monthly. Using iodized salt seems to be equally effective as non-iodized salt ( 103088 ). Asthma : Nasal irrigation with isotonic saline 60-150 mL twice daily has been used for 12 weeks in addition to standard asthma therapy ( 103084 ). Allergic rhinitis (hay fever) : With or without other conventional treatments, nasal irrigation has been used up to 7 times daily for up to 6 weeks. Types of nasal irrigation have included iodine bromide thermal water, hypertonic saline, isotonic saline (0.9%; including the specific brand Emser and NeilMed Sinus Rinse, NeilMed Pharmaceuticals, Inc., Santa Rosa, CA), or seawater as a spray or with a syringe, bottle, nasal douche, or high pressure device. Some solutions have been buffered to a pH of 7.2-7.4, although the majority are non-buffered ( 88348 , 88351 , 88352 , 88353 , 93984 , 93985 , 93991 , 97143 ). A 1% ephedrine-saline nasal wash once every 48 hours for 4 weeks has been used ( 88349 ). Some patients prefer using a squeeze bottle instead of a nasal syringe to administer nasal irrigation because of an improved fit within the nasal cavity and increased ease of water pressure control ( 103086 ). Intranasal surgery : Nasal irrigation starting 2 days after surgery for one month, with a specific isotonic sea water in a slightly pressurized bottle (Rhinomer Force 3), has been used ( 16136 , 88347 ). Alkaline nasal douches for 14 days following surgery have been used ( 88346 ). Nasal irrigation with lactated Ringer's solution three times daily for 6 weeks has been used ( 97149 ). Nasal irrigation with xylitol 1 gram dissolved in 60 mL of water and administered as a nasal irrigation in each nostril three times daily for 30 days, beginning on the second day after surgery, has been used ( 103889 ). Rhinosinusitis : Nasal irrigation has been used once or twice daily for 2 weeks to 18 months. Types of nasal irrigation have included a dead sea salt solution, isotonic saline, or hypertonic saline (1.8% to 2.7%), using a spray, pot or nasal cup, or bulb syringe. Products have included NeilMed Products Inc. and Deep Sea nasal saline spray (Major Pharmaceuticals) ( 16137 , 16142 , 88344 , 88345 , 88353 , 88354 , 88357 , 97148 ). Hypochlorous solution 30 mL twice daily for 4 weeks has been used for nasal irrigation. The hypochlorous solution was made by electrolysis of normal saline with a Salicid device ( 97144 , 97157 ). Respiratory tract infections : A daily saline spray for 10 weeks has been used ( 88360 ).

Children

Nasal:

Allergic rhinitis : With or without other conventional treatments, nasal irrigation has been used 2-3 times daily for up to 12 weeks in children aged 5-15 years. Types of nasal irrigation have included hypertonic or isotonic saline (0.9%) as a spray or with a syringe. Hypertonic solutions used were 1.25% to 2.7% saline. Some solutions have been buffered to a pH of 7.0-8.0, although the majority are non-buffered ( 16139 , 16140 , 93985 , 93990 , 93992 , 97150 ). Bronchiolitis : A single application of nasal irrigation with hypertonic or isotonic saline 1 mL has been used in infants in addition to standard treatment ( 97153 ). Rhinosinusitis : Hypertonic (3.5%) and isotonic saline nasal irrigation as drops in the nose three times daily for 4 weeks have been used in children aged 3-16 years ( 88358 ). Also, irrigation with a 1.25% buffered hypertonic saline solution, using a bottle or a disposable syringe, twice daily for 2 weeks has been used in children aged 3-15 years ( 93993 ). Nasal irrigation once daily with isotonic saline alone or with gentamicin for 6 weeks has been used in children aged 4-17 years ( 93994 ). Each nostril was irrigated with 15-20 mL normal saline1-3 times daily for 5-21 days ( 97155 , 97147 , 97152 ). Hypochlorous solution 30 mL twice daily for 4 weeks has been used for nasal irrigation. The hypochlorous solution was made by electrolysis of normal saline with a Salicid device ( 97144 ). Respiratory tract infections : Nasal irrigation with a modified seawater solution (Physiomer, Goemar Laboratoire de la Mer) 3-6 times daily, as a medium jet or fine spray, has been used along with routine treatment for 12 weeks in children aged 6-10 years ( 16141 ).

#### General

Nasal irrigation involves flushing out the nasal cavity with normal saline or hypertonic saline. Nasal irrigation is done using a variety of methods including application with a bulb syringe or using a "neti pot." The neti pots look like small tea pots. They are filled with saline solution and poured through the nostrils ( 16135 ). No method of nasal irrigation has been consistently shown to be more effective than another ( 16135 ). However, some patients prefer using a squeeze bottle instead of a nasal syringe to administer nasal irrigation due to a better fit within the nasal cavity and increased ease of water pressure control ( 103086 ).

#### Breathing effects

It is thought that saline irrigation simply flushes out mucus and irritants from the sinuses, improving the flow of air through the nose. However, nasal irrigation also appears to increase ciliary function of the sinuses and osmotically reduce edema. Saline nasal irrigation reduces nasal histamine concentrations for up to 6 hours and may reduce leukotriene and prostaglandin concentrations for up to 4 hours ( 16135 , 16136 , 93984 ).
