# Vegetarian Diet
Other Common Names Dietas Vegetariana, Flexitarian Diet, Fruitarianism Diet, Lacto-Vegetarian Diet, Ovo-Vegetarian Diet, Pescotarian Diet, Plant-Based Diet, Régime végétarien, Semi-vegetarian Diet, Vegan Diet, Vegetarianism, Whole Food Plant-Based Diet

## Overview

A vegetarian diet consists of a dietary pattern focused on consuming plant-based products and avoiding or minimizing the consumption of animal products. Major types of vegetarian diets are the lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet and the vegan diet. In lacto-ovo-vegetarianism, meats are avoided, while milk and eggs are allowed. In the vegan diet, all products originating from animals are avoided ( 98064 , 98065 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. A position statement from the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics indicates that a nutritionally adequate vegetarian diet is appropriate for adults ( 109259 ). The vegetarian diet has been used safely in clinical research without reports of adverse events ( 98055 , 98073 , 98075 , 98083 ). There is no reason to expect safety issues as long as the proper nutrition needs are being met. Nutrients of concern may include omega-3 fatty acids, iron, zinc, calcium, vitamin D, and vitamin B12 ( 109259 ).

CHILDREN: Likely Safe when used appropriately. A position statement from the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics indicates that a nutritionally adequate vegetarian diet is appropriate during infancy, childhood, and adolescence ( 109259 ). There is no reason to expect safety issues as long as the proper nutrition needs are being met. Nutrients of concern may include omega-3 fatty acids, iron, zinc, calcium, vitamin D, and vitamin B12 ( 109259 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Likely Safe when used appropriately. A position statement from the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics indicates that a nutritionally adequate vegetarian diet is appropriate during pregnancy and lactation ( 109259 ). Following a vegetarian diet when pregnant or lactating should not pose a risk to the child as long as nutritional needs are met. Nutrients of concern may include omega-3 fatty acids, iron, zinc, calcium, vitamin D, and vitamin B12 ( 109259 ). Additionally, one small observational study has found that consuming a vegetarian diet during pregnancy is not associated with lower cognitive function, as measured by intelligence quotient (IQ), in the child at 6-7 years of age ( 103641 ). Although some observational research has found an association between consuming a vegetarian diet during pregnancy and an increased risk for low birth weight or small for gestational age (SGA), most research has not accounted for diet variability or adherence or controlled for nutritional status or body mass index. When specific vegetarian diets are examined, only a vegan diet, not a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet, has been associated with an increased risk of SGA or low birth weight ( 103652 , 109238 , 109239 , 109241 ).

#### General

A nutritionally adequate vegetarian diet is well-tolerated. However, people following this diet may be at risk for deficiencies in specific nutrients, including omega-3 fatty acids, iron, zinc, calcium, vitamin D, and vitamin B12. Also, vegetarian diets that only avoid meat and contain large quantities of foods which are high in sugar and saturated fatty acids are likely to be harmful to health.

#### Other

Consuming a vegetarian diet can increase the risk for certain nutritional deficiencies due to reduced intake and/or reduced absorption: Iron from meat (heme iron) is more bioavailable than iron from plant-derived foods (nonheme iron). Additionally, meat and fish enhance the absorption of nonheme iron. People consuming a vegetarian diet require approximately 1.8 times more iron in the diet in order to meet nutritional requirements than people who consume non-vegetarian diets ( 99801 ). Zinc absorption is reduced by the presence of phytates. People who consume a vegetarian diet high in phytate-containing foods, including grains and legumes, may require as much as 50% more zinc from the diet to meet nutritional requirements ( 99801 ). Additionally, the recommended dietary allowance (RDA) for zinc is higher during pregnancy, which may increase the risk for zinc deficiency. A meta-analysis of observational research shows that those following a vegetarian diet during pregnancy have a lower intake of zinc when compared with those following a non-vegetarian diet ( 98059 ). Although lacto-ovo-vegetarian diets generally meet calcium recommendations, vegan diets have been associated with inadequate calcium intake. In addition, vegetarians may have a low vitamin D status ( 109259 ), which may increase the risk for osteoporosis. A pooled analysis of observational research has found that vegan or vegetarian diets are not associated with a clinically significant decrease in bone mineral density (BMD) when compared with non-vegetarian diets ( 98063 ). However, a more recent observational study has found that following a vegetarian or vegan diet is associated with lower BMD at the femoral neck and lumbar spine when compared with an omnivorous diet. Furthermore, following a vegan diet was associated with a 44% increased risk of fracture ( 103643 ). Vitamin B12 is not found in plant foods; thus, people following vegan diets must consume fortified foods or supplements ( 109259 ). One small clinical study found that people who follow a vegan diet are at increased risk for vitamin B12 and riboflavin deficiency when compared with those that follow a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet or a standard Western diet. Vitamin B12 and riboflavin deficiency occur in approximately 2.4% and 30% of vegans, respectively, compared with 0% and 10% of those following a standard Western diet ( 98074 ). Despite these findings, riboflavin is not normally considered a nutrient of concern for most vegetarians ( 109259 ). Neither a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet nor vegan diet seems to increase the risk for folate, thiamine, or vitamin B6 deficiencies ( 98074 ).

Possibly Effective
Diabetes.

The vegetarian diet is a healthy dietary pattern option for patients with diabetes. Some early research suggests that following a vegetarian diet may be beneficial for the prevention of diabetes.

Observational research has found that following the vegetarian diet is associated with a lower risk of diabetes. This association occurred in the Western Pacific region, Europe, and North America, but was not present in South Asia ( 98073 ). The vegetarian diet also seems to help control blood glucose in patients already diagnosed with type 2 diabetes. A meta-analysis of 8 clinical trials shows that in patients with diabetes, following vegetarian dietary patterns reduces glycated hemoglobin (HbA1C) by 0.3% and fasting glucose by 10 mg/dL when compared with a non-vegetarian diet. Compared to non-vegetarian diets, vegetarian diets also result in a modest reduction in body weight, waist circumference, and lipid levels ( 98082 ). The American Diabetes Association (ADA) states that the vegetarian diet is one of the healthy dietary pattern options available for patients with diabetes ( 97124 ).

Hypertension.

Consuming a vegetarian diet modestly reduces blood pressure and may be beneficial in patients with hypertension.

Consuming a vegetarian diet is associated with a reduction in blood pressure in observational and clinical research ( 98079 , 103647 , 103648 , 109246 , 109256 ). However, few studies have been conducted in individuals with hypertension. Meta-analyses and an additional clinical study show that following a vegetarian diet reduces systolic blood pressure by about 4-5.5 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by 2-2.5 mmHg when compared with non-vegetarian diets ( 98079 , 103647 , 109246 ). Another meta-analysis shows that consuming a vegetarian diet in combination with exercise for up to 18 months reduces systolic blood pressure by about 8 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by 4 mmHg ( 109256 ). Additionally, in a Scientific Statement from the American Heart Association, the vegetarian diet is mentioned as one of the dietary patterns that is linked to lower blood pressure ( 98053 ). However, not all research has shown benefit. One meta-analysis shows that consuming a vegan diet, usually for 12-24 weeks, modestly reduces both systolic and diastolic blood pressure only in those with a baseline systolic blood pressure of at least 130 mmHg. However, two other meta-analyses have found that consuming a vegan diet is not associated with a reduction in blood pressure in mixed populations of normotensive and hypertensive individuals ( 109246 , 109257 ).

Obesity.

Consuming a vegetarian diet for at least 3 months modestly reduces body weight and may be beneficial in overweight or obese individuals.

Clinical research shows that following a vegetarian diet for at least 3 months leads to weight reduction ( 98054 , 98064 , 98083 , 103645 , 109248 ). A meta-analysis of 4 clinical trials shows that following a vegetarian diet is associated with a 3.4 kg reduction in weight overall ( 98054 ). Another meta-analysis of 12 clinical trials shows that following a vegetarian diet for up to 18 months decreases weight by about 2 kg. In subgroup analyses of 6-7 trials, those with overweight or obesity lost about 2 kg while following the vegetarian diet, compared with 2.6 kg in those who were not obese or overweight ( 98064 ). Following a vegan diet seems to result in greater weight loss when compared with other vegetarian diets such as the lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet ( 98064 , 103645 ) or other plant-based diets such as the Mediterranean diet ( 109248 ). Additionally, the American College of Cardiology, American Heart Association, and the Obesity Society recommend a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet with calorie restriction as one of the options to help with weight loss in overweight or obese individuals ( 98068 ). However, not all vegetarian diets lead to weight loss. Observational research has found that more healthful vegetarian diets that include higher amounts of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, vegetable oils, tea, coffee, nuts, and legumes are associated with weight loss. In contrast, less healthful vegetarian diets higher in potatoes, refined grains, sweets, sweetened drinks, and juices are associated with weight gain ( 103650 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Anxiety.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for reducing anxiety.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet reduces anxiety levels overall ( 103642 ). However, a more recent meta-analysis of observational research has found no association between following a vegetarian diet and the risk of anxiety ( 109244 ). This research is limited by high heterogeneity.

Athletic performance.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for improving athletic performance; the available research is conflicting.

Most studies evaluating the vegetarian diet for athletic performance are small and utilize heterogeneous diets and endpoints ( 109258 ). In one small and preliminary clinical trial in moderate-trained CrossFit participants undergoing high-intensity functional training (HIFT), following a vegan diet for 4 weeks is no more effective than a mixed diet type for overall functional improvement ( 109243 ). Also, a large observational study in distance runners has found that following a vegetarian or vegan diet is associated with similar or slower finishing times in both half-marathon and full marathon distances when compared with the omnivorous diet. Potential confounders such as training, time since best run time, diet quality, or body mass index (BMI), were not taken into consideration in this study ( 109255 ).

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for atopic dermatitis.

In a small clinical study in patients with atopic dermatitis, following a controlled calorie-restricted vegetarian diet for 2 months improves symptoms when compared to baseline ( 98077 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Cancer.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for preventing various forms of cancer.

Observational research has found that following vegetarian, semi-vegetarian, or pesco-vegetarian diets is associated with a reduced risk of cancer when compared with non-vegetarian diets. Individual meta-analyses of observational research have also found that following a vegetarian diet is associated with a reduced risk of breast, colorectal, and prostate cancers ( 98060 , 98065 ). Additionally, the American Cancer Society recommends the consumption of fruits and vegetables and states that it is reasonable to try a vegetarian diet to reduce cancer risk ( 98071 ).

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for the treatment or prevention of CVD.

Retrospective research has found that following the vegetarian diet is associated with a 14% to 40% reduced risk of developing ischemic heart disease (IHD) and a 24% to 29% reduced risk of death from IHD when compared with following non-vegetarian diets ( 98065 , 98069 , 98072 , 109247 ). For cardiovascular benefits such as reduced CVD mortality, the composition of the vegetarian diet should be high in healthful and nutrient-dense foods such as whole grains, fruits and vegetables, and low in refined or less nutritious foods like juices, sweets, and potato products ( 103636 ). The American Heart Association also states that following a vegetarian diet with nutrient-dense foods is one of the recommended dietary patterns that helps reduce CVD risk ( 98081 ). The effect of a vegetarian diet on cardiometabolic outcomes in patients with IHD has also been examined in a small, preliminary clinical trial. Following an isocaloric lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet for 4 weeks reduces low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol levels by 4 mg/dL when compared with a traditional omnivore diet. Levels of oxidized LDL cholesterol, body weight, and body mass index (BMI) were also modestly reduced. There were no differences between groups in high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol or triglyceride levels, blood pressure, or reaching guideline values of any cardiometabolic endpoint ( 109240 ).

Cataracts.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for cataract prevention.

Observational research has found that consuming a vegetarian diet is associated with a reduced risk of cataracts. However, a sub-analysis suggests that this benefit is limited to overweight or obese individuals; consuming a vegetarian diet is associated with a 30% reduced risk of cataracts in this population ( 109253 ).

Chronic kidney disease (CKD).

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for slowing progression of CKD.

Clinical research in adults with CKD shows that following a vegetarian low-protein diet supplemented with ketoanalogues for 15 months seems to slow progression to dialysis when compared with following a low-protein diet. To avoid one patient progressing to dialysis, the number needed to treat is 23. Patients in the vegetarian group consumed 0.3 grams/kg vegetable proteins and 0.2 cups/kg of essential amino acids as ketoanalogues daily ( 98060 ).

Cognitive function.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for cognitive function.

Observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet does not seem to impact cognitive function ( 103642 ).

Crohn disease.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for maintaining remission in Crohn disease.

In a small clinical study of patients with Crohn disease who were in remission, following a semi-vegetarian diet for 2 years was associated with fewer relapses when compared with patients that switched to a regular diet ( 98056 ).

Cystinuria.

Although there is interest in using a vegetarian diet for cystinuria, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of a vegetarian diet for this condition.

Depression.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of depression.

Some observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet is associated with a 43% lower odds of depression when compared with following a non-vegetarian diet ( 103644 ). However, most research disagrees. One meta-analysis of observational research has found no difference in depression rates between those following vegetarian and non-vegetarian diets ( 109244 ). In fact, most individual observational studies and meta-analyses of population research have found that following a vegetarian diet is associated with a higher risk of depression ( 103642 , 103646 , 109245 , 109254 ). The reason for these discrepancies might be due to differences in patient populations and types of diets included in the analyses. In one meta-analysis, a sub-analysis found that only the semi-vegetarian diet was associated with an increased odds of depression; there was no association between the odds of depression and consuming either a lacto-ovo-vegetarian or vegan diet ( 109254 ). However, the majority of individual population studies were conducted in individuals following a semi-vegetarian diet. Research in vegetarians is limited.

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for fibromyalgia.

A small clinical study shows that following a raw vegetarian diet for 4 months improves pain and quality of life when compared to baseline in patients with fibromyalgia ( 98057 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD).

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for preventing GERD.

Population research has found that following a non-vegetarian diet is associated with a 2.2- or 4-fold increased odds of having any or frequent GERD episodes when compared with following a non-vegetarian diet within a one-year period ( 109250 ).

Gout.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for preventing gout.

Observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet is associated with slightly lower levels of uric acid and up to a 69% lower risk of gout when compared with following a non-vegetarian diet ( 103640 ).

Hypercholesterolemia.

Although some research suggests that a vegetarian diet may lower lipid levels in otherwise healthy patients, it is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial in adults with hypercholesterolemia.

Population research has found that following a vegetarian diet is associated with a reduction in total cholesterol (TC), low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, and high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels when compared with following omnivorous diets ( 98084 , 103648 ). Clinical research also shows a reduction in lipid levels. In meta-analyses of up to 18 preliminary clinical trials, following a vegetarian diet for about 26 weeks reduces TC and LDL cholesterol by about 12 mg/dL and increases HDL cholesterol by about 3 mg/dL when compared with following a non-vegetarian diet. Subgroup analyses found greater reductions in TC and LDL cholesterol in male patients, patients younger than 50 years, and those with lower baseline plasma lipids and a lower body mass index ( 98084 ). Another meta-analysis shows that consuming a vegetarian diet in combination with exercise for up to 18 months has small to moderate beneficial effects on total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, and triglyceride levels. However, HDL cholesterol levels were also modestly reduced ( 109256 ). These meta-analyses were limited by high heterogeneity, low methodological quality of the individual trials, and no subgroup analyses to determine whether these effects occur in people with hypercholesterolemia.

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for preventing migraine headaches.

A small clinical study shows that following a low-fat vegan diet for 16 weeks reduces migraine severity, but not migraine frequency, when compared with placebo in adults with recurrent migraines ( 98055 ).

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for NAFLD.

Preliminary clinical research in overweight or obese patients with NAFLD shows that following a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet for 3 months reduces NAFLD severity by 67%, compared with 21% in those following a standard weight-loss diet. There were also modest improvements in alanine aminotransferase, anthropometric measures, glycemic indices, low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, and systolic blood pressure. There was no effect on aspartate aminotransferase or diastolic blood pressure ( 109251 ).

Overall mortality.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for preventing overall mortality.

Observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet, including lacto-ovo-vegetarian and vegan diets, is associated with a 24% to 29% lower mortality from ischemic heart disease when compared with following a non-vegetarian diet. However, although some observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet is associated with up to a 12% lower mortality from cerebrovascular disease and up to 9% lower all-cause mortality, a meta-analysis of observational research has found no association between these endpoints and consuming a vegetarian diet ( 98065 , 98069 , 103636 , 109247 ).

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for PCOS.

A small clinical study in adults with PCOS shows that consuming a vegan diet in addition to avoiding foods with a high glycemic index for 6 months does not improve weight loss, menstrual concerns, body hair, or rates of infertility when compared with following a low-calorie diet ( 98080 ). However, this study might not have been adequately powered to detect small differences between groups.

Psychological well-being.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for psychological well-being.

Observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet does not seem to impact psychological well-being ( 103642 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for RA.

A small clinical study in adults with RA shows that following a vegan, gluten-free diet for one year improves symptoms of RA and reduces immune system activity when compared with following a non-vegan diet ( 98062 ). In another preliminary clinical trial in patients with RA, fasting for 7-10 days, then consuming a gluten-free and vegan diet for 3.5 months, and finally consuming a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet for 9 months, reduces disease activity when compared with a control group that did not alter their dietary habits ( 98070 ).

Stress.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for stress.

Observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet does not seem to affect stress levels ( 103642 ).

Stroke.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for stroke prevention.

Observational research has found that following a vegetarian diet reduces the risk of overall stroke by 48%, ischemic stroke by 59% to 74%, and hemorrhagic stroke by 76% when compared with following a non-vegetarian diet ( 103639 ).

Ulcerative colitis.

It is unclear if following a vegetarian diet is beneficial for prevention or treatment of ulcerative colitis.

A case-control study set in India has found that following a vegetarian diet is associated with a 71% lower odds of developing ulcerative colitis ( 103635 ). Furthermore, a small observational study in Japan has found that teaching patients with ulcerative colitis to follow a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet that allows the intake of meat once every two weeks reduces relapse rates when compared with baseline ( 103638 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

More evidence is needed to rate the vegetarian diet for these uses.

A number of different plant-based diets can be classified as a vegetarian diet. The strictest diet is the vegan diet, which does not allow for the consumption of any animal products, including meat, fish, poultry, eggs, and dairy. The classic vegetarian diet, also called the lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet, does not allow meat, fish, or poultry, but does permit eggs and dairy foods in addition to plant-based foods. A pesco-vegetarian diet does not allow meat or poultry but does permit fish and shellfish, in addition to plant-based foods, eggs, and dairy. Finally, a semi-vegetarian, or a flexitarian, diet includes limited amounts of meat and poultry, in addition to a diet that is primarily pesco-vegetarian ( 98078 ). The most common vegetarian diets are the lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet and the vegan diet ( 98064 , 98065 ). A less common variation of the vegetarian diet is the Eco-Atkin's diet. Eco-Atkin's diet is the vegetarian version of the Atkin's diet. It is a low-carbohydrate vegan diet containing 26% of calories from carbohydrates, 31% of calories from vegetable proteins, and 43% of calories from vegetable oils ( 98067 ). It is important to note that health benefits occur with vegetarian diets only when the vegetarian diets contain nutrient-dense foods as opposed to simply avoiding meat. Diets that just avoid meat and contain large quantities of foods which are high in sugar and saturated fatty acids are likely to be harmful to health ( 98081 ).

#### IRON

Consuming meat and fish enhances the absorption of plant-derived iron (nonheme iron). Individuals who consume vegetarian diets may have reduced absorption of nonheme iron from the diet when compared with those consuming non-vegetarian diets ( 99801 ).

Iron from meat (heme iron) is more bioavailable than iron from plant-derived foods (nonheme iron). Additionally, meat and fish enhance the absorption of nonheme iron. People consuming a vegetarian diet require approximately 1.8 times more iron in order to meet nutritional requirements than those consuming non-vegetarian diets ( 99801 ).

#### OSTEOPOROSIS

Vegetarian diets have been associated with inadequate calcium intake and increased risk for osteoporosis. Although older observational research did not find a risk ( 98063 ), more recent observational research has found that following a vegetarian or vegan diet is associated with lower bone mineral density (BMD) at the femoral neck and lumbar spine when compared with those following an omnivorous diet. Furthermore, following a vegan diet was associated with a 44% increased risk of fracture ( 103643 ).

Vegetarian diets are typically well-balanced and healthy diets that promote increased consumption of vegetables, fruits, whole grains, and legumes ( 98073 ). Vegetarian diets are of interest for maintenance of a healthy body weight and blood pressure, as well as cardiovascular health and kidney health, among others. Vegetarian dietary patterns are naturally lower in calorie intake due to the lower fat and higher fiber content of plant-based foods, which helps to explain the increased weight loss and greater glycemic control seen in clinical trials ( 98082 ). Vegetarian diets are typically lower in sodium and higher in potassium, possibly explaining some of the beneficial effects on blood pressure levels ( 109256 ). In healthy adults, a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet has been shown to have beneficial effects on kidney function, with decreases in creatinine and blood urea nitrogen levels, and improvements in the the estimated glomerular filtration rate ( 109249 ). Also, consuming a vegetarian diet for 5 days increases urinary pH and decreases urinary sodium ( 109252 ). A meta-analysis of human research shows that vegan and lacto-ovo-vegetarian diets are associated with reduced levels of C-reactive protein (CRP) when compared with an omnivorous diet. An association between vegetarian diets and levels of other inflammatory markers is unclear due to limited research ( 109242 ). There is also some evidence that consuming a vegetarian diet may result in slight shifts in the gut microbiome ( 109240 ). It is important to note that health benefits occur with vegetarian diets only when the vegetarian diets contain nutrient-dense foods as opposed to simply avoiding meat. Diets that just avoid meat and contain large quantities of foods which are high in sugar and saturated fatty acids are likely to be harmful to health ( 98081 ).
