# Chiropractic
Other Common Names Chiro Therapy, Chiropractic Physician, Chiropractie, Chiropractors, Chiropraticien, Chiropratique, Chiropraxie, Chirotherapy, Manipulation Rachidienne, Manipulation Vertébrale, Manipulative Therapy, Physical Medicine, Quiropráctica, SMT, Spinal Manipulative Therapy, Subluxation

Caution

Chiropractic should not be confused with Osteopathic Manipulation .

## Overview

Chiropractic is an alternative treatment modality that focuses on manipulation of the spine. Chiropractors believe in the inherent ability of the body to heal. This is referred to as the body's "innate intelligence". Therefore, the focus of chiropractic is to attempt to normalize bodily function in order to restore health ( 15947 ). Many believe it is so popular that it should no longer be considered "alternative" medicine. In the US, chiropractors complete 4 academic years of professional training. Licensure of chiropractors is available in 46 states ( 15947 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Chiropractic uses spinal manipulative therapy to treat a variety of conditions. This technique is safe when used appropriately ( 15948 , 15949 , 15950 , 15951 , 15952 , 15953 , 15954 , 15955 , 15956 , 15957 )( 15958 , 15959 , 15961 , 15965 , 15966 , 89105 , 98263 ); however, inappropriate use, such as neck manipulation or use of excessive force, has been linked to serious adverse outcomes including stroke, artery dissection, spinal hematoma, esophageal tearing, and spontaneous primary hemopneumothorax ( 15962 , 15964 , 15967 , 16855 , 91783 , 106822 , 111152 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when used appropriately in children short term. Chiropractic treatments have been studied in trials in both children and infants without any reported serious adverse effects ( 89135 , 89153 , 89154 , 89164 , 89168 , 112300 ). However, chiropractic manipulation in children has been linked to at least 9 cases of subarachnoid hemorrhage and several cases of less severe side effects such as headache and back pain ( 15968 ). It is important to ensure that the chiropractor has sufficient experience and skill to treat children.

PREGNANCY: Possibly Safe when used appropriately, short-term. Chiropractic treatment has been administered during pregnancy with apparent safety for up to 10 weeks ( 98264 ).

LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of chiropractic treatment during lactation. However, there is no reason to expect safety concerns with appropriate use.

#### General

Chiropractic spinal manipulation therapy is usually well tolerated when applied appropriately. Serious adverse effects reported with chiropractic therapy are typically associated with neck manipulation or the use of excessive force.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Headache, increased pain, tenderness or soreness of the treated areas, and tiredness. Sometimes these effects occur at similar rates when compared with control.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Cervical artery wall dissection, esophageal tear (Boerhave's syndrome), ophthalmic artery dissection, spinal epidural hematoma, spontaneous primary hemopneumothorax, stroke, subarachnoid-pleural fistula, subarachnoid hemorrhage, vertebral artery dissection.

#### Cardiovascular

Several cases of vertebral artery dissection have been reported following neck manipulation. In most vertebral artery dissection cases, symptoms develop within 12 hours of receiving treatment. Death has resulted in at least one case ( 15964 ). Some experts estimate that 20,000 chiropractic neck manipulations will result in one vertebral artery dissection ( 15964 , 104081 ). Two cases of carotid artery dissection and ischemic stroke are also reported following cervical chiropractic manipulation. Both patients recovered following usual care for ischemic stroke ( 111152 ). A meta-analysis of observational research found a 1.74 increased odds of cervical artery dissection associated with chiropractic manipulation of the neck ( 91783 ). Ophthalmic artery dissection after chiropractic manipulation of the neck and subsequent blindness in the right eye has also been reported in one case ( 101498 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

Adverse reactions are common in patients with neck pain who receive cervical spine manipulation. Reported adverse events occur in about 30% to 56% of patients receiving treatment for neck pain ( 15960 , 15969 ). About 13% of side effects are reported to be of severe intensity ( 15969 ). Increased neck pain or neck stiffness occurred in 25% of patients ( 15960 ). Chiropractic manipulation in children has been linked to several cases of less severe side effects such as headache and back pain ( 15968 ). In one case, a patient experienced an esophageal tear (Boerhave's syndrome) following excessive force applied to the thoracic and lumbar spine during spinal manipulation ( 16855 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

After chiropractic treatment for neck pain: ringing in the ears, tiredness, nausea, or dizziness occur in less than 8% of patients. Radiating pain and headache occur infrequently ( 15960 , 15968 , 15969 ). In one case report, a 36-year-old female reported to the emergency room with severe postural headaches. These are headaches that don't respond to standard medicine but instead are alleviated upon lying down and exacerbated when sitting or standing up. The patient had been receiving chiropractic treatments for neck soreness every two weeks for two years. The postural headaches occurred due to the vigorous chiropractic treatments that caused a subarachnoid-pleural fistula, subsequent cerebrospinal fluid leaking, and intracranial hypotension. The patient's condition only improved after surgical intervention consisting of multiple epidural blood patches ( 98267 ). Spinal epidural hematoma has also been described in several case reports following cervical manipulation. In some cases, this occurred in patients receiving oral anticoagulation therapy with warfarin (Coumadin); however, in other cases it occurred in patients who were not receiving anticoagulation therapy ( 15967 ). There have been several reports of stroke following cervical spine manipulation ( 15970 , 15971 , 15972 ). Some estimates suggest that this can occur from 1 in 400,000 cervical spine manipulations to 3-6 per 10 million manipulations ( 15947 , 15970 , 15971 , 15972 ); however, the true incidence of stroke following cervical spine manipulation is not known. There is concern that chiropractic manipulation might not be safe in children. Chiropractic manipulation has been linked to at least 9 cases of subarachnoid hemorrhage in children and at least one case in an adult ( 15968 , 104082 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

Upper spinal manipulation has also been linked to at least 14 reports of ophthalmologic adverse effects. These include loss of vision, diplopia, Horner's syndrome, ophthalmoplegia, and others. In many of these cases, the cause of the adverse outcome was cervical artery wall dissection due to chiropractic manipulation ( 15962 ). Side effects such as ringing in the ears, tiredness, nausea, or dizziness occurred in less than 8% of patients receiving chiropractic treatment for neck pain ( 15969 ). In one case report, a patient developed ophthalmic artery dissection after chiropractic manipulation of the neck, resulting in unilateral vision loss, headache, and drooping of the eyelid ( 101498 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

In one case report, a tall, thin patient with a significant smoking history presented with sudden onset dyspnea at rest and associated chest pain two days after chiropractic manipulation of the chest and back. The patient was found to have developed a life-threatening spontaneous primary hemopneumothorax requiring hospitalization and chest tube insertion ( 106822 ).

Likely Effective
Back pain.

Most clinical research shows that chiropractic manipulation and traction can reduce low back pain and improve function in adults.

In 2017, the American College of Physicians (ACP) published guidelines recommending that adults with acute or subacute low back pain try non-drug therapy, such as chiropractic treatment, prior to drug therapy ( 95773 ). Clinical practice guidelines published in 2023 and primarily developed by chiropractors for chiropractors in the US recommend a variety of active and passive interventions for the management of low back pain in adults. For acute back pain, passive approaches are preferred. These include spinal manipulation and mobilization, acupuncture, low-level laser therapy, massage, and transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation. For chronic back pain, a multimodal approach is preferred, including passive interventions along with more active interventions, such as exercise, yoga, meditation, cognitive behavioral therapy, and other lifestyle interventions, among others. These guidelines also stress the importance of multidisciplinary care in patients with back pain ( 112301 ). Most clinical research shows that chiropractic manipulation and traction can reduce low back pain and improve function in adults when compared with placebo or sham treatment ( 15957 , 15963 , 15965 , 29536 , 89105 , 89107 , 89108 , 89114 , 89116 ) ( 89118 , 89124 , 89129 , 89134 , 89132 , 91789 ). Some clinical research shows that chiropractic treatment is comparable to conventional treatments such as physical therapy, exercise, and pain medications for reducing lower back pain ( 15966 , 89106 , 89109 , 89112 , 89115 , 89122 , 101490 ). Other clinical research shows that using chiropractic care as an adjunct to conventional treatments is more effective for improving low back pain, disability, and quality of life than conventional treatment alone ( 101493 , 111154 ). While a large clinical study suggests that chiropractic care alone is slightly less effective for improving sub-acute or chronic low back pain and disability at 52 weeks than multidisciplinary integrative care, it is unclear if the difference is clinically significant ( 110782 ). Additional preliminary clinical research shows that chiropractic care is more effective for acute back pain, while physical therapy is more effective for chronic back pain ( 89110 ). One meta-analysis shows that chiropractic manipulation results in a greater magnitude of improvement in pain and disability when compared with chiropractic mobilization ( 101492 ). Although most research shows an overall improvement in back pain after chiropractic treatment, the magnitude of improvement might be small for many individual patients. Observational research has found that although adults receiving chiropractic care for low back pain have improved pain and disability on a population level, the proportion of individual adults with substantial improvement was only 13% to 30% ( 101495 , 106820 ). Despite the low magnitude of benefit in many adults, other observational research has found that receiving chiropractic treatment for spinal pain or low back pain is associated with approximately half the chance of filling an opioid or benzodiazepine prescription ( 104086 , 110783 , 111153 ). Chiropractic manipulation has also been evaluated for the management of spinal pain in children. One large clinical study in Danish children aged 9-15 years shows that adding chiropractic manipulation to standard care involving advice, exercise, and soft-tissue treatment does not reduce the number of recurrent pain episodes or improve the severity or duration of pain when compared with standard care alone ( 101496 ).

Possibly Effective
Migraine headache.

Chiropractic manipulation seems to reduce the frequency of migraine headaches.

A meta-analysis of small, heterogeneous clinical studies in patients with migraine shows that chiropractic spinal manipulation moderately reduces migraine frequency and intensity when compared with either active or passive controls. The magnitude of benefit with spinal manipulation seems to be slightly greater than with passive controls ( 104378 ). Individual clinical studies show that chiropractic spinal manipulation reduces migraine frequency and severity when compared with no additional treatment ( 98263 , 104084 ). However, chiropractic manipulation does not seem to be more effective than amitriptyline or a sham chiropractic procedure ( 89088 , 98263 ).

Neck pain.

Most clinical research shows that thoracic or cervical chiropractic manipulation can reduce pain and improve function in adults with acute or chronic neck pain. Chiropractic manipulation also seems to improve the incidence, severity, and duration of cervicogenic headache in adults.

Most clinical research shows that thoracic or cervical chiropractic manipulation can reduce pain and improve function in adults with acute or chronic neck pain when compared with inactive and active controls ( 15953 , 15961 , 89159 , 91790 ). A small clinical study of younger adults with neck pain and tension headaches shows that chiropractic manipulation of the upper cervical spine, along with exercise, reduces neck pain when compared with exercise alone. Chiropractic manipulation also improves neck disability scores when compared with myofascial release plus exercise or exercise alone ( 111155 ). Other clinical research shows that chiropractic treatment reduces neck pain only when combined with other therapies such as exercise ( 15956 , 89158 , 89161 ). Some clinical research also shows that manual chiropractic adjustments reduce neck pain and improve function after whiplash injuries ( 89175 , 89176 , 89177 ). Research in children, however, has not demonstrated similar benefits. A large clinical study in Danish children aged 9-15 years shows that adding chiropractic manipulation to standard care involving advice, exercise, and soft-tissue treatment does not reduce the number of recurrent neck pain episodes or improve the severity or duration of pain when compared with standard care alone ( 101496 ). Some preliminary clinical research has also evaluated chiropractic manipulation for the management of cervicogenic headache, a headache caused by underlying neck pain. A large clinical study in adults with chronic cervicogenic headache shows that receiving 3 treatments with spinal manipulation therapy each week reduces the incidence of headache when compared with a light massage control. The magnitude of effect seems to be duration-dependent, with patients experiencing one less headache in a 4-week period for every 6 additional therapy sessions received ( 101497 ). Some preliminary clinical studies have also shown that low-amplitude cervical manipulation or upper cervical toggle recoil adjustments administered twice weekly over 2-3 weeks reduce the duration and severity of cervicogenic headache when compared with baseline or active control ( 89089 , 89102 ). However, an earlier study did not show a benefit ( 89091 ).

Tension headache.

Chiropractic spinal manipulation seems to reduce the frequency of headaches and the associated use of analgesia in patients with tension headaches.

Most clinical research shows that chiropractic spinal manipulation therapy reduces the frequency of headaches and also reduces analgesic use in people with tension headaches ( 15951 , 15954 , 89090 , 89101 , 89103 ); however, methodological flaws such as inadequate or no control group limit the validity of these findings. A small clinical study of younger adults with tension headaches and neck pain shows that chiropractic manipulation of the upper cervical spine, along with exercise, reduces headache pain and frequency when compared with myofascial release plus exercise or exercise alone ( 111155 ). The long-term effect of chiropractic for tension headaches has not been assessed.

Possibly Ineffective
Asthma.

Chiropractic manipulation does not seem to be beneficial for asthma.

Most clinical research shows no difference between chiropractic manipulation and sham treatment for asthma ( 15958 , 89136 , 89137 , 89138 ); however, some patients report that chiropractic treatment reduces subjective measures of asthma severity ( 89136 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that adding chiropractic spinal manipulation to standard care for pediatric asthma might improve subjective measures of asthma severity and quality of life in children; however, it does not significantly improve objective measures of lung function ( 15950 , 89135 ).

Cognitive function.

Chiropractic manipulation does not seem to improve reaction or response time in healthy adults.

Clinical research in healthy active-duty military personnel shows that receiving four chiropractic manipulative therapy treatments over 2 weeks does not improve reaction or response time when compared with a waitlist control ( 101494 ).

Hypertension.

Chiropractic manipulation does not seem to lower blood pressure in patients with hypertension.

Most clinical research shows that chiropractic treatment does not reduce blood pressure in patients with hypertension ( 89147 , 89148 , 89149 , 89150 , 98265 ). Different chiropractic treatments such as toggle recoil spinal manipulation and treatment using a chiropractic hand-held device (Activator) have been shown to be ineffective for lowering blood pressure ( 89148 , 98265 ). However, there are a few singular cases where chiropractic treatment has been reported to reduce blood pressure ( 89151 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Anxiety.

It is unclear if chiropractic care is beneficial in patients with anxiety.

Preliminary clinical research in college students with phobic anxiety shows that chiropractic care reduces emotional arousal when compared to baseline ( 89142 ). The lack of a control group and the small population limit the validity of these results.

Athletic performance.

Chiropractic manipulation might improve athletic performance in adults with musculoskeletal complaints, but not in those without musculoskeletal issues.

A clinical study in military personnel with self-reported low back pain shows that receiving individualized chiropractic spinal manipulation over 4 weeks improves strength, endurance, and disability due to pain when compared with waitlist control ( 104085 ). Chiropractic manipulation also seems to be beneficial in joint dysfunction. A small clinical study in female athletes with talocrural or ankle-joint dysfunction shows that chiropractic high-velocity, low-amplitude manipulation once weekly for 3 weeks modestly increases vertical jump height when compared with sham therapy ( 91785 ). However, chiropractic therapy does not seem to improve athletic performance in adults without musculoskeletal issues. A small clinical study in healthy, recreational athletes shows that receiving a single spinal manipulation therapy session prior to exercise modestly impairs athletic performance, as measured by handgrip strength, jumping ability, and cycling performance, when compared with sham spinal manipulation ( 101500 ).

Carpal tunnel syndrome.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that chiropractic care does not significantly improve symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome ( 15955 ). However, other preliminary research in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome shows that chiropractic treatment is comparable to ibuprofen for improving comfort, function, and finger sensation and can maintain pain relief for up to 6 months ( 89139 , 89140 ).

Colic.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in infants with colic.

Some clinical research shows improvement in infantile colic after spinal manipulation ( 15973 , 89153 , 89154 ). However, more rigorous clinical studies with a sham control show no benefit of chiropractic manipulation for reducing infantile colic ( 89152 , 91786 , 10689 ).

Complex regional pain syndrome.

Although there has been interest in using chiropractic care for complex regional pain syndrome, there is insufficient reliable evidence about the clinical effects of chiropractic care for this purpose.

Dysmenorrhea.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in patients with dysmenorrhea.

Preliminary clinical research from several clinical trials shows that chiropractic spinal manipulation is not significantly more effective than sham treatment for reducing pain related to dysmenorrhea ( 15952 ).

Epilepsy.

Although there has been interest in using chiropractic manipulation for epilepsy, there is insufficient reliable evidence about the clinical effects of chiropractic manipulation for this purpose.

Fall prevention.

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic manipulation for prevention of falls in older adults.

There is interest in the use of chiropractic treatment to improve sensory and motor function which, theoretically, might help to reduce the risk of falling. Some preliminary clinical research in community-dwelling older adults over 65 years of age shows that chiropractic treatment improves sensorimotor function as measured by choice stepping reaction time and sound-induced flash illusion. It does not seem to improve postural stability ( 98266 ). Whether chiropractic care actually decreases the risk for falls is unknown.

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in patients with fibromyalgia.

A small clinical study in patients with fibromyalgia shows that a 4-week course of 10-15 sessions of chiropractic care only marginally improves range of motion and pain when compared with control ( 89143 ). A small nonrandomized clinical study in patients with fibromyalgia shows that chiropractic care 2-3 times weekly for a total of 30 treatments improves pain, sleep quality, and fatigue when compared with baseline ( 89144 ). The lack of a control group and the small population limit the validity of these results.

Frozen shoulder.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in patients with frozen shoulder.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with frozen shoulder shows that adding chiropractic manipulation to a local steroid injection does not improve movement, pain or disability when compared with the steroid injection alone ( 89172 ).

Headache.

It is unclear if chiropractic care is beneficial in patients with post-traumatic headache or in children with recurrent headaches.

Clinical research shows that manual therapy or a mobilization technique in addition to analgesics reduces headache caused by injury, also known as post-traumatic headache ( 89092 , 89093 ); however, the validity of these studies is limited by the lack of sham control groups. A large clinical trial in children with recurrent headaches shows that a 4-month course of chiropractic spinal manipulation reduces the number of days with headache compared with a validated control sham manipulation procedure ( 112300 ). The chiropractic manipulations were performed either on the spine, pelvis, or temporomandibular joints depending on the individual child's biomechanical dysfunction.

Heart failure.

Although there has been interest in using chiropractic care for heart failure, there is insufficient reliable evidence about the clinical effects of chiropractic care for this purpose.

Herniated disc.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in patients with herniated lumbar and cervical discs.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with herniated lumbar and cervical discs shows that traction, flexion distraction, spinal manipulative therapy, physiotherapy, and rehabilitative exercises improve pain and sometimes reverse disc herniation ( 89156 , 89157 ). The lack of a control group limits the validity of these results.

HIV/AIDS.

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic manipulation in patients with HIV.

A small clinical study in patients with HIV shows that receiving chiropractic upper cervical adjustments for 6 months does not increase CD4 cell counts when compared with control ( 89146 ).

Insomnia.

Although there has been interest in using chiropractic care for insomnia, there is insufficient reliable evidence about the clinical effects of chiropractic care for this purpose.

Jet lag.

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic manipulation in junior athletes with jet lag.

One very small clinical study in junior athletes during trans-Atlantic travel shows that chiropractic care does not reduce symptoms of jet lag when compared with sham chiropractic care or a control group ( 15948 ).

Joint pain.

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic care in runners with joint pain.

Preliminary clinical research in runners with sacroiliac joint dysfunction and primary hip pain shows that a manipulative technique known to affect the sacroiliac joint helps alleviate pain when compared with a mobilization technique ( 89145 ).

Menorrhagia.

Although there has been interest in using chiropractic care for menorrhagia, there is insufficient reliable evidence about the clinical effects of chiropractic care for this purpose.

Nocturnal enuresis.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial for nocturnal enuresis in children.

Preliminary clinical research in children shows that chiropractic manipulation might decrease nocturnal enuresis when compared with sham treatment ( 15959 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the poor methodological quality of the available research.

Otitis media.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in children with otitis media.

Nonrandomized, uncontrolled clinical studies show that chiropractic treatment may improve symptoms of otitis media in children when compared to baseline ( 89165 , 89166 ). The lack of a control group limits the validity of these results.

Pain (acute).

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic manipulation in patients with acute musculoskeletal chest pain.

A clinical study in patients presenting to the emergency room with acute musculoskeletal chest pain of non-cardiac origin shows that receiving about ten 20-minute chiropractic treatment sessions over 4 weeks is no more effective than receiving instruction on how to self-manage pain with posture and exercise. Patients reported no difference in pain intensity after one year ( 91787 ).

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if chiropractic care is beneficial for patients with nonspecific pain.

A clinical study in office workers aged 18-65 in Turkey with nonspecific pain shows that chiropractic care modestly improves pain scores when compared to baseline. The interpretation of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group ( 111151 ).

Pelvic pain.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in patients with pelvic pain.

Small, nonrandomized, uncontrolled clinical studies in patients with chronic pelvic pain show that chiropractic treatment improves pain and disability when compared to baseline ( 89173 , 89174 ). The lack of a control group limits the validity of these findings.

Peptic ulcers.

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic manipulation in patients with duodenal ulcers.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with duodenal ulcers shows that spinal manipulative therapy for 1-9 days improves symptoms when compared with control ( 89141 ).

Physical performance.

It is unclear if the use of chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in older adults with neck and back disability.

A clinical trial in older adults with neck and back disability shows that receiving 20-30 minutes of chiropractic spinal manipulation at least monthly, but no more than twice weekly, for 3 months or 9 months, in addition to practicing rehabilitative exercises, reduces disability when compared with baseline. The duration of treatment did not result in a noticeable difference in treatment effect ( 104083 ). The lack of a control group limits the validity of these results.

Pregnancy-related pain.

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic manipulation in pregnant patients with pelvic girdle pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with pelvic girdle pain shows that receiving chiropractic treatment is no different than standard care for reducing pain, sick leave, disability, or general health status ( 98264 ).

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic manipulation in patients with PMS.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with PMS shows that spinal manipulation therapy and soft tissue therapy given three times during the week before menstruation improves symptoms when compared with control ( 89167 ).

Sciatica.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation improves pain in patients with sciatica.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with sciatica shows that spinal manipulation for 12 weeks alleviates self-reported pain when compared with baseline ( 89131 ). Some research also evaluates the chiropractic method of traction, where chiropractors use an electric bed to provide auto-traction to the lower back. Most preliminary clinical research shows that traction does not reduce sciatica pain ( 89104 , 89126 , 89127 , 89128 ).

Scoliosis.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in children and adolescents with idiopathic scoliosis.

Preliminary clinical research in children and adolescents with idiopathic scoliosis shows that chiropractic interventions do not improve initial curve severity, curve severity as a function of age, or frequency of care ( 89168 ).

Shoulder pain.

There is limited evidence on the use of chiropractic manipulation in patients with should pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with shoulder pain shows that chiropractic therapy appears to alleviate shoulder pain faster than physical therapy ( 89171 ).

Sprains.

Chiropractic care may improve pain and range of motion in patients with recent neck and ankle sprains.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with recent neck sprains shows that early chiropractic mobilization treatment reduces pain better than physical therapy ( 89160 ). Another small clinical study in patients with recent ankle inversion sprains shows that chiropractic ankle mortise separation adjustment improves range of motion and function when compared to control ( 89169 ).

Stroke.

It is unclear if chiropractic spinal adjustments are beneficial in patients with subacute or chronic stroke.

A small clinical study in patients with subacute or chronic stroke and moderate to severe motor impairment shows that chiropractic spinal adjustments and physical therapy three times weekly for 4 weeks improves Fugl-Meyer Assessment of motor function scores by about 6 when compared with sham chiropractic treatment and physical therapy. Results of a post-hoc responder analysis suggest that this improvement is clinically relevant ( 106821 ).

Vertigo.

It is unclear if chiropractic manipulation is beneficial in patients with cervical vertigo.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with cervical vertigo shows that combining spinal manipulation, manual therapy, electrotherapy, sedation, biofeedback, and an exercise program helps reduce symptoms when compared with baseline ( 89170 ). The lack of a control group limits the validity of these results. Additionally, it is unclear if any benefit is to due chiropractic manipulation, other therapies, or the combination.

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . Chiropractic manipulation may not reduce pain in patients with TMD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with TMD shows that chiropractic treatment for 6 weeks is no different in reducing pain when compared with placebo ( 89155 ). An additional preliminary study shows that the activator method chiropractic technique appears to be no different than sham treatment or self-care given for 6 months in reducing pain from TMD ( 91784 ).

More evidence is needed to rate chiropractic for these uses.

Chiropractic treatment techniques and applications vary widely with different patients and practitioners. To initiate treatment, chiropractors assess changes in musculoskeletal anatomy and search for restricted movements. They diagnose musculoskeletal abnormalities and dysfunctions. A chiropractic treatment is usually 15-30 minutes. A typical treatment course involves about 6 sessions usually given once weekly ( 16143 ). Chiropractic treatment or chiropractic manipulation involves a variety of techniques. For example, the technique mobilization refers to movement (lateral flexion, rotation, and traction) of the joints within their passive range of motion. Spinal manipulation or adjustment technique is a high-velocity, short-amplitude thrust manually applied at the end of the passive range of motion to a specific spine segment. Usually with this treatment a cracking sound is heard or felt. The intent of spinal manipulation is to increase the passive range of motion without disturbing natural human anatomy. Usually no more than four adjustments are given per chiropractic session ( 16143 ).

#### WARFARIN (Coumadin)
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Anecdotal evidence)

Chiropractic cervical spine manipulation can increase the risk of spinal epidural hematoma in patients taking warfarin.

There are at least two reports of spinal epidural hematoma in patients taking warfarin who have received chiropractic cervical spine manipulation ( 15967 ). Advise patients taking warfarin not to receive cervical manipulation treatment.

None known.

#### ONCOLOGIC DISEASE

Patients with an oncologic condition resulting in reduced bone integrity may be at an increased risk for fracture during chiropractic manipulation. In one case report, a 66-year-old adult developed worsening back pain and new spinal compression fractures after spinal manipulation. The patient was later diagnosed with multiple myeloma, suggesting that the presence of this oncologic disorder increased the risk for spinal fracture during chiropractic treatment ( 101499 ).

None known.

Chiropractic is an alternative medicine that focuses on manipulation of the spine. Chiropractors believe in the inherent ability of the body to heal. This is referred to as the body's "innate intelligence". Therefore, the focus of chiropractic is to normalize bodily function in order to restore health ( 15947 ). Chiropractors believe that most disease is caused by impinged nerves in the spine that are called "subluxations". These abnormalities are not necessarily anatomical, but are often functional and therefore are not identifiable by any objective diagnostic test such as x-ray. It is believed that spinal manipulation or adjustment can remove the impingement and restore optimal health ( 15947 ). Chiropractic spinal manipulation is also thought to reduce sensitivity to pain through a mechanism referred to as manipulation-induced hypoalgesia. However, a meta-analysis of small clinical studies shows that spinal manipulation does not seem to reduce sensitivity to pain or affect the pain threshold when compared with sham therapy ( 101491 ). Several spinal manipulation techniques are used. Different techniques apply force or pressure to different anatomical locations or in different directions depending on the need. Force is applied in using a variety of velocities, amplitudes, durations, and frequencies ( 15947 ). Although spinal manipulation is the primary treatment methodology of chiropractic, modern chiropractors also use a variety of other therapeutic techniques including heat and cold applications, electrical stimulation, exercises, and dietary supplements ( 15947 ).
