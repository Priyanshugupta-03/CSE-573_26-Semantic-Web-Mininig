## Overview

Animal-assisted therapy (AAT) involves using a trained animal to help adults and children recover from or cope with various health conditions. The therapy can vary by the individual, animal type, or type of treatment needed ( 96110 , 96111 , 96112 , 101859 ). AAT might involve the intentional incorporation of animals into a treatment plan or be less structured and involve activities or visitation ( 28449 , 101859 ). Although robot pet therapy devices are also used in biofeedback treatment plans, these devices will not be discussed in this monograph.

Likely Safe when used appropriately along with standard treatments for diagnosed conditions ( 28452 ). There have been some reports of animals used in intervention as carriers of disease-causing organisms that have been transmitted to patients ( 28471 , 28472 , 28473 ). Routine veterinary screening of both resident and visiting animals is recommended for all nursing homes offering animal-assisted therapy (AAT) ( 28466 ). AAT should not take place of standard treatments for diagnosed conditions.

CHILDREN: Likely Safe when used appropriately. AAT has been used without adverse effects in children ( 28452 , 101855 , 101915 , 101919 , 101924 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no reason to expect significant safety issues when used appropriately.

#### General

Animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is generally well tolerated, with few adverse effects reported. However, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Transmission of zoonotic diseases to patients, particularly those prone to infection. With equine therapy, falling and low back pain.

#### Immunologic

Some people have allergic reactions to animals used in AAT ( 28462 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

AAT involving horseback riding has rarely resulted in falling and low back pain aggravation ( 101919 , 101924 , 101927 ).

#### Other

Rarely, animals used in AAT may transmit zoonotic diseases to patients, particularly those prone to infection ( 28466 , 28472 ).

Possibly Effective
Anxiety.

Single sessions of various forms of animal-assisted therapy (AAT) seem to improve anxiety in adults and children. The long-term benefits of AAT for anxiety are unclear.

A meta-analysis of various types of AAT shows that self-reported stress and anxiety moderately decrease after an AAT session when compared with baseline. Changes in self-reported symptoms are associated with reduced heart rate, but not blood pressure ( 101859 ). Patients involved in these studies include healthy adults ( 101859 ), as well as those hospitalized for psychiatric ( 28449 , 28451 , 101859 ) or other medical conditions ( 96110 , 101826 , 101854 , 101857 , 101859 , 101938 ). Some studies have included children ( 101851 , 101856 , 101859 , 101933 , 106769 , 106770 ). Most AAT has involved the use of a dog as part of individual or group therapy. While both individual and group therapy show overall benefit, individual therapy seems to be the most effective ( 101859 ). Most research uses single sessions of 8-30 minutes in duration ( 28449 , 28451 , 96110 , 101826 , 101851 , 101856 , 101857 , 101933 , 101938 ). A reduction in anxiety sometimes results in a reduced need for pain medications ( 101826 ). While most preliminary clinical research shows that a session with a therapy dog reduces anxiety in children and adult patients compared with baseline, it may not be more effective than other activities, such as a therapeutic recreation session or interaction with child life specialists or social workers ( 28451 , 101851 ). Studies investigating the use of AAT for more than one session for anxiety are limited. One small clinical study shows that daily dog visits for 6 weeks do not significantly reduce feelings of anxiety or depression when compared with baseline in long-term hospitalized patients awaiting a heart transplant, many of whom had at least moderate anxiety, depression, and/or feelings of stress. However, the quality of the hospital stay was improved ( 101854 ). This study might not have been adequately powered to detect a difference between groups. In contrast, one clinical study in patients with scleroderma also receiving traditional treatment shows that 20 weekly sessions of dog-assisted therapy reduces feelings of anxiety more than traditional treatment alone ( 101857 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

Small clinical studies suggest that animal-assisted therapy (AAT) may modestly improve symptoms in children with ADHD.

A clinical study in children aged 6-12 years with ADHD shows that equine therapy for 12 weeks is as effective as pharmacotherapy with methylphenidate or atomoxetine 1.2 mg/kg ( 98726 ). Other clinical research on the use of AAT for ADHD has only evaluated patients who are not using medication for ADHD treatment. One of these small studies, conducted in children aged 7-9 years, shows that group dog sessions, attended twice weekly for a total of 4.5 hours over 12 weeks, improves parent-reported symptoms of ADHD, including inattention and hyperactivity. The improvement was more than that seen in the waitlist control group or in a group using a control therapy involving dog puppets ( 96112 ). The other small clinical study, conducted in children aged 6-13 years, shows that equine-assisted activities and therapy twice weekly for 12 weeks improves symptoms by at least 30% in 90% of children and reduces severity of symptoms to normal or very mild in 85% of children. However, improvements were not seen in all behavior checklists used ( 101924 ).

Autism spectrum disorder.

It is unclear if equine-assisted therapy or canine-assisted therapy are beneficial in children with autism spectrum disorder.

One clinical study in patients with autism spectrum disorder shows that participating in a therapeutic horseback riding program for 10 weeks modestly improves irritability, hyperactivity, and some, but not all, measures of cognition and communication when compared with children exposed only to a barn activity as control ( 98725 ). Other small clinical studies in children with autism also suggest that equine- or canine-assisted therapy might have some benefit. However, due to differences in reported outcomes and types or duration of therapy, it is unclear which, if any, symptoms are most likely to improve ( 101855 ). A meta-analysis of three studies in patients with autism shows that equine-assisted therapy does not improve behavior when determined using one specific behavioral endpoint, the Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales (VABS) ( 101921 ). The long-term effects of AAT are unclear ( 101855 ).

Back pain.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in low back pain.

A meta-analysis of 4 small clinical studies in patients with low back pain shows that riding a mechanical horse simulator for 10-40 minutes 3-5 times weekly for 4-8 weeks improves pain when compared with physiotherapy alone, core stretching alone, or usual care alone ( 106767 ).

Cancer-related pain.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial for reducing pain associated with cancer in children and adolescents.

A meta-analysis of 8 medium- to high-quality small studies in children and adolescents aged 3-18 years hospitalized with cancer or other diseases shows that AAT has a medium effect on reducing pain when compared with control ( 106771 ).

Cerebral palsy.

It is unclear if equine-assisted therapy is beneficial in children with cerebral palsy; some meta-analyses and small clinical studies suggest that it may improve gross motor function, particularly in those with milder forms of the illness.

The available research in children with cerebral palsy examines the use of equine-assisted therapy, the goal of which is to improve balance, muscle function, and muscle mobility. Most research suggests the largest benefit occurs in children with milder symptoms, including those able to stand and walk ( 101855 , 101915 , 101916 , 101917 , 101918 , 101919 , 101920 , 106763 , 106764 , 111164 )( 111169 , 111172 ). Individual small studies suggest that equine-assisted therapy may be beneficial for children with cerebral palsy ( 101919 , 101915 ). However, results of meta-analyses that include some of these studies are mixed. A meta-analysis of 10 clinical studies shows that hippotherapy (i.e., a form of equine-assisted therapy) or other equine-assisted therapies, including therapy with a horse simulator, modestly improves gross motor function, particularly during walking, when compared with usual care. However, equine-assisted therapy did not improve quality of life ( 111169 ). A meta-analysis of 7 clinical studies and case series shows that hippotherapy or therapeutic horseback riding improves lower-limb muscle spasticity when compared to baseline. However, there was no benefit of repeated sessions when compared with a single session; long-term benefit is uncertain ( 111172 ). Conversely, a meta-analysis of 4 studies shows that hippotherapy provided by a physical therapist, in addition to physical therapy, does not provide greater improvement in gross motor function when compared with physical therapy alone. Hippotherapy also did not improve gait or walking distance ( 111164 ). Notably, the studies included in these analyses were mostly small, low-quality, and utilized heterogeneous treatment regimens, ranging from 1 to 48 weeks of therapy. Further, the magnitude of benefit with equine-assisted therapy might depend on the severity of gross motor issues; those with relatively good gross motor function or with unilateral symptoms have as much as a six-fold odds of improvement in function when compared to those with more severe problems related to gross motor function or with bilateral symptoms ( 101916 ). In addition, other clinical research shows that while overall gross motor function, attention, and quality of life are not improved, the ability to walk, run, and jump is improved ( 101917 , 101918 , 106764 ). The benefits on standing were mixed in several of these studies ( 101917 , 101918 ). Overall, this suggests that equine-assisted therapy might be more likely to assist gross muscle function in those with milder forms of the illness ( 101916 , 101917 , 101918 ).

Dementia.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in dementia.

A small open-label, nonrandomized clinical study in institutionalized patients with cognitive impairment ranging from very mild to moderately severe dementia shows that a 60-minute canine-assisted physiotherapy session once weekly for 12 weeks improves communication and mobility scores when compared with baseline, but similarly to physiotherapy alone ( 106760 ). Meta-analyses of low-quality clinical research show that canine-assisted therapy does not improve daily life activities, quality of life, or cognitive impairment in individuals with dementia. However, limited evidence from one study suggests a moderate benefit for apathy ( 101849 , 101850 ). The effect of canine-assisted therapy on behavioral and psychological symptoms of dementia is inconsistent, with no evidence of benefit in one analysis and a moderate effect shown in another ( 101849 , 101850 ). The reasons for this discrepancy are unclear but may relate to heterogeneity among patient groups and specific therapies investigated ( 28459 , 28460 , 28461 , 96111 , 101849 , 101850 ).

Depression.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial for reducing symptoms of depression; the available research is inconsistent.

One small clinical study in patients with depression shows that 30-minute canine-assisted individual therapy sessions once weekly for 10 weeks improve symptoms of depression by approximately 33% when compared with a control group not receiving canine-assisted therapy. However, there was no benefit to symptoms of anxiety or pain ( 101842 ). Another small clinical study shows that daily canine-assisted therapy visits for 6 weeks do not significantly reduce feelings of anxiety or depression in long-term hospitalized patients awaiting a heart transplant, many of whom had at least moderate anxiety, depression, and/or feelings of stress. However, the study might not have been adequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 101854 ).

Down syndrome.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in children with Down syndrome.

Some very preliminary clinical research shows that AAT might be beneficial for children with Down syndrome. AAT with elephants has been shown to improve visual motor control in children aged 6-12 years with Down syndrome. However, there was no effect on balance or postural control. Dolphin-assisted therapy seems to help with verbalization ( 101855 ).

Fall prevention.

It is unclear if equine-assisted therapy is beneficial in preventing falls.

Small clinical studies show that equine-assisted therapy has some benefit on balance and functional ability in individuals aged 60-84 years ( 101930 , 101931 ); however, it is unclear if this benefit reduces fall risk. One clinical study shows that equine-assisted therapy twice weekly for 8 weeks slightly improves balance and the number of times an elderly person can stand from a chair in 30 seconds ( 101930 ). However, there is conflicting evidence regarding the effects of equine-assisted therapy on the time needed to rise from a chair, walk 2.5 meters, and sit back down ( 101930 , 101931 ).

Fetal alcohol spectrum disorders (FASD).

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in children and adolescents with FASD.

A small clinical study in children and adolescents aged 6-18 years with FASD shows that a 45-minute session of dog-assisted therapy once weekly for 3 months along with pharmacologic therapy improves social skills, severity of symptoms, and externalization of symptoms, but not internalization of symptoms, by a medium to large amount when compared with pharmacologic therapy alone ( 106766 ).

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in fibromyalgia.

A clinical study in patients with fibromyalgia shows that a canine visitation for an average of 12 minutes while waiting for an appointment at an outpatient pain management clinic reduces pain by a meaningful amount in 34% of patients, compared with 2% of those in the waiting room ( 101936 ). It is unclear if AAT reduces pain in fibromyalgia patients, long-term.

Heart failure.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in heart failure.

Canine-assisted therapy may have some modest benefit in patients with heart failure; however, it is unclear if canine-assisted therapy reduces the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events in these patients. A small clinical study in patients hospitalized with heart failure shows that canine-assisted ambulation nearly doubles the number of steps taken when compared to a historical control sample ( 101937 ). Another small clinical study in patients with advanced heart failure shows that a 12-minute hospital visit with a therapy dog decreases anxiety by a moderate amount when compared with those not having a dog visit ( 101938 ).

Intellectual disability.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in children with mild intellectual disability.

A small clinical study in children aged 10-13 years with mild intellectual disability shows that a 45-minute session of dog-assisted therapy once weekly for 10 months improves motor planning and sequencing and sense of touch, attention, and concentration at 2-month follow up when compared with control. There were no improvements in measures of psychomotor development when tested immediately after 10 months of treatment when compared with control ( 106765 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in MS.

A small clinical study in patients with MS and impaired walking ability shows that equine-assisted therapy once weekly for 12 weeks in addition to standard care improves balance, fatigue, spasticity, and quality of life when compared with standard care alone. While the changes do not appear to be clinically relevant when the entire group is taken into consideration, there was a moderate effect on balance in patients with more severe disability ( 101927 ). A meta-analysis of 4 studies in patients with MS shows that equine-assisted therapy for 8-12 weeks improves quality of life, fatigue, and static balance, but not gait and dynamic balance, when compared with baseline ( 106762 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Pain (acute).

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in children with acute pain.

A meta-analysis of 7 randomized and nonrandomized clinical studies in children with acute pain shows that a single session of canine-assisted therapy or multiple sessions of canine-assisted therapy over 3-4 weeks improves pain when compared with control ( 106768 ).

Postoperative pain.

Small clinical studies suggest that canine-assisted therapy might be modestly beneficial for reducing postoperative pain in children or adults.

A small clinical study in adults with postoperative pain following total joint arthroplasty shows that 15-minute visitation with a therapy dog prior to three consecutive physical therapy sessions beginning on postoperative day 1 reduces pain by approximately 59% when compared with those having the physical therapy sessions without the therapy dog visit ( 101935 ). Another small clinical study in children with acute postoperative pain shows that dog visitation after surgery reduces physical and emotional pain by at least 50% when compared with baseline ( 28452 ). An additional small clinical study in children shows that dog visitation for 20 minutes upon awakening from various pediatric surgeries lowers self-reported pain perception when compared with standard treatment without dog visitation ( 101934 ).

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial for PTSD.

A small clinical study in individuals that had experienced a traumatic life event shows that a specific equine-assisted therapy (Equine Partnering Naturally) weekly for 6 weeks improves symptoms of trauma, anxiety, and depression by a small to moderate amount when compared with baseline ( 101926 ). Another small clinical study in veterans shows that an equine-assisted therapy program as part of a 5-day live-in residential course has a moderate effect on symptoms of PTSD, anxiety, and depression when compared with baseline. If the veteran's partner was part of the program, the number of veterans diagnosed with PTSD at 3 months after the program was reduced by approximately 66% ( 101925 ). There is also some preliminary research suggesting benefit of canine-assisted therapy in children with PTSD ( 101855 ). A meta-analysis of 8 low-quality, mostly nonrandomized clinical studies in children and adults with PTSD shows that AAT improves PTSD symptom severity by a moderate or large amount at the end of treatment when compared with a waitlist control, but not when compared with standard psychotherapy ( 106772 ).

Psychological well-being.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial for improving psychological well-being in elderly individuals in long-term care; the available research is conflicting.

A small clinical study in elderly nursing home residents with chronic age-related disabilities shows that feline-assisted therapy, practiced three times weekly for 6 weeks, does not improve depressive symptoms ( 28457 ). Another small clinical study in elderly patients with chronic age-related disabilities shows that AAT involving visiting with and talking about dogs for 1 hour once daily for 5 consecutive days does not improve psychosocial functioning ( 28458 ). However, 20-minute dog visitations once weekly for 12 weeks results in moderate mood improvement in elderly individuals in long-term care also undergoing standard rehabilitation when compared with rehabilitation alone ( 102823 ).

Schizophrenia.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in schizophrenia.

A very small clinical study in patients with schizophrenia shows that canine-assisted therapy, as part of ten weekly psychosocial treatment sessions, improves hedonic tone and motivation when compared with administering psychosocial treatment alone ( 28453 ). Similarly, a small clinical study in patients aged 40 or older with schizophrenia shows that adding an hour-long canine-assisted group therapy session to usual care weekly for 12 weeks modestly improves positive and negative symptoms, self-reported stress, communication and interaction, and lower body strength when compared with adding an hour-long non-animal intervention. However, canine-assisted therapy did not improve cognition, agility, or mobility ( 111165 , 111171 ). It is also unclear whether any improvements are clinically significant. Studies have also assessed the impact of animal-assisted therapy on life skills. A very small clinical study in elderly patients with schizophrenia shows that attending 4-hour therapy sessions with dogs or cats once weekly for one year improves socialization, activities of daily living, and well-being ( 28455 ). Another small clinical study in mostly male patients undergoing residential detoxification treatment for schizophrenia and substance use disorders shows that canine-assisted therapy for 10 sessions over 3 months improves daily life skills and positive symptoms. However, the impact on negative symptoms is unclear ( 111168 ).

Scleroderma.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in scleroderma.

A small clinical study in patients with systemic sclerosis shows that a 50-minute session of canine-assisted therapy as complementary support to traditional treatment reduces pain and anxiety by a moderate amount when compared with traditional therapy alone. Furthermore, a long-term benefit on feelings of anxiety and depression was observed when the therapy was conducted over 20 weekly sessions ( 101857 ).

Scoliosis.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial for scoliosis.

Two small clinical studies tested the effects of hippotherapy (i.e., a form of equine-assisted therapy), 15 sessions over 10 weeks, when used in addition to Schroth exercises in adolescents with idiopathic sclerosis. The first study shows that hippotherapy improves spinal measurements and postural stability when compared with Schroth exercises alone ( 111166 ). The second study shows that hippotherapy improves measures of pulmonary function and aerobic endurance ( 111167 ). However, it is unclear whether these improvements are clinically meaningful or maintained in the long-term.

Social anxiety disorder.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) improves social anxiety disorder.

A small clinical study in children aged 8-11 years with physical disabilities in Turkey shows that individual feline pet therapy for 45-60 minutes weekly for 7 weeks using a structured program improves social anxiety scores when compared with children who received usual education ( 111170 ). However, it is unclear whether this improvement is clinically meaningful.

Spinal cord injury.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in spinal cord injury.

A small clinical study in patients with spinal cord injury shows that participating in repeated 30-minute sessions of a specific equine-assisted therapy (Hippotherapy-K) reduces spasticity after 93% of the individual sessions when compared with baseline ( 101932 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Stress.

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) is beneficial in reducing stress.

A clinical study in emergency medicine physicians and nurses shows that a 5-minute interaction with a therapy dog during their shift reduces some measures of self-reported stress at the end of the shift by about 25%, compared with no changes in those in the control group and an increase in stress for those participating in art therapy. Salivary cortisol levels were also decreased ( 106773 ). Similarly, a small clinical study in children aged 8-11 years with physical disabilities in Turkey shows that individual feline pet therapy for 45-60 minutes weekly for 7 weeks using a structured program improves self-reported stress scores when compared with children who received usual education ( 111170 ). However, it is unclear whether this improvement is clinically meaningful.

Stroke.

Some clinical studies suggest that animal-assisted therapy may improve recovery post-stroke.

A large clinical study shows that 56% of individuals with a stroke at least 10 months prior reported an improved perception of stroke recovery following two 4-hour riding and horse interaction sessions weekly for 16 weeks compared with 17% of those in the control group. Improvements were sustained for at least 6 months. There were also improvements in balance and gait ( 101928 ). Another clinical study in patients with hemiparesis and impaired gait following stroke given conventional treatment shows that equine-assisted therapy once weekly for 16 weeks improves motor impairment in the lower limbs and balance by a moderate amount when compared with conventional treatment alone ( 101929 ). A small clinical study in patients with a stroke at least 6 months prior shows that daily neurodevelopmental treatment plus gait training with a dog once weekly for 8 weeks improves gait parameters, including cadence, speed, stride length, and symmetric index, when compared with neurodevelopmental treatment plus gait training without a dog. Improvements were also observed in psychological activity, including depression and rehabilitation motivation, as well as pulmonary function and respiratory muscle strength ( 106761 ). Similarly, a small clinical study in patients with a stroke or traumatic brain injury shows that AAT with one of a variety of animals present during a standard therapy session increases alertness and concentration when compared with the session in the absence of the animal ( 101852 ).

Traumatic brain injury (TBI).

It is unclear if animal-assisted therapy (AAT) improves recovery after TBI.

A small clinical study in patients with a TBI or stroke shows that AAT with one of a variety of animals present during a standard therapy session increases alertness and concentration when compared with the session in the absence of the animal ( 101852 ).

More evidence is needed to rate pet therapy for these uses.

Therapeutic Techniques Animal-assisted therapy (AAT) involves the structured or unstructured use of an animal for therapeutic purposes. Individual or group AAT programs involve interactions with the animal, including touching, feeding, playing with, grooming, riding, and/or talking to ( 101850 , 101851 , 101859 , 101935 ). The most commonly used animals in AAT are dogs and horses. However, cats, birds, guinea pigs, rabbits, fish, elephants, dolphins, and other animals have also been used ( 101852 , 101855 , 101859 ). The goal of AAT is to provide a trained animal best suited to meet the therapeutic need of the patient ( 101939 ). This differs from animal-assisted activities (AAA) that use animals as companions ( 101934 , 101939 ). Types of Therapies Animals have been used therapeutically in office-based medical or mental health treatment sessions, group therapy, recreation therapy, activity therapy, day-treatment programs, and as visitors in institutional settings. They are also placed as living companions - both in the community and in institutions ( 101855 , 101859 ).

None known.

None known.

Chronic stress is a risk factor in a number of medical and mental health conditions. Animal-assisted therapy (AAT) may aid in stress reduction and promote relaxation, thereby reducing risk or symptom levels in stress-related conditions. Chronic feelings of isolation or alienation are also risk factors for medical and mental health conditions. AAT may aid in reducing feelings of isolation and improving social interactions ( 28463 , 28465 , 28466 , 28468 ). Some forms of AAT reduce blood pressure and stress hormone levels ( 101933 , 101934 , 101938 ). AAT may help decrease perception of pain by activating comforting thoughts regarding companionship or home ( 28452 ). Some research suggests that interaction with animals used in AAT can help motivate individuals to comply with conventional therapy and have a more positive interaction with their therapist. This may improve the effects of treatment. This might be especially beneficial in children with behavioral or learning issues ( 96112 ).
