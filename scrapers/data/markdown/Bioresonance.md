## Overview

Bioresonance is a healing technique believed to treat illness by normalizing electromagnectic waves emitted from the body. A device called the Mora device is used and believed to receive and assess electromagnetic waves. The device identifies abnormal waves, which are associated with disease, and then normalizes the waves. The normalized waves are then sent back to the person to treat the condition ( 15913 ).

## History

Bioresonance and the related Mora device were created by Dr. F. Morrell in Germany in the 1970s while performing medical tests for electro-acupuncture ( 99777 ).

## People Use This For

Bioresonance is used to diagnose disease or medical conditions based on electromagnetic waves. It is also used for treating conditions associated with abnormal electromagnetic waves including pain, headaches, migraine, rheumatoid arthritis (RA), smoking cessation, circulatory conditions such as lymphedema, and skin diseases such as psoriasis.

Possibly Safe when bioresonance is used appropriately. The Mora and the Transponder bioresonance devices have been used short-term in clinical research without reports of adverse events ( 99775 , 99777 ). Bioresonance is a non-invasive procedure and would not be expected to cause harm; however, this technique should not be relied upon to diagnose or treat medical conditions in place of conventional diagnostic techniques or treatments.

#### General

Bioresonance seems to be well tolerated. No adverse effects have been reported in clinical research with short-term use of the Mora or the Transponder bioresonance devices ( 99775 , 99777 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Lymphedema.

A small preliminary clinical study in patients with lymphedema shows that using a specific electric bioresonance device (Transponder) daily for 6 days, in addition to wearing compression stockings, seems to reduce symptoms of heaviness, pain, and paresthesia when compared with baseline ( 99775 ). This study was limited by a very small sample size, and the lack of a control group and statistical comparisons.

Psoriasis.

A small clinical study in patients with moderate to severe plaque psoriasis shows that receiving white holographic bioresonance light for 10-12 minutes once weekly and applying a bioresonance water lotion twice daily for two weeks seems to improve symptoms and reduce markers of inflammation when compared with baseline ( 99774 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a control group.

Smoking cessation.

Preliminary clinical research in cigarette smokers shows that applying a bioresonance device (MORA-Super, Med-Tronik GmbH) once for 45 minutes, and then taping a bioresonance electrode chip to the bottom of the navel for 1 month while also taking 5 drops of a bioresonance solution no more than 6 times daily, seems to increase the prevalence of smoking cessation and decrease cravings when compared with simulated bioresonance ( 99777 ). This study was limited by lack of randomization, which might result in inequalities in the treatment groups.

More evidence is needed to rate bioresonance for these uses.

Bioresonance involves a number of different therapeutic techniques and tools. The original bioresonance device is called Mora. To use the device, a substance related to the disease, such as psoriatic skin (for psoriasis patients) or cigarette butts (for patients trying to quit smoking), is placed into glass tubes and into the input electrodes of the bioresonance device. Electrodes from the bioresonance device are then connected to the patient and a small current within a range of 1-105 Hz is applied for about 45 minutes ( 99777 ). Another bioresonance device (Transponder) is hand-held and is used to apply bioresonance micro-currents and vacuum suction to the skin ( 99775 ). White holographic bioresonance light has also been applied for about 10 minutes per cycle to the affected area ( 99774 ). Bioresonance-based treatments such as creams and gels are made by using a transfer-plate to imprint the bioresonance current to aqueous material. The gel or cream is then applied by the patient at regular intervals ( 99774 ).

Bioresonance uses a device called the Mora device that is believed to receive and measure electromagnetic waves that emanate from a person's body using plane electrodes. The device identifies abnormal waves, which are associated with disease, and then normalizes the waves. The normalized waves are then sent back to the person to treat the condition ( 15913 ). The bioresonance device is also thought to be able to use plane electrodes to collect low electromagnetic oscillations emanated by humans or by disease-related substances, such as allergens, heavy metals, and addictive substances. These oscillations are electronically superimposed on the patient for therapeutic purposes ( 99777 ). Bioresonance is thought to produce antioxidant effects in the body which might be beneficial in various diseases states, such as rheumatoid arthritis ( 99776 ). There is no reliable scientific evidence that bioresonance is an accurate indicator of medical conditions or disease or an effective treatment for any condition.
