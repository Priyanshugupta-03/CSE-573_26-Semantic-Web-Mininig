## Overview

Kampo is a Japanese thought and medicine system for illness and health. The kan (han) character means "from China," and po (ho) means "way." Although kampo shares some aspects with Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), it is a separate system that utilizes herbal combinations, forms of acupuncture, and moxibustion. Kampo medicine was the dominant form of medicine in Japan until 1885 when the Japanese government endorsed allopathic medicine. However, kampo made a resurgence after World War II and is still widely practiced in Japan ( 15974 ). Practitioners of kampo medicine are not legally recognized or licensed in North America. In Japan, kampo is integrated into the mainstream healthcare system. Herbal combinations are manufactured under strict government supervision and laws. However, most raw materials are purchased from China, which has led to concerns with herbal product quality ( 15310 , 99632 ). There have been reported issues with misidentification of herbal species, which can lead to substitution of potentially toxic plants ( 12783 , 12784 , 12785 , 12786 , 12787 ).

Kampo medicine began over 1000 years ago when traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) was brought to Japan. At that time there was no scientific concept of disease or pharmacology in terms that can be related to our modern understanding of medicine. Therefore, the principles of kampo medicine were formed based more on philosophy than on science ( 14610 , 15314 , 15975 ). In kampo medicine, people are diagnosed and treated according to their "constitutions," and by a series of signs and symptoms that cannot be directly correlated to Western medicine ( 15975 ). Kampo medicine shares many of the key components, practices, and philosophies of TCM. To understand kampo medicine, it is important to know the meaning of the terminology used in its practice. Yin and You : Similar to yin-yang in Chinese medicine, Yin and You are believed to be the two forces that control the universe. Virtually all medical problems are considered to be due to imbalances in one of these forces. Yin is the feminine side of nature and includes tranquility, darkness, cold, wetness, and depth. You is the masculine side and represents light, heat, activity, dryness, and height. Yin and You are not the same as good and bad. Instead they are considered complementary forces. Kampo therapeutics are intended to correct imbalances of these forces to cure disease ( 7126 ). The Three Major Substances of Life : In kampo medicine, health concerns are mainly understood as disturbances in three major substances of life: Ki (Qi). This term, pronounced "chee," refers to the total energy of the body ( 7126 , 15309 , 15975 ). There are 12 meridians forming a continuous pathway throughout the body. Ki circulates through the body on these meridians. Kigong (qigong) is an exercise, practiced either through physical movements or meditation, that is used to stimulate ki and maintain energy flow and balance. Ketsu and Shi. These terms are more closely related to the Western medicine understanding of blood and body fluids ( 15975 ). Diagnosis : Sho is a term used to describe the kampo diagnosis or identification of symptoms or signs of disease ( 14610 , 15975 ). Pulse diagnosis is an important diagnostic tool used in kampo medicine, often in conjunction with tongue diagnosis. Kampo medicine practitioners take a radial pulse on each side and in three different positions. The pulses are thought to provide information about the internal organs ( 15309 ). The appearance of the tongue is also used to assess potential illness. Cues such as tongue moistness, coloring, or coating suggest certain conditions ( 15309 ). Herbs and Herbal Combinations : As with all kampo medicine therapies, herbals are used to correct energy flow and balance. Herbs are described as having four qualities: Action. Herbs are often described based on their primary action such as a "tonic" or as an astringent ( 7126 ). Affinity. Herbs can be described based on which organ system they have an affinity for. In kampo medicine, most herbs are taken as a fixed formula or a fixed combination of several herbs. Each individual herb is thought to address a particular imbalance in an ill person. Specific formulations are prescribed based on kampo-diagnosed imbalances. In each herbal formula, there is usually a chief or main ingredient along with several other supportive ingredients ( 7126 ). Nature. The nature of an herb usually describes whether it has "cooling" or "heating" effects. An herb's nature might also be described as energizing or relaxing ( 7126 ). Taste. Herbs can have different effects based on tastes such as sweet, sour, bitter, spicy, salty, or bland ( 7126 ). Acupuncture : Acupuncture is a modality that originated in China and is now commonly used throughout Eastern countries. Acupuncture involves inserting needles into over 350 key points along meridians in the body. Stimulating these points through a needle or through pressure (e.g., acupressure) is thought to stimulate the body to correct energy flow and balance ( 15309 ). In kampo medicine, acupuncture is often a mixture of Chinese, Japanese, and Korean acupuncture styles ( 99633 ). Moxibustion : Moxibustion is a modality that originated in China and is now commonly used throughout Eastern countries. In moxibustion, an herb is burned above the skin or on acupuncture points for therapeutic effect. This can be performed by burning a cone, stick, or loose herb a short distance away from the skin. The burning herb may also be placed on the head of an acupuncture needle to increase the temperature gradient of the needle ( 29750 , 29751 ). In kampo medicine, moxibustion closely resembles traditional Chinese moxibustion ( 99634 ).
