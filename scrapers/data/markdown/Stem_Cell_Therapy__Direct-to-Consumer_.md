# Stem Cell Therapy (Direct-to-Consumer)

## Overview

Stem cells are cells with high self-renewal capacity that have the potential to differentiate into specialized cells in the body. Stem cells function to regenerate and replace damaged and diseased tissues in the body. Two main types of stem cells are pluripotent (embryonic) stem cells and tissue-specific (adult) stem cells. ( 99564 , 99591 ). Stem cell therapy has been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for some specific conditions including certain hematologic malignancies and solid tumors ( 99550 , 99558 ). However, "commercial stem cell therapy" or "direct-to-consumer stem cell therapy" has become a multibillion dollar industry in which stem cell therapies are marketed for unapproved indications ( 99542 ). Clinics offering these forms of stem cell therapy often target patients with ailments for which conventional medicine does not have a cure ( 99551 ). But this practice is often unsafe.

## People Use This For

Intravenously, stem cell treatments have been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for disorders affecting the hematopoietic system such as acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), acute myelogenous leukemia (AML), neuroblastoma, retinoblastoma, medulloblastoma, large B-cell lymphoma, multiple myeloma, non-Hodgkin's lymphoma (NHL), myelodysplastic syndrome, and aplastic anemia. Stem cell therapy has been marketed to consumers for numerous unapproved conditions, including age-related macular degeneration (AMD), aging, vision loss, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), interstitial lung disease, diabetes, Parkinson disease, orthopedic disease, pain, sports injuries, neurological diseases, immunological diseases, respiratory diseases, cardiovascular diseases, cosmetic problems, urologic diseases and injuries, skin diseases, hair loss, Alzheimer disease, vision loss, gastrointestinal disorders, spinal cord injuries, kidney diseases, liver disease, cancer, fatigue, dental problems, hearing loss, muscular dystrophy, autism spectrum disorders, cerebral palsy, spinal cord injury, diabetes, and other conditions.

The FDA has taken action against the marketing of unapproved stem cell therapy. On December 18, 2018, the FDA issued a warning letter to the California company Genetech, Inc. for marketing intra-articular, intravenous, or topical stem cell products without approval and not following current good tissue practice (CGTP) and current good manufacturing practice (CGMP) ( 99549 ). A recent review of clinic websites promoting stem cell therapies found that 96% of all sites contained at least one statement of misinformation, ranging from errors in basic science to outright false claims ( 102554 ). However, there have been very few criminal prosecutions and actions of regulatory enforcement against commercial providers of stem cell therapies in the US, mainly due to the lack of financial resources and legal ambiguity. So far, there are only nine easily identifiable individual and class action lawsuits filed by 19 injured patients against providers offering unapproved stem cell therapies ( 99553 ). Direct-to-consumer stem cell therapy is also controversial and poorly regulated in other countries. In Italy, the courts have allowed compassionate use of unproven stem cell therapy in terminally ill patients with no alternative treatment options, but this is controversial ( 99551 ). In Australia, direct-to-consumer stem cell therapies are only weakly regulated, requiring documented complaints and investigations before regulatory bodies can respond ( 99559 ). In Canada, stem cell treatment is only approved for treating blood cancers, blood disorders, and solid tumors. However, some physicians are promoting unapproved commercial uses of stem cell therapies, but there is no regulatory framework in place ( 99560 ). In the United Kingdom, direct-to-consumer stem cell therapies are unregulated. A review of websites found that 79% had misleading information, a lack of coverage of adverse effects, unjustifiably optimistic statements of effectiveness, and questionable advertising using celebrity testimonials. None of the websites contained enough information for a patient to be able to give informed consent ( 109542 ). Many low-quality studies have investigated direct-to-consumer stem cell therapy for various conditions. They are typically small, underpowered, and not double-blind or placebo-controlled ( 109542 ). Many experts caution that most of these reports were published in journals with weak or no peer review process. Experts consider these reports to be marketing techniques rather than a real communication of data ( 99567 ).

Likely Unsafe when used parenterally for conditions not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) or for conditions not being evaluated under an Investigational New Drug (IND) application. Stem cell therapy that is FDA-approved or being investigated under an IND helps assure that the product is safe, pure, and of appropriate strength. However, direct-to-consumer or commercial stem cell therapy is illegal and has been linked to many serious adverse effects including, but not limited to, cancer, vision loss, sepsis, meningitis, pulmonary embolism, ventricular fibrillation, stroke, cardiac arrest, and death ( 99543 , 99542 , 99545 , 99555 , 99561 ). There is also concern that adverse events from commercial stem cell therapy are underreported. A recent survey found that 65% of surveyed neurologists reported patients that had received stem cell therapies in the United States or abroad. Of those neurologists, 25% reported their patients had adverse events from stem cell therapy including strokes, meningoencephalitis, quadriparesis, deterioration of multiple sclerosis, hepatitis C, seizures, meningitis, sepsis, spinal cord tumors, and death ( 99592 ).

#### General

Injections of direct-to-consumer or commercial stem cell preparations have been associated with reports of cardiac arrest, myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, pulmonary embolism, venous thromboembolism, skin growths, cancer, sepsis, pneumonia, necrotizing ulcers, meningitis and other infections, allergic reactions, Graft-versus-host disease (GVHD), stroke, hemorrhage, paraplegia, pain, blindness, and death ( 99543 , 99731 , 109539 ). Intravitreal stem cell injection has caused loss of vision and retinal detachment ( 99542 , 99543 , 99555 ).

#### Cardiovascular

Injections of commercial stem cell preparations have been associated with cases of cardiac arrest, heart attack, arrhythmias, and pulmonary embolism. In a case report, a 77-year-old male with pulmonary hypertension developed cardiac arrest and died after receiving an intravenous infusion of adipose derived stem cells. In another case report, a 41-year-old male with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy developed ventricular fibrillation after a myocardial injection of autologous "precursor" cells. In another case report, a 41-year-old male with a herniated intervertebral disc developed pulmonary embolism after receiving an intravenous infusion of adipose derived mesenchymal stem cells. Also, a female with a neurological disorder had a heart attack and died after receiving an intravenous infusion of autologous hematopoietic stem cells ( 99543 ). Intravenously, hematopoietic stem cell transplantation has a 5% risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE); there doesn't seem to be a difference in incidence rate between allogeneic and autologous stem cell therapy ( 99731 ).

#### Dermatologic

Injections of stem cell preparations into the skin have been associated with abnormal skin growths. In a case report, a female developed bone-like growth in the eye-lid after receiving injections of adipose derived stem cells around the eye for a face lift ( 99543 ).

#### Immunologic

In a case report, a patient developed an allergic autoimmune reaction after receiving unspecified stem cells ( 99543 ). A case of Stevens-Johnson syndrome has also been reported ( 99543 ). In another case report, a 27-year-old female had an autoimmune reaction of acute disseminated encephalomyelitis after an intrathecal injection of mesenchymal stem cells ( 99543 ). Graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) is another complication of hematopoietic stem cell therapy ( 99731 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Injected direct-to-consumer stem cell preparations have been associated with reports of stroke, brain hemorrhage, and other neurological complications. In one case report, a 69-year-old female with leg numbness and difficulty walking from chemotherapy complications developed a stroke and died after receiving grossly filtered bone marrow aspirate injected into her right carotid artery. In two other case reports, an 18-month infant and a 10-year old child developed internal brain hemorrhage after receiving bone marrow derived stem cells directly injected into the brain. The infant died from this complication. A 71-year-old female with chronic kidney failure developed neurological complications after an intravenous infusion of adipose derived mesenchymal stem cells. A 17-year-old female with multiple sclerosis developed severe demyelinating encephalomyelitis after intrathecal injection and intravenous infusion of allogeneic cord blood mesenchymal stem cells and autologous adipose derived stem cells ( 99543 ). And finally, a 74-year-old man presented with progressive lower extremity weakness and urinary incontinence after receiving intrathecal stem cell therapy in Russia. He was found to have a large mass involving arachnoiditis and polyclonal lymphocytic infiltration of the intrathecal soft tissue ( 102553 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

Intravitreal stem cell injection has been associated with reports of vision loss. In three cases, females aged 72 to 88-years-old received intravitreal injections of autologous adipose derived stem cells ( 99543 ). Vision loss occurred due to ocular hypertension, hemorrhagic retinopathy, vitreous hemorrhage, growths on the retina, retinal detachment, or lens dislocation ( 99542 , 99555 ). An analysis of reports of treatment of various retinal degenerative diseases using human embryonic stem cells, induced pluripotent stem cells, or human umbilical tissue-derived cells found multiple cases of severe complications, including retinal detachment and permanent vision loss ( 109541 ).

#### Oncologic

Injected direct-to-consumer stem cell preparations have been associated with the development of various cancerous lesions. In a case report, a 66-year-old man received intrathecal infusions of mesenchymal, embryonic, and fetal neural stem cells for ischemic stroke recovery at commercial stem-cell clinics, which resulted in glioproliferative lesions in the spinal cord causing lower back pain, paraplegia, and urinary incontinence ( 99543 , 99545 ). In another case report, a 13-year-old male developed glioneural tumors after receiving fetal neural stem cells injected into the cerebellum and the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). A 58-year-old female developed pea-sized tumors after receiving a skin injection of human embryonic stem cells. A 46-year-old female developed multiple angiomyeloproliferative renal tumors after receiving percutaneous injections of hematopoietic stem cells into the renal regions on both sides. Finally, an 18-year-old female with a spinal cord injury developed an intramedullary spinal tumor mass after receiving an intraspinal transplantation of olfactory mucosal stem cells ( 99543 ).

#### Other

Injected direct-to-consumer stem cell preparations have been associated with reports of sepsis, pneumonia, necrotizing ulcers, meningitis, and other infections. Intra-articular injections of contaminated stem cells have resulted in mutiple cases of infections, most of which were bloodstream infections. Infecting micro-organisms included Escherichia coli, Proteus mirabilis, Enterococcus faecalis, Citrobacter freundii, Citrobacter koseri, and Enterobacter cloacae ( 99561 ). In a case report, a 63-year-old female developed necrotizing metachronous facial ulcerations after receiving fatty aspirate from the abdominal wall injected into the face after a "stem cell face lift" procedure ( 99543 ). In two case reports, two males over 70 years of age developed pneumonia after receiving animal-based stem cells ( 99543 ). In a review of 360 patients with adverse effects after stem cell therapy, there were 2 cases of septicemia ( 109539 ). There have been numerous case reports of death after stem cell therapy. In a review of 360 reports of adverse effects, at least one-third required hospitalization, and there were 20 deaths ( 109539 ). In a case report, a 27-year-old male with disabilities from a minor stroke died after receiving spinal and intramuscular injections of non-specified allogeneic stem cells. In a case report, a 63-year-old female with liver cirrhosis from hepatitis B infection developed coma and then died after receiving a non-specified stem cell intervention. In two case reports, two elderly males died after receiving adipose derived stem cells. One of them had developed a pulmonary embolism and the other had died of unstated causes. A 59-year-old female with idiopathic bronchiectasis died after receiving a non-specified stem cell Intervention. Finally, a 75-year-old female with dementia received autologous adipose stem cells during a liposuction procedure causing blood loss, hypovolemic shock, and death ( 99543 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Angina.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with angina despite optimal medical therapy shows that stem cell therapy administered as percutaneous trans-endocardial or intracoronary injections might reduce episodes of angina. However, stem cell therapy also might worsen left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) and perfusion defects when compared with control in these patients ( 99599 ). This analysis was limited by significant heterogeneity and errors/inconsistencies in the reports. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Back pain.

A meta-analysis of five observational studies and one clinical study found that mesenchymal stem cell injections into the lumbar disc are associated with reduced lower back pain. The validity of these results is limited by the fact that data from observational and clinical research were evaluated together. Also, the only clinical trial included in the meta-analysis suggests that allogeneic mesenchymal stem cell injections do not improve back pain when compared with control ( 99597 ). Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Cerebral palsy.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that central nervous system transplantation or intravenous/arterial infusion of stem cells does not improve gross motor movement in children with cerebral palsy when compared with control ( 99730 ).

Congestive heart failure (CHF).

A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with chronic ischemic heart disease and congestive heart failure shows that bone marrow-derived stem cell therapy does not reduce the risk of re-hospitalization for heart failure and does not affect left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) long-term when compared with control. However, stem cell therapy might reduce mortality and non-fatal myocardial infarction in this population ( 99729 ). Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Crohn disease.

A meta-analysis of observational and clinical research in patients with Crohn disease found that intravenous infusion of hematopoietic stem cells or intravenous or local injections of bone marrow-derived mesenchymal stem cells is associated with improved clinical response and increased rate of clinical remission ( 99725 ). Although this is promising, higher quality research is needed to confirm. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Diabetes.

Observational research in patients with type 1 and type 2 diabetes found that intrapancreatic and intravenous injections of stem cells are associated with temporary reductions in glycated hemoglobin A1C ( 99732 ). This is promising, but higher quality research is needed to confirm. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Diabetic foot ulcers.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients with diabetic foot ulcers shows that receiving autologous stem cells intramuscularly might improve wound healing ( 99600 ). This analysis was limited by ambiguous descriptions of outcomes and reporting inaccuracies. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Liver disease.

A meta-analysis of observational research and small clinical studies in patients with chronic liver disease found that mesenchymal stem cell therapy seems to improve liver markers such as total bilirubin and serum albumin levels ( 99733 ). It is unclear if stem cell therapy improves outcomes based on image testing or tissue analysis. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with severe and treatment-refractory MS suggests that autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplantation slows 2-year progression in patients with aggressive MS who do not yet have a high level of disability ( 99726 ). These results seem promising, but for now stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Myocardial infarction (MI).

A meta-analysis of small preliminary clinical trials in patients with acute MI shows that receiving autologous adult bone marrow stem cells does not affect mortality, re-infarction or re-hospitalizations when compared with control ( 99734 ).

Osteoarthritis.

A meta-analysis of small and low-quality clinical trials involving a total of 75 patients with osteoarthritis shows that intraarticular stem cell therapy modestly reduces pain compared to control ( 99593 ). The studies in this analysis were limited by publication bias, inconsistency, and imprecision of data. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition. Due to concerns related to standardization and safety, as well as the low quality of the available evidence, the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) strongly recommends AGAINST the use of stem cell injections in patients with knee or hip osteoarthritis ( 102114 ).

Parkinson disease.

Preliminary clinical research shows that stem cell therapy has no effect on Parkinson disease severity based on the Unified Parkinson Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS). However, there was a positive effect based on the Schwab and England scale ( 99598 ). Although this is promising, additional research is needed to confirm. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Peripheral arterial disease (PAD).

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical trials in patients with PAD shows that stem cell therapy might modestly reduce amputation and improve ulcer healing when compared with control ( 99728 ). The trials included in the analysis were limited by lack of blinding, allocation concealment, and incomplete outcome reporting. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Spinal cord injury.

A small retrospective review of adults with spinal cord injury who sought out stem cell therapy via stem cell tourism found that there was no improvement in functional outcomes, motor recovery, or bladder or bowel function when compared with baseline. In fact, 55% of participants demonstrated worse performance than expected based on historical cohorts, which might indicate a detrimental effect from the received treatment. These treatments were obtained in various countries and utilized multiple methods of administration, including lumbar puncture and subcutaneous, intramuscular, or subdural injection ( 102551 ).

Spinocerebellar ataxia (SCA).

A review of three small clinical studies administering intrathecal or intravenous multipotent mesenchymal stem cells extracted from umbilical cord and adipose tissue, either as a single dose or once weekly for 4 weeks, with follow-up for 12-15 months, found no significant improvements in functional recovery, as measured using the International Cooperative Ataxia Rating Scale, the Activities of Daily Living scale, the Berg Balance Scale, or the Scale for the Assessment and Rating of Ataxia ( 109540 ). The studies included a total of only 47 patients, had a high risk of bias and low quality of evidence, and were not randomized or blinded. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

Stroke.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients after stroke shows that receiving autologous bone marrow mesenchymal stem cells intravenously or injected into another body part might improve function as measured on the National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS) and the Barthel index (BI), but not on the modified Rankin score (mRS) when compared with control ( 99724 ). This analysis was limited by high heterogeneity and poor methodology of the included studies. Stem cell therapy is not approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this condition.

More evidence is needed to rate stem cell therapy for these uses.

Stem cells obtained from the patients themselves are called autologous stem cells, while those obtained from donors or from cadavers are called allogeneic stem cells. Stem cells can be harvested from bone marrow, umbilical cord blood, peripheral blood, and other body tissues. Mesenchymal stem cells are isolated from connective tissues surrounding organs and other tissues, also known as stroma ( 99564 , 99733 , 109542 ). Stem cells are processed in different ways. One way is by directly harvesting the bone marrow, usually from the pelvic bone of the patient using large suction needles. Another way is mobilizing bone marrow cells into blood using a growth factor and then harvesting the peripheral blood. A growth factor increases stem cell growth in the bone marrow and increases stem cell concentration in the peripheral blood. The stem cells are thereafter separated from other bone marrow or blood cells in sterile conditions. These procedures may take several hours ( 99564 , 99729 ). Autologous stem cell-based interventions that are said to be sourced from adipose tissue seem to be the most frequently used type of stem cell in commercial stem cell therapy. The second most common marketed source of stem cells is bone marrow aspirate. Fewer companies promote stem cells sourced from autologous peripheral blood, combination fat and bone marrow stem cells, amniotic fluid, placental tissue, or umbilical cord tissues ( 99564 , 99567 , 109539 ). Once harvested and processed, stem cells are usually injected or "transplanted" into the affected or diseased body part. For diabetes, stem cells can be injected into the pancreas and also intravenously ( 99732 ). For back pain, stem cells can be injected into the lumbar disc ( 99597 ).

#### General

Stem cells are cells that can self-renew and can differentiate into more specialized cells. Two main types of stem cells are pluripotent stem cells and tissue-specific stem cells. Pluripotent stem cells, also called embryonic stem cells, can self-renew and develop into all of the body's cell types. Tissue-specific stem cells, also called adult stem cells, can self-renew and develop into cell types of a specific tissue, such as the skin, skeletal muscle, or blood. In addition, tissue-specific cells are sometimes manipulated in the lab to be like embryonic cells, which are then called induced pluripotent stem cells. Stem cells are important for the repair of the body's tissues and organs ( 99546 , 99564 ).

#### Bone effects

Osteoarthritis is a degenerative disorder which involves erosion of cartilage resulting in pain and stiffness. Articular cartilage and chondrocytes are limited in their ability to regenerate, so there is interest in using stem cell therapy to help reverse osteoarthritis. In theory, pluripotent and various multipotent mesenchymal stem cells might help to repair damaged articular cartilage. Stem cell therapies might be combined with growth factors to increase their effect on the cartilage. This approach has not been shown to work in humans ( 99548 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

There is interest in using stem cells to regenerate damaged cardiac tissue in conditions such as heart failure, cardiac arrest, and myocardial infarction. One theory is that transplanted stem cells might benefit heart function directly by attaching to the injured site and differentiating into new cardiac tissues. However, there is also a school of thought that the effect of stem cells is more indirect. Another hypothesis is that stem cell therapies primarily have regenerative paracrine effect. In this hypothesis, stem cells produce cytokines that signal for an increase in vascular growth and cardiomyocyte proliferation, reduce fibrosis, and activate endogenous stem cells. This might result in reduction of scar size and reverse modeling of infarcted tissue ( 99734 ).

#### Genitourinary

There is interest in using stem cell therapy to regenerate damaged body parts associated with the genitourinary system. For example, stem cell therapy has been studied for fecal incontinence due to damaged anal sphincter. In vitro research suggests that combining stem cells with normal cells on bioengineered scaffolds can build an intrinsically-innervated anal sphincter which then can be implanted ( 99566 ).

#### Neurological effects

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is associated with great morbidity and mortality. There are no treatments approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for TBI, so there is interest in using stem cell therapy to replace damaged neurons in TBI. Although animal research has shown benefit, human clinical trials have not been able to replicate improved functional outcomes in TBI ( 99568 ). Clinical studies that have shown behavioral benefit in using stem cell therapies in TBI are limited by publication bias ( 99547 ). There is also interest in using stem cell therapy to replace endogenous neuronal and glial cells that are lost after a spinal cord injury (SCI). Stem cells such as neural progenitor cells, glial precursors, mesenchymal stem cells, and Schwann cells are thought to provide a more favorable growth environment and to increase neuronal intrinsic ability to regenerate ( 99554 ). Finally, there is interest in using stem cell therapy to aid in stroke recovery. Animal models of stroke suggest that autologous stem cells might reduce the volume of infarction and improve neurological and behavioral outcomes ( 99556 ).

#### Ocular effects

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD), retinitis pigmentosa, and other ocular conditions can lead to retinal degeneration and loss of vision. Retinal photoreceptors (PRs) and retinal pigment epithelium cells degenerate and do not regenerate naturally. There is interest in using stem cells to replace these cells in order to improve vision ( 99544 ). Early-phase trials in several of these conditions have reported safety, but no evidence of significant efficacy. Direct-to-consumer stem cell therapies for retinal diseases have been associated with serious complications, including permanent vision loss ( 109541 ).
