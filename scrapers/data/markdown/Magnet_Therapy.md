## Overview

Magnet therapy refers to the placement of static or pulsating magnets on or near the body at the site of a problematic area, often related to pain. Worldwide sales of therapeutic magnets is estimated at over $5 billion annually ( 15763 , 15773 ). In the United States, the market is about $500 million.

## History

Magnetic rings have been used for arthritis since the Common Era. However, during the late 1700s, the popularity of magnet therapy decreased when Franz Anton Mesmer, the main proponent of magnet therapy, was discredited as a fraud ( 96354 ).

## People Use This For

Magnets are worn for treating general pain, postoperative pain, back pain, foot pain, myofascial pain syndrome, plantar heel pain, neck pain, pelvic pain, postoperative pain, osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis (RA), fibromyalgia, fractures, chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), carpal tunnel syndrome, tendinopathy, dysmenorrhea, diabetic neuropathy, peripheral neuropathy, migraine headache, periodontitis edema, and wound healing. Magnets are also worn for treating breast cancer-related hot flashes, erectile dysfunction (ED), sexual dysfunction, insomnia, sleep apnea, exercise-induced muscle soreness, tinnitus, cancer, multiple sclerosis (MS), Parkinson disease, schizophrenia, obsessive compulsive disorder (OCD), post-polio syndrome, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), epilepsy, stroke, urinary incontinence, venous leg ulcers, and postpartum complications such as pelvic floor dysfunction. It is also used for athletic performance and increasing muscle strength.

Possibly Safe when used topically, short-term. Some evidence shows that placing static magnets on the skin surface and using pulsed electromagnetic fields is safe when used for up to 6 months ( 15761 , 15762 , 15767 , 15770 , 15771 , 15772 , 15775 , 15776 , 15777 , 15778 )( 88416 , 88417 , 88418 , 88421 , 88423 , 88434 , 88434 , 88436 , 88440 )( 88453 , 88458 , 88464 , 96350 , 96351 , 96353 , 96354 , 102577 , 102579 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Magnet therapy seems to be generally well-tolerated. Some side effects reported in clinical trials include more painful menstrual periods, heavier periods, nausea, diarrhea, an abnormal sensation in the arms, and a feeling of "fuzzy headedness" ( 15762 , 102577 ). However, it is unclear whether these side effects are attributable to the magnet therapy. Also, extensive exposure to high electromagnetic fields has been linked to adverse outcomes such as an increased risk of leukemia, breast cancer in males, and chromosomal abnormalities ( 15763 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Magnet therapy has been reported to cause nausea and diarrhea in clinical trials ( 15762 ).

#### Genitourinary

Magnet therapy has been reported to cause heavier periods and increased pain with menstrual periods in clinical trials ( 15792 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Magnet therapy has been reported to cause a feeling of "fuzzy headedness" in clinical trials ( 15792 ). An abnormal sensation in the arm was reported by one patient in a clinical trial ( 102577 ).

#### Other

Extensive exposure to high electromagnetic fields has been linked to adverse outcomes, including an increased risk for leukemia, breast cancer in males, chromosomal abnormalities at birth, and other potential problems. However, a clear correlation and additional details are lacking ( 15763 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Carpal tunnel syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research shows that applying a 1,000 Gauss magnet over the carpal tunnel area for 45 minutes does not improve pain when compared with placebo in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome ( 88428 ). Other preliminary clinical research in patients with mild to moderate carpal tunnel syndrome shows that wearing a 2,976 Gauss magnetic wristband for 6 weeks does not improve symptoms or nerve conduction when compared with wearing a 16 Gauss wristband ( 96351 ).

Exercise-induced muscle soreness.

Some clinical research shows that applying a static magnet of 350 Gauss for 45 minutes/day for 5 days after exhaustive exercise of the elbow flexor muscle group does not significantly reduce delayed-onset post-exercise muscle soreness when compared with placebo ( 15766 ). Other clinical research shows that continuous use of a magnetic armband of 2,250 Gauss for 7 days after maximal eccentric elbow flexion exercises does not improve pain, elbow range of motion, or strength when compared with placebo ( 88466 ). Additional clinical research shows that two 1-hour treatments with a magnet placed 5 cm proximal to the superior patellar border does not improve pain or knee extensions when compared with placebo after 10 minutes of downhill treadmill running ( 88467 ).

Foot pain.

Some clinical research shows that wearing a specific shoe insole containing a bipolar magnet of 2,450 Gauss (Active Comfort, Spenco Medical Corp) for at least 4 hours daily and for at least 30 days does not significantly reduce non-specific foot pain compared to sham therapy ( 15771 ).

Plantar heel pain.

Some clinical research shows that wearing a specific shoe insole containing a bipolar magnet of 2,450 Gauss (Active Comfort, Spenco Medical Corp) for at least 4 hours daily and for 8 weeks does not significantly reduce heel pain compared to sham therapy ( 15772 ). Additional clinical research shows that wearing a specific molded insole (PPT/Rx Firm Molded Insole, Langer Biomechanics) with a magnetic coil inserted inside does not improve pain compared to the same insole without a magnetic coil in patients with heel pain ( 88449 ).

Postoperative pain.

Clinical research shows that placing a static magnet over a surgical wound during the post-anesthesia recovery period does not reduce pain or opioid requirements compared to sham treatment ( 15761 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Athletic performance.

Preliminary clinical research shows that wearing magnetic shoe insoles of 125 Gauss or 600 Gauss during practices and games for seven weeks does not improve vertical jump, bench squat, 40 yard dash, or other soccer-specific fitness tests in male collegiate soccer players ( 88427 ).

Back pain.

Evidence on the benefit of magnet therapy in low back pain is mixed. Some preliminary clinical research shows that a bipolar magnet of 400 Gauss (Master Magnetics, Colorado) applied to the back for 6 hours daily 3 days weekly for one week, followed by a one-week washout and then repeated, does not reduce chronic low back pain when compared to sham therapy ( 15778 ). Other preliminary research in patients with chronic lumbar radicular pain shows that there is no difference in pain reduction between applying 50 Gauss or 200 Gauss static magnets to the back using a standard lumbosacral elastic corset for two 5-week periods ( 88429 ).

Breast cancer-related hot flashes.

Preliminary clinical research shows that applying a magnetic device to six acupressure sites associated with hot flashes for three days does not reduce hot flash frequency in breast cancer survivors. In fact, the placebo device reduced hot flash frequency and interference with daily activities and improved quality of life when compared with magnet therapy in these females ( 88435 ).

Diabetic neuropathy.

Some preliminary clinical research suggests that pulsed or static magnet therapy given for 3-4 months improves  symptoms of diabetic neuropathy when compared to placebo ( 15770 , 88417 ). However, a more recent meta-analysis shows that magnet therapy is not effective for alleviating pain in diabetic neuropathy ( 99562 ).

Dysmenorrhea.

A small, post-intervention survey shows that placing a specific static magnet of 2,700 Gauss (LadyCare, Magnopulse) over the pelvic area might reduce pain to a greater extent than a lower strength magnet of 140 Gauss ( 15762 ). Similarly, a small clinical study in patients with moderate primary dysmenorrhea shows that placing a concentric neodymium-iron-boron magnet of 0.4 Tesla on the area of greatest abdominal pain for 40 minutes reduces pain scores by at least 35% in 58% of patients, compared with only 18% of patients using a sham magnet ( 106607 ). Both studies excluded patients with secondary dysmenorrhea.

Fibromyalgia.

A small clinical study in females with fibromyalgia shows that sleeping on a magnetic mattress pad for 16 weeks might decrease pain and fatigue and improve sleep and physical functioning compared to non-magnetized mattress pads ( 88433 ). Additional preliminary clinical research in patients with fibromyalgia shows that sleeping on a magnetic mattress pad for 6 months improves pain but not the number of tender points compared to non-magnetized mattress pads or usual care ( 88434 ). However, these studies have been criticized because of inadequate blinding ( 99563 ).

Fractures.

Evidence on the use of magnet therapy for healing non-union fractures is mixed. Some preliminary clinical research shows that electromagnetic stimulation for 6-12 weeks improves assimilation or progress to fracture union in patients with delayed union tibial fractures or those undergoing interbody lumbar fusion ( 88445 , 88446 ). Additional clinical research shows that use of pulsed electromagnetic therapy for more than 3 hours daily is more effective for improving fracture healing in non-union fractures than using this therapy for less than 3 hours daily ( 88447 ). However, other preliminary clinical research shows that treatment with pulsed magnetic fields (1.5 mT or 75 Hz at 1.3 msec) does not improve bone fusion over 8-12 months in patients with tibial fractures or those receiving a graft after a bone tumor resection ( 88443 , 88444 ).

Insomnia.

Preliminary clinical research shows that use of a match box sized device emitting low frequency magnetic fields for 4 weeks significantly improves sleep latency, frequency of sleep disruptions, sleepiness after rising, daytime sleepiness, difficulty with concentration, and daytime headaches compared to placebo in patients with primary sleep disorders ( 88436 ).

Migraine headache.

Preliminary clinical research shows that repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation at active myofascial trigger points of the upper trapezius muscles during six sessions over 2 weeks reduces the self-reported number of migraine attacks by 2.6 headaches per month when compared with baseline. Migraine pain and disability were also improved ( 102583 ). However, repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation at active myofascial trigger points might not be more beneficial than at the deltoid muscle, a non-trigger point, for reducing muscular pain or tension, frequency of migraine, or disability in patients with migraines ( 102577 , 106605 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a control group.

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

Different forms of magnet therapy have been evaluated for the management of MS-related symptoms. Preliminary clinical research in patients with MS shows that repetitive magnetic stimulation (0.7 Tesla) twice daily for 7 days might improve spasticity but does not increase the ease of daily living activities when compared to control ( 88437 ). Additional preliminary clinical research in patients with MS shows that exposure to a magnetic pulsing device (Enermed) 50-100 millGauss for 10-24 hours daily for 2 months does not improve clinical symptoms when compared to control; however, magnet therapy might improve bladder control, cognitive function, fatigue, mobility, spasticity, and vision in some patients ( 88438 ). Also, exposure to a magnetic pulsing device (MAGNETOMED 7200) 4 mT intensity and 15-Hz frequency for 20 minutes twice weekly for 2 months appears to improve paresthesia when compared with placebo ( 96350 ). In patients with underactive bladder related to MS, repetitive cortical or sacral magnetic stimulation improves quality of life associated with incontinence by a small amount when compared with baseline. However, there was no benefit on overall quality of life in these patients ( 102582 ).

Muscle strength.

Preliminary clinical research shows that ten sessions of repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation of the suprahyoid muscles for 2 weeks improves cervical flexor strength and tongue pressure when compared with baseline. These benefits were not seen in those doing head exercises. Additionally, there was no benefit of magnetic stimulation on jaw-opening force ( 102581 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that flexible static magnets of 700 Gauss do not increase hand muscle strength when compared to sham therapy ( 15775 ). These differing findings might be due to the use of a control group, as well as to the variable forms of magnet therapy used.

Myofascial pain syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research shows that placing a magnet with a static magnetic field of 500 Gauss on the affected shoulder for one hour improves pain in patients with myofascial shoulder pain due to spinal cord injury ( 88439 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of control group.

Neck pain.

Research on the effect of magnet therapy for neck pain is conflicting. Small clinical studies show that wearing a neck unit that provides a pulsed high frequency electromagnetic therapy of 27 MHz for 8 hours daily decreases pain and improves range of motion when compared with placebo in patients with persistent neck pain or those suffering acute whiplash injuries ( 88440 , 88441 ). However, wearing a magnetic necklace for 24 hours daily for 3 weeks does not reduce pain in patients with chronic neck and shoulder pain when compared with placebo ( 88442 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Evidence on the use of magnet therapy for improving symptoms of osteoarthritis is mixed. Some research shows that static or pulsed magnetic therapy helps to reduce pain related to osteoarthritis. However, other research disagrees ( 15767 , 15768 , 15776 , 88419 , 88420 , 88421 , 88422 , 88423 , 88425 , 88425 , 96353 , 96354 ).

Some clinical research shows that wearing a static magnetic bracelet with a field strength of 170-200 mTesla for 12 weeks significantly improves pain related to osteoarthritis of the knee or hip when compared with placebo ( 15767 ). Preliminary research also shows that a static magnet in a knee sleeve significantly reduces osteoarthritis-related knee pain in some patients ( 15768 , 88425 ). Other research also shows that exposure to a low-amplitude and low-frequency magnetic field for 48 minutes per treatment, with 8 treatments over 2 weeks, reduces knee pain significantly more than placebo ( 15776 ). However, wearing a magnetic wrist strap (MagnaMax) with a field strength of 201 mT for a minimum of 8 hours per day for 4 weeks does not improve pain, stiffness, or physical function or decrease pain medication use when compared with either a magnetic wrist strap (MagnaMax) with a field strength of 45 mT, a demagnetized magnetic wrist strap, or a copper bracelet ( 96354 ). Other research has evaluated the use of pulsed magnetic therapy in patients with osteoarthritis. Some clinical research shows that 18 thirty-minute sessions of pulsed electromagnetic therapy over one month improves osteoarthritis symptoms compared to control in patients with knee osteoarthritis ( 88421 , 88423 ). Pulsed electromagnetic therapy may also improve osteotomy healing in hip osteoarthritis patients with osteotomy ( 88419 ). Also, placing pulsed electromagnetic field therapy (Magnetofield device, F&B International) devices on the sides of the knee for 30 minutes 3 times per week for 6 weeks, decreases pain and stiffness and improves physical functioning 3 months later in patients with knee osteoarthritis when compared to no electromagnetic therapy. However, patients receiving magnet therapy also received an intravenous infusion containing ketoprofen, sodium clodronate, glucosamine sulfate, calcitonin, and ascorbic acid. It is unclear if these benefits are due to the magnet therapy, the infusion, or the combination ( 96353 ). Conversely, some clinical studies comparing pulsed electromagnetic therapy to a control therapy show no change in pain, range of motion, activities of daily living, and other symptoms of arthritis in patients with knee or spine osteoarthritis or periarthritis of the shoulder ( 88420 , 88422 , 88424 ).

Parkinson disease.

An exploratory study in 5 patients with mild to severe Parkinson disease with bilateral involvement and gait problems shows that using a specific device (MagVenture MagPro R20) to provide transcutaneous magnetic stimulation of the spinal cord at the level of the fifth thoracic vertebra over 3 sessions improves gait symptoms and the progression and severity of motor symptoms by 22% and 17%, respectively, after 7 days ( 106601 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the very small size of the study and the lack of a comparator group.

Pelvic pain.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that applying a 600 Gauss magnet to abdominal trigger points 24 hours daily for 4 weeks might decrease pain and disability compared to placebo in females with chronic pelvic pain ( 88431 ); however, females in this study were able to correctly identify the therapy they received, potentially limiting the validity of these findings. Additional preliminary evidence shows that electromagnetic field application (Thelf Systems) on both iliac regions twice daily for 2 hours for 20-40 days decreases pain in females with chronic pelvic pain ( 88432 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of control group.

Periodontitis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that electromagnetic stimulation does not have long term benefits, such as improved healing, in patients undergoing periodontal therapy after periodontal surgery ( 88448 ).

Peripheral neuropathy.

Preliminary clinical research shows that 9 one-hour sessions of pulsed electromagnetic therapy (20 Gauss at 30 Hz) might decrease pain in patients suffering from moderate-to-severe neuropathic foot pain from a variety of causes ( 88418 ).

Postpartum complications.

Postpartum pelvic floor dysfunction can lead to bladder or fecal incontinence or sexual dysfunction. Preliminary clinical research in patients with pelvic floor dysfunction following vaginal delivery shows that ten treatments with a high-intensity focused electromagnetic procedure (BTL EMSELLA, BTL Industries Inc.) of the pelvic floor muscles over 4 weeks improves pelvic floor activity and functionality when compared with electrical stimulation. A complete lack of distress related to quality of life was reported by approximately 36% of patients given the electromagnetic therapy compared with 12% of those using electrical stimulation ( 102580 ).

Post-polio syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research shows that applying a 300-500 Gauss magnetic device to the affected area for 45 minutes significantly improves muscular and arthritic pain when compared with placebo in patients diagnosed with post-polio syndrome ( 88450 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Preliminary clinical research in patients with rheumatoid arthritis of the knee shows that taping four steep magnetic field gradients to the knees using a specific device (MagnaBloc) for one week does not improve pain when compared with a control device that is 1 steep magnetic field gradient. However, this device improves patient-rated assessment of disease activity ( 88426 ).

Sciatica.

A small clinical study in patients with sciatica shows that repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation once daily 5 times a week for 2 weeks, alone or in combination with physiotherapy and kinesiotherapy, reduces pain and improves peripheral nerve regeneration, as assessed by electromyography, when compared with baseline ( 106606 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Sexual dysfunction.

A small preliminary clinical study in females with stress urinary incontinence shows that receiving twice weekly pulsed magnetic stimulation for at least 8 weeks seems to improve sexual function and satisfaction compared to baseline as rated by the female patients and their partners ( 99502 ).

Sleep apnea.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that use of an appliance consisting of a maxillary and a mandibular occlusal acrylic splint with four cylindrical neodymium-iron-boron magnets embedded and oriented to pull the jaw forward might improve daytime sleepiness and snoring in males with severe snoring or sleep apnea ( 88451 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of control group. Additionally, one preliminary clinical report shows that magnet therapy may not be effective in patients with severe obstructive sleep apnea ( 88452 ).

Stroke.

Generally low-quality clinical research shows that repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation after a stroke offers limited benefit for improving upper or lower extremity function or spasticity ( 102578 , 102584 , 102585 , 106603 ). One clinical study shows that repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation for 29 minutes twice daily, five days weekly for 2 weeks, in combination with occupational therapy does not improve motor function, but modestly reduces spasticity in some areas, when compared with sham stimulation with occupational therapy ( 102585 ). A small clinical study shows that a single 2-3 hour session of repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation at the tibialis anterior muscle improves ankle strength and mobility by a small amount when compared with baseline. However, muscle strength is not improved when compared with sham treatment ( 102578 , 102584 ). A small clinical study in hospitalized adults with severe upper extremity dysfunction following a recent stroke shows that repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation applied to the entire upper extremity along with standard care improves the progress rate of upper extremity recovery and motor ability, but not dexterity, when compared with standard care alone ( 106603 ). Another small clinical study in 12 adults with acute stroke and resulting hemiplegia shows that a conventional rehabilitation program with repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation once daily for 5 days a week for 2 weeks attenuates stroke-induced loss of muscle mass in the paretic lower limb at 2 weeks, but not changes in muscle mass from baseline, when compared with a conventional rehabilitation program alone ( 106604 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of randomization and/or the differences in baseline characteristics between the groups.

Tendinopathy.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that wrapping magnetic coils set at 15 Hz with pulses lasting 200 microseconds around the elbow for 8 weeks does not improve symptoms in patients with lateral humeral epicondylitis, a type of tendinopathy commonly referred to as tennis elbow ( 88454 ). However, other preliminary clinical research shows that placing pulsed electromagnetic field coils set at approximately 73 Hz on the shoulder might improve symptoms in patients with a type of tendinopathy known as persistent rotator cuff tendinitis ( 88453 ).

Tinnitus.

Evidence on the benefits of magnet therapy for tinnitus is mixed. Some preliminary clinical research shows that pulsed electromagnetic therapy placed over the mastoid bone 15 minutes daily for one week improves tinnitus when compared to control in patients with long-standing tinnitus ( 88455 ). However, other preliminary research shows that magnet therapy with Theraband headsets (Audimax Inc.) or by placing rare earth magnets close to the tympanic membrane does not seem to improve tinnitus ( 88456 , 88457 ).

Urinary incontinence.

Several small clinical studies show that short-term magnet therapy may improve some symptoms of urinary incontinence in females with stress incontinence, urge incontinence, or overactive bladder ( 88458 , 88459 , 88460 , 99503 ). Several meta-analyses show that magnet therapy offers benefit to patients with urinary incontinence when compared with sham treatment ( 95758 , 102576 , 102579 , 106608 ). In one meta-analysis, those receiving magnet therapy had a 2.3-fold increased chance of improved continence when compared with those receiving sham treatment ( 95758 ). Another meta-analysis shows that magnet therapy over at least 2 months has a moderate effect on symptom improvement and frequency of incontinence when compared with sham treatment. Furthermore, the odds of cure, based on a subjective cure rate, was over 8-fold higher than with sham stimulation ( 102576 ). In the third meta-analysis, magnetic stimulation near the pelvis reduced the number of leaks over 3 days by about 1.4 and reduced urine leakage by about 5 grams over 24 hours when compared with sham stimulation ( 102579 ). However, most of the studies included in these analyses have been low-quality and lacked appropriate blinding. Furthermore, the optimum frequency and pulse duration for magnet therapy has not been established ( 95758 , 95759 , 102576 , 102579 ).

As of 2018, the European Association of Urology advises that healthcare providers do not offer magnetic stimulation for the treatment of urinary incontinence or overactive bladder in adult females ( 95759 ). The 2014 clinical practice guidelines for the nonsurgical management of urinary incontinence in females from the American College of Physicians did not evaluate magnet therapy, stating that this treatment is not commonly used or available to most physicians ( 95760 ). There is a lack of evidence regarding the effects of magnet therapy in males with urinary incontinence.

Venous leg ulcers.

Evidence on the benefits of magnet therapy for venous stasis ulcer healing is mixed. Some preliminary clinical research shows that low frequency magnetic field therapy might improve wound healing success when compared to placebo in patients with venous skin ulcers ( 88462 ). Additional preliminary clinical research shows that 8-12 weeks of magnet therapy with magnetic leg wraps or a portable pulsed electromagnetic limb ulcer therapy device appears to reduce ulcer size in patients with leg ulcers. However, these devices do not seem to improve quality of life, and their effects on pain reduction are mixed ( 88463 , 88464 ). Conversely, some preliminary clinical research shows that magnet therapy (Magnetoplus 1500) twice weekly for 5 weeks does not improve ulcer healing, ulcer size, pain, or infection rates in patients with chronic varicose ulcers ( 88465 ).

Wound healing.

Preliminary clinical research shows that placing magnets in the form of patches over cosmetic surgical wounds might reduce pain, edema, and discoloration ( 15777 ).

More evidence is needed to rate magnet therapy for these uses.

Magnet therapy is provided in a variety of formats. The dose of the magnetic field can be provided at variable strengths. Most magnet therapy is conducted at a strength of 200-10,000 Gauss ( 15763 ). The frequency of dosing is also highly variable. Magnetic strength can be emitted in a continuous or pulsing mode ( 15766 , 15767 , 15768 , 102578 ) with overall exposure provided on a continuous or intermittent basis. Applications can last from 1-24 hours per day on 2-7 days per week. The placement and location of the magnet is dependent upon the goals of the therapy. The magnets can be applied to different parts of the body using devices or pouches to hold them in place. Magnets can also be placed into a piece of furniture, such as a mattress or chair ( 88433 , 88434 , 88459 , 88460 , 88461 ).

#### EPILEPSY

There has been some concern that magnet therapy could potentially exacerbate seizure activity in people with epilepsy. However, there is no reliable evidence to support this. Until more is known, advise patients with epilepsy to use magnet therapy cautiously or avoid.

#### IMPLANTABLE DEFIBRILLATORS

There has been concern that some magnets could interfere with implantable defibrillators and pacemakers. Some research shows that magnetic mattress pads can interfere with or deactivate defibrillators; however, other static therapeutic magnets do not appear to alter defibrillator function if kept at least 6 inches away from the device ( 15769 ).

#### MAGNETIC RESONANCE IMAGING (MRI)

There is concern that magnets may interfere with magnetic resonance imagining (MRI) scans. In some cases, magnets might not be detected on the metal detector scan. Magnets have the potential to reduce the quality of an MRI image and cause serious adverse events. Advise patients to disclose the use or presence of magnets prior to an MRI scan ( 96352 ).

#### General

At one time it was theorized that abnormal magnetic fields in the body might result in certain disease states and that magnets could play a role in normalizing these magnetic fields ( 15763 ). Magnet therapy usually refers to the use of static magnets that are placed on the body at the site of a problematic area, particularly related to pain. The strength of magnets is described in terms of gauss or Tesla. A Tesla is equivalent to 10,000 gauss. Therapeutic magnets usually have a higher magnetic strength than typical refrigerator-type magnets. Therapeutic magnets are most often in the range of 200-10,000 gauss. Typical household magnets are typically around 200 gauss. There is interest in magnet therapy for medical conditions due to the variety of electromagnetic fields that naturally occur within the body. For example, nervous system transmissions and related muscle contractions are associated with magnetic activity. The heart generates the largest magnetic field in the body. Several other physiological activities are associated with magnetic activity ( 15763 ). Exposure to electromagnetic fields has been linked to various physiological changes such as reduced production of melatonin, chromosomal alterations, altered enzyme activity, changes in electroencephalography (EEG), reduced immune response, and other changes. Extensive exposure has been linked to a small increased risk of leukemia, male breast cancer, and other potential health problems ( 15763 ). This suggests that magnets and magnetic fields have the potential to have a significant effect on physiology.

#### Bone effects

Magnet therapy may have an anabolic effect on osteoblasts and chondrocytes ( 96353 ).

#### Central nervous system effects

In the central nervous system, magnetic field therapy may alter calcium transport in cell membranes. This may improve conduction in nerves. Magnet therapy also appears to affect the release of melatonin from the pineal gland and exhibit immunomodulatory actions ( 96350 ). In rats with cervical spinal cord injury, a combination of simultaneous early locomotor exercise and injury-site magnetic stimulation improves spasticity and limb coordination and strength, potentially through upregulation of neurotransmitters, neuromodulators, and neurotrophic factors ( 106602 ).

#### Musculoskeletal effects

A small clinical study in healthy young males shows that application of repetitive peripheral magnetic stimulation further improves the thickness of the stimulated muscle following low-intensity isometric exercise when compared with baseline ( 106609 ).
