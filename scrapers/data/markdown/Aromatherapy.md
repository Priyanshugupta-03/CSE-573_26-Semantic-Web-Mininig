## Overview

Aromatherapy involves the therapeutic use of essential oils via inhalation or on the skin to improve physical, emotional, or spiritual well-being ( 90895 , 94039 ). Essential oils are aromatic constituents of certain plant parts such as flowers, fruits, and seeds. The term essential is used because the oil contains the plant's "essence" or the smell and the taste characteristic of the plant ( 90537 ).

This topic discusses aromatherapy as a class, including the indications for which aromatherapy is most commonly used and/or well-studied. Always keep in mind that the evidence varies for each essential oil. Some oils may be beneficial for some indications, while others have not been studied or may not be beneficial. For detailed information on the use of specific essential oils as aromatherapy, refer to individual monographs.

Possibly Unsafe when used orally. Although oral use is not a standard route of administration for aromatherapy, essential oils are sometimes marketed and used in this way. The safe oral use of essential oils depends on the specific oil being used. Some essential oils can cause severe side effects, including convulsions and kidney failure, when ingested in large amounts ( 8 , 12 , 19 , 515 , 43419 ). ...when applied topically in large amounts or on broken skin. #### General

The safety of aromatherapy oils, when used topically or by inhalation, depends on the type of oil used. See individual monographs for specific safety information. The oral use of essential oils is often unsafe.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Topically : Skin irritation, photosensitization.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

All routes of administration : Systemic absorption of large quantities of certain essential oils can cause seizures. Orally : Certain essential oils have been reported to cause hepatotoxicity, kidney failure, and seizures.

#### Dermatologic

Angelica root, bergamot, cumin, distilled or expressed grapefruit, expressed lemon, expressed lime, orange, expressed bitter, and rue essential oils may have photosensitizing effects ( 6 , 18 , 11019 , 90515 , 90521 ). Bay, caraway, cinnamon, citronella, clove, cumin, lemongrass, lemon verbena, oregano, peppermint, tagetes, and thyme essential oils may have irritant effects when in contact with skin or mucus membranes ( 90515 , 90521 ). The following essential oils may have dermal sensitizing effects: cassia cinnamon, cinnamon bark, peru balsam (Myroxylon pereirae), Camellia sinensis, lemon verbena, turpentine oil, lemon myrtle, lemongrass, and inula ( 59513 , 90515 , 90521 ). In a case report, lavender, jasmine and rosewood essential oils resulted in airborne allergic contact dermatitis in an elderly patient with relapsing eczema ( 57032 ).

#### Hepatic

In a case report, a 15-month male ingested 10 mL of clove oil and developed fulminant hepatic failure; N-acetylcysteine was administered after which liver function began to improve over next 4 days ( 43419 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

In a case report, a patient applied menthol and methyl salicylate (constituents of wintergreen oil) topically and used a heating pad concurrently. This resulted in increased absorption, resulting in skin and muscle necrosis ( 68552 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Ingestion of undiluted oils can sometimes result in significant adverse events including convulsions, particularly in children ( 8 , 12 , 19 ). Essential oils with proconvulsant effects include rosemary oil, eucalyptus, camphor, fennel oil, hyssop oil, pennyroyal, sage oil, cedar, and thuja ( 105042 ). Ingestion of camphorated oil 5-30 mL by children 5 years and younger has resulted in twitch, seizure, ataxia, and coma ( 13444 , 39583 , 39634 , 39660 , 39666 , 90520 ). Ingestion of eucalyptus oil 5-30 mL by children 3 years and younger has resulted in seizure, lethargy, and coma ( 13444 , 48983 , 48994 , 48995 , 49013 , 90520 ). This adverse effect is likely due to the eucalyptus oil constituent cineole, which can cause seizures when consumed in high doses ( 12868 ). Topical and inhaled essential oils have also been linked with seizures and other adverse effects. Applying excessive amounts of highly concentrated oils to a large surface of the skin, or to broken skin, can result in significant systemic absorption and increase the chance of serious side effects, such as convulsions ( 68552 ). For instance, prolonged topical exposure or topical application of large amounts of eucalyptus oil can cause agitation, drowsiness, slurred speech, and seizures. The risk of toxicity may be greater in children ( 12868 , 12869 ). One systematic review describes eucalyptus oil-induced seizures in 49 children and 61 adults, with most cases associated with topical use, followed by inhalation and then ingestion ( 107494 ). In one prospective observational study, there were 22 reports of new onset seizures and 33 reports of recurrent seizures in adults and children after use of camphor and eucalyptus oil, either alone or in combination. Most cases of seizure with topical use occurred 0.5-24 hours after topical application to the chest, neck, or face. Most cases of seizure with inhalation occurred about 2-30 minutes after steam inhalation of camphor and/or eucalyptus oil ( 105028 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

In one case report, a 34-year-old Japanese female presented with complaints of dyspnea, cough, and fever 2 weeks after initiating lavender essential oil therapy delivered via humidifier. The patient had an oxygen saturation of 88% and was diagnosed with acute eosinophilic pneumonia. Symptoms improved after a course of corticosteroids and discontinuation of aromatherapy ( 109979 ).

#### Renal

Ingestion of undiluted oils can sometimes result in significant adverse events including kidney failure ( 8 , 12 , 19 ). In a case report, a patient applied menthol and methyl salicylate (constituents of wintergreen oil) topically and used a heating pad concurrently. This resulted in increased absorption, leading to interstitial nephritis ( 68552 ).

Possibly Effective
Anxiety.

Lavender oil aromatherapy via inhalation or massage seems to improve chronic and situational anxiety in some patients. The use of various essential oils as aromatherapy may also reduce anxiety in hospitalized patients; however, it is unclear which essential oils may be most beneficial.

Meta-analyses of clinical trials in patients with chronic or situational anxiety show that aromatherapy via inhalation or massage using lavender oil moderately reduces anxiety when compared with control. Most often, the control group received no intervention, although some studies utilized standard or routine care and others used plain water or diluted lemon juice as controls. Most treatments lasted 1-20 minutes ( 101720 , 103061 ). Similarly, a meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with chronic or situational anxiety shows that aromatherapy via inhalation or massage, often with lavender oil or citrus oils, reduces anxiety when compared with control ( 105045 ). Clinical research also suggests that aromatherapy with various essential oils modestly improves anxiety in patients with cancer and in hospitalized patients, including conscious patients admitted to the intensive care unit, those admitted for cardiovascular indications, and patients in labor, when compared with routine care or placebo. The included studies used essential oils of bergamot, bitter orange, black pepper, chamomile, eucalyptus, geranium, lavender, lemon, lemon balm, orange peel, peppermint, pine, rose hip, sweet almond, tea tree, and valerian ( 108752 , 108831 , 108840 , 109974 , 109977 ). In patients admitted for cardiovascular indications, including acute myocardial infarction or coronary angiography, aromatherapy also reduced heart rate and blood pressure when compared with standard care or placebo ( 108831 ). Due to the differences in treatment methods and types of aromatherapy oil used, it is unclear which treatments may be most beneficial.

Dysmenorrhea.

Aromatherapy via inhalation or massage seems to improve dysmenorrhea symptoms; however, it is unclear which essential oils and application methods are most beneficial.

Two separate meta-analyses of clinical research show that both aromatherapy massage and aromatherapy administered via inhalation reduce menstrual pain in people with dysmenorrhea ( 98784 , 108832 ). A subgroup analysis suggests that patients receiving aromatherapy massage may have greater improvements in menstrual pain when compared with aromatherapy via inhalation ( 108832 ). Most of the studies included in these meta-analyses used lavender or rose essential oil, although some studies used combinations of various essential oils ( 98784 , 108832 ). However, there is significant heterogeneity among the included trials, which employed various treatment methods and durations and enrolled patients with differing severities of menstrual pain.

Fatigue.

Aromatherapy via inhalation or massage seems to reduce fatigue in patients with chronic conditions; however, it is unclear which essential oils are most beneficial.

A meta-analysis of 19 small clinical studies in patients with various chronic health conditions shows that aromatherapy administered via inhalation or massage modestly reduces fatigue when compared with standard care or placebo. Around half of the studies used lavender essential oil and the others used various essential oil mixtures ( 109978 ). A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients receiving routine hemodialysis shows that aromatherapy administered via inhalation or massage before or during hemodialysis improves fatigue when compared to standard care or placebo. The included studies used essential oils of almond, chamomile, lavender, orange, or rosemary ( 108836 ). The validity of the findings from these analyses is limited due to the high heterogeneity of included studies and differences in treatment methods, duration of treatment, and type of aromatherapy oil used. Also, one clinical study in cardiac patients with fatigue shows that aromatherapy with either peppermint or lavender essential oil for 7 nights reduces fatigue to a greater extent than control ( 107959 ).

Insomnia.

Most small clinical studies suggest that inhaled or topical aromatherapy improves sleep quality in healthy patients or those with various underlying conditions. Most studies have evaluated lavender or rose essential oils; however, it is unclear which essential oils may be most beneficial.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in various patient populations shows that using aromatherapy for 1-8 weeks improves Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index scores when compared with placebo or no intervention. A subgroup analysis suggests this effect is independent of treatment duration, type of essential oil, or patient population ( 108829 ). These studies provided aromatherapy with the essential oils of lavender, peppermint, rosemary, sweet orange, or a combination. Similarly, two meta-analyses of clinical research in a wide population of patients with situational sleep concerns show that aromatherapy generally improves sleep quality ( 102161 , 108828 ). Most of these studies used lavender essential oil, although some studies used peppermint or rose essential oils. The validity of these analyses is limited due to high heterogeneity and mixed populations of the included studies. Two meta-analyses, both including 10 small clinical trials, have evaluated the benefits of aromatherapy on sleep quality in patients with cancer. Aromatherapy via inhalation or massage seems to greatly improve sleep quality when compared with standard care or placebo. Lavender essential oil appears to have the largest effect, while mixed essential oils may not be beneficial for improving sleep quality. Aromatherapy may be most effective when delivered via inhalation and used in patients undergoing chemotherapy ( 109975 , 109976 ). A meta-analysis has also evaluated the benefits of rose essential oil, specifically, as aromatherapy in patients with situational sleep concerns, including those who are hospitalized or are acutely ill in the home setting. This analysis shows that inhalation aromatherapy, typically over 3-5 consecutive nights, improves sleep quality when compared with either routine sleep care or placebo ( 108838 ).

Labor pain.

Adding aromatherapy to standard treatment seems to be beneficial for reducing labor pain. Most research has evaluated lavender essential oil; however, it is unclear which essential oils may be most beneficial.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in healthy nulliparous or primiparous adults shows that inhaled aromatherapy modestly reduces labor pain when compared with controls such as usual care only or use of unscented liquids. Most studies used lavender essential oil ( 102159 ). Similarly, a meta-analysis of clinical research evaluating only lavender essential oil shows that aromatherapy modestly reduces labor pain when compared with standard treatment alone ( 104440 ). A more focused meta-analyses assessing first-stage labor pain in nulliparous patients shows that aromatherapy with various essential oils modestly reduces labor pain when compared with standard treatment alone ( 105048 ). Another meta-analysis of clinical research in healthy singleton pregnancies shows that aromatherapy via massage or inhalation reduces labor pain, with the greatest improvements observed with cervix dilations of 8-10 centimeters. Most of the included studies used lavender or rose essential oils, although some studies used other individual or combination essential oils ( 108837 ). Many of the studies for this indication have been conducted in Iran; it is unclear if these findings are generalizable to other geographic areas.

Postoperative pain.

Lavender oil aromatherapy, when used along with analgesics, may help reduce postoperative pain in some patients. The benefits of aromatherapy with other essential oils are unclear.

Clinical studies in patients post-Cesarean section show that, when used in conjunction with standard analgesic therapy, applying 2 drops of lavender oil 2% to a face mask or applying 3 drops of lavender to a cotton ball for inhalation seems to reduce postoperative pain when compared with control ( 58093 , 101724 ). Also, a small clinical study in children aged 6-12 years shows that inhaling lavender oil for 3 minutes every 6 hours as needed after tonsillectomy reduces acetaminophen use when compared with control ( 90513 ). A clinical study in adults that received abdominal surgery shows that topically applying one drop of lavender oil does not improve pain scores or reduce analgesic use for up to 24 hours when compared with standard care. However, an exploratory subgroup analysis of patients who received a regional nerve block suggests that lavender aromatherapy might reduce pain as the nerve block wears off ( 105049 ). A meta-analysis of small clinical trials in patients that underwent Cesarean section shows that adding inhaled aromatherapy with various essential oils to standard analgesia reduces pain for up to 24 hours when compared with standard analgesia alone ( 105050 ). However, the included studies were conducted in the Middle East, and it is unknown if these results are generalizable to other geographic areas.

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

Aromatherapy may alleviate psychological symptoms of PMS such as anxiety and depression. However, it is unclear which essential oils are most beneficial.

A meta-analysis of 7 small and heterogenous clinical trials shows that aromatherapy improves certain PMS symptoms such as anxiety, depression, and fatigue, but not physical symptoms such as pain and bloating, when compared with control. Most commonly, lavender essential oil was used for aromatherapy. Bitter orange and rose have also been studied ( 105044 ).

Pre-procedural anxiety.

Inhaled and topical aromatherapy may help to alleviate anxiety before surgical procedures. However, it is unclear which essential oils are most beneficial.

Two meta-analyses of low-quality, small clinical trials show that inhaled aromatherapy and aromatherapy massage seem to modestly reduce preoperative anxiety when compared with placebo control, usual care, or no additional intervention. Benefit seems to be greatest when aromatherapy is given for 20 minutes or less and when given prior to low-risk surgery. Essential oils that have shown benefit were derived from lavender, rose, eucalyptus, and citrus species ( 105046 , 105047 ).

Stress.

Inhaled aromatherapy may help to alleviate stress in healthy persons; however, it is unclear which essential oils are most beneficial.

A meta-analysis of four clinical trials in healthy adults shows that aromatherapy with lavender and other essential oils may reduce subjective measures of stress when compared with control ( 90538 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Cancer-related pain.

Using lavender oil as aromatherapy via inhalation or massage does not seem to provide any additional pain relief in patients with advanced or terminal cancer. Other essential oils have not been evaluated for this purpose.

Clinical research in patients with advanced or terminal cancer shows that diffusing lavender oil as aromatherapy or using lavender oil for massage does not reduce pain scores when compared with control treatments without lavender oil ( 29504 , 58053 ). There is insufficient evidence about the effects of other aromatherapy oils.

Parturition.

Administering inhaled aromatherapy during labor does not seem to improve perinatal outcomes.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that using inhaled aromatherapy during labor does not seem to impact labor duration or the rates of Caesarean section and spontaneous vaginal delivery when compared with control ( 102159 ).

Burns.

Aromatherapy via inhalation or massage seems to reduce anxiety in patients with burn injuries. However, it is unclear which essential oils are most beneficial.

A meta-analysis of small, randomized controlled trials in patients with burn injuries shows that aromatherapy via inhalation or massage with various essential oils modestly reduces pain and improves anxiety following wound dressing changes when compared with placebo or standard care. Most studies used essential oils of lavender and/or rose ( 108833 ). The validity of this finding is limited due to the high heterogeneity and poor methodology of included studies. Small clinical studies suggest that aromatherapy does not improve fatigue in patients with cancer.

Small clinical studies suggest that peppermint oil aromatherapy might reduce CINV when compared with control ( 102603 , 105122 , 105123 ). It is unclear if these findings are generalizable to most patients receiving chemotherapy or whether other aromatherapy oils are beneficial.

Cognitive function.

It is unclear if aromatherapy can improve cognitive function; research is limited and conflicting.

Small clinical studies have yielded conflicting findings on the use of aromatherapy with various essential oils for improving cognitive function in healthy adults. Some research suggests that peppermint aromatherapy may modestly improve memory and performance on certain cognitive tasks ( 56954 , 68416 , 68466 ), whereas aromatherapy with sage oil or ylang ylang oil has identified limited or no benefit ( 72658 , 98616 ).

Colic.

Although there is interest in using inhaled and topical aromatherapy for colic, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of aromatherapy for this condition.

Dementia.

Small clinical studies suggest that inhaled or topical aromatherapy may improve behavioral symptoms in adults with dementia.

Small clinical studies in patients with dementia are too heterogeneous to be analyzed together; however, they suggest that aromatherapy may reduce behavioral symptoms and agitation when compared with control. Aromatherapy regimens most often used lavender essential oil, although lemon balm, orange, and cedar extracts, either alone or as a mixture, were also evaluated ( 105043 ). Research on the use of lavender, specifically, suggests that diffused aromatherapy may be beneficial ( 16393 , 58052 , 95632 ), whereas application of lavender oil to the patient or the patient's clothes may not be beneficial ( 12213 , 93014 , 99710 ).

Depression.

Small clinical studies suggest that aromatherapy via inhalation or massage might improve depression in patients with cancer. It is unclear if aromatherapy is beneficial in patients with major depressive disorder.

A meta-analysis of five small clinical studies in patients undergoing treatment for cancer shows that aromatherapy via inhalation or massage modestly reduces symptoms of depression when compared with placebo or usual care. Three of the included studies used lavender essential oil and two studies used a mixture of various essential oils ( 109977 ). Whether these findings can be generalized to patients with major depressive disorder is unclear.

Menopausal symptoms.

A meta-analysis of small low-quality clinical studies in postmenopausal patients shows that lavender aromatherapy for 4-12 weeks, either alone or in combination with other essential oils, reduces menopausal symptoms such as hot flushes when compared with placebo ( 103067 ). #### Presentation

There are numerous cases in which pure essential oils have caused toxicity upon ingestion, especially in children. For example, wintergreen oil contains menthol and methyl salicylate. A teaspoonful of wintergreen oil may contain enough methyl salicylate to equal 4 grams of aspirin, which is toxic to a child. There have been numerous cases of children poisoned after ingesting wintergreen oil ( 90517 , 90518 ). There are reports of poisonings in children after ingestion of lemongrass oil ( 59576 ), thuja ( 17078 ), pennyroyal ( 291 , 5601 ), and sage and hyssop oils ( 72704 ). Also, the National Poison Data System has reported that exposure to cinnamon oil, clove oil, eucalyptus oil, and tea tree oils have resulted in major adverse outcomes ( 90519 ). See individual monographs for more information about the presentation of overdose with these aromatherapy essential oils.

#### General

Aromatherapy is the therapeutic use of essential oils. The essential oils are the volatile oils of aromatic plants. They are usually extracted from the plants through steam distillation ( 15783 ). In aromatherapy, the essential oils are usually vaporized by heating the oil or by applying the oil in a hot bath. In other cases, the oil is applied topically, for example, during massage, or to a specific tissue area such as the scalp, or to a painful joint. Much less commonly, essential oils are ingested. However, there is debate among practitioners as to whether topical or oral uses of essential oils are actually a part of aromatherapy. The vaporized essential oils of aromatic plants are used to elicit physiological and pharmacological effects ( 18240 , 98783 ). Some constituents of vapors may pass into the bloodstream via the lungs. Lipophilic constituents might cross the blood-brain barrier and act directly on brain neurons ( 15783 , 18240 ). Inhaled aromas are thought to act much more quickly than applying essential oils to the skin or oral ingestion ( 15783 ).

#### Analgesic effects

Preliminary research suggests that some essential oils have analgesic properties. For example, lavender leaf essential oil has been shown to reduce formalin-induced paw licking and acetic acid-induced writhing in mice ( 12219 ). Additionally, eucalyptus oil, which is made from the leaves and branches of eucalyptus, contains 60% to 90% eucalyptol (1,8-cineole). Preliminary research suggests eucalyptol might block the production of arachidonic acid metabolites that mediate pain. It might also inhibit cyclooxygenase pathways ( 12866 , 13302 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

Preliminary research suggests that some essential oils have anti-inflammatory properties. For example, lavender leaf essential oil has been shown to reduce carrageenan-induced inflammation in mice ( 12219 ). Additionally, eucalyptus oil, which is made from the leaves and branches of eucalyptus, contains 60% to 90% eucalyptol (1,8-cineole). Preliminary research suggests eucalyptol might inhibit the production of cytokines responsible for inflammation such as tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-alpha, interleukin (IL)-1beta, leukotriene B4, and thromboxane B2 ( 12866 ). Other essential oils, such as anise, lemongrass, and spearmint oil, have shown anti-inflammatory activity in laboratory and animal studies ( 31412 , 48930 , 59540 ).

#### Antibacterial effects

In vitro, essential oils of cinnamon bark and tea tree have been shown to inhibit Gram positive bacteria such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Gram negative bacteria, yeast, molds, and bacteriophages. Essential oil of coriander has been shown to inhibit Gram positive bacteria and fungi. Essential oils of lemongrass and Roman chamomile have been shown to inhibit bacteriophages. Essential oils of angelica and pine have been shown to inhibit Gram positive and Gram negative bacteria ( 90539 ). Essential oils of cinnamon bark, lemongrass and thyme oils, which contain high levels of terpene alcohol constituents, have been shown to inhibit the growth of H. influenzae, S. pneumoniae, S. pyogenes, and S. aureus in vitro ( 59492 ). Tea tree, peppermint, sage, and other essential oils have been shown to inhibit oral bacteria in laboratory research ( 43638 , 48954 ). Other laboratory research shows that various essential oils, such as tea tree, eucalyptus, palmarosa, peppermint, lavender, savory, rosewood, and spearmint have antibacterial properties ( 316 , 10026 , 10028 , 13414 , 15325 , 39870 , 39882 , 43478 , 43486 , 43533 , 43538 , 49447 , 59506 , 59546 , 59667 , 59689 , 59667 , 90539 ).

#### Anticonvulsant effects

Some preliminary research suggests that certain essential oils used in aromatherapy may have anticonvulsant effects. For instance, essential oil from the anise fruit has been shown to reduce tonic convulsions caused by pentylenetetrazole or maximal electric shock in male mice when administered intraperitoneally ( 13490 ). However, other essential oils may actually increase the risk of seizures. For instance, ingestion or topical application of large amounts of eucalyptus essential oil has been shown to induce seizures, particularly in children. This effect has been attributed to the eucalyptus oil constituent cineole, which can induce convulsions ( 12868 , 12869 , 13444 , 48983 , 48994 , 48995 , 49013 , 90520 ).

#### Antifungal effects

Sandalwood, eucalyptus, tea tree, and other essential oils have been shown to inhibit Candida fungus in vitro ( 316 , 4446 , 39625 , 43493 , 52026 ). Other essential oils, such as palmarosa, tea tree, rosemary, thyme, and cinnamon, have shown antifungal activity as well ( 4063 , 4446 , 13453 , 15338 , 16350 , 31191 , 33809 , 43692 ).

#### Antiparasitic effects

Laboratory research shows that various essential oils, such as tea tree, lemon balm, and thyme, have antiparasitic activity against Leishmania major and Trypanosoma brucei parasites ( 59326 ).

#### Antiviral effects

Some essential oils, such as hyssop, thyme and ginger, have been shown to inhibit herpes simplex virus in laboratory studies ( 31418 , 43490 , 78169 , 78283 ).

#### Immunologic effects

Some essential oils have shown immunostimulatory properties. For instance, essential oils of clove and ginger have been shown to recover humoral immune response in immunosuppressed mice when administered orally once daily for a week ( 43541 ). However, other essential oils, such as sage essential oil, do not appear to have this effect ( 43541 ).

#### Insecticidal effects

Ginger, rosemary, thyme, lemongrass, cinnamon, and other essential oils have shown mosquito-repellant activity ( 31384 , 40201 , 43338 , 43427 , 59617 , 59690 ). Lotions containing eucalyptus and peppermint essential oils have shown activity against permethrin-resistant head lice ( 68459 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS effects

The vaporized essential oils of aromatic plants are used to elicit physiological and pharmacological effects, including feelings of relaxation, stress relief, calming, and other effects. Molecules from the vapor bind to olfactory receptors in the nose, sending signals to the brain via the olfactory nerve. The effects of a particular odor can be modified by psychological factors such as emotions, previous experiences, and expectations ( 18240 ). Some constituents of vapors may pass into the bloodstream via the lungs. Lipophilic constituents might cross the blood-brain barrier and act directly on brain neurons ( 15783 , 18240 ). Inhaled aromas are thought to act much more quickly than applying essential oils to the skin or oral ingestion ( 15783 ). Aromatherapy is thought to increase the excretion of endorphins, which may reduce stress hormone levels ( 95050 ).

#### Sedative effects

Inhalation of lavender or hyssop essential oil has been shown to decrease motility and/or reduce caffeine-induced hyperactivity in mice, suggesting a sedative effect ( 51902 , 58069 ).

#### Stimulant effects

Inhalation of essential oil from ginger, thyme, peppermint, or cypress has been shown to reduce immobility resulting from a forced swimming test, suggesting a stimulant effect ( 51902 ).
