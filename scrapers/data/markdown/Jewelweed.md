## Overview

Jewelweed is a plant native to parts of the United States and Canada. It is often found in wet woodland areas and riverbanks. The native species is Impatiens capensis. It produces orange or yellow flowers. The species Impatiens balsamina is native to China and produces pink or purple flowers ( 98702 ).

## History

Native Americans, specifically the Southern Cherokee, Potawatomi, Chippewa, Meskwaki, and Omaha tribes, have historically used jewelweed topically to treat rashes, itching, hives, and stings caused by other plants and insects ( 98702 ). The aerial parts of jewelweed species are an ingredient used in Traditional Chinese Medicine for their antimicrobial properties and for the treatment of pain and swelling ( 98702 ). In 1999, a US patent was issued to Brian Albert for a poison ivy remedy containing mostly jewelweed along with plantain and oatmeal ( 98702 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, jewelweed is used for mild digestive disorders, fever, rheumatic conditions, stomach cramps, and pain. Topically, jewelweed is used to treat and prevent poison ivy dermatitis. It is also used topically to treat skin rash, hives, and insect bites.

#### General

According to secondary sources, jewelweed has a high mineral content, particularly calcium oxalate; consumption of excess amounts is considered dangerous.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Poison oak and poison ivy dermatitis.

Evidence on the use of jewelweed for the treatment or prevention of poison oak and poison ivy dermatitis is limited and mixed. Some clinical research shows that topical application of jewelweed extract or juice does not significantly reduce dermatitis caused by poison ivy or urushiol, a constituent of poison ivy and poison oak, when compared to placebo ( 57049 , 57050 , 98702 ). However, some research shows that topical application of a mash made from the whole plant of either jewelweed species does appear to reduce rash caused by poison ivy compared to a control ( 98702 ). These studies are all small and likely underpowered to detect statistically significant differences between treatment groups.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of jewelweed for its other uses.

#### General

Jewelweed flowers contain kaempferol, quercetin, and lawsone. Other aerial parts of jewelweed contain flavonols, phenolics, quinones, saponins, and anthocyanins. The roots contain lawsone. The seeds of jewelweed contain saponins. The amount of lawsone in jewelweed can vary depending on the time of harves ( 98702 ).

#### Dermatologic effects

Certain plants, including poison ivy, produce urushiol, which is the component responsible for eliciting an allergic reaction in some people. Lawsone, a component of jewelweed flowers and roots, may be a cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) inhibitor, thus decreasing pain and swelling. For preventing dermatitis, lawsone is thought to act as a competitive inhibitor and bind the same site as urushiol oil, thus preventing a reaction ( 98702 ).
