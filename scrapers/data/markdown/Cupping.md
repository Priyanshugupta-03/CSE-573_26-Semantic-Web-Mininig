## Overview

Cupping is a therapeutic method employed in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), classical (five element) acupuncture, and Japanese acupuncture. The method refers to the application of a heated cup over an area of the body. As the air inside cools, its volume decreases, thus creating a slight suction on the area, which is thought to stimulate blood circulation ( 27951 , 99198 ). In dry cupping, heated cups are placed on intact skin. In wet cupping, a small incision is made on the skin so that when the heated cup is applied, the suction results in bloodletting ( 96592 ).

Possibly Safe when applied appropriately by experienced practitioners, short-term. Adverse effects from cupping are rare ( 88308 ), and most clinical research evaluating cupping therapy lacks reports of serious adverse events ( 27941 , 96592 , 96595 , 96597 , 99203 , 101853 , 108784 , 108789 , 108791 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid use.

#### General

Cupping is generally well tolerated.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Burning and bruising on the skin sites where the cupping devices are placed.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Most clinical research evaluating cupping therapy lack reports of serious adverse effects. However, cases of localized or systemic infection have been reported.

#### Dermatologic

Cupping can cause skin burns, erythema, ecchymosis, purpura, keloids, bullae, and panniculitis ( 27919 , 96593 , 99205 , 99206 ). Skin burns usually heal quickly. However, rare post-healing sequelae have included hypopigmentation, hyperpigmentation, and scars ( 99205 ). Prolonged cupping procedure, such as 30-minute suction, can result in painful bullae. The pain can feel like burning, prickling, or scorching ( 96593 ). Cases of cupping-associated infectious complications have been described. One such case report suggests that cupping may rarely cause factitial panniculitis infection ( 27942 ). In another case, an otherwise healthy 35-year-old patient developed Pseudomonas-positive necrotizing fasciitis following wet cupping ( 108790 ). In one case, a patient with psoriasis experienced an exacerbation of psoriasis due to dry cupping treatment intended to help with this condition ( 96594 ).

#### Hematologic

Two case reports show that cupping can precipitate hematoma, especially in susceptible patients such as those with a bleeding disorder ( 27914 , 96588 ). Anemia has occurred as a result of excessive bleeding from wet cupping ( 99205 , 99206 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

Cupping may be associated with a tingling feeling in the arms and hands, worsening pain near the neck or back, and/or shivering ( 27933 , 108781 ). These adverse effects are typically transient, lasting 4 hours or less ( 27933 ). Muscle soreness and minor hematoma at the treatment site have also been reported after cupping therapy with massage ( 27934 , 99201 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Case reports suggest that cupping might cause vasovagal shock and fainting in some patients. This adverse effect may be treated by having patients sit or lie down for 5-10 minutes ( 27911 ). Cupping may cause mild headache or sleepiness, both of which typically resolve within four hours ( 27933 ). In a case report, a 54-year-old male patient reported to the emergency room with neck pain and fever due to an infected cervical spinal epidural abscess following acupuncture and wet cupping treatment of the neck region for upper back pain. After admission, the patient developed sepsis resulting in lower body paralysis and bladder dysfunction. The infection was effectively treated with 6 weeks of antibiotics; however, the patient refused surgical intervention to decompress and drain the abscess. The infection was controlled and over time his body function was partially restored ( 96596 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

Cupping may cause blurred vision, which typically resolves four hours or less after treatment ( 27933 ).

Possibly Effective
Back pain.

Most research shows that various forms of cupping may reduce low back pain when compared with no treatment or analgesics alone.

Meta-analyses of clinical studies in patients with low back pain show that cupping, including dry cupping, wet cupping, and pulsatile cupping, reduces low back pain by modest to large amounts when compared with no treatment or control treatment ( 96595 , 101972 ). An individual clinical study included in one of the analyses shows that wet cupping also decreases pain-related disability and the use of pain medications when compared with usual care ( 27911 ). Clinical research also shows that minimal cupping and pulsatile dry cupping, used twice weekly for 4 weeks along with paracetamol as needed, reduces pain by an additional 28% and 38%, respectively, when compared with as needed paracetamol alone. Pain reduction was maintained for an additional 8 weeks after completion of pulsatile dry cupping ( 99698 ). Another clinical study shows that wet cupping for one month is as effective for pain reduction as usual care, which involved an extended duration of rest and a 3-week treatment with oral non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and short-acting muscle relaxants. After another 1-2 months, cupping was superior to usual care in terms of both pain reduction and functional mobility ( 101971 ). However, not all research has been positive. A clinical study in patients with non-specific low back pain shows that dry cupping is no more effective than sham cupping for reducing pain intensity ( 108781 ). Also, results of a small clinical study in patients with non-specific back pain show that wet cupping does not reduce pain or acetaminophen use when compared with a waitlist control ( 27910 ). However, this study was powered to detect only a medium or large effect on pain reduction. Cupping with massage and medicated steam has also been evaluated. A small clinical study in patients with nonspecific low back pain shows that wet cupping on days 1 and 8, followed by massage with medicated steam on days 3-7 and 10-14, improves pain and disability when compared with baseline ( 108782 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group. It is unclear if these effects are due to cupping, other therapies, or the combination.

Possibly Ineffective
Metabolic syndrome.

Wet cupping does not seem to improve coronary risk factors in patients with metabolic syndrome.

A clinical study in adults with metabolic syndrome shows that wet cupping along with dietary advice is no more effective than dietary advice alone for improving lipid profiles and anthropometric parameters ( 27931 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Acne.

It is unclear if cupping is beneficial in patients with acne.

A small clinical study in patients with acne conglobata shows that cupping along with acupuncture and venesection improves acne similarly to isotretinoin 10 mg three times daily ( 27908 ). Also, a clinical study in young adults with moderate to severe facial acne vulgaris receiving azithromycin three times weekly shows that 2 sessions of wet cupping every 6 weeks for 12 weeks is associated with less severe acne lesions at 6 and 12 weeks when compared with sham cupping ( 108791 ). It is unclear if the improvement from baseline differed between groups. Cupping has also been evaluated in combination with ear point bloodletting. A small clinical study shows that ear point bloodletting along with cupping on Back-shu points reduces the risk of acne recurrence when compared with treatment with oral tetracycline and topical cuochuangping ointment ( 27909 ). It is not clear if this effect is due to cupping, ear point bloodletting, or the combination.

Ankylosing spondylitis.

Cupping seems to be beneficial in patients with ankylosing spondylitis.

A meta-analysis of four small clinical studies shows that adding cupping to conventional treatment increases the response rate by about 13% when compared with conventional treatment alone. Conventional treatments include non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs), or tumor necrosis factor (TNF) inhibitors. Cupping also improved function and overall disease activity, including pain, swelling, and fatigue, when compared with conventional treatments alone ( 99200 ).

Asthma.

Although there has been interest in using cupping for asthma, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of cupping for this purpose.

Bell palsy.

Small clinical studies suggest that cupping as an adjunct to acupuncture may be beneficial in patients with Bell palsy.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical studies shows that cupping along with acupuncture improves the cure rate of Bell palsy when compared with acupuncture alone ( 27912 ).

Carpal tunnel syndrome.

Cupping may improve symptoms in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome.

A small clinical study in adults with carpal tunnel syndrome shows that a single application of wet cupping for 5-10 minutes at two different sites reduces pain and symptoms and improves quality of life when compared with a heat compress control ( 27914 ). Furthermore, a small clinical study in adults with carpal tunnel syndrome shows that when used in combination with transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) and ultrasound, dry cupping reduces symptoms, such as pain and weakness, by 58% more than TENS and ultrasound alone. There was also improvement in some, but not all, measures of nerve conduction velocity. However, cupping did not result in additional improvements in function ( 101974 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

It is unclear if cupping is beneficial in patients with CFS.

A small clinical study in patients with CFS in China shows that receiving dry cupping for 10 minutes twice weekly for 5 weeks reduces fatigue, anxiety, and depression and improves sleep when compared with baseline ( 105090 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Constipation.

Dry cupping seems to improve functional constipation in children.

A clinical study in children aged 4 years or older with functional constipation shows that dry cupping every other day for 4 weeks is similarly effective to polyethylene glycol 0.4 grams/kg once daily for reducing the number of painful or hard bowel movements or increasing the number of bowel movements to at least 2 weekly. Polyethylene glycol led to a more rapid improvement in symptoms; however, the treatments were similarly effective at 4 weeks ( 99199 ).

Dysmenorrhea.

It is unclear if dry cupping is beneficial in patients with primary dysmenorrhea.

A small clinical study in patients with primary dysmenorrhea shows that dry cupping on the abdomen, in conjunction with taking fenugreek seed 3 grams twice daily from days 1-3 of the menstrual cycle for 3 consecutive months, is no more effective than fenugreek alone for reducing pain associated with dysmenorrhea. Both treatment regimens are as effective as mefenamic acid 500 mg twice daily during the same time frame ( 99203 ).

Exercise-induced muscle soreness.

It is unclear if dry cupping is beneficial for exercise-induced muscle soreness.

A very small clinical study in young, healthy untrained adults shows that dry cupping after performance of bicep curls improves delayed muscle fatigue (i.e., after 24 hours), but not immediate muscle fatigue, when compared with sham control ( 108786 ). Another small clinical study in healthy, physically active young adults shows that dry cupping after performance of weighted calf raises does not improve immediate muscle stiffness when compared with control ( 108787 ).

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if dry cupping is beneficial in patients with fibromyalgia.

One clinical study in patients with fibromyalgia shows that receiving 5 sessions of dry cupping with 4-8 cups for 30 minutes reduces pain when compared with usual care, but not when compared with sham cupping. Dry cupping also seems to improve quality of life measures, but not sleep quality or physical activity, when compared with usual care ( 96591 ). Sham cupping involved placing cups with a mini hole so that the suction did not last more than 1 minute; it is unclear if this is an appropriate sham procedure. Combining cupping with acupuncture has also been tested for fibromyalgia. An analysis of 2 trials in patients with fibromyalgia shows that cupping plus acupuncture might decrease pain and improve depression when compared with conventional therapy in some patients ( 27919 ). Additional higher quality research is needed to confirm.

Frozen shoulder.

It is unclear if wet cupping is beneficial in patients with frozen shoulder.

A meta-analysis of two small clinical studies shows that wet cupping is similar to acupuncture for improving symptoms of frozen shoulder ( 96597 ).

Herpes zoster (shingles).

Cupping has only been evaluated in combination with other alternative therapies; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Some, but not all, clinical research in patients with shingles shows that cupping, in conjunction with other alternative therapies, might provide some benefit ( 27912 , 27922 , 27923 ). Clinical research in patients with shingles shows that treatment with cupping and bloodletting plus acupuncture or ultraviolet irradiation reduces pain and improves disease course when compared with treatment with acyclovir or no drug therapy ( 27924 , 27925 , 27926 , 27927 ). It is unclear if these effects are due to cupping, other alternative therapies, or the combination.

Hiccups.

Cupping has only been evaluated in combination with other alternative therapies; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A clinical study in adults with hiccups associated with a cerebrovascular accident (CVA) shows that cupping plus acupuncture might be more effective than methylphenidate for alleviating hiccups ( 27928 ).

Hypertension.

It is unclear if wet cupping is beneficial in patients with hypertension.

A clinical study in patients with hypertension shows that administering wet cupping three times on alternating days reduces systolic and diastolic blood pressure by 3 mmHg and 7 mmHg, respectively, when compared with no treatment at the 4-week follow-up. However, this benefit was no longer present at the 8-week follow-up ( 96586 ).

Melasma.

Cupping has only been evaluated in combination with other alternative therapies; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A large clinical study in patients with melasma shows that combined treatment with dry cupping, bloodletting, and acupuncture might be more effective than oral vitamin E plus vitamin C ( 27930 ). It is unclear if this effect is due to cupping, other alternative therapies, or the combination.

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if cupping is beneficial in patients with migraine.

A meta-analysis of two small, low-quality studies in adults with migraines shows that dry cupping along with acupuncture is no more effective than acupuncture alone for reducing migraine symptoms ( 108783 ). A small observational study in patients with chronic migraines has found that one session of wet cupping is associated with reductions in pain at both 1 hour and 1 month when compared with baseline ( 108784 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Neck pain.

Various methods of cupping seem to provide short-term benefit in patients with chronic neck pain.

A meta-analysis of 7 trials shows that short-term use of various types of cupping is more effective than no treatment for neck pain, and two studies show that it is as effective as active treatment ( 101972 ). Although some preliminary clinical research shows that a home-based cupping massage treatment over 12 weeks is no more effective than progressive muscle relaxation for chronic neck pain ( 99201 ), most individual research shows short-term benefit with cupping for chronic neck pain ( 27933 , 27934 , 99201 ). However, cupping might not reduce analgesic use when compared with no therapy ( 27933 ). Additional clinical research shows that cupping plus massage reduces pain and improves quality of life when compared with conventional treatment with analgesics and physical therapy in patients with chronic neck pain ( 27934 ).

Neuropathic pain.

It is unclear if wet cupping is beneficial in patients with neuropathic pain.

A meta-analysis of two small clinical studies shows that wet cupping is similar to acupuncture for improving symptoms of neuropathic pain of the thigh or lateral femoral cutaneous neuritis ( 96597 ). It is unclear how cupping compares with conventional therapies.

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

Wet cupping seems to improve fatty infiltration in patients with NAFLD.

A clinical study in patients with NAFLD shows that adding wet cupping 3 times during the fourth month of a 6-month lifestyle modification period improves fatty infiltration in the liver in 23% of patients, compared with 10% of those only undergoing lifestyle modification. Insulin resistance and serum liver enzyme levels were also improved ( 101853 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of blinding.

Osteoarthritis.

Dry cupping seems to improve pain in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee.

A meta-analysis of small studies shows that adding dry cupping to standard treatment modestly improves pain, stiffness, and physical function based on the Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Osteoarthritis Index (WOMAC) when compared with standard treatment alone. However, wet cupping does not seem to provide any benefit ( 96592 ). Another meta-analysis of small studies shows that cupping has a moderate effect on chronic pain associated with osteoarthritis of the knee ( 101972 ). Dry cupping has also been compared with massage. A small clinical study in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that dry cupping for 15 minutes on alternating days for 20 days is as effective as soft and prolonged massage with chamomile oil for reducing pain and improving knee osteoarthritis outcome scores ( 108789 ).

Osteoporosis.

Cupping has only been evaluated in combination with other alternative therapies; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A small clinical study in patients with primary osteoporosis shows that cupping along with acupuncture and moxibustion improves quality of life when compared with baseline ( 27937 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Pain (chronic).

Cupping seems to provide short-term benefit in patients with various types of chronic pain.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients with chronic pain of various etiologies shows that short-term treatment with dry cupping, wet cupping, or pulsatile cupping has a large effect on pain when compared with no treatment and is as effective as various types of active treatment. It also seems to have moderate effects on disability. However, some limited research shows no effect when cupping is compared with sham cupping ( 101972 ).

Plantar heel pain.

It is unclear if dry cupping is beneficial in patients with plantar heel pain.

A clinical study in patients with plantar heel pain shows that adding dry cupping to calf/plantar fascia stretching and other exercises moderately reduces morning pain two days later when compared with stretching and exercises alone. Immediately after treatment, pain was reduced in those given cupping when compared with baseline. Cupping also improved ankle range of motion and plantar flexor strength immediately after treatment, but not two days later ( 101973 ).

Postherpetic neuralgia.

It is unclear if cupping is beneficial for the treatment and prevention of postherpetic neuralgia.

Some, but not all, clinical research shows that cupping, alone or in combination with other therapies (e.g., acupuncture, moxibustion, Western medicine), might provide some benefit in terms of cure and pain reduction in patients with postherpetic neuralgia when compared with the other therapies alone. It may also reduce the incidence of postherpetic neuralgia in those with active herpes zoster infection at the time of treatment ( 27912 , 27922 , 108785 ).

Postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV).

Dry cupping seems to reduce PONV in patients undergoing laparoscopic cholecystectomy.

A  clinical study in patients undergoing laparoscopic cholecystectomy shows that dry cupping on the P6 acupoint prior to surgery reduces PONV for 24 hours after surgery when compared with sham cupping ( 96587 ).

Postpartum complications.

It is unclear if dry cupping is beneficial for postpartum complications.

A small clinical study in postpartum patients with perineal pain shows that dry cupping for 10-20 minutes on alternating days up to three times weekly for 3 weeks reduces pain when compared with no intervention ( 96584 ). Another small clinical study in primiparous patients with postpartum low back pain shows that receiving dry cupping for 15-20 minutes daily for up to 4 days reduces pain when compared with control ( 96585 ).

Post-stroke fatigue.

Cupping has only been evaluated in combination with electroacupuncture; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A large clinical study shows that cupping along with electroacupuncture reduces post-stroke fatigue when compared with aminobutyric acid, vitamin E, magnesium gluconate, and sertraline ( 27918 ). It is unclear if this effect is due to cupping, acupuncture, or the combination.

Psoriasis.

It is unclear if cupping is beneficial in patients with plaque psoriasis.

A meta-analysis of small, low-quality clinical studies in patients with plaque psoriasis suggests that a specific type of cupping therapy, called moving cupping, may reduce symptoms and the recurrence of psoriasis when compared with various control treatments, such as non-moving cupping and herbs ( 105093 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies and the variability in control treatments. All included studies have been conducted in China; it is unclear if these findings are generalizable to other geographic locations.

Respiratory tract infections.

Although there has been interest in using cupping for respiratory tract infections, including bronchitis, the common cold, and tuberculosis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of cupping for this purpose.

Urticaria.

Cupping, alone or in combination with antihistamines, may improve symptoms in patients with chronic urticaria.

A meta-analysis of small clinical trials in patients with chronic urticaria shows that wet cupping may be similarly effective to antihistamines such as cetirizine and loratadine. A subgroup analysis suggests that adding dry or wet cupping to treatment with antihistamines may further improve symptoms when compared with antihistamines alone ( 105092 ). All included studies have been conducted in China; it is unclear if these findings are generalizable to other geographic locations.

Cervical spondylosis . Cupping may improve pain and other symptoms in patients with cervical spondylosis.

Clinical research in patients with cervical spondylosis shows that cupping has a modest effect on pain ( 96597 , 99202 ). Furthermore, a meta-analysis of 6 studies in patients with cervical spondylosis shows that cupping is modestly better than acupuncture for improving pain and overall symptoms ( 96597 ). It is unclear how cupping compares with standard treatments, such as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), physical therapy, or surgery.

More evidence is needed to rate cupping for these uses.

Cupping is applied to prescribed locations on the body depending on specific symptoms and diagnostic considerations. The locations can be traditional acupoints, as well as other locations ( 27910 , 27917 ). The cup can be made of silicone, glass, bamboo, metal, rubber, and wood ( 99198 , 99205 , 99698 ). The cup can be left in place for a certain amount of time such as 5-20 minutes (fixed cupping), it can be placed for a very short time and reapplied (flash cupping), or it can be moved around with the help of a lubricant on the skin (moving or gliding cupping). The movement is thought to transport stagnant blood or chi, or lift tissue ( 27911 , 27919 , 96587 , 99199 , 99698 , 105093 ). Two common types of cupping are dry cupping and wet cupping. Dry cupping traditionally involves applying heated cups on intact skin which create suction. Modern dry cupping methods use mechanical forces, such as pumps, to create suction. Wet cupping, or cupping with blood-letting, involves first making a small incision on the skin so that when the heated cup is applied, the suction results in blood-letting ( 96592 , 99199 , 99202 ). In Chinese medicine, cupping is classified into different groups: weak/light, medium, strong, moving, needle, moxa/hot needle, empty/flash, full/bleeding, herbal, and water cupping ( 99199 ). Pulsatile cupping is a type of dry cupping that uses a pulsatile cupping device, instead of heat, to form a suction. The device provides a specified negative pressure with suction intervals ( 99698 ).

The exact mechanism of action of cupping is not known. In the theory of traditional Chinese medicine, cupping is thought to have therapeutic effects by removing stagnant energy or "qi". For example, in wet cupping, or cupping with bloodletting, the released blood is thought to be stagnant. Release of stagnant blood is thought to remove waste or morbid humor, and bring about balanced and renewed qi ( 27910 , 96587 , 96592 ). Dry cupping moves stagnant energy or morbid humor by applying vigorous strokes with the cups on intact skin. Additionally, the suction of dry cupping pulls the underlying tissues and increases the local blood and lymphatic circulation, which is also thought to move stagnant qi ( 96587 , 99198 ). Physically, cupping causes local hyperemia and disturbs the skin vasculature, resulting in local redness and swelling ( 96588 ). In human research, cupping has been shown to increase local blood perfusion ( 99202 ). Hyperemia may induce the release of neurotransmitters such as nitric oxide and endogenous opioids like endorphins. These chemicals may in turn cause euphoria and reduce the perception of pain. Additionally, cupping therapy has been theorized to have antioxidant effects and support the immune system ( 96592 ). In human research, cupping has been shown to reduce levels of inflammatory markers such as C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate ( 99200 ). A laboratory analysis shows that cupping-induced blisters contain proteins involved in immune response, anti-oxidation, and tissue repair. These proteins differ from those found in the blisters of scalds ( 99204 ), suggesting that cupping might have both local and systemic effects.
