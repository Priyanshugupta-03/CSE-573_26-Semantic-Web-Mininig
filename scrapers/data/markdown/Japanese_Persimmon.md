## Overview

Japanese persimmon is a tree that is widely distributed and cultivated throughout Asia ( 100447 , 100448 ). The fruit is eaten as food, while the fruit and leaves have been used as medicine ( 13391 , 13392 , 100449 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, Japanese persimmon leaf or fruit are used for hypertension, constipation, hiccups, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, and stroke. Other uses include inducing diuresis, improving blood flow, and reducing body temperature. Topically, Japanese persimmon leaf is used for skin problems such as eczema, skin eruptions, and acne.

Possibly Safe when the leaf extract or fruit fiber are used orally and appropriately. Japanese persimmon leaf extract 2 grams daily has been used with apparent safety in clinical research for up to 8 weeks ( 100449 ). Japanese persimmon fruit fiber 9-15 grams daily has also been used with apparent safety for up to 12 weeks ( 100446 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Orally, Japanese persimmon fruit may cause allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis, in sensitive people; however, this is rare ( 13391 , 13392 ). Excessive consumption of Japanese persimmon fruit can cause bezoars ( 100444 , 100456 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, excessive consumption of Japanese persimmon fruit can cause bezoars. In a case report, a 50-year-old male presented with a bezoar due to eating unpeeled Japanese persimmons with seeds for 10 years ( 100444 ). In another case report, a 73-year-old male with a history of partial stomach removal due to gastric cancer, presented with a bezoar due to consumption of 30-40 Japanese persimmon fruits in one season ( 100456 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Hyperlipidemia.

One small clinical study in patients with elevated cholesterol levels shows that consuming cookie bars containing tannin-rich fiber from unripe Japanese persimmon fruit 3 or 5 grams three times daily before meals for 12 weeks lowers cholesterol levels when compared with placebo ( 100446 ).

Impaired glucose tolerance (prediabetes).

Preliminary clinical research in patients with prediabetes shows that taking tablets of Japanese persimmon leaf extract 1 gram twice daily for 8 weeks modestly reduces two-hour postprandial glucose when compared with placebo ( 100449 ). It is unknown if Japanese persimmon reduces the risk of developing diabetes in people with prediabetes.

More evidence is needed to rate Japanese persimmon for these uses.

#### Adult
Oral:

Hyperlipidemia : Cookie bars containing tannin-rich fiber from unripe Japanese persimmon fruit 3 or 5 grams three times daily before meals for 12 weeks has been used ( 100446 ). Impaired glucose tolerance (prediabetes) : Japanese persimmon leaf extract 1 gram twice daily for 8 weeks has been used ( 100449 ). Each 1-gram dose of extract reportedly contained 7.5 mg of quercetin and kaempferol.

#### ANTICOAGULANT/ANTIPLATELET DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

There is concern that Japanese persimmon leaf constituents might potentiate effects of anticoagulant and antiplatelet drugs. In vitro research shows that certain Japanese persimmon leaf extract protein fractions have anticoagulant activity. These fractions are thought to bind fibrinogen leading to thrombin inactivation ( 100454 ). This effect has not been shown in humans.

Some anticoagulant and antiplatelet drugs include aspirin, clopidogrel (Plavix), dalteparin (Fragmin), enoxaparin (Lovenox), heparin, indomethacin (Indocin), ticlopidine (Ticlid), warfarin (Coumadin), and others.

---

#### ANTIHYPERTENSIVE DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence PROBABLE Level of Evidence B (Nonrandomized clinical trial)

Japanese persimmon is thought to have hypotensive effects ( 13389 ). Theoretically, concurrent use might increase risk of hypotension with drugs that lower blood pressure. These include captopril (Capoten), enalapril (Vasotec), losartan (Cozaar), valsartan (Diovan), diltiazem (Cardizem), amlodipine (Norvasc), hydrochlorothiazide (HydroDiuril), furosemide (Lasix), and many others.

|

#### ANTICOAGULANT/ANTIPLATELET HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS

Concomitant use of Japanese persimmon with herbs with anticoagulant or antiplatelet effects might increase the risk of bleeding in some people ( 100454 ). These herbs include angelica, clove, danshen, garlic, ginger, ginkgo, Panax ginseng, red clover, willow, and others.

---

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOTENSIVE EFFECTS

Japanese persimmon is thought to have hypotensive effects. Theoretically, combining Japanese persimmon with other herbs or supplements with hypotensive effects might increase the risk of hypotension. Some of these herbs include andrographis, casein peptides, cat's claw, coenzyme Q-10, fish oil, L-arginine, lycium, stinging nettle, theanine, and others.

|

#### BLEEDING DISORDERS

Japanese persimmon might have anticoagulant effects ( 100454 ). Theoretically, taking Japanese persimmon might increase the risk of bleeding and bruising in patients with bleeding disorders.

#### HYPOTENSION

Japanese persimmon is thought to have hypotensive effects ( 13389 ). Theoretically, Japanese persimmon might worsen hypotension or interfere with drug therapy to increase blood pressure.

#### SURGERY

Japanese persimmon might affect blood pressure or have anticoagulant effects. Theoretically, Japanese persimmon might interfere with blood pressure control or increase the risk of excessive bleeding during and after surgical procedures. Tell patients to discontinue Japanese persimmon at least 2 weeks before elective surgical procedures.

None known.

#### General

The applicable parts of Japanese persimmon are the leaf and fruit ( 13389 ). Biologically active constituents in Japanese persimmon include amino acids, carotenoids, flavonoids, tannins, catechins, procyanidins, polyphenols, vitamin A, and vitamin C ( 100450 , 104761 ). The main polyphenol constituents of Japanese fruit extract include gallic acid, catechin, epicatechin, gallocatechin, gallocatechin gallate, and epicatechin gallate ( 104761 ). Fermenting Japanese persimmon fruit appears to increase the concentration of gallic acid and decrease the concentration of other polyphenol constituents ( 104760 ).

#### Anti-arthritic effects

Animal research shows that Japanese persimmon fruit extract reduces erythema, swelling, synovitis, and cartilage damage in rats with chemically-induced arthritis. This effect is thought to be a result of anti-inflammatory or anti-oxidant activity ( 104761 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

Animal research shows that Japanese persimmon fruit extract reduces chemically-induced paw edema. This effect is thought to be a result of anti-inflammatory action ( 104761 ).

#### Anti-obesity effects

Fermented Japanese persimmon fruit extract may reduce fatty acid synthesis via activation of 5' adenosine monophosphate-activated protein kinase (AMPK) and downregulation of related genes in mice. Animal research shows that fermented Japanese persimmon fruit extract may reduce body weight, abdominal fat, liver fat, and cholesterol in mice that are fed high-fat diets ( 104760 ).

#### Anticancer effects

Preliminary research suggests that the peel of Japanese persimmon fruit might have antitumor effects ( 13390 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

Japanese persimmon extracts have antioxidant effects, which are thought to reduce the harm of gamma radiation, cardiovascular disease, and aging ( 100448 , 100452 , 100458 , 104761 ). Naoxinqing (NXQ) tablet, a standardized herbal product that contains extract of Japanese persimmon leaves, is traditionally used for its purported antioxidant effects in patients with cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases. In vitro research shows that quercetin and kaempferol, ingredients isolated from Japanese persimmon leaf, have antioxidant activity ( 100448 ). In vitro research also shows that proanthocyanidins extracted with acetone from Japanese persimmon peel reduce oxidative damage and expression of sirtuin 1 in a hydrogen peroxide-induced cellular senescence model. This suggests an anti-aging effect ( 100452 ).

#### Antipyretic effects

Japanese persimmon fruit appears to lower peripheral human body temperature ( 13389 ).

#### Antiviral effects

There is interest in using Japanese persimmon for its antiviral activity. In vitro research shows that Japanese persimmon extracts containing 22% tannins inhibit viral attachment to cells and inhibit viral infectivity. Tested viruses included influenza virus H3N2, H5N3, herpes simplex virus-1, poliovirus, adenovirus, rotavirus, and others ( 100455 ).

#### Bone effects

There is interest in using Japanese persimmon for slowing osteoporosis. Animal research shows that polysaccharides isolated from Japanese persimmon leaves reduce osteoclast activity and bone loss in mice models of postmenopausal osteoporosis ( 100447 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

Both Japanese persimmon leaf and fruit juice reportedly have hypotensive effects ( 13389 ). Japanese persimmon leaf contains flavonoids such as astragalin. Astragalin seems to have hypotensive effects, possibly by inhibiting angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE) activity ( 13388 ). Extracts of Japanese persimmon leaf seem to have vasorelaxant activity, which might explain its use for hypertension. It might stimulate the release of the endothelial vasodilator, nitric oxide ( 13385 ). The leaf extract might also have free radical scavenging activity ( 13386 ). Other preliminary research suggests it might have immunological activity ( 13387 ). Extracts of Japanese persimmon leaf also seem to have anticoagulant properties. Research in vitro shows that certain Japanese persimmon leaf extract protein fractions have anticoagulant activity. These fractions are thought to bind fibrinogen leading to thrombin inactivation ( 100454 ).

#### Dermatologic effects

Japanese persimmon leaf extract and astragalin appear to inhibit histamine release from mast cells ( 13388 , 100450 ). Animal models of atopic dermatitis suggest that oral administration might relieve symptoms ( 13388 ). There is also interest in using Japanese persimmon leaves to prevent wrinkles and dark coloration. In vitro, ethanolic extracts of Japanese persimmon leaves inhibit xanthine oxidase inhibition, tyrosinase, collagenase, and elastase enzymes, which suggests that this extract has whitening and anti-wrinkle effects ( 100445 ).

#### Neurological effects

There is interest in using flavonoids from Japanese persimmon to slow progression of Alzheimer disease. Research from animal models shows that Japanese persimmon leaf extracts reduce learning and memory impairment, B-amyloid peptide production, and senile plaque activation ( 100453 ).

#### Ocular effects

There is interest in using Japanese persimmon to slow retinitis pigmentosa, age-related macular degeneration (AMD), and diabetic retinopathy because of its antioxidant effects and effects on angiogenesis. Animal models of AMD suggest that Japanese persimmon leaf extract increases retinal thickness and reduces oxidant effects of N-methyl-N-nitrosourea ( 100451 ). Animal research also suggests that Japanese persimmon ethanol extract improves angiogenesis and corneal neovascularization after alkali burns to the cornea ( 100457 ).
