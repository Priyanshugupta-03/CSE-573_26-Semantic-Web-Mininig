## Overview

Colonic irrigation involves the installation of water through the anus into the colon and rectum. Colonic hydrotherapy utilizes large volumes of water that are immediately expelled.  Enemas utilize small volumes of liquid that are usually held for minutes before expelling ( 96868 ).

## History

There is evidence of colonic irrigation being used as far back as 1500 BC in Ancient Egypt. In 1987, it became popular for use in children with spina bifida ( 96871 ).

## People Use This For

Colonic irrigation is used to help with bowel function in patients with constipation or fecal incontinence due to various etiologies such as idiopathic causes, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), problems related to surgery, or neurogenic bowel dysfunction associated with spinal cord injury, spina bifida, and multiple sclerosis. It is also used for opioid withdrawal and to reduce endoscopy-associated spasm. Colonic irrigation is used to cleanse the colon and treat conditions that practitioners believe are caused by colonic waste accumulation or "autointoxication." It is also used by some people for hypertension, asthma, ulcerative colitis, arthritis, alcoholism, and sinus congestion. Coffee given by enema is used for cancer.

Possibly Safe when warm water is used rectally. Colonic irrigation with water has been used in clinical trials without reported serious adverse effects ( 88497 , 88499 , 96870 , 96872 ).

Possibly Unsafe when coffee enemas are used rectally for colonic irrigation. Coffee enemas have been linked to cases of proctocolitis, severe electrolyte abnormalities, and septicemia leading to severe side effects including death ( 3026 , 3347 , 3349 , 6652 , 103273 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of other methods of colonic irrigation.

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when warm water or soap suds are used rectally for colonic irrigation. Colonic irrigation with water or soap suds has been used in clinical trials without reported serious adverse effects ( 96871 , 100585 ). However, one case of severe hyponatremia following colonic irrigation with tap water has been reported in a 12-year-old female ( 103272 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information; avoid using.

#### General

The safety and associated side effects of colonic irrigation depend on the method and substances used. While rare, colonic irrigation with tap water has been associated with electrolyte imbalances, namely hyponatremia ( 96868 , 96872 , 103272 ). The most common side effects are gastrointestinal. Other rare side effects may include pain on insertion with the catheter, leakage after irrigation, abdominal cramps, anal bleeding, bloating, and rectal perforation requiring surgery ( 96868 , 96871 , 96872 , 96873 ). Amebiasis, with bloody diarrhea, has resulted in death in some cases due to contaminated irrigation systems ( 96868 ). Coffee enemas used for detoxification have been linked to at least three deaths. Two of these deaths are related to severe electrolyte imbalance, and a third is associated with polymicrobial septicemia following use of coffee enema ( 3026 , 3347 , 3349 , 6652 ). Coffee enemas have also been reported to cause proctocolitis ( 96868 , 103273 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Pain on insertion of the catheter has been described ( 96871 ). Rarely, leakage after irrigation, abdominal cramps, anal bleeding, and bloating have also occurred ( 96872 , 96873 ). Rectal perforation requiring surgery has occurred very rarely ( 96868 ). At least 36 cases of amebiasis, with bloody diarrhea, occurred following colonic irrigation at a clinic in Western Colorado between 1978 and 1980. Ten patients required colectomy and 6 died. This outbreak is hypothesized to be due to contaminated irrigation systems ( 96868 ). At least 5 cases of proctocolitis related to the use of coffee enemas have been reported ( 96868 , 103273 ). A case of colitis related to the use of a hydrogen peroxide enema mixed with warm water has been reported in an adult with constipation-predominant irritable bowel syndrome. Symptoms of pain, cramping, and bloody diarrhea started a few hours after the use of the enema and continued for 1 week until the patient presented to the emergency department ( 108619 ).

#### Hematologic

Rectally administered coffee enemas used for detoxification have been linked to at least three deaths. One of these deaths was associated with polymicrobial septicemia following use of coffee enema ( 3026 ). Two of these deaths were related to severe electrolyte imbalance ( 3347 , 3349 , 6652 ). One case of severe hyponatremia after colonic irrigation with tap water has been reported in a 12-year-old female ( 103272 ). Colonic irrigation-induced hyponatremia has also been reported in a 42-year-old female ( 96868 ). While electrolyte imbalance following irrigation with tap water seems to be rare, periodic evaluation of serum electrolytes should be considered ( 96868 , 96872 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

There is a case report of a 42-year-old woman female with transient confusion and memory loss due to acute water intoxication-mediated hyponatremia associated with colonic irrigation ( 96868 ). In another case, a 12-year-old femalegirl reported persistent hiccups provoked by severe hyponatremia after colonic irrigation with tap water ( 103272 ).

Possibly Effective
Fecal incontinence.

Overall, evidence from preliminary clinical research shows that colonic irrigation improves fecal incontinence when compared with baseline in both adults and children. Using colonic irrigation (Biotrol Irrimatic pump, Braun) successfully treated fecal incontinence in approximately 60% of adult patients with fecal incontinence of various idiopathic or surgical causes. Overall quality of life was also improved ( 96872 ). Additionally, colonic irrigation (Peristeen, Anal Irrigation System, Coloplast A/S) 1-7 times weekly for 10 weeks improves fecal incontinence when compared with conservative bowel management in patients with neurogenic bowel dysfunction due to spinal cord injury. Scores on the neurogenic bowel dysfunction scale improved from 14.8 to 10.4, compared to no improvements in the conservative treatment group ( 96870 ). In patients with neurogenic bowel dysfunction due to multiple sclerosis, starting with daily colonic irrigation (Peristeen, Coloplast A/S) and reducing to every 2-3 days as needed reduces episodes of fecal incontinence by approximately 81% when compared to baseline ( 96873 ). Other clinical reports suggest that self-administered colonic irrigation using 500 to 1000 mL of warm water as needed might benefit patients with fecal soiling or incontinence ( 88499 ).

Preliminary clinical research in children with incontinence or constipation and overflow soiling due to spina bifida, anorectal anomaly, Hirschsprung disease, or other complex causes shows that use of a specific type of colonic irrigation (Peristeen, Anal Irrigation System; Coloplast A/S) over a 2-year period improves soiling frequency or achievement of fecal continence in 83% of children when compared to baseline ( 96871 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Constipation.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with constipation of various etiologies shows that using colonic irrigation with a pump (Biotrol Irrimatic pump, Braun) or irrigation bag eliminates straining associated with constipation in 30% of patients and improves straining in an additional 50% of patients. Colonic irrigation also improves overall quality of life and feelings of incomplete evacuation in these patients ( 96872 ). Furthermore, preliminary clinical research in patients with neurogenic bowel dysfunction due to spinal cord injury shows that colonic irrigation 1-7 times weekly for 10 weeks improves constipation when compared to conservative bowel management ( 96870 ).

Clinical research in otherwise healthy children of all ages presenting to the emergency room due to constipation shows that administration of a soap suds enema 20 mL/kg one time successfully relieves constipation in 82% of patients ( 100585 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Endoscopy-associated adverse effects.

Preliminary clinical research shows that warm water irrigation might reduce discomfort, but does not affect pain nor the incidence of colonic spasm, when compared to using room temperature water in patients undergoing a colonoscopy ( 88497 ).

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

Preliminary clinical research shows that using colonic irrigation twice daily for 1-4 weeks improves symptoms of abdominal pain, satisfaction, and discomfort in patients with IBS. It also more than doubles the frequency of bowel movements and reduces hardness of stools in patients with constipation-predominant IBS when compared to baseline ( 96869 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Opioid withdrawal.

Preliminary clinical reports suggests that colonic irrigation with a Chinese herbal solution for 3-8 days along with dihydroetorphine (DHE) and methadone therapy might improve withdrawal symptoms when compared to DHE and methadone alone in heroin addicts ( 88498 ).

Quality of life.

An observational study in patients with a terminal colostomy has found that practicing colonic irrigation is associated with a median quality of life score of 69, compared with a score of 58 in those not practicing colonic irrigation ( 108618 ).

More evidence is needed to rate colonic irrigation for these uses.

Colonic hydrotherapy is the term used to describe colonic irrigation involving large amounts of water that are almost immediately evacuated ( 96868 ). Enemas involve the use of smaller amounts of fluid that are typically retained prior to evacuation ( 96868 ). Retrograde colonic irrigation involves the administration of a liquid into the colon using a pump or an irrigation bag. A colonic irrigation pump set consists of a pump with a tube and a cone-shaped tip or balloon catheter. The tip or catheter is placed into the anal canal while the patient is sitting on a toilet. Sometimes an inflatable balloon is used to prevent the leakage of fluid until needed. Other types use the cone-shaped tip to prevent leakage ( 96870 , 96871 , 96872 , 96873 ). An irrigation bag uses gravity instead of a pump to eject water for irrigation ( 96872 ). Tap water at body temperature is used for irrigation in amounts of 500-2000 mL ( 88499 , 96870 , 96872 ) or 10-20 mL/kg in children as young as 2 years ( 96871 ). Colonic irrigation can be done more than once per day or every 2-3 days as needed and can take up to 40 minutes to complete ( 96869 , 96870 , 96871 , 96872 , 96873 ). Soap or laxatives are sometimes added to the water for irrigation if needed ( 96872 , 100585 ). In some types of colonic irrigation, herbs are added to assist with "cleansing" ( 88498 ). Despite serious safety concerns, coffee enemas involve the use of coffee as the fluid ( 96868 ).

#### IRRITABLE BOWEL SYNDROME (IBS)

In one case report, an adult with constipation-predominant IBS developed colitis after the use of a hydrogen peroxide enema mixed with warm water ( 108619 ). Colonic irrigation should be used with caution in patients with IBS.

None known.

Colonic irrigation is used mainly for fecal incontinence and constipation. Some people, especially those with neurogenic or other types of bowel dysfunctions, are unable to normally control the function of the bowels. Colonic irrigation is used to remove impacted feces associated with severe constipation. It also removes feces from the rectum and colon to prevent leakage in those with fecal incontinence. Colonic irrigation prevents leakage for up to 2 days in some people ( 96869 , 96870 , 96871 , 96872 ). Some people promote the use of colonic irrigation for the elimination of toxins. However, there is no reliable scientific evidence to support the idea that the body retains toxins from normal diet or activity.
