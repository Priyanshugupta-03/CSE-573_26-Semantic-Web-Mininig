## Overview

Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) is a designation given by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). It is applied to substances that are intentionally added to food (food additives), and does not apply to substances that are administered by any other route or method ( 101501 ). GRAS was created by the FDA in 1958 under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FFDCA). It is not recognized outside of the United States. The FDA defines "safe" as reasonable certainty that the substance in question will not be harmful, provided it is used as intended. Demonstration of safety depends on data regarding the characteristics of the substance and the consumer population for every category in which the substance will be used in the intended quantity. A substance may be exempt from these requirements and granted GRAS status if qualified experts have recognized that the substance, under the conditions of intended uses, has been shown to be adequately safe ( 101501 ). If a substance is not currently listed as GRAS, a manufacturer must submit a GRAS notification providing justification for the addition of the substance to the GRAS list prior to using it as a food additive. The FDA can accept the application as-is or reject the application due to insufficient information ( 101501 ). Substances are granted GRAS status only on the basis of their intended uses; all other uses of these substances may or may not be harmful and require separate review. For example, a sweetener such as tagatose, which received GRAS status in foods in 2001, needed separate review to obtain GRAS status as a sweetener in toothpaste and mouthwash, and other cosmetic products ( 101502 ).

GRAS status is granted to a wide range of substances. It is only intended to indicate the safety of these substances when used as food additives in the intended amounts. See specific monographs for safety information.

GRAS status does not indicate that the substance will not cause an allergic reaction. Additionally, consuming GRAS substances in greater amounts than intended can cause undesired effects, including toxicity. GRAS status is not comprehensive; therefore, it does not indicate that a substance that is safe in one product is safe in all circumstances ( 101501 ).

GRAS status pertains solely to the safety of a substance for use as a food additive; it does not consider effectiveness for any purpose. See specific monographs for effectiveness information.

GRAS status is granted to a wide range of substances. See specific monographs for information about whether an ingredient has been granted GRAS status.
