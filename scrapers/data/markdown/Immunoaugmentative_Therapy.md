# Immunoaugmentative Therapy
Other Common Names IAT, Immunoaugmentation, Immuno-augmentative Therapy, Terapia inmunoaumentativa, Thérapie Immuno-Augmentative
|

## Overview

In the 1950s, a zoologist named Lawrence Burton developed the theory of immunoaugmentative therapy. It involves injecting blood proteins from healthy human donors into cancer patients. He claimed that this therapy caused cancer to go into remission in mice. In the 1970s, he began offering this treatment to patients with cancer, stating that it was a safe, non-toxic method of augmenting the patient's own immune system ( 102928 , 102929 ). KEY HIGHLIGHTS

- Marketed for the treatment of cancer, but there is no evidence to support its use.
- Possibly unsafe when used subcutaneously as directed. Some patients have developed serious infections after therapy, possibly due to the use of contaminated blood products.
- No known major interactions.
- Immunoaugmentative therapy is purported to augment a patient's immune system. However, evidence to support this claim has never been published.

After Burton moved his practice to the Bahamas, the Bahamian Ministry of Health determined that there was no evidence of benefit with the use of immunoaugmentative therapy. The US National Cancer Institute also attempted to evaluate this therapy, but Burton did not comply with requests for experimental evidence or administration and processing techniques (
102928
,
102929
). The Bahamian clinic was threatened with closure by Bahamian authorities after multiple patients developed serious infections from receiving immunoaugmentative therapy. This led the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) to ban the import of any immunoaugmentative therapy products to the US in 1986. Despite these concerns, the center in the Bahamas remains open, as do centers located in Germany and Mexico (
102928
).

## People Use This For

Subcutaneously, immunoaugmentative therapy has been used for cancer.

Possibly Unsafe when administered subcutaneously. Multiple case reports suggest that immunoaugmentative therapy might cause serious infections, such as HIV and hepatitis, due to the use of contaminated products. Additionally, use of immunoaugmentative therapy in lieu of proven medical treatments can cause serious harm and death ( 102928 , 102929 ).

#### General

Immunoaugmentative therapy has never been thoroughly evaluated in clinical research. Case reports suggest that subcutaneous immunoaugmentative therapy can cause fatigue, flu-like illness, pain at the injection site, generalized pain, and swelling ( 102927 ). Some patients have developed serious infections after receiving immunoaugmentative therapy, raising concerns regarding the source and sterilization processes for these blood-derived products ( 102928 , 102929 ). Additionally, use of immunoaugmentative therapy in lieu of proven medical treatments can cause serious harm and death ( 102929 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Subcutaneously, flu-like illness has been reported after immunoaugmentative therapy ( 102927 ).

#### Immunologic

Subcutaneously, serious infections, such as HIV and hepatitis B, have been reported after receiving immunoaugmentative therapy. This has raised the concern that the products used in therapy may be derived from infected individuals, without undergoing adequate sterilization processes ( 102928 , 102929 ). In fact, a laboratory analysis of 18 vials of immunoaugmentative therapy treatment materials tested identified the presence of various viruses ( 102928 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

Subcutaneously, pain at the injection site and generalized pain have been reported after immunoaugmentative therapy ( 102927 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Subcutaneously, fatigue has been reported after immunoaugmentative therapy ( 102927 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Cancer.

A retrospective evaluation of 46 cancer patients consecutively treated at an immunoaugmentative therapy clinic in Mexico suggests that this therapy does not alter the course of cancer or provide any measurable benefits ( 102927 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the retrospective nature of the chart review. However, the providers of immunoaugmentative therapy have refused multiple opportunities to participate in controlled clinical trials ( 102928 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the effectiveness of immunoaugmentative therapy for this use.

Immunoaugmentative therapy is only available at designated centers, which have been established in the Bahamas, Germany, and Mexico. Patients can receive immunoaugmentative therapy only if they have been diagnosed with cancer. The patient is then screened to determine the appropriateness of immunoaugmentative therapy for his/her specific condition. The prescription for therapy is based upon the patient's immune status, tumor activity status, and the effects of any previous immunoaugmentative therapy. It is unclear how the patient's immune status is determined, as it is calculated using a patented method on a private computer program ( 102928 ). During therapy, patients may receive anywhere from 1-12 injections per day. These injections are self-administered subcutaneously. In general, treatment lasts about 10-12 weeks. Patients typically return to the clinic for about two weeks every four to six months ( 102928 ).

Immunoaugmentative therapy is based on the assumption that the human immune system has an anticancer surveillance function made up of discrete substances that can be extracted from the blood. It also assumes that these substances can be injected into a patient with cancer in the appropriate amounts to reactivate the natural anticancer mechanism in the human body ( 102928 ). Immunoaugmentative therapy treatment products can contain three different human blood components: tumor necrosis factor (TNF), tumor complement factor (TCF), and de-blocking protein factor (DPF). It has been suggested that injections containing TNF may increase the body's ability to destroy cancerous cells, and that injections containing TCF may help increase the immune system's response to cancerous cells. The third component, DPF, is believed to remove another protein, called blocking protein factor, from the blood. In theory, the blocking protein factor prevents the immune system from detecting cancer, allowing the cancer to grow in the body. Therefore, proponents believe that injections containing DPF may increase the body's ability to detect cancerous cells in the body. However, there is debate over whether or not DPF and blocking protein factor even exist ( 102928 ). The only evidence that Burton has ever provided in support of immunoaugmentative therapy are patient charts. Additionally, he has declined participation in controlled clinical trials ( 102928 ). After review of available patient charts, regulatory bodies and researchers have been unable to confirm any evidence of benefit from immunoaugmentative therapy ( 102927 , 102928 ).
