# Japanese Apricot
Scientific Name Prunus mume (synonym: Armeniaca mume) Family Rosaceae Other Common Names Abricotier du Japon, Abricotier Du Japon, Abricotier Japonais, Albaricoque de Flores Japonesas, Albaricoque Japonés, Albaricoquero del Japón, Albaricoquero Japonés, Apricot Tree, Árbol de Chabacano, Bainiku-ekisu, Beni Chidori, Chabacano Japonés, Chidori Beni, Chinese Green Plum, Ciruela China, Japanese Flowering Apricot, Japanese Plum, Japanese Ume, Jugo de Wu-Mei, Mae-Sil-Na-Moo, Mei, Mume, Omae, Pickled Ume, Smoke-Dried Ume, Ubai, Ume, Umé, Ume Brandy, Ume en Escabeche, Umeboshi, Umezu Polyphenols, Wu-Mei Juice

Caution

Do not confuse Japanese apricot with Apricot or with Apricot Kernel.

## Overview

Japanese apricot is a deciduous tree that produces a small yellow fruit. The fruit and juice have been used as food and traditional medicine for at least 1000 years ( 100020 , 100023 , 100026 , 100029 , 114030 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, Japanese apricot is used for fever, cough, common cold, gastrointestinal disorders, gastrointestinal infections, gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), Helicobacter pylori, dyspepsia, diarrhea, insomnia, fatigue, hepatitis C, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), menopausal symptoms, neck stiffness, gout, cancer, hypertension, and prevention of cardiovascular disease. It is also used for detoxification and thirst. Topically, Japanese apricot is used for sunburn and as a beauty lotion. As food, the fruit of Japanese apricot is dried and pickled for use. A liqueur is made by steeping the fruit in alcohol and sugar.

Likely Safe when the processed fruit is used orally in food amounts. Advise patients to only consume Japanese apricot fruit products that have been processed through pickling or other means to eliminate toxic constituents. The raw fruit is considered toxic because it contains the cyanogenic glycosides prunasin and amygdalin ( 13198 ).

Possibly Safe when Japanese apricot dried fruit extract is used orally and appropriately, short-term. A specific Japanese apricot dried fruit extract has been used safely in doses of up to 300 mg daily for up to 12 weeks ( 100027 ).

Possibly Unsafe when the raw fruit is used orally in food amounts. The raw fruit is considered toxic because it contains the cyanogenic glycosides prunasin and amygdalin ( 13198 ). Advise patients to only consume Japanese apricot fruit products that have been processed through pickling or other means to eliminate these toxic constituents. There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of other forms of Japanese apricot.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Orally, Japanese apricot dried fruit extract seems to be well tolerated. Side effects reported in clinical research include gastric intolerance ( 100027 ). Diluted Japanese apricot juice concentrate may cause constipation ( 100029 ). Allergies to Japanese apricot are rare ( 100028 ). The raw fruit of Japanese apricot is thought to be toxic due to the cyanogenic glycosides prunasin and amygdalin ( 13198 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, Japanese apricot dried fruit extract 300 mg daily has been reported to cause gastric intolerance in one patient in one clinical study ( 100027 ). In other clinical research, dilute Japanese apricot juice concentrate has been reported to cause constipation in one patient ( 100029 ).

#### Immunologic

Orally, food-dependent, exercise-induced anaphylaxis related to Japanese apricot has been reported, but this is rare. In one case, symptoms including loss of consciousness occurred in a 12-year-old girl who had eaten Japanese apricot at breakfast and went jogging 90 minutes later ( 100028 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD).

Population research suggests that individuals who consume dried or pickled Japanese apricot at least once daily are 37% less likely to have GERD and approximately 28% less likely to have symptoms of gastrointestinal dysmotility when compared to those who consume Japanese apricot less than once daily. However, consumption of Japanese apricot does not seem to be associated with a reduced risk of reflux symptoms. Also, benefits do not occur in elderly individuals or in those with Helicobacter pylori infection ( 100020 ).

Helicobacter pylori.

A small preliminary clinical trial shows that taking a 1% Japanese apricot solution, known as Bainiku-ekisu solution, 130 mL twice daily for 12 weeks does not reduce Helicobacter pylori infection compared with baseline ( 100029 ). Also, population research shows that consuming dried or pickled Japanese apricot at least three times daily is not associated with a reduced rate of H. pylori infection when compared to consuming Japanese apricot less often. However, when stratified by age, there is some evidence that higher intake of dried or pickled Japanese apricot may reduce the risk of H. pylori infection in younger but not elderly patients ( 100026 ). Further clinical research is needed in a larger population.

Hepatitis C.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with hepatitis C shows that taking Japanese apricot extract (Misatol ME, AdaBio Co. Ltd.) 6.5 grams twice daily for 12 weeks reduces levels of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) by 31% and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) by 29% when compared with baseline ( 100023 ). It is unclear if taking if this product affects viral load or the progression of chronic liver disease and cirrhosis.

Hypertension.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with mild to moderate hypertension shows that taking Japanese apricot polyphenols 800 mg daily for 12 weeks does not reduce blood pressure when compared to placebo ( 100021 ). A larger study is needed in order to confirm these findings.

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

Preliminary clinical research in patients with NAFLD shows that taking Japanese apricot extract (Misatol ME, AdaBio Co. Ltd.) 6.5 grams twice daily for 12 weeks reduces levels of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) by 31% each when compared with baseline ( 100023 ). It is unclear if taking if this product reduces the risk of NAFLD complications.

More evidence is needed to rate Japanese apricot for these uses.

#### Adult
Oral:

Hepatitis C : Japanese apricot extract (Misatol ME, AdaBio Co. Ltd.) 6.5 grams twice daily for 12 weeks has been used ( 100023 ). Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) : Japanese apricot extract (Misatol ME, AdaBio Co. Ltd.) 6.5 grams twice daily for 12 weeks has been used ( 100023 ).

#### ANTICOAGULANT/ANTIPLATELET DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Some constituents of Japanese apricot flower extract might have antiplatelet properties ( 13199 ). Theoretically, combining Japanese flower extract with drugs that have antiplatelet or anticoagulant effects might increase the risk of bruising or bleeding. Some of these drugs include aspirin, clopidogrel (Plavix), dalteparin (Fragmin), enoxaparin (Lovenox), heparin, indomethacin (Indocin), ticlopidine (Ticlid), warfarin (Coumadin), and others.

---

#### ANTIDIABETES DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Theoretically, taking Japanese apricot in combination with antidiabetes drugs might lower blood glucose and increase the risk of hypoglycemia. Japanese apricot fruit extract has been shown to reduce levels of fasting glucose in a diabetic animal model ( 100022 ). However, this has not been shown in humans. Until more is known, use caution.

Some antidiabetes medications include glimepiride (Amaryl), glyburide (DiaBeta, Glynase PresTab, Micronase), insulin, pioglitazone (Actos), rosiglitazone (Avandia), chlorpropamide (Diabinese), glipizide (Glucotrol), tolbutamide (Orinase), and others.

|

#### ANTICOAGULANT/ANTIPLATELET HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS

Some constituents of Japanese apricot flower extract might have antiplatelet properties ( 13199 ). Theoretically, combining Japanese flower extract with other herbs and supplements that inhibit platelet aggregation might increase the risk of bleeding in some people due to Japanese Apricot's effects on platelet aggregation. Some other herbs with antiplatelet effects include angelica, clove, danshen, garlic, ginger, ginkgo, and many others.

---

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOGLYCEMIC POTENTIAL

Japanese apricot fruit extract might reduce fasting blood glucose levels ( 100022 ). While this has not been shown in humans, theoretically, taking Japanese apricot along with other herbs and supplements with hypoglycemic potential might increase the risk of hypoglycemia. Some of these herbs and supplements include devil's claw, fenugreek, guar gum, Panax ginseng, Siberian ginseng, and others.

#### PERIOPERATIVE

Japanese apricot has antiplatelet and hypoglycemic effects ( 13199 , 100022 ). Japanese apricot might cause excessive bleeding or affect blood glucose control if used perioperatively. Tell patients to discontinue Japanese apricot at least 2 weeks before elective surgical procedures.

None known.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the presentation or treatment of overdose with Japanese apricot.

#### Absorption

In rats, constituents of Japanese apricot fruit, including malic, protocatechuic, vanillic, quinic, citric, ferulic, syringic, cryptochlorogenic, neochlorogenic, and chlorogenic acids and amygdalin, are rapidly absorbed after oral administration, with times to peak plasma concentrations ranging from 0.15 to 0.92 hours ( 114032 ).

#### Distribution

In rats, the constituents of Japanese apricot fruit with the highest peak plasma concentrations and areas under the plasma concentration-time curves are malic and citric acids. Ferulic and vanillic acids undergo enterohepatic recirculation, giving double-peaked plasma concentration-time curves ( 114032 ).

#### Excretion

In rats, malic acid, citric acid, quinic acid, and amygdalin are excreted rapidly with half-lives of about 3, 2.75, 5, and 4 hours respectively. Constituents with longer half-lives include neochlorogenic acid (about 8 hours), syringic acid (about 11 hours), and protocatechuic, vanillic, ferulic, cryptochlorogenic, and chlorogenic acids (all about 9 hours) ( 114032 ).

#### General

The applicable parts of Japanese apricot are the seed, flower, branch, root, and fruit. The fruit contains phenols, organic acids (citric, malic, chlorogenic, protocatechuic, vanillic, caffeic, D-quinic, ferulic, syringic, cryptochlorogenic, neochlorogenic, maslinic, and corosolic acids), furfural, furfuryl alcohol, 3,4-dihydroxybenzaldehyde, benzyl beta-D-glucopyranoside, rutin, lignans including syringaresinol, sterols, terpenes, alkaloids, flavonoids, amino acids, carbohydrates, lipids, hydroxycinnamic acids, and triterpenoic acids ( 13198 , 13201 , 100021 , 100023 , 100027 , 114030 , 114032 ). The fruit is also a source of fiber ( 100016 ). The raw fruit is considered toxic because it contains the cyanogenic glycosides prunasin and amygdalin ( 13198 ). Processing by pickling in vinegar, liquor, or syrup to produce a fruit juice concentrate, eliminates the cyanogenic glycosides ( 13198 ). Japanese apricot flowers contain two polyacylated sucroses referred to as prunose I and prunose II, as well as 2-O-acetylrutin and 2-O-acetyl-3-O-methylrutin ( 13199 ). The seeds contain an aminopeptidase ( 13197 ).

#### Anti-bacterial effects

Japanese apricot has shown anti-bacterial effects against Helicobacter pylori in vitro and in animal models. The lignan syringaresinol, found in Japanese apricot, is thought to inhibit the migration of Helicobacter pylori. However, a small study in humans does not support a clinical effect for most patients ( 100023 , 100026 , 100029 ).

#### Anti-stress effects

Japanese apricot is traditionally used for menopause. In an animal menopause model, two constituents of Japanese apricot - benzyl beta-D-glucopyranoside and chlorogenic acid - inhibited bradykinin and prostaglandin E2 and stimulated the recovery of levels of epinephrine, norepinephrine, and dopamine. The chlorogenic acid constituent decreased levels of adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) following induced stress ( 13201 ).

#### Anticancer effects

Japanese apricot is traditionally used for cancer. The extract has shown anticancer effects in various cell lines in vitro. Possible mechanisms include inducing apoptosis and cell cycle arrest by altering cellular signaling and/or by affecting cellular-mediated immunity ( 100023 , 100025 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

Several constituents of Japanese apricot have been shown to increase blood fluidity in vitro, including citric and malic acids, and furfuryl alcohol, as well as mumefural and 5-hydroxymethyl-2-furfural which are produced by heating Japanese apricot ( 13198 ). An ethanol extract of Japanese apricot bark has vasorelaxant and hypotensive effects in rats. The extract produces vascular smooth muscle relaxation via the nitric oxide / soluble guanylate cyclase / cyclic guanosine monophosphate and prostaglandin-I2 pathways and potassium channel activation ( 114030 ).

#### Exercise effects

Japanese apricot is traditionally used for fatigue. In an animal model of exercise-induced fatigue, it protects against the breakdown of muscle glycogen, possibly by inducing the use of fatty acids for fuel ( 100013 ). It also reduces lactate dehydrogenase activity and increases citrate synthase activity in rat skeletal muscle, inhibits accumulation of lactate and inorganic phosphate in serum, and reduces muscle injury by decreasing malondialdehyde and increasing glutathione peroxidase activity ( 114031 ). In humans with unexplained chronic muscle fatigue, taking Japanese apricot fermented juice orally, 70 mL daily for 8 weeks, reduces fatigue severity scale scores, but there is a strong and similar effect with placebo ( 114031 ).

#### Hepatoprotective effects

Japanese apricot has shown hepatoprotective effects in human and animal studies, resulting in reduced plasma levels of liver enzymes ( 100018 , 100023 , 100027 ). The hepatoprotective effects of Japanese apricot are possibly related to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects ( 100027 ). Possible active constituents based on laboratory research are the triterpenoic acids ( 100023 , 100027 ). In an animal model, various compounds were found in the blood after giving Japanese apricot, including saikosaponin BK1, N-2-[4-(3,3-Dimethylallyloxy) phenyl] ethylcinnamide, and ganoderiol 1. It is possible that these are constituents or metabolites of the fruit that play a role in the hepatoprotective effects of Japanese apricot ( 100018 ).

#### Hypoglycemic effects

In an animal diabetic model, Japanese apricot normalizes fasting glucose levels. The mechanism of action seems to be related to the regulation of peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor (PPAR)-gamma ( 100022 ).

#### Hypolipemic effects

Japanese apricot is traditionally used to prevent cardiovascular disease. In human research, the extract reduces levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and triglycerides in healthy individuals ( 100027 ). Japanese apricot extract has also been shown to inhibit an enzyme involved in the synthesis of cholesterol in vitro ( 100027 ).

#### Hypouricemic effects

Japanese apricot is traditionally used as part of gout medications. One potential mechanism is the inhibition of xanthine oxidase, resulting in the normalization of uric acid levels, as shown in an animal model ( 100015 ).
