Caution

Some types of vibration therapy use sound waves along with music and should not be confused with Music Therapy , which is a separate technique that specifically uses music as therapy.

## Overview

Vibration therapy is the use of vibration to promote health and recovery. Some types of vibration therapy involve the application of mechanical vibration to all or part of the body. This vibration is delivered through a variety of massage tools and/or specialized equipment ( 110039 , 109320 , 109324 , 109336 , 109350 , 109353 , 109354 ). Another type of vibration therapy, sometimes referred to as vibroacoustic therapy, uses low-frequency vibrations from sound waves to cause resonance in the body ( 109796 , 109803 ).

Possibly Safe when vibration therapy using mechanical vibration is applied appropriately. This type of vibration therapy has been used without safety concerns in numerous clinical trials ( 109323 , 109346 , 109348 , 109349 , 109352 ). There is insufficient reliable information about the safety of vibration therapy using low-frequency sound waves (vibroacoustic therapy). However, there is no reason to expect safety issues from short-term use.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable evidence about the safety of vibration therapy. However, there is no reason to expect safety issues from short-term use.

Possibly Ineffective
Athletic performance.

Mechanical vibration therapy does not seem to improve most measures of athletic performance in athletes.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in elite and/or competitive athletes shows that whole-body vibration over 4-8 weeks has a small beneficial effect on maximal leg force when compared to controls consisting of active or passive activities. However, whole-body vibration does not seem to improve power or general athletic performance ( 109359 ). This meta-analysis is limited by the small number of studies, as well as the heterogeneity of populations, testing protocols, control protocols, and endpoints. A small clinical study in badminton players shows that the use of vibration foam rolling at targeted locations after dynamic stretching during warm-up is no more effective than dynamic stretching alone for improving flexibility, power, and agility when compared with baseline. However, it does seem to modestly improve muscle stiffness ( 110039 ). Whole-body vibration therapy has also been evaluated for improving performance recovery following a short-term high-intensity training bout. A small clinical trial in athletes shows that performing stretches on a vibration platform for ten minutes following a series of sprints does not improve muscle or performance recovery when compared with an active recovery involving cycling and stretching ( 109342 ).

Osteoporosis.

Most clinical research suggests that whole-body mechanical vibration therapy does not improve bone mineral density (BMD) in adults with osteoporosis.

A meta-analysis of seven preliminary clinical trials in patients with postmenopausal osteoporosis shows that whole-body vibration therapy for at least 12 weeks does not improve overall BMD when compared with receiving only conventional therapy, such as routine drug treatment ( 109333 ). However, only a small number of studies evaluated BMD at each specific location in the body; further research is needed to determine if vibration therapy is beneficial at any specific location. Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research in aging populations with or without osteoporosis have also been conducted. One meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in postmenopausal females shows that low-magnitude, but not high-magnitude, whole-body vibration therapy modestly attenuates loss of BMD in the lumbar spine over the following 6-18 months. However, it is unclear if this change is clinically meaningful. Whole-body vibration therapy does not seem to attenuate loss of BMD in the femoral neck when compared with controls receiving either no treatment, sham treatment, or other treatment ( 109335 ). Another meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in adults at least 50 years of age shows that vibration therapy does not affect BMD of the lumbar spine or hip when compared with control groups continuing daily activity or exercise or wellness therapy. However, sub-group analyses show that side-alternation vibration therapy, but not vertical vibration therapy,  modestly attenuates loss of BMD in the lumbar spine ( 109366 ). Whole-body vibration therapy has also been evaluated in children with disabilities. A small, uncontrolled clinical study in non-ambulatory children shows that whole-body vibration therapy twice weekly for 6 months modestly improves BMD ( 109327 ). However, it is unclear if the findings are clinically meaningful.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Anxiety.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for reducing anxiety, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Back pain.

It is unclear if vibration therapy is beneficial for chronic back pain; the available research is conflicting.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with non-specific chronic back pain shows that whole-body mechanical vibration therapy with exercises twice weekly for 18 weeks does not improve most measures of pain, limitation, or postural stability when compared with baseline ( 109339 ). However, this study was not designed to detect differences in pain and did not incorporate a comparator group. Additional preliminary clinical research shows that whole-body vibration therapy twice weekly for 12 weeks modestly improves most measures of pain and function when compared with not receiving vibration therapy ( 109346 ). Vibroacoustic therapy using low-frequency sound waves has also been evaluated. Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic back or shoulder pain shows that sound wave vibration therapy through the hands and feet three times weekly for 12 weeks modestly reduces pain and improves pain-related disability when compared with baseline. Approximately 65% of patients experienced a clinically significant pain reduction. There was also some improvement in physical components of quality of life ( 109796 ).

Bruises.

It is unclear if sound wave vibration therapy is beneficial for reducing postoperative bruising.

Preliminary clinical research in early postoperative total knee and hip replacement patients shows that vibroacoustic therapy using low-frequency sound waves, in addition to postoperative physiotherapy, for 4 days does not reduce bruising when compared with physiotherapy alone ( 109786 ).

Cerebral palsy.

Early research suggests that various forms of vibration therapy may improve walking speed and motor function in children and young adults with cerebral palsy.

Small clinical studies in children and young adults with cerebral palsy shows that treatment with mechanical vibration therapy increases the 6-minute walking distance by 8% to 12% when compared with baseline or sham vibration. This improvement seems to be associated with improvements in lean body mass and bone mass. Vibration therapy has been given for 9-10 minutes every 2-3 days for 8-20 weeks ( 109334 , 109361 , 109362 ). Some preliminary clinical research has also examined the effect of vibroacoustic therapy using low-frequency sound waves. However, most studies are small and uncontrolled, and some also include music listening ( 109338 , 109803 ). One preliminary clinical trial in children ages 4-6 years with cerebral palsy undergoing physiotherapy shows that sound wave vibration therapy twice weekly for 3 months modestly improves spasticity and gross motor function when compared with physiotherapy alone ( 109338 ).

Constipation.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for constipation.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic functional constipation shows that six, 15-minute sessions of low-intensity whole-body mechanical vibration therapy over two weeks modestly reduces symptom severity, but does not improve pain or quality of life, when compared with no vibration therapy ( 109347 ).

Depression.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for depression, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Diabetic neuropathy.

Early research suggests that mechanical vibration therapy modestly improves pain and function in patients with diabetic neuropathy.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with diabetic neuropathy shows that receiving ten minutes of focal vibration therapy with mechanical vibration to each of the quadriceps, gastrocnemius, and tibialis anterior muscles three times weekly for 4 weeks modestly improves gait speed and cadence when compared with baseline ( 109357 ).Another small clinical study shows that whole-body vibration therapy three times weekly for 4 weeks is beneficial for reducing pain associated with diabetic neuropathy when compared with sham vibration therapy. The time before return of pain was at least 3 weeks ( 109363 ). Also, when compared with lower-leg therapy with no vibration treatment in elderly patients with diabetic neuropathy, whole-body vibration therapy three times weekly for 6 weeks seems to modestly improve vibration threshold, but not warm or cold pain thresholds ( 109364 ).

Down syndrome.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for improving physical function in patients with Down syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research in adolescents with Down syndrome shows that mechanical vibration therapy four times weekly for 20 weeks increases 6-minute walking distance by 9% and modestly improves jumping ability and power when compared with baseline. These benefits were associated with improvements in bone density and lean body mass ( 109340 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Essential tremor.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for essential tremor.

A small preliminary clinical trial in elderly adults with essential tremor shows that using a hand-held mechanical vibration therapy device for 5 minutes modestly improves tremor in about 50% of patients when compared with baseline ( 109349 ). The validity of this study is limited by its small size and lack of a comparator group.

Exercise-induced muscle damage.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of exercise-induced muscle damage.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies shows that mechanical vibration therapy modestly induces muscle recovery in the first 24 hours after eccentric exercise when compared with either no intervention or stretching ( 109365 ). However, some other preliminary clinical research shows that vibration therapy twice daily after eccentric exercise is no more effective than stretching and sports massage for inducing recovery from muscle damage ( 109337 ). Mechanical vibration therapy has also been evaluated for the prevention of exercise-induced muscle damage. Preliminary clinical research in female non-athletes shows that vibration therapy for 5 minutes just before eccentric exercise results in a faster improvement in markers of muscle damage for up to 48 hours after exercise when compared with no treatment ( 109330 ).

Exercise-induced muscle soreness.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that mechanical vibration therapy seems to reduce muscle soreness after exercise. However, it is unclear if it is more beneficial than other treatments.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies shows that mechanical vibration therapy modestly reduces exercise-induced muscle soreness and improves tolerance for up to 72 hours after eccentric exercise, but does not affect joint mobility, when compared with no intervention or stretching ( 109365 ). Limitations of the meta-analysis include the generally low quality and small size of the available studies. One small clinical study shows that vibration therapy is more beneficial than stretching alone for muscle pain and tightness after exercise ( 109341 ). However, some preliminary clinical research shows that vibration therapy twice daily after eccentric exercise is no more effective than stretching and sports massage for reducing muscle soreness ( 109337 ). Vibration therapy has also been investigated for the prevention of exercise-induced muscle soreness. Preliminary clinical research in female non-athletes shows that vibration therapy for 5 minutes just before eccentric exercise results in less muscle soreness and improved range of motion for up to 72 hours after exercise when compared with no treatment. Although it was no more effective than 15 minutes of massage therapy, the benefits of vibration therapy seemed to start earlier (24 vs. 48 hours) ( 109330 ).

Fall prevention.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for fall prevention.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in older adults shows that mechanical vibration therapy reduced the rate of falls by 33% and the risk of experiencing falls by 24% when compared with either continued daily activity or exercise or wellness therapy ( 109366 ).

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for fibromyalgia.

Two small clinical trials in patients with fibromyalgia show that whole-body mechanical vibration therapy for 3 months is modestly more effective than no treatment for improving pain and function, but not quality of life ( 109329 , 109356 ). However, benefits are not maintained for an additional 3 months ( 109329 ).

Fractures.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for fracture prevention, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

HIV/AIDS-related peripheral neuropathy.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for HIV/AIDS-related neuropathy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for improving function in adults with MS.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with MS shows that mechanical vibration therapy modestly improves balance and walking endurance, but does not affect function, gait speed, fatigue, or quality of life, when compared with either no treatment or regular exercise. Benefits did not seem to depend on the length of treatment, although symptom severity at baseline may alter outcomes ( 109350 ).

Neck pain.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for chronic neck pain.

A small clinical trial in patients with chronic non-specific neck pain shows that ten sessions of self-administered mechanical vibration therapy into myofascial trigger points modestly reduces pain and improves disability when compared with no intervention ( 109325 ).

Obesity.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for weight loss.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical studies show that whole-body mechanical vibration therapy modestly reduces fat mass when compared with control, although any effect may not be considered clinically significant ( 110040 , 109353 , 109367 ). The meta-analyses included studies focusing on overweight or obese ( 110040 , 109367 ), non-overweight ( 109353 ), and otherwise healthy adults. One meta-analysis also shows that whole-body vibration therapy modestly increases lower body strength and reduces systolic and diastolic blood pressure by 7 mmHg and 2 mmHg, respectively. However, there was no effect on body weight, body mass index, or metabolic indices ( 109367 ).

Osteoarthritis.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for osteoarthritis.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical studies in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that whole-body mechanical vibration therapy, administered at a low- or high-frequency, in conjunction with strengthening exercises over 4-24 weeks has moderate to large benefits on pain and most measures of function and muscle strength when compared with exercises alone. However, there was no effect on stiffness, balance, quality of life, and other measures of function and muscle strength ( 109331 ).

Pain (acute).

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for acute, injection-related pain in children.

A large preliminary clinical trial in children aged 2 months to 3 years shows that mechanical vibration therapy using a specific device (Buzzy vibrating device) without cold analgesia does not reduce pain associated with an immunization when compared with standard care. In children aged 4-7 years, pain scores were increased. The vibrating device was placed just above the injection site, starting ten seconds before and continuing during the injection ( 109319 ).

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if vibration therapy with sound waves is beneficial for chronic pain.

Some preliminary clinical research has examined the effect of vibroacoustic therapy using low-frequency sound waves, alone or in combination with music, on chronic pain in various populations. Although most research shows some evidence of benefit on pain levels with vibroacoustic therapy, most studies are small and uncontrolled, and statistical comparisons are usually lacking ( 109799 , 109804 ).

Parkinson disease.

It is unclear if various forms of vibration therapy are beneficial for Parkinson disease.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical studies in patients with Parkinson disease shows that whole-body vibration therapy does not seem to have clinically meaningful benefits when compared to placebo, no treatment, or other control treatments. There was no effect on gait and balance; any benefits on motor symptoms and mobility were small and endpoints were heterogeneous ( 109354 ). However, one small preliminary clinical trial suggests that giving whole-body vibration three times weekly for a total of 20 sessions in combination with stretching and strengthening modestly improves balance when compared with conventional therapy alone. However, when used alone, it is no more effective than stretching and strengthening ( 109326 ). A small clinical trial in patients with Parkinson disease shows that vibroacoustic therapy using low-frequency sound waves for 12 weeks modestly improves symptoms, including tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, posture, and gait, when compared to a placebo therapy ( 109801 ). Some preliminary clinical research also shows that these benefits may occur after a single treatment ( 109802 ).

Plantar heel pain.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for plantar heel pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Postoperative pain.

It is unclear if sound wave vibration therapy is beneficial for reducing pain after joint replacement.

Preliminary clinical research in early postoperative total knee replacement patients shows that vibroacoustic therapy using low-frequency sound waves, in addition to postoperative physiotherapy, for 4 days modestly reduces pain when compared with physiotherapy alone. However, pain was not significantly reduced in patients undergoing total hip replacement ( 109786 ).

Postoperative recovery.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for recovering muscle strength and function after anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) surgery.

One meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that whole-body mechanical vibration therapy in combination with conventional rehabilitation after ACL surgery does not increase lower limb strength or anterior posterior stability when compared with conventional rehabilitation alone. However, there were some benefits on overall and medial-lateral stability ( 109715 ). Another meta-analysis of mainly small and preliminary clinical studies shows that mechanical vibration therapy in combination with conventional rehabilitation after ACL surgery modestly increases hamstring muscle torque, but not postural control, when compared with conventional rehabilitation alone. Sub-analyses suggest that the most benefit occurs with local muscle vibration ( 109321 ).

Postoperative swelling.

It is unclear if sound wave vibration therapy is beneficial for reducing postoperative swelling after joint replacement.

Preliminary clinical research in early postoperative total knee and hip replacement patients shows that vibroacoustic therapy using low-frequency sound waves, in addition to postoperative physiotherapy, for 4 days does not reduce swelling when compared with physiotherapy alone ( 109786 ).

Pressure ulcers.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for pressure ulcers.

Preliminary clinical research in older adults in long-term care with mild pressure ulcers shows that applying vibration therapy (RelaWave; Matsuda Micronics Corp) around the mattress level for 15 minutes three times daily for up to 7 days results in healing of 40% of pressure ulcers, compared with 10% of those treated with standard care alone ( 109348 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for RA, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Rhinosinusitis.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for rhinosinusitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Sarcopenia.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for sarcopenia.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in older adults with sarcopenia shows that whole-body mechanical vibration therapy moderately improves muscle strength and physical performance, but not muscle mass, when compared with no intervention ( 110041 ). However, each of these endpoints was only evaluated in two small studies.

Shoulder pain.

It is unclear if sound wave vibration therapy is beneficial for chronic shoulder pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic shoulder or back pain shows that vibroacoustic therapy using low-frequency sound waves through the hands and feet three times weekly for 12 weeks modestly reduces pain and improves pain-related disability when compared with baseline. Approximately 65% of patients experienced a clinically significant pain reduction. There was also some improvement in physical components of quality of life ( 109796 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Spinal cord injury.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for spinal cord injury, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Sprains.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for sprains, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Stress.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for reducing stress, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Stroke.

It is unclear if whole-body or local mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for improving balance and mobility following a stroke.

One small clinical trial in patients who experienced a stroke at least 6 months prior shows that a single treatment with local plantar vibration therapy on the affected foot has a large effect on balance when compared with placebo treatment. There was also a modest beneficial effect on ankle spasticity ( 109351 ). Another trial shows that local plantar vibration therapy, conducted for 15 minutes three times weekly before physical therapy for 4 weeks, is more effective than conventional physical therapy alone for improving balance ( 109343 ). Conversely, a small clinical study shows that whole-body vibration therapy twice daily for 5 days a week for 2 weeks is no more effective than conventional physical therapy for improving sitting balance in patients who are within 2 months of a stroke and unable to sit independently for longer than 30 seconds ( 109336 ). Although this research suggests that vibration therapy may be beneficial for improving balance after a stroke, any conclusions are limited due to the size of the available studies and the heterogeneity of treatments and endpoints. Whole-body vibration has also been evaluated for improving other functional outcomes post-stroke. Although some preliminary clinical research shows that whole-body vibration therapy over 2-8 weeks in combination with stroke rehabilitation modestly improves stair negotiation, walking speed, and falls, most research shows that adding vibration therapy to other treatment is no more effective for improving functional outcomes, including obstacle crossing, walking speed, muscle strength, motor function, mobility, or falls ( 109322 , 109323 , 109343 , 109352 , 109355 , 109358 ).

Tendinopathy.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for tennis elbow.

In patients with tennis elbow, a small preliminary clinical trial shows that mechanical vibration therapy with a portable mechanical device (Tenease) three times daily for 6 weeks in addition to standard treatment does not improve functional outcomes when compared with standard treatment alone ( 110038 ). The validity of this study is limited by its high withdrawal rate.

Tinnitus.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for tinnitus, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Traumatic brain injury (TBI).

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for TBI, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Urinary incontinence.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for urinary incontinence, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Venous leg ulcers.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for venous leg ulcers, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Vocal cord dysfunction.

It is unclear if mechanical vibration therapy is beneficial for vocal cord dysfunction.

Preliminary clinical research in singers shows that mechanical vibration therapy around the throat modestly improves objective acoustic measures, but not perceived feelings of effort, when compared with placebo ( 109345 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that, following karaoke singing, both whole-body vibration and local perilaryngeal vibration for ten minutes modestly improve recovery from vocal fatigue when compared with self-generated body vibration. However, it is unclear whether vibration therapy is more effective than a sham hand-held device ( 109320 , 109324 ).

Wound healing.

Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for wound healing, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS) . Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for reducing pain associated with EDS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . Although there is interest in using vibration therapy for TMD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of vibration therapy for this purpose.

More evidence is needed to rate vibration therapy for these uses.

Mechanical vibration therapy : Some vibration therapy involves mechanical oscillations to the whole body or specific sites. The therapy can be used in single sessions, or for one or more sessions daily or weekly ( 110038 , 110041 , 109321 , 109322 , 109327 , 109355 , 109359 , 109715 ). Time of exposure for a single session generally ranges from 20 seconds to 20 minutes ( 109320 , 109327 , 109331 , 109345 , 109349 , 109359 , 109366 ). In clinical research, vibration therapy has been used for up to 24 months ( 110038 , 110040 , 110041 , 109321 , 109323 , 109327 , 109331 , 109333 , 109334 , 109346 )( 109350 , 109356 , 109361 , 109364 , 109366 , 109715 ).  Most research has investigated the use of whole-body vibration therapy ( 109320 , 109321 , 109323 , 109352 , 109359 ). However, local vibration or focal muscle vibration therapy has also been used. In research, local vibration therapy has been applied to the head and neck, hands, feet, legs, and elbows ( 110038 , 110039 , 109320 , 109325 , 109337 , 109343 , 109345 , 109349 , 109351 , 109357 ). Whole-body vibration therapy generally involves standing, squatting, sitting, or doing exercises or specific stances on a platform, sometimes with side-alternating, tilting, rotating, or multi-directional vibrations. Many specific devices have been used in clinical research, including TurboSonic Ovation, Sonix SW-VM10, Sonic World, Crazy Fit Massage, Physio Wave 700, Lion FitnessVR Brand-Smart Trainer, FitvibeVR Excel Pro, BioplateRF, Galileo Basic vibration plate, and PowerPlate ( 110041 , 109320 , 109322 , 109329 , 109331 , 109334 , 109336 , 109339 , 109340 , 109342 )( 109346 , 109347 , 109355 , 109356 , 109361 , 109362 , 109363 , 109364 , 109366 , 109367 ). Local vibration has usually been self-applied, but may also be applied by a practitioner/examiner. Specific devices evaluated in clinical research include Novafon GmbH, Siri, Tenease, Vibrasens, Shindo, Vilim Ball, Myovolt, and Vyper ( 110038 , 110039 , 109320 , 109325 , 109343 , 109345 , 109349 , 109351 , 109357 ). The rate and intensity of vibrations applied in clinical research varies. Vibration frequencies range between low (5 Hz to 50 Hz) and high (up to about 300 Hz) ( 110040 , 110041 , 109321 , 109322 , 109323 , 109325 , 109327 , 109329 , 109331 , 109333 )( 109334 , 109336 , 109339 , 109343 , 109345 , 109348 , 109349 , 109350 , 109351 , 109355 )( 109356 , 109357 , 109359 , 109361 , 109362 , 109363 , 109365 , 109366 , 109367 , 109715 ). Amplitude (0.4 to 12.0 mm) and acceleration (10 to 386 m/s 2 ) also vary ( 110040 , 109320 , 109329 , 109331 , 109333 , 109334 , 109349 , 109350 , 109352 , 109355 )( 109357 , 109359 , 109361 , 109362 , 109363 , 109366 , 109367 ). In clinical research, vibration therapy has occasionally been used in combination with conventional modes of rehabilitation (i.e., physical therapy, pain relief) or exercise (i.e., squats) ( 109322 , 109336 , 109339 , 109343 , 109355 ). Sound wave vibration therapy : Vibration therapy using sound waves is commonly known as vibroacoustic or physioacoustic therapy ( 109802 ). Vibrations are provided through speakers embedded in equipment such as a chair, table, or mattress, usually for whole-body vibrations. However, some equipment provides local stimulation ( 109338 , 109802 , 109803 , 109804 ). Treatments may occur as single or multiple treatments daily, over several consecutive days, or as one or more sessions a week for up to 12 weeks ( 109786 , 109796 , 109801 , 109802 , 109804 ). Sound wave frequencies are usually between 30 Hz and 80 Hz, and most often about 40 Hz ( 109338 , 109801 , 109804 ). Vibroacoustic-type vibration therapy is sometimes used in combination with physiotherapy and/or music therapy. Some devices provide both vibroacoustic stimulation and music therapy ( 109338 , 109799 , 109803 , 109804 ).

#### General

The mechanism of action behind vibration therapy is unclear; however, several theories have been put forth and vary depending on the intended effect. Vibration therapy is of interest for increasing bone and muscle mass, improving mobility and balance, and reducing pain, among others.

#### Bone effects

Vibration therapy is of interest for the prevention or treatment of osteoporosis. Although some research suggests that vibration therapy might improve bone density ( 109340 ), most research disagrees or indicates that any changes may not be clinically meaningful ( 109327 , 109333 , 109335 , 109366 ). Clinical research in postmenopausal females shows that whole-body vibration therapy reduces the shift from osteoblast cell formation to fat cell formation in the bone marrow, possibly maintaining bone strength and bone density ( 109328 ). However, a meta-analysis of clinical research shows that whole-body vibration therapy does not affect markers of bone formation or absorption ( 109333 ).

#### Ergogenic effects

Whole-body vibration is of interest for exercise recovery. Clinical research shows that whole-body vibration increases blood flow and tissue oxygenation, which may be of benefit before exercise or during recovery to prevent pain and stiffness ( 109342 , 109345 , 109348 ). Use of a vibration foam roller is thought to mobilize soft tissue to increase muscle length and blood flow to soft tissue ( 110039 ).

#### Musculoskeletal effects

Vibration therapy is of interest for improving balance and preventing falls. Some researchers have found that whole body vibration stimulates the spinal motor neurons, possibly improving proprioception, balance, and mobility ( 109336 , 109352 ). However, clinical research shows that whole-body vibration does not increase the mass of muscles and tendons around the tibia, a site clinically relevant for fall prevention ( 109332 ). Vibration therapy is also of interest for improving muscle mass and strength in various populations, as well as for reducing muscle pain. Vibration is thought to stimulate receptors in the skin and increase blood circulation ( 109786 , 109799 , 109803 ). This is thought to induce a stretch and contraction of the muscle cell, possibly resulting in increased muscle mass and reduced pain ( 109346 , 109799 , 109803 ). Some research has found that vibration of the muscle leads to increased alpha-motor neurons, increased activation of muscle spindles, and enhanced muscle contraction, possibly increasing muscle strength ( 109331 , 109350 , 109352 ). Although not all research agrees, most research shows that vibration therapy increases lean body mass and muscle strength ( 110041 , 109331 , 109332 , 109334 , 109340 , 109344 , 109358 , 109361 , 109362 , 109367 ).  This stimulation of muscle contraction at specific locations such as the quadriceps might have beneficial effects for osteoarthritis ( 109331 ).

#### Neurological effect

Vibration therapy using sound waves most often uses vibration frequencies of 40 Hz. It has been hypothesized that this frequency matches the brain frequency, possibility benefiting neurological function ( 109804 ).
