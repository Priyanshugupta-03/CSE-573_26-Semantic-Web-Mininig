## Overview

Bayberry is a type of bush that grows in the southeastern areas of the United States The root and fruit are used in medicine ( 4 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, bayberry is used for colds, mucous colitis, diarrhea, fever, and as a circulatory stimulant. It is also used as an emetic in large doses. Topically, bayberry is used as a gargle for sore throat, as a douche for leukorrhea (vaginal discharge), for indolent ulcers, and wound healing.

Possibly Unsafe when used orally. The root, bark, and berries contain high amounts of tannins ( 6 ). Large doses may have mineralocorticoid activity ( 4 ). The root bark can also contain a carcinogen ( 5 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of bayberry for its other uses.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Unsafe when used orally or topically because of possible carcinogenic, or mineralocorticoid activities; avoid using ( 4 , 5 ).

#### General

Orally, bayberry can cause gastrointestinal irritation, vomiting, and liver damage, possibly due to its tannin content. It can also act as an irritant and sensitizer ( 6 ).

#### Dermatologic

#### Hepatic

#### Adult
Oral:

General : Traditionally, bayberry has been consumed as a tea, prepared by steeping 0.6-2 grams of powdered bark in boiling water and straining, three times a day ( 4 ). The liquid extract of bayberry, prepared 1:1 in 45% alcohol, is typically used 0.6-2 mL three times a day ( 4 ).

#### HYPERTENSION

Consuming large amounts of tannin-containing herbs such as bayberry can cause sodium and water retention and possibly increase blood pressure ( 4 ).

#### General

The applicable parts of bayberry are the root bark and berry. Bayberry contains tannins ( 4 ). It also contains monoterpenes, triterpenes, and flavonoids, including myricadiol, myricitrin, taraxerol, and taraxerone ( 33875 , 33884 , 33912 , 33919 , 33927 , 33932 )

#### Astringent effects

The tannin constituents of bayberry are responsible for its astringent activity ( 4 ).
