## Overview

Buckhorn plantain is a perennial, herbaceous plant that grows to 10-50 cm in height and is distributed widely throughout the world ( 98010 ). Avoid confusion with common plantain (Plantago major). CAUTION: Digitalis leaves resemble plantain leaves; adulteration of plantain with digitalis has been reported ( 3905 ). Be careful not to confuse buckhorn plantain with digitalis, which is an unsafe product ( 2 , 12 ).

## History

Buckhorn plantain has been used in traditional medicine since Greek and Roman times ( 98010 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, buckhorn plantain is used for the common cold, cough, bronchitis, fevers, interstitial cystitis, nocturnal enuresis, liver disease, stomach cramps, diarrhea, and as a diuretic. Topically, buckhorn plantain is used for pharyngeal mucous membrane inflammation, inflammation of the skin, wound healing, conjunctivitis, and to arrest hemorrhages.

#### General

Orally, topically, or by inhalation, buckhorn plantain can cause allergic reactions ( 3901 , 98010 ).

#### Dermatologic

There are case reports of phototoxic reactions on sun-exposed skin after oral ingestion of buckhorn plantain, or skin contact with the plant. An erythematous, burning rash develops, with vesicles and bullae, followed by necrotic crusting ( 98010 ).

#### Immunologic

Buckhorn plantain can cause allergic reactions after oral ingestion, skin contact, or inhalation of pollen ( 3901 , 98010 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of buckhorn plantain.

#### General

The applicable parts of buckhorn plantain are the above ground parts. The primary active components are mucilage constituents ( 7 ). Other constituents of the plant include flavonoids, coumarins, lipids, cinnamic acids, and phenolic compounds. Individual constituents identified include quercetin and its glycosides, apigenin, luteolin, kaempferol, rhamnetin, aucubin, and various acids (vanillic, gallic, cinnamic, caffeic, and chlorogenic) ( 98009 , 98010 ).

#### Anti-irritant effects

Mucilage constituents of buckhorn plantain are believed to be responsible for reducing local irritation and protecting mucous membranes from irritants ( 7 ).

#### Antibacterial effects

A solution prepared by steeping buckhorn plantain leaves and flowers in water has antibacterial effects in vitro. It has activity against Staphylococcus aureus, Staphylococcus epidermidis, Serracia marscesens, and Proteus vulgaris ( 98009 ). When the solution is used as a mouthwash three times daily for 7 days, levels of Streptococcus mutans in the saliva are reduced, but there is no effect on levels of Lactobacillus casei ( 98009 ).
