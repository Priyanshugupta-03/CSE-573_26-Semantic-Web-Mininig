## Overview

Hypnotherapy is the use of hypnosis for therapeutic purposes ( 106400 ). Hypnosis is a state of consciousness with a reduced peripheral awareness and an increased capacity for response to suggestion or instruction ( 106382 , 106400 , 106946 , 106985 ). Hypnotherapy may involve self-hypnosis using an audio-recording and/or sessions with a hypnotherapist or an individual trained in hypnotherapy ( 106946 , 106981 ).

Possibly Safe when hypnotherapy is used appropriately. There are no known reports of serious adverse events; however, adverse event information is reported rarely in clinical research ( 106393 , 106973 ). Also, the use of hypnotherapy should not delay the time to diagnosis or treatment with more proven therapies.

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when hypnotherapy is used appropriately. Serious adverse events have not been reported ( 100746 , 106377 ); however, adverse event information is reported rarely in clinical research ( 106973 ). Also, the use of hypnotherapy should not delay the time to diagnosis or treatment with more proven therapies.

PREGNANCY: Possibly Safe when hypnotherapy is used appropriately. Use of self-hypnosis during labor following teaching sessions during pregnancy has not been shown to affect birthweight, well-being of the newborn, admission or re-admission for neonatal care, maternal stay in the hospital, readmission to hospital, lactation establishment, or ability to care for the infant ( 106387 , 106947 ). However, the use of hypnotherapy should not delay the time to diagnosis or treatment with more proven therapies.

LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available. However, there is no reason to expect safety issues with hypnotherapy when administered correctly. Also, use of hypnotherapy should not delay the time to diagnosis or treatment with more proven therapies.

#### General

Hypnotherapy seems to be well tolerated; however, a thorough evaluation of adverse effects in clinical research is lacking.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Anxiety, dizziness, drowsiness, headache, insomnia, and nausea.

#### Gastrointestinal

Nausea has been reported in clinical research ( 106960 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Anxiety, dizziness, drowsiness, headaches, insomnia, and nervousness have been reported in clinical research ( 106960 ).

Possibly Effective
Abdominal pain.

Hypnotherapy seems to be beneficial for reducing abdominal pain in children, although it is unclear if specific types of hypnotherapy are preferable.

Due to the difficulties of separating functional abdominal pain from irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) symptoms in children, both conditions are included in available clinical research. In children ages 8-18 years with long-lasting functional abdominal pain or IBS, six sessions of gut-directed hypnotherapy with a therapist over a 3-month period reduces abdominal pain intensity and frequency over baseline. One year after the end of therapy, pain intensity and frequency were both improved by at least 80% in 85% of patients receiving hypnotherapy, compared with 25% of those given standard medical therapy with six sessions of supportive therapy ( 106391 ). After approximately 5 years, 68% of children given gut-directed hypnotherapy were still in remission ( 106977 ). A home-based hypnotherapy program may be just as effective in a similar group of children. Clinical research shows that home-based self-hypnotherapy exercises using a CD at least five times weekly for three months was as effective as six sessions of individual gut-directed hypnotherapy with a therapist for reducing abdominal pain intensity and frequency. Treatment success, defined as having at least a 50% reduction in pain frequency and intensity, occurred in 68% of those using the home-based program and 71% of those using the therapist-based program at 6-year follow-up ( 106392 , 110651 ). It is unclear how gut-directed hypnotherapy compares with other types of hypnotherapy. Research by another group shows that home-based gut-directed hypnotherapy for 3 months may be slightly less effective than home-based unspecific hypnotherapy in children ages 6-17 years ( 106956 ). However, there was a large loss at follow-up in this study and statistical differences were lacking for most endpoints.

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

Hypnotherapy seems to be beneficial for reducing symptoms of IBS in some adults and children.

Preliminary clinical research conducted in adults prior to 2006 shows that gut-focused hypnotherapy has a short-term beneficial effect on overall symptoms of IBS. However, the long-term effects could not be determined. Also, due to heterogeneity with respect to outcome measures and comparison groups (e.g., psychotherapy, wait-list, or usual treatments) no meta-analysis of these studies was possible ( 106376 ). A meta-analysis of more recent preliminary clinical research shows that 5-12 sessions of gut-directed hypnotherapy with or without home practice modestly reduces abdominal pain for up to 3 months when compared with conventional or no treatment; however, benefits do not seem to be maintained at 12 months. Also, in the two studies that evaluated constipation and diarrhea as outcomes, no benefits were identified ( 106398 ). Some clinical studies show that 4-12 individual or group sessions of gut-directed hypnotherapy over 4-12 weeks with or without home practice reduces overall or individual IBS symptom severity for up to one year when compared with supportive therapy, standard treatments, or baseline ( 106388 , 106393 , 106397 , 106976 , 110652 , 110653 ). When comparing approaches, a moderate-size clinical study suggests 8 sessions of either individual or group gut-focused hypnotherapy over 12 weeks has similar effects on IBS symptoms at 6-month follow-up ( 110653 ). In one study in patients with refractory IBS using standard treatments, the number of patients with mild or moderate IBS increased from 24% at baseline to 63% one year after treatment, compared with 25% to 36% in patients given standard treatments alone ( 106976 ). In a retrospective study, approximately 73% of patients that were moderately or very much better immediately after undergoing gut-directed hypnotherapy for 12 weeks experienced further improvement over the next 2-7 years ( 106964 ). In addition, six weekly sessions of hypnotherapy seem to be as effective as a low fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides and polyols (FODMAP) diet for improving overall symptoms and quality of life in patients with IBS ( 106994 ). Recent research has evaluated the effectiveness of digitally delivered gut-directed hypnotherapy. A large clinical study in adults with IBS shows that participating in a self-administered, digital gut-directed hypnotherapy program (Regulora) for 30 minutes once weekly for 7 weeks improves abdominal pain, stool consistency, and frequency similarly to digital muscle relaxation in the 4 week post-treatment period ( 116264 ). Another large clinical study in adults with IBS shows that a digitally delivered gut-directed hypnotherapy program (Nerva) used for 15 minutes daily for 42 sessions for 6 weeks in addition to psychoeducation, cognitive behavioral therapy, and relaxation reduces the severity of IBS symptoms and pain more than a program consisting of psychoeducation, cognitive behavioral therapy, and relaxation without hypnotherapy. Benefits appeared to be maintained for at least 6 months after program completion ( 116263 ). However, not all research agrees that hypnotherapy reduces symptoms or medication use when compared to control groups ( 106393 , 106397 , 106964 ). A large trial shows that individual or group gut-directed hypnotherapy over 12 weeks increases the odds of having adequate relief by about 3-fold when compared with supportive therapy; however, there were no differences in severity of overall symptoms or work absence ( 106962 ). Also, some research shows that gut-directed hypnosis is no more effective than holistic hypnosis or simple relaxation therapy ( 106397 ). Furthermore, evidence related to improvements in quality of life or disease impact is mixed ( 106393 , 106397 , 106976 ). Hypnotherapy has also been evaluated in children with IBS; however, due to the difficulties of separating functional abdominal pain from IBS in children, both conditions are included in available clinical research. In children ages 8-18 years with long-lasting functional abdominal pain or IBS, six sessions of gut-directed hypnotherapy with a therapist over a 3-month period reduces abdominal pain intensity and frequency over baseline. One year after the end of therapy, pain intensity and frequency were both improved by at least 80% in 85% of patients receiving hypnotherapy, compared with 25% of those given standard medical therapy with six sessions of supportive therapy ( 106391 ). After approximately 5 years, 68% of children given gut-directed hypnotherapy were still in remission ( 106977 ). A home-based hypnotherapy program may be just as effective in a similar group of children. Clinical research shows that home-based self-hypnotherapy exercises using a CD at least five times weekly for three months was as effective as six sessions of individual gut-directed hypnotherapy with a therapist for reducing abdominal pain intensity and frequency. Treatment success, defined as having at least a 50% reduction in pain frequency and intensity, occurred in 68% of those using the home-based program and 71% of those using the therapist-based program at 6-year follow-up ( 106392 , 110651 ). Research by another group shows that home-based gut-directed hypnotherapy for 3 months may be slightly less effective than home-based unspecific hypnotherapy in children ages 6-17 years ( 106956 ). However, there was a large loss at follow-up in this study and statistical differences were lacking for most endpoints.

Pain (acute).

Preliminary clinical research suggests that hypnotherapy may be beneficial for various types of acute pain in adults and children.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that hypnotherapy of various durations moderately improves pain intensity or unpleasantness when compared to usual care and/or other types of stress-reducing activities. There was also some evidence of a decreased need for opioids. However, studies included in the analyses enrolled patients with various types of acute pain, including burn pain, medical procedural pain, postoperative pain, and unspecified types of pain ( 106989 ). One study included in the analysis specific to patients with acute pain in a hospital setting shows that hypnotic suggestion modestly reduces pain when compared with psychoeducation ( 106990 ). When compared with baseline or various types of control treatments, other preliminary clinical studies show that hypnotherapy can reduce acute pain and anxiety associated with procedures such as venipuncture, bone marrow aspirations, lumbar punctures, or dental procedures in children and adults ( 106949 , 106969 , 106991 ). The American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) moderately recommends offering hypnotherapy to adults with cancer and pain during procedures for treatment or diagnostic workup ( 109161 ).

Pain (chronic).

Preliminary clinical research suggests that hypnotherapy can improve chronic pain of various types in adults. The benefits of hypnotherapy for chronic pain in children are unclear.

In older hospitalized patients with musculoskeletal or neuropathic chronic pain lasting at least three months, preliminary clinical research shows that hypnotherapy including three weekly sessions plus self-hypnosis modestly reduces pain intensity when compared with massage. There were also improvements in feelings of depression, but not anxiety. Not all measures of pain relief were significant between groups and any pain relief was no longer significant three months after discharge ( 106946 ). In adults with chronic chest pain not related to cardiac or esophageal conditions, preliminary clinical research shows that twelve sessions of hypnotherapy over 17 weeks, in conjunction with home practice, results in 80% of patients reporting a complete or moderate improvement, compared with 23% of those given supportive therapy and a placebo tablet. There were also improvements in feelings of overall well-being and pain severity, but no change in pain frequency or levels of anxiety or depression ( 106385 ). Hypnotherapy has also been shown to reduce other types of chronic pain in adults, such as pain associated with hemophilia ( 106997 ). Observational research in children with chronic pain has found that following use of self-hypnosis as part of a therapeutic education program is associated with approximately 61% of patients experiencing a clinically relevant reduction in pain intensity and 38% of patients achieving their therapeutic goals related to pain, quality of sleep, schooling, and functional activity. An additional 35% had partial achievement of goals ( 106951 ).

Postoperative pain.

Clinical research suggests that hypnotherapy may be beneficial for postoperative pain; however, most research is in patients undergoing breast surgery. Evidence in children with postoperative pain is limited.

A meta-analysis of 7 moderate-quality clinical studies that includes 942 adults with breast cancer shows that hypnotherapy prior to minor surgeries (i.e. lumpectomy, breast biopsy) reduces postoperative pain when compared with standard care ( 110662 ). However, the validity of the findings from this meta-analysis is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which used various hypnosis methods and durations. A small clinical study in Mexican adults undergoing mastectomy due to breast cancer shows that hypnotherapy, provided as an audio recording before and after surgery, reduces pain scores and interference with daily activities when compared with standard care ( 110661 ). A small observational study in pediatric patients aged 8 years on average undergoing outpatient dermatologic surgery has found that hypnotherapy is associated with reduced pain scores and doses of sedative and analgesic medications when compared with attention distraction techniques (i.e., cartoons, music videos) ( 110660 ). The American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) moderately recommends offering hypnotherapy to adults with cancer and pain during procedures for treatment or diagnostic workup ( 109161 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Parturition.

Practicing self-hypnosis does not seem to reduce complications during labor.

A large clinical study shows that three lessons in self-hypnosis with self-practice, starting after 30 weeks' gestation, does not affect the duration of labor or lactation success, reduce birth complications, or alter the need for most interventions when compared with relaxation training or usual antenatal care ( 106387 ). Further research is needed to determine if therapist-led hypnotherapy may be beneficial.

Allergic rhinitis (hay fever).

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for reducing symptoms of hay fever.

Clinical research in adults with moderate to severe hay fever and mild allergic asthma related to grass or birch pollen shows that self-hypnosis over 2-5 sessions, while continuing the use of standard anti-allergic treatments, does not affect nasal flow following provocation when compared with baseline. Also, there was no improvement in diary reports of pollinosis symptoms and use of eye drops when compared to waitlisted patients. However, use of a visual analog scale to determine pollinosis symptoms shows a modest benefit of self-hypnosis over the wait-list group ( 106379 ).

Alopecia areata.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for well-being or hair loss associated with alopecia areata.

In patients with extensive alopecia areata refractory to conventional steroid treatment, preliminary clinical research shows that 3-8 sessions of hypnotherapy plus self-hypnosis as a sole or additional treatment modestly reduces feelings of anxiety and depression when compared with baseline. Also, scalp hair grew in some patients. However, relapse over a period of up to 6 years occurred in almost 50% of patients ( 106399 ).

Anxiety.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for anxiety; the available research is conflicting.

Although some clinical studies show that hypnotherapy before and during radiation treatment does not reduce anxiety levels when compared with not receiving hypnotherapy ( 106378 ), a meta-analysis of clinical research shows that hypnosis has a moderate to large effect on anxiety, both immediate and sustained, in patients with cancer. However, sub-analyses found that evidence of benefit only occurred in children or in studies where the hypnotherapy was delivered by a therapist ( 106968 ). A meta-analysis of research using hypnotherapy for anxiety related to procedures such as dental work, surgery, or examinations shows modest beneficial effects on anxiety when compared with controls such as usual care or wait list. However, the most benefit occurred when hypnotherapy was used along with other psychological interventions ( 106993 ). Conversely, a meta-analysis of 5 studies in adults suggests that hypnotherapy is not beneficial for reducing anxiety prior to dental procedures when compared with control or other therapeutic interventions ( 110654 ). However, the validity of the findings from this meta-analysis is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which used various study designs and types of hypnotherapy.

Asthma.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for asthma symptoms.

Most preliminary clinical research shows that hypnotherapy or hypnosis may be beneficial for reducing symptoms of asthma ( 106978 ).  In one small study, hypnotherapy over 6 weeks shows overall symptom reduction of 41% from baseline, with modestly improved expiratory flow rates and decreased bronchodilator use ( 106978 ). However, studies are small and changes are not seen in all lung function tests ( 106978 ).

Back pain.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for chronic low back pain.

In patients with chronic low back pain, preliminary clinical research shows that therapist guided sessions of self-hypnosis reduces pain intensity when compared with baseline; 52% of patients had at least a 30% reduction in pain. In addition, interference from pain was reduced and sleep quality was modestly improved. Receiving eight guided sessions alone, or two guided sessions in conjunction with six telephone calls, seemed to improve back pain modestly more than biofeedback ( 106350 ).

Breast cancer-related hot flashes.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for reducing hot flashes in patients with breast cancer.

In patients with breast cancer, preliminary clinical research shows that five weekly sessions of hypnotherapy with self-practice modestly reduces frequency and severity of hot flashes when compared with no treatment as control. There was also an improvement in sleep and feelings of anxiety and depression ( 106979 ).

Bulimia nervosa.

Although there is interest in using hypnotherapy for bulimia nervosa, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of hypnotherapy for this condition.

Burns.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for pain or pain-related anxiety in patients with burns.

In male patients with moderate pain related to burns, preliminary clinical research shows that four hypnotherapy sessions every other day modestly reduces background pain quality, based on the description or criteria of pain, as well as background pain anxiety, when compared with neutral hypnosis. However, there was no effect on background pain intensity based on a visual analog scale ( 106974 ). This study did not examine the effect of hypnotherapy on procedural pain or breakthrough pain with movement. A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical studies, only 3 of which evaluated burn pain, shows that hypnotherapy of various durations moderately improves pain intensity or unpleasantness when compared to usual care and/or other types of stress-reducing activities ( 106989 ).

Cancer-related pain.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for reducing pain in patients with cancer.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with head and neck cancer shows that a single session of hypnotherapy may modestly reduce pain when compared with usual care alone. All patients had gone through radiotherapy with or without chemotherapy and/or surgery and were using analgesics ( 106980 ). Other preliminary clinical research in cancer survivors with pain of moderate to high intensity shows that daily use of a hypnosis recording for 4 weeks has a small clinical effect on pain when compared to patients on a wait list ( 106981 ). The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) includes hypnotherapy provided by a licensed mental health professional as an option to consider in conjunction with analgesic treatment for pain management in adults with cancer ( 110317 ). The American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) moderately recommends offering hypnotherapy to adults with cancer and pain during procedures for treatment or diagnostic workup. However, ASCO does not recommend for or against hypnotherapy to treat other types of cancer pain or cancer pain in pediatric patients due to insufficient evidence ( 109161 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for reducing CINV.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies, most of which were conducted in children, shows that hypnosis may be beneficial for reducing both anticipatory and CINV. Hypnotherapy was found to be preferable to treatment as usual and at least as effective as cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) ( 106982 ). One study included in the meta-analysis shows that self-hypnosis modestly reduces the need for on-demand antiemetics during chemotherapy courses in children with CINV ( 106975 ).

Crohn disease.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial in adolescents with Crohn disease.

In adolescents with Crohn disease given standard care, preliminary clinical research shows that a single session of hypnotherapy, followed by home practice with recordings for 8 weeks, modestly reduces the most severe abdominal pain and improves parent-reported quality of life. However, there was no effect on usual abdominal pain, child-reported quality of life, school absences, sleep quality, or symptoms of anxiety or depression ( 106972 ). In adolescents and adults in clinical and biochemical remission from Crohn disease or ulcerative colitis and remaining irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)-type symptoms, six sessions of gut-directed hypnotherapy over 12 weeks is as effective as standard medical treatment for reducing IBS overall symptom severity over a 9-month period. A reduction in symptom severity of at least 50% occurred in 30% of patients treated with hypnotherapy, with adequate relief in 40% ( 106395 ). About half of the patients in this study had Crohn disease and half had ulcerative colitis.

Depression.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for improving symptoms of depression.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that hypnosis modestly reduces symptoms of depression ( 106983 ). However, studies in this analysis included patients with various diagnoses and were not limited to patients with major depressive disorders. In patients with mild to moderate major depression, preliminary clinical research shows that 16-20 sessions of hypnotherapy over 6 months is as effective as a similar schedule of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) for reducing symptoms of depression. Benefits seem to last up to 12 months after treatment ( 106966 ). Another small clinical study conducted in Iran in adults with severe major depressive disorder shows that mindful hypnotherapy, provided as 8 weekly sessions along with daily use of mindful hypnotherapy audio tapes, improves depressive symptoms when compared with a waitlist control ( 116265 ). It is unclear how hypnotherapy compares with standard drug treatments.

Dysmenorrhea.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for dysmenorrhea.

Preliminary clinical research shows that undergoing 14 group hypnotherapy sessions over 3 months is as effective as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) for the reduction of pain associated with dysmenorrhea. There was no evidence of pain reduction three months later; however, there was some continued improvement related to functional restriction of activity ( 106984 ).

Dyspepsia.

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for dyspepsia.

In patients with functional dyspepsia, preliminary clinical research shows that hypnotherapy over 16 weeks improves quality of life and overall symptoms, such as nausea, bloating, and decreased appetite, modestly more than supportive care or ranitidine 150 mg twice daily. Improvements were maintained for at least 56 weeks. During the follow-up period, patients treated with hypnotherapy did not start any new antacids or antidepressants, compared with 90% of patients receiving medication instead of hypnotherapy and 82% of those receiving only supportive care ( 106386 ).

Fibromyalgia.

Small clinical studies suggest that hypnotherapy may be beneficial for fibromyalgia, although there is a lack of consistency related to treatment and endpoints in the available research.

The most recent research in patients with fibromyalgia who are undergoing usual physical or psychological therapeutic activities shows that daily use of audio-recorded self-hypnotherapy for one month modestly reduces symptoms of depression, as well as pain and fatigue intensity and interference, when compared to patients not using hypnotherapy. Benefits seem to last at least 6 months ( 106985 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that five sessions of hypnotherapy over a 2-month period with recommended daily practice modestly improves the overall patient global impression of change when compared to a wait-list control. There were also modest improvements in sleep and some measures of cognitive function, but not pain, physical impairment, work missed, and other outcomes ( 106945 ). Another small study shows that eight one-hour treatments over a three-month period, in conjunction with an audio tape provided for daily practice, reduces muscle pain, fatigue upon wakening, sleep disturbance, and overall symptoms, but not joint tenderness, when compared with physical therapy ( 106953 ).

Insomnia.

Small clinical studies suggest that hypnosis may improve some measures of sleep quality in patients with insomnia.

A meta-analysis of a small number of generally low-quality clinical trials shows that hypnotherapy decreases the amount of time to fall asleep when compared to being on a waitlist. However, there was no effect when compared with a sham intervention. Also, there was no effect on the number of awakenings ( 106965 ). A larger, higher-quality study not included in the meta-analysis shows that undergoing one-hour hypnotherapy sessions weekly for 4 weeks with a disease-specific focus modestly improves sleep efficiency, or the amount of time asleep in bed, over a 9-week period when compared with baseline. However, it is no more effective than sessions involving suggestions that are generic and promote general self-confidence and self-care. Also, although some specific sleep measures were improved, including the time to fall asleep and total sleep time, benefits were not consistent over the 9-week period ( 106960 ).

Labor pain.

Research on the use of antenatal hypnotherapy for improving labor pain and reducing the use of analgesics is mixed.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research shows that patients using hypnotherapy during pregnancy were 27% less likely to use pharmacological pain relief or other analgesia during labor when compared with control groups given standard care, supportive counselling, or relaxation training. However, many individual studies included in the analysis disagree with these findings, and due to the number and heterogeneity of the included studies, there was no clear effect of hypnotherapy on other endpoints such as coping with labor, spontaneous vaginal birth, pain, complications, or others ( 106947 ). The largest study included in the analysis, evaluating over 1200 patients, showed that practicing self-hypnosis following three training sessions was no more effective than relaxation or no treatment for reducing the use of epidural analgesia or for levels of self-reported pain ( 106948 ). Most studies have investigated the use of two or more individual or group hypnotherapy training sessions, combined with home practice, starting during the first to third trimester ( 106390 , 106947 , 106948 ). However, hypnotherapy during labor has also been investigated ( 106389 , 106947 ). Some research has shown benefits with antenatal hypnotherapy training based on the philosophy of hypnobirthing. A small clinical study found that receiving nursing care based on the philosophy of hypnobirthing during labor is more effective than routine care for reducing labor fear, pain, and duration, and improving satisfaction ( 106389 ). Another small clinical study in Turkish patients shows that antenatal hypnobirthing sessions provided by a certified instructor and use of this technique during labor reduces labor fear, pain, duration, and analgesic use, and improves satisfaction when compared with standard care ( 110656 ). More research is needed to determine the optimal timing of antenatal hypnotherapy as well as the benefits of hypnotherapy during labor.

Lactation.

Clinical research suggests that self-hypnosis does not affect lactation initiation or duration.

Preliminary clinical research shows that three lessons in self-hypnosis with self-practice, starting after 30 weeks' gestation, does not affect self-reported lactation success or lactation duration when compared with relaxation training or usual antenatal care ( 106387 ).

Migraine headache.

Small clinical studies suggest that hypnotherapy may be beneficial for reducing the frequency and/or severity of migraine headaches in children.

In children aged 6-12 years with classic migraine, a small clinical study shows that using self-hypnosis twice daily for 3 months reduces headache frequency from a mean of approximately 13-14 events during the 3-month period preceding self-hypnosis to 5 events during the 3-month period during which self-hypnosis was conducted ( 106952 ). Other research has enrolled children with mixed headache types. One small clinical study enrolled children aged 9-18 years with headaches occurring more than two times a month, with 16% of patients identifying migraine headaches as their most common presentation and 18% identifying a combination of tension-type and migraine headache. This study shows that six sessions of hypnotherapy with daily home practice over a 3-month period reduces headache frequency from 18.2 days at baseline to 12.5 days at 3 months and 10.5 days at 9 months. However, hypnotherapy was no more effective than transcendental meditation or progressive muscle relaxation ( 100746 ). In another study in children and adolescents with migraines and other types of chronic headache, training in self-hypnosis reduced headache frequency from approximately 4.5 events to 1.4 events weekly, with a reduction in average duration from 23.6 hours at baseline to 3 hours. The intensity was also modestly reduced ( 106377 ).

It is unclear if hypnotherapy is beneficial for MS.

A meta-analysis of 3 clinical studies in patients with MS shows that hypnotherapy, administered for up to 10 sessions, reduces pain intensity compared with control ( 115558 ). One clinical study, included in the analysis above, shows that performing self-hypnosis at least 10 times daily after six weekly training sessions reduces the intensity of pain immediately after each self-hypnosis session when compared to standard care alone. However, benefit was not maintained up to 4 weeks after treatment cessation ( 106950 ). It is unclear from this study whether any benefit was related to the on-going daily treatment or to the individual self-hypnosis session. Other research in patients with MS shows that eight weekly sessions of group-based cognitive hypnotherapy, a combination of hypnotherapy and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), is beneficial for improving feelings of well-being when compared to patients not undergoing hypnotherapy ( 106967 ). It is unclear whether any benefit is related to hypnotherapy, CBT, or the combination.

Another moderate-size study in Malaysian individuals with overweight or obesity shows that three hypnotherapy sessions at least a month apart, with encouragement to perform self-hypnosis, leads to about 1.4 kg more weight loss at 12 weeks when compared with lifestyle interventions alone, but does not reduce body fat or waist circumference. Similar to the prior study, more frequent self-hypnosis was associated with greater weight loss ( 110657 ). In individuals with obesity and obstructive sleep apnea, two sessions of hypnotherapy directed at stress reduction results in a small, and likely clinically insignificant, increase in weight loss over 18 months when compared with dietary advice alone or with hypnotherapy aimed at caloric reduction ( 106958 ).

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that hypnotherapy of various durations moderately improves pain intensity when compared to control groups, such as usual care and/or other types of stress-reducing activities. There was also some evidence of a decreased need for opioids. However, studies included in the analyses enrolled patients with various types of acute pain, including acute pain in a hospital setting, burn pain, procedural pain, and postoperative pain. In patients in remission from ulcerative colitis, seven sessions of hypnotherapy increased the number of days to clinical relapse by 78 when compared with an attention control. About half of the patients in this study had ulcerative colitis and half had Crohn disease.

Hypnotherapy may be conducted by a hypnotherapist or another individual, such as a physician or nurse, trained in hypnotherapy. It can also be delivered using audio recordings either alone or combined with directed sessions ( 106946 , 106947 , 106956 , 106962 , 106963 , 106979 , 106981 , 106985 ). When delivered by a clinician, a relationship between clinician and patient can be developed and the session can be tailored to the individual needs of the patient ( 106385 , 106386 , 106945 , 106972 , 106979 , 106981 ). Hypnosis can be offered individually or in a group. Sessions may vary from a single brief encounter to regularly scheduled longer appointments. Sessions are generally 30-60 minutes long and may take place weekly or at other intervals for up to 15 weeks ( 106385 , 106393 , 106395 , 106397 , 106399 , 106945 , 106946 , 106947 , 106960 , 106962 , 106963 , 106967 , 106979 , 106984 , 106986 , 106988 ). Usually, hypnotherapy sessions are supplemented with self-hypnosis one or more times daily ( 106377 , 106391 , 106399 , 106935 , 106960 , 106962 , 106963 , 106967 , 106984 , 106986 ). Occasionally, patients do not receive hypnotherapy by a therapist but instead attend sessions providing instruction in self-hypnosis ( 106948 , 106949 , 106975 ). Hypnotherapy can be divided into phases, including pre-suggestion (induction), suggestion (deepening), and post-suggestion or post-hypnotic phases ( 106378 , 106374 , 106946 , 106955 , 106971 , 106981 ). The pre-suggestion component may include selective attention focusing with distraction, imagery, and relaxation methods ( 106955 , 106377 , 106967 , 106971 , 106979 , 106981 , 106984 , 106988 ). In some situations, hypnosis is induced by eye closure with progressive muscular relaxation or visualization of slowly walking downstairs ( 106377 , 106385 , 106967 , 106988 ). In the suggestion phase, certain ideas are presented, or questions are asked to explore the patient's memory. For example, for pain reduction, suggestions are related to reducing the pain or changing the meaning of sensations ( 106947 , 106967 , 106979 , 106981 , 106984 ).  The post-suggestion phase occurs when the patient returns to the normal state and new behaviors based on hypnotic suggestions may be practiced. This may involve suggestions to help maintain benefits and for practicing self-hypnosis in order to give the patient control over their condition ( 106385 , 106946 , 106963 , 106967 , 106981 ). A hypnotherapist may test how responsive an individual would be to suggestion. The degree of susceptibility to hypnosis and suggestion appears to vary between individuals. Common tests that measure the degree of hypnotizability include the Stanford Hypnotic Susceptibility Scale, Barber Suggestibility Scale, Harvard Group Scale of Hypnotic Susceptibility, and the Eye Roll Test ( 106948 , 106963 , 106998 , 106999 ). There is wide variation in the training and credentials of hypnotherapists. Certification is granted by multiple organizations, with different requirements. In the United States, there is no universally accepted standard or licensing for hypnotherapists. Although many therapists are not licensed medical professionals, some doctors, dentists, and psychologists are trained in hypnotherapy and may use hypnosis in practice. Various forms of hypnotherapy are practiced. Some examples of those used in clinical research include: - Mindful hypnotherapy: This technique uses hypnosis to integrate mindfulness as part of a therapeutic program ( 106382 ). - Erikson's hypnosis: This technique avoids referring directly to the pain or analgesia suggestions and allows the patient to use personal resources by activating memory and knowledge ( 106966 , 106971 , 106988 ). - Cognitive hypnotherapy: This type is a combination of clinical hypnosis and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT). It is most commonly used for psychological disorders and aids in coping, problem solving, emotional regulation, and interpersonal skills ( 106967 ). - Gut-directed hypnotherapy: This type uses gut-directed imagery to focus on symptoms of irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) ( 106391 , 106393 , 106395 , 106397 , 106956 ).

The way hypnosis works is not well understood. Hypnosis has been associated with various changes in the body, some of which may be directly related to relaxation, such as reduced feelings of anxiety. During hypnosis, heart rate and blood pressure reductions have been noted. These changes may be associated with changes in flow-mediated vasodilation and increased blood vessel diameter ( 106384 ). Hypnosis is also associated with changes in the immune response. This is thought to explain beneficial effects in patients with allergies, asthma, and related illnesses ( 106384 , 106399 , 107000 ). Respiratory system benefits include improvements in expiration and bronchial hyperresponsiveness ( 106384 ). In terms of the gastrointestinal system, visceral sensations have been shown to be normalized and there are changes in the levels of perceived discomfort. Also, some research shows that hypnosis seems to reduce the secretion of gastric acid, suggesting it may be beneficial for those with acid reflux ( 106384 , 106385 , 106956 ). Hypnotherapy-related pain reduction is associated with imaging changes in the anterior cingulate cortex. There is some evidence that pain reduction is due to reduced feelings of anxiety or changes in neuroendocrine pathways in the brain ( 106947 , 106981 , 107000 ). Hypnotizability or hypnotic susceptibility is thought to explain some of the interindividual variability in outcomes ( 107000 ). Although it is not known why some individuals are more susceptible to hypnotic suggestion than others, levels of some brain neurotransmitters and the presence of specific genetic polymorphisms may play a role ( 107000 ). Also, it is possible that hypnotic susceptibility increases expectancy effects ( 106963 ). However, at least some research shows that hypnotizability does not predict the efficacy of hypnosis ( 106947 , 106963 ).
