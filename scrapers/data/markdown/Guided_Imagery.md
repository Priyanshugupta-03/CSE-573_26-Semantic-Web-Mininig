# Guided Imagery
Other Common Names Guided Health Imagery, Guided Visualization, Imágenes guiadas, Imagerie Guidée, Imagery, Imaginación Guiada, Visualisation, Visualisation Guidée, Visualization, Visualization Therapy, VT
|

---

Caution

Do not confuse guided imagery with Relaxation Therapy .

## Overview

Guided imagery is the use of guided or directed thoughts and visualizations to enhance relaxation and to visualize solutions to problems or disease fighting processes ( 16237 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Guided imagery has been safely used once to twice daily for up to 24 months in many clinical trials ( 16236 , 16237 , 16238 , 16239 , 16240 , 16241 , 16242 , 16243 , 16244 , 16245 , 16246 , 16247 , 16248 , 104985 , 104986 , 104987 , 104988 , 104988 , 104989 , 104990 , 104991 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no known reason to expect adverse effects.

#### General

Guided imagery is well tolerated. There have been no reports of adverse effects in clinical research.

|
Possibly Effective
Postoperative pain.

Guided imagery may moderately reduce postoperative pain, but it is unclear which guided imagery protocol may be most beneficial.

A meta-analysis of clinical trials in adults undergoing various surgeries shows that guided imagery moderately reduces postoperative pain when compared with control. The effectiveness of guided imagery may vary based on the type and duration of the intervention or the type of surgery; however, there is not enough available data to evaluate these factors ( 104991 ). Protocols for guided imagery vary widely among clinical trials. Guided imagery once to three times daily has been initiated as early as 7 days prior to the procedure and continued for up to 7 days after the procedure ( 104991 , 104987 , 98798 , 16244 , 104987 ). Preliminary clinical research also shows that a specific type of guided imagery called "Guided Meditation for Procedures or Surgery" improves pain and anxiety in patients for the first 2 days after elective joint replacement when compared to usual care. However, guided imagery was found to be no better than the "M" Technique, a type of therapeutic touch ( 98798 ). In addition, some clinical research in adults with colorectal cancer shows that listening to a 12-minute guided imagery tape three times daily does not reduce pain or analgesic use after resection surgery when compared with progressive muscle relaxation or a control group ( 16244 ).

Stress.

Guided imagery, alone or in combination with relaxation training, may reduce the stress of patients in stressful medical situations.

Some low-quality clinical research shows that guided imagery moderately reduces some measures of psychological distress, improves relaxation, decreases anxiety and depression, and improves quality of life when compared with control in patients undergoing stressful situations such as cancer treatment or hospitalization ( 16236 , 16237 , 16238 , 16239 , 16245 ). However, some of these clinical trials used guided imagery in combination with relaxation training.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Abdominal pain.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in children with abdominal pain.

Preliminary clinical research in children aged 6-15 years with stomach pain shows that listening to a guided imagery tape for 10-25 minutes at least 5 days per week for a total of 8 weeks, in addition to standard care, reduces functional abdominal pain when compared with standard treatment alone. Three children would need to be treated with guided imagery for one to experience an improvement in stomach pain ( 98866 ). Other preliminary clinical research in children aged 5-18 years with recurrent abdominal pain shows that listening to a guided imagery tape twice daily for a total of 2 months, along with progressive muscle relaxation, decreases the average number of days with pain by 47% when compared with breathing exercises ( 16240 ).

Anxiety.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial for reducing anxiety in hospitalized patients.

Preliminary clinical research in hospitalized adults with mild to moderate coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) shows that listening to a 25-minute guided imagery tape twice daily for 5 days modestly reduces anxiety scores when compared with control ( 107357 ). Other preliminary clinical research in hospitalized adults with acute and/or chronic pain shows that listening to a 30-minute guided imagery tape twice daily for 48 hours reduces anxiety scores by 50% when compared with baseline. The validity of this study is limited by the lack of a comparator group ( 107353 ).

Bulimia nervosa.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for bulimia nervosa, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Cancer-related pain.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with cancer-related pain.

A non-randomized clinical study in adults with cancer-related pain shows that listening to a 15-minute guided imagery tape once daily for 3 days reduces baseline pain scores by approximately 7%. The effect of guided imagery on pain scores was similar to that of Reiki therapy ( 104985 ). Other clinical research in adults admitted to hospice care with cancer-related pain shows that undergoing a single practitioner-led guided imagery session for 20 minutes, in combination with progressive muscle relaxation therapy, reduces baseline pain scores by 45%. Guided imagery with progressive muscle relaxation therapy also reduced the need for rescue analgesics in the following 24 hours by 48% when compared with control. The reduction in pain was no different than the control group; however, this study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups. It is unclear if any effect is due to guided imagery, progressive muscle relaxation therapy, or the combination ( 104986 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

Guided imagery has only been evaluated in combination with other modalities; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in cancer patients receiving chemotherapy shows that practicing guided imagery for 15 minutes and progressive muscle relaxation for 10 minutes daily for 4 weeks modestly reduces self-reported nausea and vomiting severity and frequency when compared with control. Practicing guided imagery also reduces distress due to nausea, vomiting, and retching when compared to usual treatment ( 98795 ). It is unclear if this effect is due to guided imagery, progressive muscle relaxation therapy, or the combination.

Chemotherapy-related fatigue.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with chemotherapy-related fatigue.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with cancer-related pain shows that listening to a 15-minute guided imagery tape once daily for 3 days reduces baseline fatigue scores by approximately 6%. The effect of guided imagery on fatigue scores was similar to that of Reiki therapy ( 104985 ). Other preliminary clinical research in cancer patients receiving chemotherapy shows that practicing guided imagery for 15 minutes and progressive muscle relaxation for 10 minutes daily for 4 weeks modestly reduces perceived fatigue when compared with usual treatment ( 98795 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for CFS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with COPD.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with COPD shows that practicing guided imagery for 6, 30-minute sessions very modestly improves oxygen saturation when compared with baseline. Guided imagery did not improve other physiological measurements; however, this study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 16246 ).

Cocaine dependence.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with cocaine dependence.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with primary cocaine dependence shows that a specific type of guided imagery called Memory Focused Cognitive Therapy reduces immediate, self-reported cravings by 32% when compared with baseline. Subgroup analyses suggest that use of positive framing images is more effective than negative framing images ( 104990 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Depression.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for depression, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Diabetes.

Guided imagery has only been evaluated in combination with other modalities; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in children with type 1 diabetes shows that listening to a 7-minute guided imagery tape with background music twice weekly for 12 weeks does not improve glycated hemoglobin when compared with listening to background music only ( 98799 ).

Fatigue.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for fatigue, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with fibromyalgia; the available research is conflicting.

Some research shows that guided imagery does not improve fibromyalgia pain ( 16242 , 16247 , 98867 ), while other research shows that guided imagery decreases fibromyalgia pain when compared to control ( 16247 , 98862 , 98865 ). Similarly, preliminary clinical research has found conflicting effects of guided imagery on pain self-efficacy scores and functional status scores ( 16242 , 98867 ). There is also conflicting information about the effect of guided imagery on depression. Some research in patients with fibromyalgia shows that guided imagery improves depression based on the Beck Depression Inventory and the Center for Epidemiological Studies-Depression (CES-D) scale when compared with control; however, it does not seem to improve depression as measured by the visual analogue scale ( 98862 , 98865 ). The exact reasons for the conflicting results of guided imagery in fibromyalgia are unclear. The disparity between results might be due to differences in content, duration, and frequency of guided imagery exercises, differences in patient population, or the use of different scales to measure effects ( 16242 , 16247 , 98862 , 98865 , 98867 ).

Headache.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for headache, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT).

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients undergoing a HSCT.

Preliminary clinical research in adults admitted to the hospital for an HSCT shows that participating in a 10-minute guided imagery session via a virtual reality device 3 times weekly throughout hospitalization does not improve quality of life scores when compared with control. However, the study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 107355 ).

Hypertension.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for hypertension, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).

Guided imagery has only been evaluated in combination with other modalities; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with stable ulcerative colitis or Crohn disease shows that participating in a specific guided imagery session with music therapy, called the Bonny Method of Guided Imagery with Music, for 2 hours once weekly for 8 weeks does not improve IBD-related quality of life or depression and anxiety scores when compared with no intervention. Guided imagery with music did modestly improve some emotional state score domains, including sadness, fear, and anger. It is unclear if any potential effects are due to guided imagery, music therapy, or the combination ( 107358 ).

Insomnia.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for insomnia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Kidney failure.

It is unclear if guided imagery can improve psychological well-being in adults receiving hemodialysis.

Preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing hemodialysis shows that practicing guided imagery by imagining beautiful scenery and breathing deeply for 30 minutes before hemodialysis three times weekly for 4 weeks moderately improves depression and anxiety scores when compared with routine care ( 98794 ).

Labor pain.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with labor pain.

Preliminary clinical research in adults undergoing uncomplicated vaginal delivery shows that watching a 13-minute guided imagery video once at the beginning of the active labor phase decreases pain scores 1.8 times more than no intervention. The reduction in pain with guided imagery was similar to that seen in patients receiving foot reflexology therapy ( 107356 ).

Migraine headache.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for migraine headache, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Neuropathic pain.

Guided imagery has only been evaluated in combination with other modalities; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with neuropathic pain secondary to spinal cord injury shows that listening to a 15-minute guided imagery tape in combination with laterality training 5 days per week for 4 weeks reduces pain scores by approximately 50% when compared with baseline. Guided imagery with laterality training also reduces pain scores by 1.5-2.3 times more than control. It is unclear if this effect is due to guided imagery, laterality training, or the combination ( 104989 ).

Obesity.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with obesity.

Preliminary clinical research in obese and normal weight adolescents 14-17 years of age shows that practicing guided imagery with a focus on either stress reduction or lifestyle modifications weekly for 12 weeks does not improve diet or exercise habits when compared with didactic education or no intervention. Some subgroup analyses in students with high participation rates suggest that both guided imagery interventions may modestly increase physical activity when compared with no intervention. However, this study did not evaluate changes in weight, body mass index, or clinical outcomes ( 107354 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Guided imagery has only been evaluated in combination with other modalities; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research shows that listening to a 12-minute guided imagery tape, in combination with relaxation training, twice daily for 12-16 weeks improves pain, mobility, and health-related quality of life in adult females with osteoarthritis when compared with baseline ( 16241 , 98793 ). The validity of these studies is limited by a lack of reported comparisons to the control groups. It is also unclear if these effects are due to guided imagery, relaxation training, or the combination.

Pain (acute).

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial for reducing acute pain in hospitalized patients.

Preliminary clinical research in hospitalized adults with mild to moderate coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) shows that listening to a 25-minute guided imagery tape twice daily for 5 days modestly reduces pain scores when compared with control ( 107357 ). Other preliminary clinical research in hospitalized adults with acute and/or chronic pain shows that listening to a 30-minute guided imagery tape twice daily for 48 hours does not reduce pain scores, but may modestly reduce average daily opioid requirements, when compared with baseline. The validity of this study is limited by the lack of a comparator group ( 107353 ).

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial for reducing chronic pain in hospitalized patients.

Preliminary clinical research in hospitalized adults with acute and/or chronic pain shows that listening to a 30-minute guided imagery tape twice daily for 48 hours does not reduce pain scores, but may modestly reduce average daily opioid requirements, when compared with baseline. The validity of this study is limited by the lack of a comparator group ( 107353 ).

Parturition.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial for reducing the duration of labor.

Preliminary clinical research in adults undergoing uncomplicated vaginal delivery shows that watching a 13-minute guided imagery video once at the beginning of the active labor phase decreases the duration of active labor by 50% when compared with no intervention. The duration of active labor in those participating in guided imagery was similar to that seen in patients receiving foot reflexology therapy ( 107356 ).

Postoperative ileus.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with postoperative ileus.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with colorectal cancer shows that listening to a 12-minute guided imagery tape three times daily, beginning 2 days prior to resection surgery, does not reduce the incidence or duration of postoperative ileus when compared with progressive relaxation therapy or a control group. However, the study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 16244 ).

Postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV).

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with PONV.

Preliminary clinical research in adults undergoing an elective surgery shows that listening to a guided imagery tape does not reduce self-reported nausea scores or the number of vomiting episodes when compared with no intervention. Subjects listened to the guided imagery tape for 50 minutes prior to the surgery and then for 40 minutes once on postoperative day 1 and twice daily on postoperative days 2 and 3 ( 104987 ).

Postoperative recovery.

It is unclear if guided imagery can improve postoperative recovery.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with colorectal cancer shows that listening to a 12-minute guided imagery tape three times daily, beginning 2 days prior to resection surgery, does not improve postoperative recovery, including postoperative fatigue or pulmonary function, when compared with progressive relaxation therapy or a control group. However, the study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 16244 ).

Postoperative sleep disturbance.

Small clinical studies suggest that guided imagery may improve sleep quality after surgery.

Clinical research in adults undergoing an elective surgery shows that listening to a guided imagery tape improves self-reported sleep quality scores for the first 48 hours after the procedure when compared with no intervention. Subjects listened to the guided imagery tape for 50 minutes prior to the surgery, followed by 40 minutes once on postoperative day 1 and twice daily on postoperative days 2 and 3 ( 104987 ). In addition, a clinical study in elderly patients undergoing abdominal surgery shows that listening to a guided imagery tape while conducting breathing relaxation therapy twice daily improves self-reported sleep quality for 96 hours after the procedure when compared to no intervention. Patients began guided imagery and breathing relaxation therapy the day before the procedure and continued through postoperative day 4. It is unclear if this effect is due to guided imagery, breathing relaxation therapy, or the combination ( 104988 ).

Postpartum depression.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for postpartum depression, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Guided imagery has only been evaluated in combination with other modalities; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with PTSD shows that practicing guided imagery with healing touch in 6 sessions over 3 weeks, in addition to daily home-based sessions, may reduce PTSD symptoms and depression scores when compared with usual treatment. It is unclear if this effect is due to guided imagery, healing touch therapy, or the combination. The guided imagery used in the study was a 1-hour recorded tape of "Healing Trauma (PTSD)-Healthy Journeys" by Belleruth Naparstek ( 98800 ).

Pregnancy-induced hypertension.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with pregnancy-induced hypertension.

Preliminary clinical research in pregnant patients with hypertension shows that listening to a guided imagery tape for 15 minutes twice daily for 4 weeks, or until delivery, modestly reduces mean arterial pressure, but not diastolic or systolic blood pressure, when compared with sitting quietly. However, after adjustment for baseline blood pressure, guided imagery did not reduce mean arterial pressure when compared with sitting quietly ( 98863 ).

Pre-procedural anxiety.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial for reducing preoperative or pre-procedural anxiety in adults and children.

A meta-analysis of clinical trials that used guided imagery prior to adult surgeries shows that guided imagery moderately reduces anxiety when compared with control. However, the validity of this finding is limited by significant heterogeneity between included studies ( 104991 ). The effectiveness of guided imagery may vary based on the type and duration of the intervention or the type of surgery, but there isn't enough available data to evaluate these factors ( 104991 , 104987 ). Conversely, preliminary clinical research in adults receiving cutaneous surgical procedures or coronary angiography found that listening to a guided imagery tape for 13-18 minutes prior to the procedure does not reduce preoperative anxiety when compared with no intervention or with wearing headphones without sound ( 98792 , 98796 ). In children, guided imagery does not appear to reduce anxiety or pain before venipuncture or vaccination. Preliminary clinical research in female children aged 11-12 years shows that practicing guided imagery and relaxation therapy prior to receiving the human papilloma virus vaccination does not reduce self-reported anxiety or pain when compared with control ( 98864 ). Also, a non-randomized clinical study in 12-year-old children suggests that practicing guided imagery and relaxation therapy for 10 minutes prior to venipuncture does not reduce anxiety when compared with standard care ( 98797 ).

Quality of life.

It is unclear if guided imagery can improve quality of life in patients with various medical conditions; the available research is conflicting.

Preliminary clinical research in adults admitted to the hospital for a stem cell transplant shows that participating in a 10-minute guided imagery session via a virtual reality device 3 times weekly throughout hospitalization does not improve quality of life scores when compared with control. However, the study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 107355 ). Similarly, preliminary clinical research in adults with ulcerative colitis or Crohn disease shows that participating in a specific guided imagery session with music therapy, called the Bonny Method of Guided Imagery with Music, for 2 hours once weekly for 8 weeks does not improve quality of life scores when compared with no intervention ( 107358 ). In contrast, preliminary clinical research in cancer patients receiving chemotherapy shows that practicing guided imagery for 15 minutes and progressive muscle relaxation for 10 minutes daily for 4 weeks improves quality of life scores by 17%, compared with a 21% reduction in the control group. Guided imagery and progressive muscle relaxation also reduced fatigue, pain, depression, and nausea and vomiting scores when compared with usual treatment ( 98795 ). It is unclear if these effects are due to guided imagery, progressive muscle relaxation, or the combination.

Sexual dysfunction.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for sexual dysfunction, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Smoking cessation.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial for smoking cessation.

Preliminary clinical research shows that regularly listening to a 20-minute guided imagery tape for 24 months results in smoking abstinence rates of 26%, compared with 12% in a placebo group ( 16243 ).

Substance use disorder.

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for substance use disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Tension headache.

It is unclear if guided imagery is beneficial in patients with tension headache.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic tension headache shows that listening to a guided imagery tape for 20 minutes once daily for 29 days reduces the number of patients reporting severe headaches and more than 1 headache per week by 21% and 17%, respectively, when compared with baseline. However, these improvements were similar when compared with the control group. In contrast, overall patient-rated improvement was higher in the guided imagery group when compared with control ( 16248 ). Other preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic tension headache shows that listening to a guided imagery tape or using the guided imagery technique of remembering a happy memory for 20 minutes three times weekly for 3 weeks reduces headache frequency by 64% and 54% more, respectively, when compared with control. Guided imagery and guided imagery with a happy memory also improved headache intensity and duration when compared with control ( 98791 ).

Upper respiratory tract infection (URTI).

Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for URTIs, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

Juvenile idiopathic arthritis . Although there has been interest in using guided imagery for JIA, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of guided imagery for this purpose.

More evidence is needed to rate guided imagery for these uses.

Guided imagery is the use of guided or directed thoughts and visualizations to enhance relaxation and to visualize solutions to problems or disease fighting processes. The visualization process can be guided by an instructor or teacher or prerecorded tapes. Guided imagery is often used with other techniques such as relaxation therapy or biofeedback ( 16237 ).

Guided imagery uses directed thoughts to enhance relaxation and to visualize solutions to problems. It is theorized that the visualization may evoke sensory memory and strong emotions which may have beneficial effects on health. For example, cancer patients might envision bodily defense systems fighting and destroying cancer cells which might improve their psychological well-being and concurrently improve their quality of life ( 16237 , 98795 ). Researchers speculate that guided imagery reduces stress such and might also enhance immune function ( 16237 ).
