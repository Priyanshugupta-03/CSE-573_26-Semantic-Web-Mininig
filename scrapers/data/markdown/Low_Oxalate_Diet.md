# Low Oxalate Diet
Other Common Names Dieta baja en oxalatos, Low-Oxalate Diet, Régime Faible en Oxalate
|

---

Caution

See separate listing for Low Purine Diet.

## Overview

A low oxalate diet eliminates foods and beverages rich in oxalate, a compound commonly found in plant-based foods. This diet is frequently recommended for people who have increased levels of oxalic acid in their urine (hyperoxaluria) or who have a history of kidney stones ( 103120 , 103121 ). Low oxalate diets typically recommend avoiding plant-based foods that are high in oxalate. However, measures of oxalate content are inconsistent, and many references differ on which foods are considered to be oxalate-rich ( 103120 , 103121 ).

## People Use This For

A low oxalate diet has been used for kidney stones (nephrolithiasis) and vulvar pain.

Likely Safe when followed appropriately, short-term. The low oxalate diet has been used without apparent safety concerns for up to 3 months in clinical trials ( 103121 , 103122 , 103124 , 103125 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the low oxalate diet in pregnancy and lactation. However, there is no reason to expect safety issues as long as nutritional needs are met.

#### General

The low oxalate diet seems to be generally well-tolerated. No adverse effects have been reported in clinical trials ( 103121 , 103122 , 103123 , 103124 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis).

The low oxalate diet has been evaluated in clinical research for the management of kidney stones. However, the research with a low oxalate diet to reduce kidney stones is limited to small, preliminary studies ( 103121 , 103122 ). Additionally, some exploratory studies suggest that dietary oxalate is not directly correlated with urinary oxalate excretion, and that urinary oxalate excretion appears to be affected by multiple factors ( 103123 , 103124 ).

One small clinical study in adults with a history of kidney stones and hyperoxaluria shows that following the low oxalate diet is similarly effective to the Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet for improving urinary oxalate excretion. However, the DASH diet is more effective for reducing calcium oxalate supersaturation. This may be due to a simultaneous increase in magnesium and citrate excretion with the DASH diet ( 103121 ). Another small clinical study in adults with idiopathic hyperoxaluria and a history of calcium oxalate stone formation, shows that consuming a low oxalate diet in addition to low salt intake, moderate protein intake, and increased fluid intake for 6 weeks lowers urinary oxalate levels, but it is unclear if this affects stone formation ( 112764 ). In adults with a history of kidney stones and hypercalciuria, following a low calcium, low oxalate diet for 4 weeks results in reduced urinary excretion of calcium, but does not affect urinary excretion of oxalic acid, when compared with baseline ( 103122 ).

Vulvar pain.

Clinical evidence indicates that there is no significant difference in urinary oxalate excretion between females with vulvar pain syndrome and healthy controls, although in a sub-population of females with vulvar pain syndrome and increased urinary oxalate excretion, following a low oxalate diet for 3 months results in an objective response in 24% of the patients when compared with baseline, suggesting that high levels of urinary oxalate excretion may exacerbate symptoms ( 103125 ). A very small clinical study in females with secondary provoked vulvar pain suggests that eating a low oxalate diet for 3 weeks improves the diversity of the vaginal microbiota and is associated with a trend towards pain reduction ( 112765 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the low oxalate diet for these uses.

The low oxalate diet recommends increasing water intake and limiting oxalate intake by reducing consumption of oxalate-rich foods. Oxalates are primarily found in plant-based foods, including spinach, rhubarb, nuts, plums, cereals, wheat bran, tea, beetroot, and strawberries ( 112764 ). Animal-based foods contain little to no oxalate. However, references for oxalate content are often inconsistent. An online survey of references for oxalate content in foods found that the information from 13 different sources varied widely, with the  content for some food items differing up to 100-fold ( 103120 ). Regardless of the concerns related to oxalate content accuracy, oxalate levels are classified into low, moderate, and high. Low oxalate foods have less than 2 mg of oxalate per serving. Moderate oxalate foods have 2-6 mg of oxalate per serving, and are often limited to being consumed 3 to 5 times a week. High oxalate foods have more than 7 mg of oxalate per serving and are avoided ( 103120 ). More recently, recommendations to follow a low oxalate diet tend to be accompanied with other dietary recommendations as well, including increased calcium and magnesium consumption ( 103121 ).

The low oxalate diet is based on the premise that reduced dietary intake of oxalate will reduce urinary oxalate excretion, thus reducing the risk for accumulation of oxalate in the kidneys. However, research with this diet has shown that it is unlikely that oxalate consumption alone will adequately modify urinary oxalate excretion. Much of the oxalate present in the body is produced endogenously as an end-product of liver metabolism ( 112764 ). Vitamin C is partly converted to oxalate; therefore, high intakes may increase urinary oxalate ( 112764 ). A small, exploratory study in healthy volunteers compared the effects of a low oxalate diet, a low oxalate vegetarian diet, and a standard vegetarian diet over a 2-day period. The low oxalate diet and the standard vegetarian diet resulted in similar oxalate absorption and urinary excretion, while the low oxalate vegetarian diet resulted in increased oxalate absorption and urinary excretion. This suggests that there is a complex interaction between various nutritional factors, and that consumption of oxalate alone does not directly dictate urinary oxalate excretion ( 103123 ). A separate study in healthy volunteers found that following a low oxalate diet for 2 days results in lower oxalate absorption than following a high oxalate diet, as might be expected. However, when the high oxalate diet is followed for 6 weeks, absorption decreases to a similar rate as that seen with a low oxalate diet ( 103124 ). Additionally, various factors appear to alter the absorption of oxalate in the gastrointestinal tract. Some studies suggest that oxalate absorption from the gastrointestinal tract is only 5% to 10%, while other studies estimate an absorption rate of up to 50%. The rate might be influenced by the consumption of other nutrients, including calcium and magnesium. Also, gastrointestinal colonization with Oxalobacter formigenes seems to be associated with lower urinary oxalate excretion ( 103121 ). Considering all of these factors, it is unclear to what extent dietary oxalate consumption affects urinary oxalate excretion.
