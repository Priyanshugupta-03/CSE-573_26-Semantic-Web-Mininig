## Overview

Balneotherapy is a broad term that means bath therapy. The term balneotherapy is often used to describe many activities associated with "spa" treatments relating to water, including therapeutic water baths, mineral baths, thermal baths, baths containing gases such as carbon dioxide, hydrogen sulfide, or radon, mud baths or packs, peloids, and others ( 16193 , 98395 , 103528 , 103538 , 108285 ). Although hydrotherapy and balneotherapy are sometimes used interchangeably to refer to water therapy, hydrotherapy usually refers to therapy utilizing plain tap water containing very low levels of minerals. Balneotherapy, on the other hand, generally refers to therapy with natural thermal mineral waters ( 103533 ). Both therapies are covered in this monograph.

## History

The Romans are thought to be the first to use mineral baths and spa therapy for therapeutic purposes. The use of spa therapy for musculoskeletal conditions was rediscovered in the sixteenth century ( 98395 ).

## People Use This For

Balneotherapy is used for many conditions including cardiovascular disease (CVD), chronic venous insufficiency (CVI), fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), depression, anxiety, generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), insomnia, headache, muscle soreness, injury, back pain, neck pain, shoulder pain, osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis (RA), psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, spinal cord injury, spasticity, tendinopathy, stroke, acne, psoriasis, atopic dermatitis (eczema), lichen planus, scleroderma, and many other conditions.

Likely Safe when used appropriately, short-term. Balneotherapy has been used with apparent safety in several studies for up to 3 weeks ( 16185 , 16186 , 16187 , 16188 , 16189 , 16190 , 16191 , 16192 , 16196 , 16197 )( 98387 , 98390 , 98393 , 103528 , 103531 , 103534 , 103539 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when used appropriately, short-term in children aged 1-14 years. Balneotherapy using water at a temperature of 27°C has been used with apparent safety for up to 2 weeks ( 103540 ).

PREGNANCY: Possibly Unsafe when heated balneotherapy is used. Conditions that increase maternal core temperature above 39°C have been associated with increased risk of teratogenicity ( 95709 ). Some early research also found an association between maternal heat exposure in the form of a hot tub or sauna during the first trimester and increased risk of neural tube defects ( 95710 ). While a more recent analysis of clinical research shows that it is safe for pregnant women to sit in hot baths at 40°C (104°F) or in saunas at 70°C (158°F) for up to 20 minutes, regardless of trimester ( 95709 ), clinical practice guidelines by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists still discourage pregnant women from using hot water baths during pregnancy due to possible risks ( 95711 ). Until more is known, pregnant women should avoid hot balneotherapy baths and other treatments.

PREGNANCY: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of balneotherapy heated to less than 39°C; avoid using.

LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Balneotherapy is well tolerated when used appropriately, short term. Adverse effects attributed to balneotherapy have rarely been reported in clinical trials ( 98387 , 98390 , 98393 ). They may include changes in blood pressure, cardiac arrhythmia, mild redness and burning of the skin, increased risk of infections, weakness, and insomnia ( 103537 , 103540 , 103541 ).

#### Cardiovascular

Hypotension, hypertension, and cardiac arrhythmia possibly related to balneotherapy have been rarely reported ( 103537 , 103541 ).

#### Dermatologic

Mild redness and burning of the skin were reported in 43% of patients in one clinical trial ( 103540 ).

#### Immunologic

Respiratory tract, skin, and urinary tract infections possibly related to balneotherapy have been reported ( 103537 , 103541 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Adverse effects in clinical trials thought to be related to balneotherapy include weakness and insomnia ( 103541 ). Mild neurological disorders and worsening pain have also been reported ( 103526 ).

Possibly Effective
Back pain.

Several clinical trials and meta-analyses show that balneotherapy using a mineral bath or shower modestly improves most measures of back pain and function when compared with control groups using tap water, a waiting list, or no bath treatment. Most studies also show an improvement in lumbar flexibility ( 16191 , 103526 ). Most treatments last 20-30 minutes, involve the use of water at a temperature between 31°C and 40°C, and are conducted 5-7 days per week for 3 weeks ( 103526 , 103535 , 103537 ). When used in combination with various physiotherapy treatments such as hot pack, ultrasound, transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS), and exercises, some research shows that balneotherapy reduces back pain and function by a small to moderate amount more than physiotherapy used alone. Any benefit might last up to 9 weeks after cessation of treatment ( 103526 , 103535 , 103537 ). More high-quality, double-blind clinical trials are needed to confirm these results.

Fibromyalgia.

A meta-analysis of five preliminary clinical studies in patients with fibromyalgia shows that receiving balneotherapy for about 2 weeks modestly reduces pain and number of tender points and improves quality of life when compared with control ( 98393 ). Individual clinical studies in fibromyalgia patients also show that receiving balneotherapy reduces pain, fatigue, stiffness, and tender points when compared with baseline, no treatment, or education regarding fibromyalgia ( 16185 , 16189 , 16196 , 16197 , 98382 , 98389 ). Although balneotherapy was typically given over 2-4 weeks, there is evidence from some clinical studies that the therapeutic effects might last for up to 12 months after treatment discontinuation ( 98382 , 98389 , 111197 ). Typical balneotherapy involved warm mineral baths given for 20 minutes 5 days weekly for up to 4 weeks. In some cases, warm mudpacks were also used for 20 minutes per balneotherapy session ( 98382 , 98389 ).

Spa therapy, combining balneotherapy with hydromassage, hydromineral mud applications, and exercise in a mineral water pool, for 6 days per week for 3 weeks reduces the fibromyalgia impact questionnaire score by at least 14% in 45% of patients, compared with 28% of control patients who did not receive spa therapy. Improvements were seen in pain, fatigue, and symptom severity scores, but not in sleep, physical activity, or quality of life ( 111197 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Most preliminary clinical research shows that balneotherapy reduces pain and improves function in patients with knee, hand, or general osteoarthritis.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that balneotherapy modestly improves pain, stiffness, and function in patients with knee osteoarthritis when compared with control ( 98392 , 111196 ). Balneotherapy for knee osteoarthritis can include mineral baths, with or without hot mud packs for up to 20 minutes, five times weekly for 2 or 3 weeks, every other day for 20 days, twice weekly for 6 weeks, or at various frequencies for between 8 days and 10 weeks, with benefits lasting up to 9 months. However, methods, interventions, blinding, and statistics are highly variable between the studies ( 16187 , 98392 , 103527 , 103534 , 111196 , 111199 ). Some preliminary clinical evidence shows that 5 sessions weekly for 3 weeks is more effective for pain reduction than only 2 weeks. However, it does not seem to be more effective for improving function or physical activity ( 103534 ). When used in addition to physiotherapy involving exercises, balneotherapy increased physical function and stiffness, but not pain, more than physiotherapy alone. These benefits lasted for up to one month after treatment ( 103527 ). Balneotherapy has also been studied in patients with hand osteoarthritis. Overall, most research shows that adding balneotherapy to usual treatment reduces pain in patients with hand osteoarthritis when compared with usual treatment alone or a control treatment such as warm tap water baths. Balneotherapy might also improve grip strength, but conflicting results exist. In clinical research, balneotherapy mineral baths are heated and given for 15-20 minutes 5-6 times weekly over 12 days to 3 weeks ( 98384 , 98386 , 98391 , 111199 ). A clinical study in patients with primary hand osteoarthritis shows that heat, provided by paraffin baths or as dry heat by convection (fluidotherapy with a Chattanooga FLU115 device) used for 20 minutes daily for 2 weeks, improves pain scores, activities of daily living scores, hand grip strength, and quality of life ( 111198 ). Additional preliminary clinical studies show that balneotherapy added to exercise or used alone can reduce pain and improve quality of life in patients with general osteoarthritis ( 16186 , 98384 , 98390 ). Overall, a greater proportion of patients (76% to 55%) seem to achieve minimal clinically important improvement in osteoarthritis severity when balneotherapy mineral baths, mudpacks, and hydro-jets  are added to exercise when compared with exercise alone (40% to 36%) ( 98385 , 98390 ). In patients with spine osteoarthritis, spa therapy involving balneotherapy with heated mineral water, massage, and exercise sessions given 5 days a week for 3 weeks showed longer-lasting improvements in pain, functional efficiency, and quality of life than massage and exercise therapy given at an outpatient physiotherapy clinic ( 111201 ). Limited research has compared balneotherapy to drug therapy. One preliminary clinical study in geriatric patients with generalized osteoarthritis suggests that outpatient or inpatient spa therapy including balneotherapy for 2 weeks improves pain, anxiety, and quality of life compared to taking etodolac 300 mg twice daily for 2 weeks. Spa therapy included heated mineral-water baths, jet-showers, hot mud packs, and transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation applied to painful joints ( 98394 ). The effects of balneotherapy alone are unclear from this study.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Ankylosing spondylitis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that balneotherapy with a mineral bath plus an exercise program reduces pain and improves functionality when compared with an exercise program alone. However, most of these benefits were only significant at the end of treatment, but not at 6 months after treatment ( 16190 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that balneotherapy, with or without non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), reduces symptoms more than NSAIDs alone. These benefits were significant at the end of treatment and at 6 months after treatment ( 16192 ).

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

Preliminary clinical research in children aged 1-14 years with mild to moderate atopic dermatitis shows that balneotherapy once or twice daily for 2 weeks reduces the severity of disease by 26% from baseline. Topical corticosteroids were more effective, reducing disease severity by 46%. However, using other scoring methods, balneotherapy was as effective as the corticosteroids. At 4 months, the number and duration of relapses were reduced in patients given balneotherapy ( 103540 ). Preliminary clinical research in adults with chronic hand atopic dermatitis shows that coating the hands in a bath of melted paraffin wax and keeping the coating in place for 15 to 20 minutes once daily on 5 days per week for 12 weeks, in addition to routine skin care, reduces the severity of atopic dermatitis assessed with the Scoring Atopic Dermatitis (SCORAD) scale by 28.6% when compared with baseline, while routine skin care alone reduces it by 0.4%. Quality of life, measured with the Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI), improves by 60% and 4% respectively ( 111202 ).

Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI).

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that balneotherapy moderately improves pain and quality of life when compared with no treatment in patients with CVI. However, there was no effect on disease severity, leg ulcers, or swelling. Any benefit of balneotherapy for CVI is unclear from this meta-analysis due to the availability of only 1-2 low- to moderate-quality studies for each endpoint ( 103528 ).

Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD).

Preliminary clinical research shows that daily balneotherapy for 8 weeks reduces the severity of anxiety when compared with paroxetine 20-50 mg daily in patients with GAD. Remission occurred in 19% of patients receiving balneotherapy, compared with 7% of those taking paroxetine. About 50% of patients using balneotherapy had at least a 50% reduction in anxiety severity, compared with 28% of those taking paroxetine ( 103541 ).

Neck pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic neck pain shows that 15 sessions each of balneotherapy and physical therapy with a hot pack, ultrasound, and transcutaneous electrical stimulation (TENS), reduces pain by a small amount more than physical therapy alone. These benefits last for at least 3 weeks after cessation of treatment. Disability was also improved 3 weeks after treatment ( 103538 ).

Psoriasis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that Leopoldine spa water, which is hypotonic water rich in sulfate, reduces the area of the skin affected by psoriasis when compared with distilled water ( 16195 ). Preliminary clinical research in adults with chronic plaque psoriasis shows that a standardized 3-week course of spa therapy, including balneotherapy, improves the Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI) to a score of less than 10 in 66% of patients, compared with 42% of patients receiving standard care without spa treatment. However, it does not improve pain or stress scores, or overall quality of life ( 111200 ).

Psoriatic arthritis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that balneotherapy in the Dead Sea mineral baths reduces tender points and active joints in patients with fibromyalgia and psoriatic arthritis when compared to baseline ( 16185 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Research regarding the effects of balneotherapy in patients with RA is conflicting. One small clinical trial in patients with RA who are receiving standard therapy, shows that giving daily 20-minute warm saline baths 6 days weekly for 2 weeks, seems to improve pain, tender and swollen joints, and disease activity when compared with standard therapy alone. However, these benefits were not maintained at 3 months or later ( 98387 , 98388 ). Other balneotherapy forms such as application of mud packs do not seem to be beneficial. Preliminary research shows that, when compared with placebo control, balneotherapy mudpacks do not reduce pain or swollen joints in RA ( 98395 ).

Balneotherapy has also been compared to active control treatments. Preliminary research shows that seated immersion balneotherapy was no different for improving pain and function in RA when compared to water exercises or relaxation therapy. Additionally, balneotherapy seems to be inferior to treatment with cyclosporine for improving pain and swollen joints in RA ( 98395 ).

Shoulder pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that balneotherapy reduces pain and disability related to chronic shoulder pain due to various etiologies, including biceps tendinitis, impingement syndrome, bursitis, or rotator cuff syndrome ( 103531 , 103536 , 103539 ). Preliminary clinical research shows that, when used in combination with usual treatments, balneotherapy with supervised mobilization 6 days a week for 3 weeks results in a minimal clinically important improvement in 59% of patients at 6 months, compared with 18% of those using usual treatments while waiting for balneotherapy ( 103531 ). In addition, 3-4 of weeks of treatment with both balneotherapy and physical therapy modestly improves pain and disability and most measures of quality of life when compared with physical therapy alone, immediately and one month after treatment ( 103536 , 103539 ). Physiotherapy included transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) with exercise, and possibly hot packs ( 103536 , 103539 ). Some improvement in quality of life also occurred in these studies ( 103531 , 103536 , 103539 ).

In patients with shoulder pain related to subacute supraspinatus tendinopathy, preliminary clinical research shows that treatment with balneotherapy five times weekly for 3 weeks, in addition to physical therapy with transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS), hot pack, ultrasound, and exercise, improves pain, function, and quality of life by a small amount when compared to physical therapy.

More evidence is needed to rate balneotherapy for these uses.

Balneotherapy is a broad term that means "bath therapy." The term balneotherapy is often used to describe many activities involving mineral "spa" treatments, including therapeutic water baths, mineral baths, baths containing natural gases (carbon dioxide, hydrogen sulphide and radon), mud baths, packs, or medical peloids such as peat or fango of volcanic origin ( 16193 , 98395 , 103528 , 103531 , 103532 ). Minerals can include sulfur, sodium, bicarbonate, magnesium, calcium, potassium, iron, strontium, and many others. Mineral baths can also be described as isotonic, hypotonic, or hypertonic ( 16185 , 16186 , 16194 , 98392 , 103534 , 103535 , 103537 , 103541 ). The temperature of the mineral water in the bath usually ranges between 31°C and 40°C, but can sometimes be as high as 45°C or as low as 27°C ( 98392 , 98395 , 103526 , 103535 , 103536 , 103537 , 103538 , 103540 ). Although cold balneotherapy exists, it does not seem to be used as commonly as warm and hot balneotherapy. Balneotherapy baths might be described as cold (less than 20°C), hypothermal (less than 35°C), thermal (35-36°C), or hyperthermal (more than 36°C or 40°C) ( 16194 , 103528 , 103538 ). Balneotherapy might involve standing, sitting, or walking in the water, an underwater massage or waterpool bath, a shower, intermittent temperature changes, and/or exercise ( 103528 , 103541 ). Balneotherapy is usually administered 5-7 days per week for a duration of 3 weeks. Each session lasts for 20-30 minutes ( 103526 , 103528 , 103534 , 103535 , 103537 , 103538 , 103539 , 103540 ). Spa therapy is the broadest term and refers to a general soothing atmosphere that provides numerous therapeutic approaches, often including both hydrotherapy and balneotherapy ( 16193 ). Hydrotherapy utilizes simple water, rather than thermal mineral water.

#### General

Balneotherapy is thought to have several therapeutic effects including reducing markers of inflammation and immunomodulation and reducing formation of free radicals ( 16188 , 16189 , 16195 , 98395 , 103532 , 103535 , 103540 ). In addition, balneotherapy might increase the production of endorphins ( 103535 ). Some believe that the benefits of balneotherapy are due to the absorption of bath minerals through the skin; however, evidence is limited ( 16193 , 16194 , 111196 ). Some minerals such as sulfur might be absorbed through the skin and result in reduced inflammation ( 16193 ). Effects of thermal baths may be due, at least in part, to heat ( 111196 ). The hydrostatic and physical effect of the water might reduce peripheral resistance of blood flow, increasing muscle perfusion. This is thought to increase healing and reduce muscle spasms ( 103535 ). According to Archimedes' principle, hydrostatic force of warm water alleviates pain by reducing the load and impact of gravity on painful and rheumatic joints. The heat and buoyancy of water might also act on thermal receptors and mechanoreceptors reducing the sensation of pain. Finally, the spa environment can have psychological benefits such as mental relaxation, which might contribute to pain relief ( 98395 , 103535 ).

#### Effects on bone

An analysis of three studies of balneotherapy in mainly postmenopausal women reported a reduction in parathyroid hormone levels, suggesting a slowing of bone resorption. There were conflicting results about the effects on bone formation ( 108285 ).
