## Overview

Miracle fruit is an evergreen tropical shrub that is native to West and Central Africa, where it is used in traditional medicine. The berry, seed oil and leaf are used medicinally, and the berry is used as food ( 15654 , 102330 ).

## History

Miracle fruit is used as part of traditional medicine in parts of West Africa ( 102330 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, miracle fruit berry is used for obesity and chemotherapy-related taste disturbances. The berry and leaf are used for diabetes. Topically, miracle fruit seed oil is used for motor skills. In foods, miracle fruit is used as a low-calorie sweetener.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of miracle fruit. The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) has determined that the acceptable intake of the pitted and dried pulp and skin of miracle fruit is 10 mg/kg daily, or 0.7 grams daily for adults ( 106574 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

None reported; however, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted. Potential for cross-allergenicity exists ( 106574 ).

#### Immunologic

Cross-allergenicity with miracle fruit exists with peanut allergens and might also exist with latex, peach, and soy allergens ( 106574 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Cancer-related anorexia.

Preliminary clinical research in malnourished patients undergoing cancer treatment for at least 3 months shows that dissolving tablets containing 150 mg miracle fruit, equivalent to miraculin 1.4 mg, on the tongue 5 minutes before breakfast, lunch, and dinner for 3 months improves taste perception, total calorie intake, fat-free mass, weight gain, and nutritional status when compared with placebo ( 114042 , 114043 ).

More evidence is needed to rate miracle fruit for this and other uses.

#### Adult
Oral:

Research is limited; typical dosing is unavailable.

#### Standardization & Formulation

The lyophilized pulp and skin of pitted miracle fruit contains the glycoprotein miraculin in amounts up to 2.5% (w/w) ( 106574 ).

#### ANTIDIABETES DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Theoretically, miracle fruit leaf might have an additive effect with antidiabetes drugs and increase the risk of hypoglycemia. Evidence from an animal model shows that miracle fruit leaf increases insulin production and lowers fasting blood glucose and HbA1c in rats with streptozotocin-induced diabetes ( 102330 ).

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOGLYCEMIC POTENTIAL

Miracle fruit leaf might increase insulin production and lower fasting blood glucose and HbA1c ( 102330 ). Theoretically, concomitant use might have additive effects when used with other herbs and supplements with hypoglycemic potential. These include devil's claw, fenugreek, guar gum, Panax ginseng, Siberian ginseng, and others.

#### CROSS-ALLERGENCITY

Miracle fruit contains the glycoprotein miraculin. Glycoproteins are often allergenic. Preliminary research suggests that cross-allergenicity exists with peanut allergens and might also exist with latex, peach, and soy allergens ( 106574 ).

None known.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the presentation or treatment of overdose with miracle fruit.

#### Metabolism

In vitro, the constituent miraculin is degraded by pepsin and trypsin ( 106574 ).

#### General

The applicable parts of miracle fruit are the berry, seed oil, and leaf ( 98005 , 102330 ). The lipid fraction of the seed oil contains polyphenols, triterpenes, phytosterols, and linoleic acid ( 98005 ). The leaf contains quercitrin, maltose, proline, quinic acid, gallic acid, xanthurenic acid, gallocatechin, neochlorogenic acid, tryptophan, epigallocatechin, 5-p-coumaroylquinic acid, epicatechin, 1,3-diphenylguanidine, myricetin, myricitrin, kaempferol, and afzelin ( 102330 , 114040 ). An ethanolic extract of miracle fruit contains greater flavonoid and tannin content, but similar phenolic content, when compared with an aqueous extract ( 106573 ).

#### Antidiabetes effects

The leaves of the miracle fruit plant are traditionally used for diabetes. In one animal model of diabetes, miracle fruit leaf extract reduces levels of fasting blood glucose and glycated hemoglobin ( 102330 ). However, in a mouse model of diabetes, high doses of an ethanolic extract of miracle fruit produces only modest reductions in blood glucose ( 106573 ). Potential mechanisms include an increase in insulin synthesis, a reduction of inflammatory cytokines as shown in the animal model, and/or the inhibition of the carbohydrate metabolizing enzymes alpha-amylase or alpha-glucosidase as shown in vitro ( 102330 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

In vitro, an ethanolic extract of miracle fruit has greater antioxidant activity when compared with an aqueous extract, with quercetin being the most abundant antioxidant present ( 106573 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

In a mouse model of diabetes, high doses of an ethanolic extract of miracle fruit reduce total cholesterol levels when compared with control ( 106573 ). In hamsters receiving a high cholesterol diet, aqueous and ethanolic extracts of miracle fruit leaf and seed, but not pulp, reduce levels of total cholesterol, triglycerides, and non-high-density lipoprotein (non-HDL) cholesterol. An ethanolic extract of miracle fruit seed has the most potent effect, which may be related in part to two major triterpenoids, lupeol acetate and beta-amyrin acetate ( 106572 ).

#### Lipid effects

An ethyl acetate extract of miracle fruit leaf has antihyperlipidemic activity in zebrafish, due to the constituent quercitrin ( 114040 ).

#### Musculoskeletal effects

There is interest in using miracle fruit to improve motor function. One small clinical study in healthy volunteers shows that applying a wrist band containing miracle fruit seed oil for 3-8 hours per day for 4-6 weeks improves hand motor skills more than a placebo wrist band ( 98005 ). The mechanism of this effect is unclear.

#### Taste effects

Miracle fruit contains the 24.6 kDa tetrameric glycoprotein miraculin that acts on the taste buds on the tongue to block sour taste receptor sites and convert sour tastes into sweet ( 15651 , 15652 , 15653 , 114040 ). It activates sweet taste receptors at an acidic pH of 3-6 and must coat the taste buds before sour-tasting foods are consumed. It is denatured by heat, high pH (>12) and low pH (<2). The effect of miraculin on taste buds lasts between 30 minutes and 2 hours ( 15652 , 114041 ). Preliminary clinical research in adults with diabetes or prediabetes shows that dissolving a tablet containing miracle fruit extract 400 mg on the tongue 10 minutes prior to intake of sour foods such as green apples, pickles, or plain yogurt increases acceptability of the foods when compared with placebo ( 114041 ).

#### Weight loss effects

In a mouse model of diabetes, high doses of an ethanolic extract of miracle fruit attenuate diabetes-induced increases in weight ( 106573 ). In humans, dissolving a tablet containing miracle fruit extract 400 mg on the tongue 10 minutes before meals reduces caloric intake, possibly by reducing use of added sugar ( 114041 ).
