## Overview

Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension, also known as the DASH diet, is an eating plan designed to reduce blood pressure. The DASH diet encourages individuals to increase the consumption of vegetables, fruits, and low-fat dairy products while decreasing the consumption of total fat, saturated fat, cholesterol, and sodium ( 15976 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. The DASH diet has been safely used in several clinical trials ( 15976 , 15977 , 15978 , 15982 , 94501 ). The diet focuses on lowering, but not completely restricting, salt intake.

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when used appropriately, short-term. The DASH diet or a modified version of the DASH diet has been used with apparent safety in children at least 6 years of age for 10-12 weeks ( 108776 , 108777 ).

PREGNANCY: Possibly Safe when used appropriately, short-term. DASH diets have been used with apparent safety during pregnancy for 4-12 weeks ( 104784 ). A modified DASH diet called MAMA-DASH, with adequate nutrition and calories for pregnancy, has been used for 20 weeks without reports of adverse events ( 101693 ).

LACTATION: Possibly Safe when used appropriately while breastfeeding for up to 6 months. The DASH diet is a balanced diet that is not expected to be harmful during breastfeeding. Observational research in exclusively breastfed infants has found that high adherence to the DASH diet for 4-6 months while breastfeeding is not associated with changes in infant weight, height, or head circumference when compared with low adherence to the DASH diet ( 109953 ).

#### General

The DASH diet is well tolerated. It has been used in several clinical trials without reports of adverse effects.

Likely Effective
Hypertension.

Following the DASH diet lowers systolic blood pressure by about 5-11 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by about 3-6 mmHg in patients with hypertension.

In 2017, the American College of Cardiology and the American Heart Association (ACC/AHA) published guidelines recommending that the DASH diet be considered as one of several lifestyle modifications to help manage high blood pressure in patients with hypertension ( 95680 ). Clinical research shows that following the DASH diet lowers systolic blood pressure by about 5-11 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by about 3-6 mmHg in patients with hypertension, with improvements seen after 1 week ( 15976 , 15977 , 15978 , 15981 , 93590 , 93591 , 93593 , 93596 , 94501 , 94503 )( 95577 , 95681 , 95682 , 98327 , 108372 , 108779 , 108780 , 109246 ). The DASH diet has also been shown to reduce blood pressure in specific types of hypertension, such as isolated systolic hypertension. Some clinical research shows that following the DASH diet lowers blood pressure in patients with stage 1 isolated systolic hypertension after 8 weeks ( 15980 ). The DASH diet seems to be more effective for lowering blood pressure in Black patients when compared with White patients ( 15976 , 95680 , 108372 ). The antihypertensive effects of the DASH diet might also be more evident in older patients ( 108372 ). Additionally, combining the DASH diet with losartan 50 mg daily results in additive blood pressure reduction ( 15982 ). The DASH diet has also been studied in combination with other diets or weight management approaches. Combining the DASH diet with weight management is more effective than either treatment alone ( 93590 , 95680 ). Also, combining the DASH diet with a sodium-reduced diet is more effective at reducing blood pressure than either diet alone ( 15978 , 15981 , 95577 , 95680 , 98325 , 112718 ). However, one large clinical trial in patients with higher normal blood pressures or grade 1 hypertension shows that the Mediterranean diet lowers systolic blood pressure by about 3 mmHg more than the DASH diet when both are combined with salt restriction, but both diets lower the odds of hypertension and improve ambulatory blood pressure when compared with salt restriction alone during a 3-month intensive dietary intervention ( 112718 ).

Possibly Effective
Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

Clinical and observational research suggests that following a DASH-style diet may be associated with a reduced risk of CVD.

Meta-analyses of observational studies have found that higher adherence to a DASH-style diet for 7-24 years is associated with a 20% reduction in the risk of CVD, an 18% to 21% reduction in the risk of coronary artery disease, a 19% reduction in the risk of stroke, and a 29% reduction in the risk of heart failure when compared with lower adherence ( 93599 , 101694 ). Also, a clinical trial in adults 22-75 years of age without CVD shows that following the DASH diet for 8 weeks reduces estimated 10-year atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) risk by around 10% when compared with a control diet ( 109954 ).

Colorectal cancer.

Observational research has found that following a DASH diet may be associated with a reduced risk of colorectal cancer.

A meta-analysis of 8 observational studies in adults has found that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with a 20% lower risk of colorectal cancer when compared with lower adherence. A subgroup analysis has found that these benefits extend to both males and females ( 104782 ). However, at least one individual clinical study has suggested that the DASH diet may not affect the risk of colorectal cancer in females specifically ( 101691 ).

Diabetes.

Following the DASH diet may be beneficial for the management of diabetes. The DASH diet may also be beneficial for reducing the risk of gestational and type 2 diabetes.

An observational study has found that high adherence to the DASH diet for 1 year is associated with 69% lower odds of developing type 2 diabetes when compared to lower adherence in White, but not Black or Hispanic, adults ( 93595 ). Also, a small clinical study in postmenopausal patients with type 2 diabetes shows that following the DASH diet for 12 weeks reduces glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by around 0.5%, reduces fasting blood glucose by around 26 mg/dL, and reduces body weight by around 2.7 kg when compared with a control diet. Significant improvements in sleep quality, sleep duration, and symptoms of anxiety, depression, and stress were also reported with the DASH diet over the control diet ( 109952 ). While the evidence is limited, the DASH diet is generally recognized as a healthy lifestyle choice in patients with or at risk of diabetes. The American Association of Clinical Endocrinologists and American College of Endocrinology recommends the DASH diet in patients with type 2 diabetes without kidney insufficiency as part of a healthy lifestyle to help reduce blood pressure ( 98323 ). A meta-analysis of several small randomized controlled studies in pregnant adults with gestational diabetes, obesity, and/or hypertension shows that following the DASH diet for 4 weeks reduces average fasting blood glucose by an average of 6 mg/dL when compared with a control diet ( 104784 ). One of the included clinical studies also shows that following the DASH diet for 4 weeks reduces the percentage of patients with gestational diabetes requiring insulin therapy by 50% when compared with a control diet ( 93589 ). Population research also suggests that following the DASH diet is associated with a lower risk of gestational diabetes ( 98327 ).

Hyperlipidemia.

Following the DASH diet seems to reduce low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and total cholesterol in patients with hyperlipidemia or other metabolic risk factors.

Some clinical research in patients with hyperlipidemia or other metabolic risk factors shows that following the DASH diet significantly reduces LDL and total cholesterol levels ( 15979 , 94503 , 108779 ). However, most clinical research has shown no effect of the DASH diet on high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels ( 94503 , 108779 ).

Metabolic syndrome.

Following the DASH diet with sodium restriction seems to reduce risk factors for metabolic syndrome.

A clinical study in patients with metabolic syndrome shows that following the DASH diet with sodium restriction for 6 months increases high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels and reduces triglycerides, blood pressure, and fasting blood glucose levels ( 15983 ).

Obesity.

Following the DASH diet seems to reduce weight in patients with obesity.

Meta-analyses of clinical studies show that following the DASH diet can reduce body weight by about 3 pounds when compared with a control diet ( 94504 , 108779 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the fact that the analyses did not assess overweight and obese patients separately from patients with normal weight at baseline. An individual clinical study in patients with obesity and hypertension shows that following the DASH diet for 3 months increases weight loss by a greater magnitude, with a reduction of 8 pounds, when compared with no nutritional intervention ( 98327 ). The DASH diet has also been evaluated for weight loss in adults with overweight and obesity who are pregnant. A clinical study in those at less than 16 weeks' gestation shows that following a modified DASH diet, called MAMA-DASH, for 20 weeks and participating in physical activity attenuates gestational weight gain when compared with no intervention ( 101693 ). It is unclear if the reduced weight gain occurred due to the DASH diet, physical activity, or the combination.

Overall mortality.

Observational research has found that following the DASH diet seems to be associated with a reduced risk of mortality.

A meta-analysis of 17 observational studies in generally healthy adults has found that higher adherence to the DASH diet reduces the risk of mortality from any cause when compared with lower adherence. Subgroup analyses have found a reduction in cardiovascular-, stroke-, and cancer-related mortality. The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies ( 104780 ). An individual observational study in healthy middle-aged adults has also found that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with a 6% to 11% lower risk of overall mortality when compared with lower adherence ( 98329 ).

Pre-eclampsia.

Following the DASH diet seems to reduce the risk of pre-eclampsia.

A meta-analysis of several small randomized controlled trials in pregnant patients with gestational diabetes, obesity, and/or hypertension shows that following the DASH diet for 4 weeks reduces the risk of pre-eclampsia by 33% when compared with a control diet ( 104784 ). Also, an observational study in pregnant patients has found that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with 39% to 51% lower odds of developing pre-eclampsia when compared with lower adherence ( 104781 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

It is unclear if following the DASH diet is beneficial in children with ADHD.

A clinical study in males aged 6-12 years who were recently diagnosed with ADHD and not receiving medication or behavioral therapy shows that following the DASH diet for 12 weeks improves the severity of ADHD symptoms according to certain scales (abbreviated Conner's scale, strength and difficulties questionnaire) but not others (Swanson, Nolan and Pelham [SNAP-IV]), when compared with a control diet ( 108776 ).

Biliary stones.

It is unclear if following the DASH diet is beneficial for patients with biliary stones.

A case-control study in Iranian adults with gallstone disease suggests that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with 64% lower odds of pancreatic steatosis when compared with low adherence to the DASH diet ( 115633 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the possibility of residual confounding factors.

Bladder cancer.

It is unclear if following the DASH diet is beneficial for the prevention of bladder cancer.

Observational research in Iranian adults has found that high adherence to the DASH diet is associated with 84% lower odds of developing bladder cancer when compared with low adherence to the DASH diet ( 109650 ).

Cirrhosis.

It is unclear if following the DASH diet is beneficial for patients with liver cirrhosis.

An observational study in Iranian adults with liver cirrhosis suggests that higher adherence to the DASH diet, determined on a baseline assessment, is associated with a greater 4-year survival rate, with an absolute difference of 14% compared to lower adherence ( 115634 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the possibility of residual confounding factors.

Depression.

Observational research has found that following the DASH diet seems to be associated with a reduced risk of depression.

An observational study in adults with hypertension has found that moderate adherence to the DASH diet for approximately 8 years is associated with a 24% to 37% reduced risk of developing depression when compared with low adherence ( 93597 ). Additionally, a cross-sectional clinical study in adolescent females has found that high adherence to the DASH diet is associated with a 53% lower odds of depression when compared with low adherence ( 98326 ).

Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis).

It is unclear if following the DASH diet is beneficial in patients with kidney stones.

A cross-sectional clinical study in males with a history of nephrolithiasis has found that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with lower odds of having urinary risk factors for nephrolithiasis, such as hypercalciuria and hyperuricosuria, when compared with lower adherence ( 104783 ). It is unclear whether this reduces the risk for kidney stone recurrence.

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if following the DASH diet is beneficial for reducing the frequency or severity of migraine headaches.

A small clinical study in normotensive females aged 20-50 years with migraine shows that following the DASH diet for 12 weeks reduces the frequency of migraines by 3 attacks monthly and the severity of pain scores by almost 2 points on a 10-point scale, compared with reductions of 1.4 attacks and less than 1 point, respectively, in a control group receiving usual dietary advice ( 108774 ). Some changes in oxidative stress parameters, which have been implicated in the pathogenesis of migraine, have also been observed ( 108775 ). Duration of migraine attack and overall quality of life, as measured by the headache impact test, were not affected ( 108774 ).

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

It is unclear if the DASH diet is beneficial in patients with NAFLD.

A small clinical study in patients with NAFLD and overweight or obesity shows that following a calorie-restricted DASH diet for 8 weeks reduces body weight by about 4 kg and alanine aminotransferase levels by 8 IU/L, compared with a reduction of 2 kg and an increase of 4 IU/L, respectively, with a calorie-restricted control diet ( 93598 ).

Parkinson disease.

It is unclear if the DASH diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of Parkinson disease.

A cross-sectional study in Iranian adults suggests that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with 78% to 86% lower odds of Parkinson disease when compared with lower adherence ( 115635 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the possibility of residual confounding factors.

Prostate cancer.

It is unclear if following the DASH diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of developing highly aggressive prostate cancer.

An observational study has found that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with 35% lower odds of highly aggressive prostate cancer when compared with lower adherence ( 101692 ).

Stroke.

Observational research has found that following the DASH diet is associated with a modestly reduced risk of stroke.

A meta-analysis of observational studies has found that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with a 12% reduced risk of stroke when compared with lower adherence. This benefit is more apparent in Asian populations when compared with Western populations ( 98322 ).

Abdominal aortic aneurysm . It is unclear if following the DASH diet is associated with a reduced risk of abdominal aortic aneurysm.

An observational study has found that higher adherence to the DASH diet is associated with up to a 40% lower risk of developing abdominal aortic aneurism when compared with lower adherence ( 101689 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the DASH diet for these uses.

The DASH diet is a special diet used to help lower blood pressure. It focuses on including lots of fruits and vegetables, grains, and a low amount of fat. Specifically, for a 2000 calorie diet, the DASH diet suggests 7-8 servings of grains, 4-5 servings of fruits, 4-5 servings of vegetables, 2-3 servings of low-fat dairy products, and 2 or less servings of lean meats. Snacks and sweets are limited to 5 per week ( 15976 ). The DASH diet does not restrict salt intake. However, combining it with a salt-restricted diet can improve blood pressure lowering ( 15978 , 15981 ).

The DASH diet is a special diet used to help lower blood pressure. Over time, uncontrolled high blood pressure may result in serious health conditions such as heart disease, stroke, and kidney disease. The main purpose of the DASH diet is to help control high blood pressure and to prevent the development of disease. DASH stands for "dietary approaches to stop hypertension." It focuses on including lots of fruits and vegetables, grains, and a low amount of fat. Specifically, for a 2000 calorie diet, the DASH diet suggests 7-8 servings of grains, 4-5 servings of fruits, 4-5 servings of vegetables, 2-3 servings of low-fat dairy products, and 2 or less servings of lean meats. Snacks and sweets are limited to 5 per week ( 15976 ). Proponents state that the DASH diet may promote more adherence than other diets because it focuses on the foods that are permitted, rather than the restriction of foods. The DASH diet does not specifically restrict salt intake; however, combining it with a salt-restricted diet can improve blood pressure lowering ( 15978 , 15981 ). In addition to the blood pressure reductions seen in patients with hypertension, research in normotensive adults suggests that following the DASH diet can lower systolic blood pressure by about 3 mmHg ( 95681 , 95682 ). The DASH diet has also been considered for gout. Clinical research shows that following the DASH diet for 30 days lowers uric acid levels. Greater decreases in uric acid were associated with higher sodium intakes and also higher baseline uric acid levels ( 93594 ).
