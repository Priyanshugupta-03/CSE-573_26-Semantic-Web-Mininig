## Overview

The maple syrup diet is promoted as a detoxification diet that can result in rapid weight loss. The diet involves drinking a mixture of maple syrup, lemon juice, spring water, and cayenne pepper for 7-10 days ( 103140 ). KEY HIGHLIGHTS

- Sometimes used for obesity, but there is no strong evidence to support its use for any condition.
- Possibly unsafe due to the risk for electrolyte imbalances and malnutrition.

## History

Stanley Burroughs originally created the maple syrup diet in the 1950s. His book, The Master Cleanser, described the process as a detoxification plan aimed at clearing the body of toxins and internal waste, which he claims build up from an unhealthy lifestyle ( 103141 ).

## People Use This For

The maple syrup diet is used for obesity, detoxification, and to improve overall health.

Possibly Unsafe when followed as directed. The maple syrup diet is not intended to provide adequate nutrition and involves the consumption of large amounts of fluid. This can result in electrolyte imbalances when used short-term, as well as malnutrition when used long-term ( 103181 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Unsafe during pregnancy and lactation. The maple syrup diet is not intended to provide adequate nutrition and can result in electrolyte imbalances when used short-term, as well as malnutrition when used long-term ( 103181 ).

#### General

There is limited reliable information available about the adverse effects of the maple syrup diet. However, the maple syrup diet does not provide adequate nutrition and involves the consumption of large quantities of water. This can lead to electrolyte imbalances when used short-term and malnutrition when used long-term ( 103181 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Obesity.

Clinical research in overweight Korean women shows that following the maple syrup diet for 7 days improves body mass index, body fat percentage, and waist-hip ratio when compared with a control group following a normal diet. Women who followed the maple syrup diet using a lemon-flavored liquid in place of lemon juice experienced similar improvements to those using lemon juice. This suggests that any benefit from this diet is likely related to its severe calorie restriction ( 103140 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the maple syrup diet for this use.

The maple syrup diet requires adherents to drink a mixture of maple syrup, lemon juice, spring water, and cayenne pepper for 7-10 days ( 103140 ). There are different variations of the diet in terms of how much daily food intake is replaced by the maple syrup mixture. The "full detox" is most common and requires dieters to drink only the maple syrup mixture without eating any food for 10 days. The "master plan" requires adherents to follow the "full detox" twice a year, as well as once a week ( 103141 ). The maple syrup diet drink is made by mixing two tablespoons of fresh lemon juice, two tablespoons of maple syrup, 1/10 of a teaspoon of cayenne pepper, and eight ounces of spring water. Two tablespoons of lime juice may be used instead of lemon juice and the lemon or lime skin and pulp can be blended with the drink ( 103141 ). Drinking 6-10 glasses of the lemonade mixture daily is recommended. Dieters are also recommended to drink more when experiencing hunger or a lack of energy. While performing the diet, drinking water is highly encouraged ( 103140 , 103141 ). After the diet is complete, a transition period is recommended, in which standard foods are slowly incorporated into the diet over the course of 3-4 days ( 103140 ). Many sources stress the importance of healthy eating and limiting calorie intake to avoid gaining back the weight lost during the dieting period.

Although the maple syrup diet claims that weight loss is due to the consumption of maple syrup, lemon juice, and cayenne pepper, it is likely that any weight loss obtained while on this diet is due to severe calorie restriction ( 103140 ). Proponents of the maple syrup diet also claim that maple syrup has beneficial health effects due to its unique constituents. The main constituents of maple syrup are sucrose and water. It also contains minor concentrations of glucose, fructose, polysaccharides, minerals, vitamins, amino acids, and organic acids. Maple syrup also contains a variety of polyphenols, including phenolic acids, coumarin derivatives, stilbenes, lignans, flavonoids, and quebecol ( 103139 ). Any evidence for health benefits associated with maple syrup are limited to preclinical research. Animal research shows that maple syrup extract can reduce low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol levels in mice. It can also upregulate hepatic expression of genes related to fatty acid degradation, cholesterol catabolism, and stress responses ( 103138 , 103139 ). Advocates of the maple syrup diet also claim that the diet helps the body get rid of toxins. However, there is no scientific evidence to support this claim.
