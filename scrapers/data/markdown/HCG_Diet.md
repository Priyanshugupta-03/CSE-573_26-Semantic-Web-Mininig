## Overview

The HCG diet involves a very low-calorie diet and administration of human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) and is intended to induce weight loss and redistribute fat. HCG is a hormone produced during pregnancy. HCG is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for use in underdeveloped testes in young males and ovulation induction in females, but not for weight loss ( 92916 ).

HCG weight-loss products are often marketed over-the-counter and identified as "homeopathic." The FDA has not approved HCG for this use and warns consumers to avoid HCG diet products. Despite this warning, HCG diets continue to be marketed on the internet and at retailers and weight loss clinics ( 92916 ).

Possibly Safe when used appropriately, short-term. Some clinical research shows that the HCG diet can be safely used under close medical supervision. However, because it utilizes the prescription drug HCG and a very low-calorie diet, it should only be used under the supervision and monitoring of a healthcare professional ( 17043 , 17045 , 17046 , 17047 , 17048 , 17049 , 17050 ).

PREGNANCY: Likely Unsafe when used by injection. HCG, which is a component of the HCG diet, is teratogenic. It has been linked to a high rate of congenital abnormalities in animal models ( 17042 ). Do not use HCG or follow the HCG diet while pregnant.

LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

The HCG diet seems to be generally well tolerated when used under medical supervision.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Depression, edema, fatigue, gynecomastia, headache, irritability, restlessness.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Ascites, hypogonadism (in males), ovarian cysts, pleural effusion, testicular tumors, thromboembolism.

#### Cardiovascular

The HCG diet can cause edema, thromboembolism, ascites, and pleural effusion ( 17042 , 17301 ). In a case report, a 64-year-old female presented to the emergency room with a deep venous thrombosis and a pulmonary embolism after taking 20 drops of HCG sublingually twice daily for 2 weeks. The patient was treated with anticoagulants and started on chronic warfarin therapy ( 92915 ).

#### Genitourinary

The HCG diet can cause testicular tumors and hypogonadism in males, enlargement and rupture of ovarian cysts in females, and gynecomastia in both males and females ( 17042 , 17301 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

The HCG diet can cause headache, fatigue, irritability, restlessness, and depression ( 17042 , 17301 ).

#### Other

The HCG diet can cause refeeding syndrome. In one case report, a patient developed refeeding syndrome, including severe muscle weakness, nausea, hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, elevated creatine kinase, and ST-segment depression with marked U waves, after taking HCG 1000 IU subcutaneous daily and restricting food consumption to less than 500 calories per day for 3 weeks ( 109513 ).

Obesity.

Clinical studies show that the HCG diet does not improve weight loss in patients with obesity.

Most clinical trials evaluating the use of the HCG diet are low quality. The highest quality studies consistently show that using an HCG diet, including HCG (125-250 IU daily) with significant calorie restrictions (500-1000 calories per day) for 3-8 weeks does not redistribute fat or reduce body weight when compared with a very low-calorie diet alone ( 17045 , 17046 , 17047 , 17048 , 17049 , 17050 ). Although very low-calorie diets have demonstrated weight loss, short-term ( 17043 , 17044 ), HCG does not appear to provide additional benefits.

---

The HCG diet consists of a very low-calorie diet of 500-800 calories per day in conjunction with administration of HCG. HCG may be administered orally, sublingually, or by intramuscular injection. For intramuscular injection, proponents of the HCG diet recommend 125-200 IU daily ( 92916 ).

None known.

None known.

#### PROSTATE CANCER

Theoretically, the HCG diet might exacerbate prostate and other androgen-mediated cancers. HCG stimulates production of androgens ( 17042 ). This may worsen androgen-mediated cancers.

|

#### HUMAN CHORIONIC GONADOTROPIN (HCG)

The HCG diet can cause false-positives on pregnancy and other gonadotropin tests. HCG can cross-react with radioimmunoassays for gonadotropins, including HCG ( 17042 ). HCG screening tests are often used to detect early pregnancy.

#### LUTEINIZING HORMONE (LH)

The HCG diet can cause false-positives on LH tests. HCG has endocrine action similar to LH. HCG can cross-react with radioimmunoassays for LH ( 17042 ).

The HCG diet, also known as the Simeons method, combines a very low-calorie diet with the use of human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) hormone for weight loss. Proponents of HCG as a means to lose weight also suggest that using the hormone could accelerate weight loss and redistribute fat from the thighs and buttocks to different areas of the body ( 17301 ). However, clinical research has failed to confirm these claims. The use of HCG for obesity and weight loss was suggested by a London physician named Albert T Simeons. He thought that fat distribution in obese patients resembled the fat distribution in patients with Frolich's syndrome, which was treated with HCG. Simeons hypothesized that HCG might therefore be an effective adjunctive treatment for obesity ( 17301 ). However, some promoters of HCG diets have been cited by the US Federal Trade Commission for making false claims about HCG diets ( 17303 ).
