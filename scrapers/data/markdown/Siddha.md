## Overview

Siddha is a system of medicine that originated in Southern India. The name is derived from the Tamil word for sage, "Siddhi," which indicates the ability to achieve physical and spiritual perfection and prevent mortality. Although Siddha medicine has many similarities with Ayurveda, the Northern Indian system of medicine, Siddha has unique diagnostic and therapeutic techniques ( 102307 ). It remains prevalent in Southern India and has also gained popularity in Sri Lanka ( 102306 ). The individuals who diagnose and treat patients in this system of medicine are known as Siddhars or Siththars. They aim to bring the body into a state of perfection through meditation, yoga, breathing exercises, and the use of certain herbal formulas. It is through these practices, as well as other unique treatments, that some followers of Siddha believe the Siddhars may become immortal ( 102306 , 102307 ).

Siddha is a system of medicine that was developed in Southern India. Although it resembles Ayurvedic medicine in many ways, it utilizes unique diagnostic and therapeutic techniques. According to Siddha practitioners, the universe is made of different combinations of five primordial elements (bhutas). These are defined as Nilam (earth), Neer (water), Thee (fire), Kaatru (air), and Vin (sky). Everything in the universe is considered a manifestation of different combinations of these elements and their interactions. Organs, bodily processes, colors, and voice tones are just a few of the aspects of the body that are considered to be associated with a particular element. These five elements form three basic forces (Mukkuttram) that regulate bodily function. These three forces are: Vatham, formed by space and air; Pitham, formed by fire; and Kapham, formed by water and earth. An imbalance in the Mukkuttram is thought to cause disease ( 102305 ). Diagnosis in Siddha can involve the examination or evaluation of up to eight different aspects, including pulse, tongue, tactile response, body color, speech, eyes, stool, and urine. The most important of these is thought to be the evaluation of urine, which is considered to result from all of the processes of the body working together. Thus, the color, odor, cloudiness, sedimentation, surface tension, and other features of urine are evaluated ( 102305 ). The purpose of treatment in Siddha medicine is to restore the original balance of the three forces and five elements. If one force or element is considered deficient, then the selected treatment will have properties similar to that particular force or element. Alternatively, the purported correction in the patient's energy balance may take place by using a drug of the opposite nature from the element that is considered the primary source of the problem. Thus, if one element in the body is overacting, a patient may take that element's counterbalance, in order to offset the overacting element ( 102305 , 102306 , 102307 ). Each treatment is individualized with consideration for specific patient factors ( 102306 ). Siddha practitioners also often make recommendations on lifestyle modifications, including breathing techniques, meditation, yoga, and eating patterns, as part of the prescribed regimen for healing ( 102305 , 102306 ).
