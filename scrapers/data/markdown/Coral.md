## Overview

Coral is a type of marine invertebrate. Like sea anemone, coral has a simple polyp structure. Coral polyps can exist as individual entities or in colonies, which may grow large enough to form reefs.  Coral polyps secrete calcium carbonate, which forms a hard exoskeleton ( 10739 , 10741 ).

## History

Solid coral blocks have been used as bone graft substitutes in clinical orthopedics since the early 1980s ( 10739 ). In 2003, the Federal Trade Commission charged the marketers of a coral supplement called "Coral Calcium Supreme" with making unsubstantiated health and medical claims about the product's ability to cure cancer or other diseases such as multiple sclerosis and heart disease ( 10742 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, coral is used for multiple sclerosis (MS), cancer, heart disease, other chronic health problems, and as a calcium supplement. Orthopedically, coral is used as a bone substitute and as a substrate for growing new bone in areas damaged by trauma, including maxillofacial reconstruction, cosmetic facial surgery, and damaged weight-bearing bones.

Likely Safe when used surgically as a bone substitute ( 10736 , 10737 , 10738 , 10739 , 10741 , 46062 ). There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of coral for its other uses.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Orally, no adverse effects have been reported. However, a thorough safety evaluation has not been conducted. Some coral calcium supplements contain lead ( 9598 ). Surgically, coral is generally well tolerated. However, it can cause infections ( 10739 , 10741 ).

#### Immunologic

Surgically, coral can cause infections. However, it doesn't carry the risk of transmitting infections such as HIV, hepatitis C, and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease. The local infection rate was 4% for coral grafts as compared to the bone allograph infection rate of 20% ( 10739 , 10741 ).

Bone substitute.

Coral can be used in place of allographic or autographic bone for spinal fusions and bone tumors; as well as in periodontal, craniofacial, orthopedic, and neurosurgery surgery ( 10736 , 10737 , 10738 , 10739 , 10741 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of coral for its other uses.

#### General

Coral (calcium carbonate matrix) is derived from coral exoskeletons ( 10739 , 10741 ).

#### Bone effects

The structure of coral is similar to that of cancellous (spongy) bone. Coral seems to act as an adequate carrier for bone growth factors. It seems to allow osteocyte attachment, growth, spreading, and differentiation. New bone and fibrovascular tissue grow within the implanted coral. Over time, the calcium carbonate framework is resorbed ( 10737 , 10739 , 10741 ). In ovariectomized mice, adding coral calcium as 1% of the diet improves bone mineral density in the vertebrae, femur, and tibia when compared with ovariectomized mice not receiving calcium supplementation. These improvements were similar to those seen in mice receiving calcium carbonate as 1% of the diet. Coral calcium also appears to increase bone forming markers and bone resorption markers similarly to calcium carbonate ( 104280 ).
