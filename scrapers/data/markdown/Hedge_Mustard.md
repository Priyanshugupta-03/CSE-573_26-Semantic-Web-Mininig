# Hedge Mustard
Scientific Name Sisymbrium officinale (synonym: Erysimum officinale) Family Brassicaceae/Cruciferae Other Common Names English Watercress, Erísimo, Erysimum, Herb of Singers, Herbe au Chanvre, Mostaza de Cobertura, Moutarde des Haies, Singer's Plant, Sisymbre, Sisymbre Officinal, St. Barbara's Hedge Mustard, Thalictroc, Vélar, Vélar Officinal

Caution

Hedge mustard should not be confused with Black Mustard , Clown's Mustard Plant , and White Mustard , which are also sometimes referred to as mustard.

## Overview

Hedge mustard is a plant found in Europe, Asia, and northern Africa. The above ground parts are traditionally used in medicine ( 18 , 103592 ). Traditional and historical uses of hedge mustard include sore throat and cough, laryngitis, bronchitis, asthma, mastitis, urinary tract diseases, orchitis, gallbladder problems, and cancer.

Possibly Unsafe when the fresh flowering parts are used orally due to the presence of cardioactive glycosides ( 18 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of hedge mustard when used topically.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Unsafe when used orally ( 18 ); avoid using.

|

#### General

Orally, hedge mustard might be unsafe due to its cardiac glycoside constituents. Topically, no adverse effects have been reported. However, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted for either route of administration.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Orally : Theoretically, digitalis-like effects including cardiac rhythm disorders, diarrhea, headache, and vomiting.

#### Cardiovascular

Orally, hedge mustard may theoretically cause digitalis-like effects such as cardiac rhythm disorders ( 18 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, hedge mustard may theoretically cause digitalis-like effects such as vomiting and diarrhea ( 18 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Orally, hedge mustard may theoretically cause digitalis-like effects such as headache ( 18 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of hedge mustard.

#### Adult
Oral:

Research is limited; typical dosing is unavailable.

Topical:

Research is limited; typical dosing is unavailable.

#### DIGOXIN (Lanoxin)
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Theoretically, taking hedge mustard with digoxin might increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity.

The flowering above ground parts of hedge mustard contain cardioactive glycosides ( 2 , 18 ). Theoretically, taking hedge mustard with digoxin might increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity.

---

#### DIURETIC DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Theoretically, taking hedge mustard with diuretic drugs might increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity.

The flowering above ground parts of hedge mustard contain cardioactive glycosides ( 2 , 18 ). Theoretically, concomitant use of potassium depleting diuretics and hedge mustard can increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity due to potassium depletion ( 506 ).

---

#### MACROLIDE ANTIBIOTICS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Theoretically, taking hedge mustard with macrolide antibiotics might increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity.

The flowering above ground parts of hedge mustard contain cardioactive glycosides ( 2 , 18 ). Macrolide antibiotics appear to increase the gastrointestinal absorption of oral digoxin. Theoretically, concomitant use of macrolide antibiotics might increase the absorption of the cardiac glycosides found in hedge mustard ( 17 ).

---

#### QUININE
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Theoretically, taking hedge mustard with quinine might increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity.

The flowering above ground parts of hedge mustard contain cardioactive glycosides ( 2 , 18 ). Quinine may reduce the clearance of oral digoxin. Theoretically, concomitant use of quinine with hedge mustard might reduce the clearance of cardiac glycosides found in hedge mustard ( 506 ).

#### HEART DISEASE

The flowering above ground parts of hedge mustard contain cardioactive glycosides ( 2 , 18 ). Theoretically, cardiac glycosides may exacerbate heart disease or interfere with existing drug therapy.

#### POTASSIUM DEFICIENCY

Do not use hedge mustard in people with potassium deficiency. Using hedge mustard in patients with potassium deficiency can precipitate cardiac glycoside toxicity ( 3805 ).

None known.

#### Presentation

Theoretically, digitalis-like effects including cardiac rhythm disorders, diarrhea, headache, and vomiting can occur due to the cardiac glycosides in hedge mustard ( 18 ).

#### Treatment

There is insufficient reliable information available about the treatment of overdose with hedge mustard.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the pharmacokinetics of hedge mustard.

#### General

The applicable parts of hedge mustard are the above ground parts. The foliage contains vitamin C, 0.05% cardenolides (cardioactive glycosides), glucosinolates including sinigrin and gluconapin, terpenes, and flavonoids. Hedge mustard also contains the volatile mustard oil allyl isothiocyanate and 3-butenylisothiocyanate ( 18 , 103592 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

In vitro, hedge mustard extract has antioxidant effects ( 103592 ). These effects may play a role in the potential clinical effects of hedge mustard.
