Caution

Yoga should not be confused with Ayurveda , the system of medicine from which yoga originated.

## Overview

Yoga is a key part of traditional Ayurvedic medicine. Yoga uses several exercises involving controlled breathing, meditation, and body posturing. Many different styles of yoga exist that use a variety of techniques at a range of intensities. Western yoga practice most often focuses on physical yoga poses specifically. This monograph does not include mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) programs, which are sometimes also considered types of yoga ( 105372 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Yoga has been safely used in numerous clinical trials ( 16259 , 16260 , 16261 , 16262 , 16263 , 16264 , 16265 , 16266 , 16267 , 16268 )( 16269 , 16270 , 16276 , 16277 , 16278 , 16279 , 16280 , 16281 , 16282 , 16283 )( 16284 , 16285 , 16286 , 16288 , 16289 , 16290 , 16291 , 16292 , 16293 , 16294 )( 16295 , 16296 , 16297 , 16298 , 16299 , 16300 , 16501 , 16502 , 16503 , 16504 )( 18146 , 28412 , 28413 , 28414 , 28415 , 88829 , 88983 , 88992 , 89006 , 89013 )( 92917 , 99761 , 101530 , 105349 , 105351 , 105365 , 105366 , 105370 , 105372 , 114856 )( 114873 , 114874 ). A pooled analysis shows yoga to be a safe form of exercise ( 92923 ). However, musculoskeletal pain and injury can occur, especially with power-type yoga ( 101533 , 101534 , 114877 ). Some aggressive forms of yoga exercises might not be safe, especially for beginners and when practiced without a licensed yoga teacher. Reports of serious adverse events have occurred in people using the "Kapalabhati pranayama" techniques ( 16505 , 16506 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when used appropriately and under supervision of a licensed yoga teacher. Yoga has been studied in several clinical trials without reports of serious adverse events ( 18146 , 88959 , 88985 , 89008 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Safe when used appropriately. Some clinical research shows that yoga during pregnancy does not adversely affect birth outcomes ( 16276 , 16291 , 105374 , 107270 ). However, some aggressive forms of yoga exercises might not be safe to use during pregnancy.

#### General

Yoga is generally safe and well tolerated.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Temporary musculoskeletal pain.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Cerebral hemorrhage, chronic back, neck, shoulder, knee, or hip pain, chronic headache, fractures, sciatica, strains and sprains, and tendon shortening. Hot yoga has been reported to cause heat stroke. Yoga techniques involving aggressive breathing have been associated with pneumothorax and subcutaneous emphysema.

#### Cardiovascular

A 35-year-old adult at 12 weeks postpartum was admitted to the emergency room for hyperthermia, or heat stroke, from practicing hot yoga. The heat stroke resulted in ventricular fibrillation and cardiac arrest requiring cardiopulmonary resuscitation and supportive care ( 97199 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

Like other physical exercises, yoga can cause or worsen musculoskeletal pain ( 97476 , 101529 , 105351 , 105362 ). A retrospective study found that practicing yoga recreationally is associated with worsened musculoskeletal pain in the wrist and hand ( 97468 ). Musculoskeletal injuries are most commonly associated with power yoga, the use of props, and employing hand-, shoulder-, and head-stands, as well as forward and backward bends and sitting positions. Chronic back, neck, shoulder, knee, or hip pain, tendon shortening, ligament rupture, strains and sprains, fractures, and sciatica have all been rarely reported ( 101533 , 101534 , 114868 , 114872 , 114877 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Chronic headache, sleep problems, cerebral hemorrhage, tingling, vertigo, and depressive symptoms have all been reported rarely by individuals practicing yoga ( 101533 , 105351 ). A case of acute cervical myelopathy with concurrent cervical arterial dissection is reported in a 63-year-old after performing the Vrischikasana yoga pose. The patient was hospitalized and recovered gradually following surgery and antithrombotic treatment ( 111578 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

In patients with glaucoma, intraocular pressure has been reported to increase during the practice of inversion asanas or head-down yoga poses such as headstand, downward facing dog, standing forward bend, plough, and legs up the wall ( 114869 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

A 40-year-old adult experienced subcutaneous emphysema following an aggressive yoga exercise called "pranayam". This exercise involves using the Valsalva maneuver ( 16505 ). In another case, a 29-year-old adult experienced pneumothorax following an aggressive yoga breathing technique called "Kapalabhati pranayama", also known as "breath of fire" or "skull shining breath" ( 16506 ).

Possibly Effective
Asthma.

Yoga appears to slightly improve symptoms and quality of life in patients with asthma. The effectiveness of yogic breathing exercises (pranayama) specifically is less clear.

Pooled analyses of clinical trials in patients with asthma suggest that yoga slightly improves symptoms and quality of life when compared with usual care or sham intervention ( 92922 , 92929 , 111580 ). Individual clinical studies demonstrate that yoga improves asthma symptoms when compared with no intervention or when compared with baseline, but not when compared with certain active controls. For example, some clinical research in patients with mild to moderate asthma shows that practicing yoga for 4-16 weeks does not significantly improve symptoms of asthma or quality of life when compared with stretching or cognitive behavioral therapy and relaxation exercises ( 16289 , 88953 , 88954 ). However, other clinical research in adolescents and adults suggests that practicing yoga for 75-90 minutes once or twice daily for up to 40 days improves exercise capacity, physical fitness, and lung function when compared with baseline ( 88957 , 88958 , 88960 , 88962 ). Furthermore, one clinical study in patients with asthma shows that practicing yoga for 1 hour at least 16 times monthly for 4.5 years decreases asthma severity, attacks, and need for medications when compared to matched controls ( 88956 ). Finally, some research in patients with asthma suggests that yoga combined with diet and hydrotherapy may also improve lung function when compared with baseline ( 88961 ). Research regarding yogic breathing exercises (pranayama) for asthma is mixed. Some clinical research in patients with mild asthma suggests that practicing yogic breathing for 15 minutes twice daily for 2 weeks does not improve symptoms or frequency of inhaler use when compared with placebo ( 88952 ). However, other research in the same population suggests that practicing yogic breathing for 3 weeks improves symptoms ( 88955 ). Additionally, research in children with asthma shows that practicing yogic breathing for 12 months decreases inhaler use ( 88959 ).

Back pain.

Yoga seems to alleviate pain in some patients with chronic low back pain.

Guidelines published by the American College of Physicians in 2017 recommend that patients with chronic low back pain try nonpharmacologic therapy, such as yoga, prior to starting pharmacologic therapy ( 95773 ). It is important to note that these guidelines recommend yoga as only one of several nonpharmacologic options for first-line therapy ( 95773 ). This is likely because most clinical research shows that yoga is comparable to physical therapy and other exercises for improving chronic low back pain ( 16286 , 97475 , 97476 , 101530 , 105362 ). A number of meta-analyses of clinical research show that practicing yoga reduces back pain.  One meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with chronic low back pain shows that different yoga styles, including hatha, iyengar, and viniyoga, modestly reduce pain for up to 6 months and improve disability for up to 12 months when compared with non-exercise treatments such as usual care or education. However, yoga was no more effective than non-exercise treatments for pain reduction in two long-term studies lasting 12 months. It was also no more effective for pain reduction or improvement of disability or quality of life than physical therapy at any time ( 105362 ). A network meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with chronic low back pain shows that practicing yoga reduces back pain and improves physical function when compared with no intervention or conventional rehabilitation. Additionally, yoga was ranked first for improving physical function and second for alleviating low back pain among a wide variety of different types of exercise-based interventions ( 114862 ).  Earlier meta-analyses of clinical research in patients with low back pain show that yoga moderately relieves pain and improves disability when compared with control ( 92927 , 107263 ). Additional individual clinical studies in these patients shows that practicing yoga daily for just one week improves flexibility and reduces disability when compared with physical exercise ( 89003 ). In patients with non-disabling low back pain, guidance on the practice of kundalini yoga for up to 12 months does not reduce work absenteeism or improve back pain when compared with evidence-based advice or strength training. However, in individuals practicing kundalini yoga at least twice weekly as directed, absenteeism was reduced by over 50% when compared with evidence-based advice ( 105348 ).

Breast cancer.

Yoga seems to be beneficial in patients with breast cancer, with effects comparable to other exercise regimens.

Yoga has been shown to improve quality of life, pain, gastrointestinal symptoms, and well-being in patients with breast cancer ( 16263 , 16269 , 16285 , 89016 , 97469 , 100378 , 101522 , 101523 , 105366 ). Meta-analyses of clinical research in patients with breast cancer show that yoga improves quality of life and fatigue better than no therapy and to a similar degree as other exercise regimens. Practicing yoga also seems to reduce sleep disturbance, anxiety, and depression in these patients ( 97469 , 105366 , 114860 ). However, there were no long-term benefits identified in a meta-analysis of clinical studies limited to patients receiving chemotherapy ( 105366 ). The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that yoga may be offered to patients experiencing aromatase inhibitor-related joint pain in breast cancer. This guideline also recommends offering hatha yoga to patients experiencing pain after treatment for breast cancer. These are weak recommendations based on low-quality evidence suggesting that benefits outweigh harms ( 109161 ).

Cancer-related fatigue.

Yoga seems to be beneficial in patients with cancer-related fatigue.

Meta-analyses of clinical research in patients with breast cancer shows that practicing yoga regularly for approximately 1-6 months reduces cancer-related fatigue and improves quality of life better than no therapy and to a similar degree as other exercise regimens ( 97469 , 114866 , 114868 ). Clinical research in patients with various cancers and mild to moderate fatigue shows that practicing yoga weekly for 8 weeks modestly reduces symptoms of general and physical fatigue when compared with a waitlist control group ( 105365 ). The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for cancer-related fatigue list yoga as a potential nonpharmacologic treatment option for patients during and following completion of cancer treatment ( 112413 ).

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

Yoga, alone or in combination with other lifestyle changes, appears to improve risk factors for CVD. However, evidence in patients with established coronary artery disease (CAD) is mixed.

Although there is limited research assessing the effects of yoga on CVD outcomes, a number of studies assess the effects of yoga for risk factors of CVD. Pooled analyses of clinical research show that practicing yoga appears to lower blood pressure, improve cholesterol levels, and reduce body mass index ( 92920 , 92926 , 111603 ). However, evidence in patients with established CAD is mixed. A meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with CAD shows that practicing yoga for 3-12 months reduces body mass index, but does not reduce systolic blood pressure, blood glucose, triglycerides, low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, or total cholesterol when compared with standard care or a cardiac rehabilitation program ( 111589 ). Conversely, other clinical research in patients with CAD suggests that yoga training for a year reduces total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, episodes of angina, and progression of coronary lesions when compared with control ( 16294 , 88979 ). Yoga practiced in combination with other lifestyle changes also appears to have beneficial effects. Some research in patients with coronary artery disease suggests that implementing lifestyle changes involving hatha yoga, meditation, visualization, group therapy, walking, and a reduced-fat vegetarian diet for 12 months improves exercise capacity, weight, stress, and angina pain ( 88982 ).

Depression.

Yoga seems to be beneficial for improving symptoms of depression in patients with major depressive disorder and those with mild or new-onset depression. Yoga also seems to be beneficial as an adjunct to conventional therapy.

Generally, yoga appears to be beneficial for patients with depression in the short term ( 16501 , 28414 , 92921 , 111616 ). A meta-analysis of 12 clinical studies in patients with depressive disorders as well as individuals with elevated levels of depression shows that practicing yoga for about 8 weeks modestly improves the severity of depression when compared to usual care, relaxation therapy, or aerobic exercise ( 92921 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with major depressive disorder shows that practicing yoga modestly improves symptoms of depression when compared with non-exercise control ( 114856 ). Overall, the interpretation of results is limited by inclusion of lower quality evidence. Clinical research shows that practicing kundalini yoga for 1 hour before breakfast for 6 months may improve symptoms of depression ( 28414 ). Yoga seems to be more beneficial in patients that have mild or new-onset depression who are naïve to standard care with antidepressants. Some clinical research in this population shows that practicing yoga for 30 days can reduce severity of depression and subjective ratings ( 88984 ). The 2016 clinical practice guidelines published by the Canadian Network for Mood and Anxiety Treatments (CANMAT) recommend physical and meditative modalities such as yoga as a first- or second-line treatment for mild-to- moderate depression ( 95691 ). However, yoga, such as hatha yoga, generally does not appear to be more effective than usual care, group therapy, or social support groups ( 92921 , 100386 ). Conversely, a moderate-size clinical study in adults with moderate-to-severe depression treated with or without antidepressants shows that practicing hatha yoga in a room heated to 105°F for 90 minutes approximately once weekly for 8 weeks improves clinician-reported depression scores when compared with a waitlist control group. Approximately 59% of patients in the yoga group experienced a 50% or greater decrease in depressive symptoms compared with 6% of the control group ( 111616 ). However, the validity of this study is limited by the high rate of dropouts in the yoga group, which suggests the practice may be poorly tolerated. Some clinical research in patients with major depression also taking conventional antidepressants shows that practicing Sahaj yoga, a specific meditative technique, can improve some measures of cognitive function and decrease symptoms of depression ( 16268 , 16283 ). There is also interest in using yoga in various other populations. Clinical research suggests that yoga can improve depression in patients with alcohol use disorder, breast cancer, cancer- or chemotherapy-related fatigue, perimenopausal symptoms, recent cardiac surgery, and in older adults who are community or non-community dwelling ( 16281 , 88983 , 105365 , 105366 , 105367 , 114857 , 114860 , 114872 , 114874 ). Several meta-analyses of clinical research in individuals with and without prenatal depression show that practicing yoga during pregnancy reduces depression ( 92924 , 100384 , 111582 , 111585 , 111586 ). However, some meta-analyses in pregnant individuals shows that yoga is beneficial for treating, but not preventing, prenatal depression, while another meta-analysis suggests yoga isn't beneficial for improving perinatal depression ( 111583 , 111584 , 114858 ). The reasons for the discrepant findings among analyses may be explained by differences in yoga methods, study duration, or comparator groups of included studies.

Diabetes.

Yoga seems to modestly improve glycemic control in patients with type 2 diabetes.

Meta-analyses that include several small-to-moderate size clinical studies in patients with type 2 diabetes show that practicing yoga reduces fasting blood glucose by about 25-30 mg/dL, postprandial blood glucose by about 25 mg/dL, and glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by 0.5-1%, when compared with control ( 97471 , 111607 , 114870 , 114871 ). However, the validity of these analyses is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which employ various types and durations of yoga practice and controls. Practicing yoga also modestly reduces total cholesterol and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and increases high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol in these patients ( 97471 ). Additional clinical research in patients with diabetes shows that practicing yoga also reduces pulse rate and blood pressure when compared with baseline ( 16293 , 89015 ). Some research in patients with type 2 diabetes has compared yoga with other exercises for improving glycemic control. A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that practicing yoga seems to lower fasting and postprandial blood glucose, but not HbA1c, when compared with an exercise control ( 100380 ). This analysis is limited because it did not account for differences in physical exertion between groups. Also, although yoga practice was monitored and enforced, exercise control was only verbally recommended and not enforced. A more recent meta-analysis shows that yoga slightly reduces fasting blood glucose and HbA1c when compared with walking ( 114870 ). Additionally, a network meta-analysis of clinical research shows that yoga improves glycemic control more effectively than Tai Chi, Badu Anjin, dance, and pilates ( 114871 ).

Hypertension.

Yoga seems to modestly reduce blood pressure in patients with hypertension.

While most research shows that practicing yoga can modestly reduce blood pressure, much of the available research is low quality. Meta-analyses of clinical research in patients with hypertension shows that yoga decreases systolic blood pressure by about 5-7 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by about 4 mmHg ( 97473 , 111604 ). These studies often evaluated pranayama (breath-work), asana (postures), and meditative yoga ( 16299 , 16504 , 88963 , 88969 , 92928 , 97473 , 111604 ). Another small meta-analysis of 5 clinical studies in a mixed population of untreated patients with prehypertension, as well as treated patients whose blood pressure remained above goal, shows that practicing yoga moderately reduces systolic and diastolic blood pressure when compared with non-yoga control ( 107271 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which was not further explored in subgroup analyses. Additional research in patients with hypertension shows that practicing shavasana, or a supine yoga posture without movement, for 1-6 months decreases blood pressure and heart rate ( 88966 , 88967 , 88968 ). However, there is some evidence that such blood pressure reduction might be less than with other meditative practices ( 97473 ). Also, some research shows that practicing a type of kundalini yoga called Mediyoga does not reduce blood pressure when compared with control ( 99761 ).

Menopausal symptoms.

Yoga seems to help alleviate menopausal symptoms, with beneficial effects comparable to other exercise interventions.

Most research shows that practicing yoga helps alleviate certain menopausal symptoms such as hot flashes and sleep disturbances ( 16270 , 16279 , 89006 , 89007 , 97470 , 105367 ). Some research also shows that practicing yoga helps alleviate feelings of anxiety or depression when used in combination with information support ( 105367 ). A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that yoga reduces menopausal symptoms, such as hot flashes, and also psychological, somatic, and urogenital symptoms, when compared with no intervention. The beneficial effects of yoga are comparable to other exercise interventions. All studies with yoga interventions used yoga postures and some also used breathing and meditation exercises, with hatha yoga as the most commonly studied form ( 97470 ).

Neck pain.

Yoga seems to alleviate neck pain and disability due to neck pain.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies shows that practicing yoga reduces neck pain and disability due to neck pain when compared with most other types of exercise. Yoga seems to work as well as Pilates for reducing neck pain and disability from neck pain ( 100382 ). Clinical research in patients with low back pain, many of whom also have neck pain, shows that guidance on practicing kundalini yoga for up to 12 months modestly improves neck disability, but not neck pain, when compared with evidence-based advice. In the individuals practicing kundalini yoga at least twice weekly as directed, absenteeism was reduced by over 50% when compared with evidence-based advice ( 105348 ).

Schizophrenia.

Yoga seems to improve symptoms, social functioning, and quality of life in patients with schizophrenia; however, yoga may not be more effective than other forms of exercise.

Meta-analyses of up to 19 small to moderate-sized clinical studies in patients with schizophrenia show that yoga modestly reduces positive and negative symptoms and improves quality of life and social functioning when compared with usual treatment or waitlist control. ( 114875 , 114876 ). Additionally, a network meta-analysis of clinical research shows that yoga ranks as the first and second most effective intervention for negative and positive symptoms, respectively, among 5 different types of mind-body therapies ( 114875 ).  An earlier meta-analysis of mainly low-quality studies and an individual clinical trial show that practicing yoga as an adjunct to standard care for schizophrenia modestly improves most symptoms, social functioning, and quality of life when compared to standard care alone ( 92917 , 105373 ). However, meta-analyses shows that adding yoga to standard care does not seem to be more beneficial than adding other forms of exercise ( 97200 , 114876 ). Also, adding yoga as an adjunct to other exercise and counseling for schizophrenia does not seem to improve symptoms better than exercise and counseling alone ( 97467 ). Most studies included in these analyses involved either hatha yoga or another specific type of yoga ( 16265 , 92917 , 97200 ).

Stress.

Yoga seems to reduce stress, with beneficial effects comparable to relaxation therapy and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).

Clinical research in people experiencing mild to moderate levels of stress shows that practicing yoga for 10-16 weeks improves measures of stress and anxiety. Yoga appears to be similarly effective to relaxation therapy or CBT for reducing stress ( 16266 , 16284 ). Clinical studies in college students and in middle-aged adults show that practicing yoga up to 3 times weekly for 5-14 weeks improves stress, mood, and fatigue ( 88972 , 88973 , 88974 , 100375 ). Furthermore, in school children, practicing yoga decreases anxiety and tension ( 88975 ). Finally, a small study in patients undergoing cholecystectomies suggests that practicing yoga 4 times for up to 70 minutes may reduce post-operative stress ( 88976 ). A meta-analysis of clinical research in pregnant individuals shows that practicing yoga during pregnancy modestly reduces perceived stress scores when compared with  standard care or other controls ( 111582 ).

Tuberculosis.

When combined with anti-tuberculosis therapy, yoga seems to improve symptoms, weight, and some measures of lung function.

Some clinical research in patients with pulmonary tuberculosis shows that practicing yoga for 2 months in addition to anti-tuberculosis therapy can decrease symptoms, increase weight, improve some measures of lung function, and increase negative sputum cultures when compared with breath awareness combined with anti-tuberculosis therapy ( 16298 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Abdominal pain.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for abdominal pain in children.

A meta-analysis of 2 small, low-quality clinical studies in children aged 8-18 years with functional abdominal pain shows that practicing yoga for 6-10 weeks does not improve the rate of treatment success when compared with no intervention. However, one study included in this analysis suggests that practicing yoga may decrease pain frequency and intensity ( 111614 ).

Adjustment disorders.

Yoga has only been evaluated in combination with massage in children with adjustment disorder; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Clinical research in children with adjustment disorder and depressed mood shows that practicing yoga and receiving massage can decrease anxiety and anxious behavior when compared with a control group that watched a relaxing video ( 88985 ).

Age-related cognitive decline.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for age-related cognitive decline.

Clinical research in older community-dwelling adults shows that practicing hatha yoga 3 times weekly for 8 weeks modestly improves some measures of cognitive function when compared with a control group undergoing a stretch-strengthen exercise regimen. However, most endpoints related to cognitive function were not improved ( 105375 ). Other clinical research in healthy seniors shows that practicing Iyengar yoga weekly in combination with recommended daily home practice for 6 months does not improve measures of cognitive function when compared with walking as exercise or being put on a waitlist ( 105376 ).

Alcohol use disorder.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with alcohol use disorder.

Clinical research in patients with alcohol use disorder shows that practicing yoga may improve rehabilitation and general condition when compared with baseline ( 88997 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Additional clinical research in this population shows that yoga can improve depression when compared with control ( 16281 ).

Altitude sickness.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with altitude sickness.

Clinical research in those temporarily relocated from sea level to a 5,050-foot altitude for 2 weeks shows that yoga trainees have better ventilatory efficiency and less hypoxia when compared with controls ( 88971 ).

Anxiety.

Small clinical studies suggest that yoga may reduce anxiety in certain populations, but it is unclear which forms of yoga may be most beneficial.

A meta-analysis of 2 clinical studies in patients with anxiety disorders shows that practicing yoga for 3-12 weeks reduces anxiety symptoms when compared with control l. Interpretation of findings is limited by very low certainty of evidence ( 114859 ). A previous meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients with various types of anxiety disorders shows that practicing different styles of yoga modestly improves the severity of anxiety when compared with control or active treatment ( 100375 ). However, not all research agrees. A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with anxiety with or without depression shows that hatha yoga does not improve anxiety when compared with other active treatments ( 100386 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of 5 clinical studies in patients with depressive disorders shows that yoga does not improve anxiety symptoms when compared with control ( 114859 ). There is also interest in using yoga in various other populations.  Clinical research shows that yoga can reduce feelings of anxiety when compared with baseline or control for patients with cancer, recent cardiac surgery, during pregnancy, during perimenopause, and for musicians ( 16280 , 105366 , 105367 , 105371 , 111582 , 111583 , 114874 ).

Aromatase inhibitor-induced arthralgia.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in survivors of breast cancer with aromatase-induced arthralgia.

A small clinical study in survivors of breast cancer with aromatase inhibitor-induced arthralgia shows that practicing instructor-led hatha yoga twice weekly for 6 weeks improves knee joint pain scores by 55%, compared with 10% improvement with home-based massage. Results were sustained for at least 1 week, but not as long as 8 weeks ( 107259 ). However, the Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that yoga may be offered to patients experiencing aromatase inhibitor-related joint pain in breast cancer. This is a weak recommendation based on low-quality evidence suggesting that benefits outweigh harms ( 109161 ).

Athletic performance.

It is unclear if yoga improves athletic performance.

Clinical research in adolescent males shows that practicing yoga for one year improves cardiovascular endurance, muscle density, and anaerobic power when compared to baseline ( 89000 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Another small clinical study in adolescent and young adult male recreational cricket bowlers shows that while practicing yoga 3 times weekly for 12 weeks improves bowling performance and physical fitness similarly to conventional conditioning exercises, there were no differences in improvements between groups ( 107267 ).

Attention.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for improving attention.

Clinical research in healthy volunteers shows that practicing yogic breathing exercises (pranayama) for six 30-minute sessions over 2 weeks improves mental energy, physical energy, and feelings of alertness and enthusiasm when compared with practicing relaxation or visualization ( 88977 ).

Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in children with ADHD.

Clinical research in young males with ADHD shows that practicing yoga improves some measures of ADHD when compared to controls. However, yoga does not improve all measures of ADHD ( 16295 ). Additional clinical research in children with ADHD shows that yoga is as effective as practicing conventional motor exercises for improving symptoms ( 88998 ).

Autism spectrum disorder.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in children with autism spectrum disorder.

Some very small clinical studies show that practicing various types of yoga helps to improve gross motor skills, behavior, and imitation skills in some children with autism. In one small study in children aged 5-13 years, physical therapist-delivered creative yoga with pictures and singing twice weekly over 8 weeks, with two additional parent-led sessions weekly, modestly improved gross motor performance and reduced imitation errors related to yoga poses when compared with baseline. However, there was no effect on balance or fine muscle control ( 101525 ).

Bronchitis.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with chronic bronchitis.

Clinical research in patients with chronic bronchitis shows that practicing yoga for 30 minutes daily for 4 weeks can improve subjective feelings of dyspnea when compared with baseline ( 16300 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Cancer-related pain.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with cancer-related pain.

Despite limited evidence, the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for adult cancer pain include yoga as a potential integrative, cognitive-based intervention aimed at enhancing a sense of control over the pain or underlying disease ( 113680 ). The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that yoga may be offered to patients experiencing aromatase inhibitor-related joint pain in breast cancer. This guideline also recommends offering hatha yoga to patients experiencing pain after treatment for breast or head and neck cancer. These are weak recommendations based on low-quality evidence suggesting that benefits outweigh harms ( 109161 ).

Carpal tunnel syndrome.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome.

Clinical research in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome shows that yoga might improve grip strength and decrease pain when compared with splinting or no treatment ( 16502 ).

Cerebral palsy.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in children with cerebral palsy.

A small clinical study in children with cerebral palsy shows that practicing a specific type of intervention (MiYoga) based on hatha yoga principles involving mindfulness and mindful movement techniques for 90 minutes weekly for 8 weeks improves attention, but not physical function, when compared with waitlist control ( 100383 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with CINV.

Clinical research in patients with breast cancer undergoing chemotherapy shows that practicing yoga decreases nausea frequency and intensity, as well as anticipatory nausea and vomiting, when compared with control ( 16261 ).

Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in survivors of cancer with chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy.

A small clinical study in survivors of breast, colorectal, and/or gynecologic cancer with chronic chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy shows that practicing yoga for 8 weeks improves depression and fatigue, but not pain, physical function, or pain interference, when compared with a waitlist control ( 107260 ).

Chemotherapy-related fatigue.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with chemotherapy-related fatigue.

A meta-analysis of studies limited to patients with breast cancer undergoing chemotherapy shows that while practicing yoga for up to 6 months improves fatigue when compared with control, there is no long-term benefit ( 105366 ). A small clinical study included in this analysis shows that practicing Dru yoga for 75 minutes once weekly for 12 weeks does not improve fatigue or quality of life when compared with standard care ( 100381 ). However, the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for cancer-related fatigue list yoga as a potential nonpharmacologic treatment option for patients during and following completion of cancer treatment ( 112860 ).

Chest trauma.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with blunt chest trauma.

Clinical research in patients with isolated blunt chest injury shows that practicing yoga, starting at hospital admission and continuing after discharge, modestly improves most measures of pulmonary function 4 weeks after hospital discharge when compared with a control group. There were also modest improvements in respiratory muscle endurance, but not in overall quality of life. All patients received chest physiotherapy during hospital admission ( 105368 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with CFS.

One observational study in patients with CFS has found that yoga is associated with improved fatigue levels when compared with baseline ( 43944 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the observational nature of the study and the lack of a comparator group.

Chronic kidney disease (CKD).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with CKD undergoing dialysis.

Clinical research in clinically stable patients undergoing dialysis shows that practicing yoga for 12 weeks reduces pain, sleep disturbances, and grip strength when compared with control. Yoga also appears to improve biochemical measures including blood levels of urea, creatinine, alkaline phosphatase, and hematocrit in these patients ( 16264 ).

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with COPD.

Some small clinical studies in patients with COPD show that practicing yoga can improve exercise tolerance, exertion recovery, dyspnea, and general health in patients. The most beneficial type of yoga for these patients appears to be yoga with a breathing focus ( 88990 , 101526 , 101527 ). A meta-analysis of a small number of clinical trials in patients with COPD shows that breathing-focused yoga improves quality of life, exercise capacity over 6 minutes, and lung function by a small amount when compared with no therapy. More complex yoga interventions without a breathing focus only seem to improve exercise capacity ( 101526 ). Similarly, another meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with COPD, that includes some of the same studies as the prior analysis, shows that practicing yoga modestly improves lung function and exercise capacity over 6 minutes but not quality of life when compared with usual care. However, these are the results of subgroup analysis which limits the validity of these findings ( 111579 ).

Cognitive function.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for improving cognitive function in healthy children and adults.

A pooled analysis of a mixed population shows that yoga slightly improves cognitive functions such as attention, processing speed, executive function, and memory. Acute effects were more pronounced than chronic effects when compared with waitlist or other exercise control ( 92925 ).  A meta-analysis of clinical studies in older adults shows that, overall, yoga does not improve memory or executive function. However, results were deemed inconclusive due to heterogeneity in study methods, results, and effect sizes ( 114857 ). Clinical research in healthy patients also shows that using the kundalini yoga technique of one nostril breathing may improve cognitive performance ( 28412 , 28413 ). In school children aged 7-9 years, clinical research shows that performing yoga for 3 months improves cognitive function tests to a similar extent as other forms of physical activity ( 18146 ). Further clinical research in children and adolescents shows that practicing yogic breathing (pranayama) for 10 days may improve spatial memory when compared with control ( 89008 ).

Cognitive impairment.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with mild cognitive impairment.

A small clinical study in patients with mild cognitive impairment shows that practicing kundalini yoga for 12 weeks seems to improve memory and thinking skills about as effectively as following a memory enhancement training program, which is considered to be a gold standard ( 99759 ).

Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients undergoing rehabilitation following CABG surgery.

Clinical research in patients undergoing cardiac rehabilitation following CABG shows that practicing yoga, starting in the hospital after surgery and continuing daily at home for 12 months, does not improve left ventricular ejection fraction when compared with physiotherapy-based treatment. Practicing yoga seemed to improve some aspects of mood when compared with the physiotherapy group ( 105379 ).

Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for hospitalized patients with COVID-19.

A large clinical study in hospitalized adults with mild-to-moderate COVID-19 treated with standard care in India shows that practicing yoga via recorded videos for 10-15 minutes twice daily for 14 days increases the likelihood of better clinical status at day 14 when compared with standard care alone. However, this benefit was not maintained at the day 28 follow up. In addition, practicing yoga did not reduce hospital length of stay or all-cause mortality. The yoga practice consisted of flexibility exercises, breathing exercises (pranayama), and relaxation ( 111615 ).

Dyslexia.

Although there has been interest in using yoga for dyslexia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of yoga for this purpose.

Dysmenorrhea.

Small clinical studies suggest that yoga may improve menstrual pain in patients with primary dysmenorrhea.

A meta-analysis of four small clinical trials in patients with primary dysmenorrhea shows that practicing yoga has a large effect on menstrual pain when compared with not practicing yoga ( 101532 ). A small clinical study in nursing students with primary dysmenorrhea shows that practicing yoga once weekly for 12 weeks improves pain when compared with baseline and results in lower pain scores at different timepoints throughout the study when compared with not practicing yoga ( 107268 ). It is unclear if the improvement from baseline differed between groups.

Epilepsy.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with epilepsy.

Clinical research in patients with epilepsy shows that adjunctive therapy with Sahaja yoga or yoga exercise and meditation might reduce seizure frequency when compared with control or sham yoga ( 16260 , 16282 , 88825 , 88988 ).

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with fibromyalgia.

Clinical research in patients with fibromyalgia shows that practicing yoga for 8 weeks reduces pain in patients with fibromyalgia when compared with baseline ( 16259 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with GAD.

Clinical research in patients with GAD shows that practicing kundalini yoga weekly for 12 weeks with daily homework modestly increases the percentage of individuals with a treatment response, defined as much improved or very much improved symptoms, from 33% in those receiving stress education to 54%. However, this benefit was not maintained at 6 months follow-up. In addition, yoga practice was not as effective as cognitive behavioral therapy, which resulted in a 71% response rate ( 105351 ). Other clinical research in adults with GAD shows that practicing kundalini yoga for 8 weeks reduces anxiety severity when compared with individualized psychotherapy using cognitive techniques ( 105377 ).

Glaucoma.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with glaucoma.

A meta-analysis of 3 clinical studies in patients with glaucoma shows that practicing yoga, in the form of breath or meditation practices, reduces intra-ocular pressure in both eyes when compared with control. Interventions included diaphragmatic breathing 30 minutes daily for 6 months, breathing meditation 45 minutes daily for 6 weeks, and gazing steadily at a point or a candle 55 minutes daily for 1 month. However, in observational studies in patients with glaucoma, intra-ocular pressure increased while practicing certain head-down yoga poses ( 114869 ).

Head and neck cancer.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in survivors of head and neck cancer.

Clinical research in survivors of head and neck cancer shows that practicing a specifically tailored hatha yoga program for 30-90 minutes 2-3 times weekly for 8 weeks reduces pain and anxiety and improves shoulder range of motion when compared with a waitlist control ( 100374 ).

Heart failure.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with heart failure.

Clinical research in heart failure patients shows that adding yoga practice to standard therapy for 12 weeks improves exercise capacity and flexibility when compared with standard therapy alone. Additionally, yoga as an adjunct to standard medical therapy does not appear to improve quality of life when compared with standard therapy alone ( 88981 , 92919 ).

HIV/AIDS.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with HIV/AIDS.

A meta-analysis of small clinical trials in patients with HIV/AIDS shows that practicing yoga improves anxiety and mood, but not depression or quality of life, when compared with controls ( 100377 ).

Insomnia.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with insomnia.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that practicing yoga modestly improves overall sleep quality and reduces insomnia severity when compared with control groups either receiving education or placed on a waitlist. However, not all studies included in the analysis limited inclusion to individuals with insomnia ( 105378 ). Another meta-analysis of 4 clinical studies in adults with sleep disturbances shows that practicing yoga 3-7 times weekly for 4-12 weeks improves sleep quality when compared with various non-active controls ( 114865 ). However, the effect size is small and may not be clinically significant. Clinical research in patients with sleep onset insomnia shows that practicing kundalini yoga for 8 weeks modestly increases total sleep time and improves sleep efficiency when compared with sleep hygiene advice. However, there was no difference between groups in sleep onset latency ( 105370 ). Other clinical research in elderly patients and patients with chronic insomnia shows that practicing yoga can decrease sleep onset latency, increase total sleep time, and improve feelings of being well-rested and other sleep measures when compared with baseline ( 16290 , 16292 ). However, the validity of the findings in these latter studies is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Yoga has also been evaluated for sleep disturbances in patients with cancer with mixed results. A meta-analysis of 20 clinical studies in adults with cancer and sleep disturbance shows that practicing yoga improves wake after sleep onset, but not sleep efficiency or sleep onset latency, when compared with usual care or waitlist control. ( 114864 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of up to 4 clinical studies in patients with a history of cancer shows that practicing yoga does not improve sleep quality or most other sleep measures when compared with control ( 114867 ).

Intellectual disability.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in children with intellectual disability.

Clinical research in children with intellectual disability shows that yoga practice for 1 year may improve the intelligence quotient (IQ) and social adaptation ability when compared with an age- and IQ-matched control group ( 89005 ).

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with IBS.

A meta-analysis of 5 small clinical studies in patients with IBS shows that practicing yoga does not improve self-reported IBS symptoms when compared with various controls. However, yoga seems to improve quality of life, but not self-reported IBS symptoms or abdominal pain, when compared with walking ( 111590 ). The validity of this analysis is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies. Some clinical research in adolescents with IBS shows that practicing yoga for 4 weeks reduces functional disability and anxiety, but not gastrointestinal symptoms, when compared with control ( 16278 ).

Labor pain.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for labor pain.

Clinical research shows that participating in a yoga program during pregnancy improves some measures of maternal comfort during labor and delivery and 2 hours post-delivery when compared with a control group ( 16276 ).

Lymphedema.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with breast-cancer related lymphedema.

Clinical research in females with breast cancer-related lymphedema shows that practicing yoga 3 times weekly for 8 weeks improves some, but not all, measures of quality of life, and does not improve edema severity, when compared to not practicing yoga ( 101523 ).

Metabolic syndrome.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with metabolic syndrome.

Clinical research in patients with metabolic syndrome shows that practicing yoga for 90 minutes for a total of 15 sessions over 10 weeks improves energy levels, but not wellbeing, stress, or blood pressure, when compared with a waitlist control ( 88980 ).

Migraine headache.

Small studies suggest that yoga may improve frequency, intensity, and pain in patients with migraine.

Meta-analyses of 5 small, mostly low-quality clinical studies in patients with migraine show that practicing yoga reduces migraine frequency when compared with various controls ( 111581 , 111588 ). Yoga also reduces pain intensity and headache duration and improves function scores ( 111588 ). The validity of this these analyses is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, that employed various yoga methods and controls. Other clinical research in patients with migraine shows that practicing yoga for 3 months reduces patient-reported migraine frequency, intensity, and pain when compared with a self-care control group ( 16267 , 89011 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with MS.

A meta-analysis that includes 2 small clinical studies in patients with MS shows that practicing yoga for 2-3 times a week for 8-24 weeks improves balance scores when compared with no exercise ( 111587 ). However, a pooled analysis in patients with MS shows that practicing yoga does not improve quality of life or mobility when compared with usual care or exercise, but may result in small improvements in fatigue when compared with usual care ( 92918 ). Additional clinical research in this population shows that yoga does not significantly improve cognitive measures or mood ( 16296 ).

Muscle soreness.

It is unclear if yoga improves muscle soreness.

Clinical research shows that females trained in yoga have less muscle soreness after exercising when compared with those not trained in yoga ( 89004 ).

Myocardial infarction (MI).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial as part of cardiac rehabilitation in patients receiving conservative medical management for a recent MI.

Clinical research in patients on conservative medical management for a recent MI shows that incorporating yoga therapy during cardiac rehabilitation 3 days weekly for 12 weeks, with recommendations for home practice on the remaining days, does not improve left ventricular cardiac recovery or lipid parameters when compared with standard care alone. However, depression, anxiety, and overall quality of life were modestly improved ( 105364 ).

Obesity.

It is unclear if yoga improves weight loss in adults or children with overweight or obesity.

A meta-analysis of up to 6 low- or very-low-quality, clinical studies in adults with overweight or obesity show that practicing yoga for 60-90 minutes 2-5 times weekly for 2-12 months reduces body weight by about 3.5 kg when compared with calorie restriction, but not when compared with control.  Additionally, yoga reduces waist circumference by about 2 cm when compared with control, but not when compared with calorie restriction. However, yoga does not reduce body mass index, or percent body fat, when compared with control or calorie restriction ( 114873 ). A small clinical study in adults with obesity shows that practicing yoga for 14 weeks reduces weight and improves body composition when compared with baseline ( 100385 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Another small clinical study in adults with overweight or obesity who had previously participated in a 3-month behavioral weight loss program shows that practicing Iyengar yoga, which is a form of hatha yoga, twice weekly for 12 weeks does not increase weight loss when compared with cooking and nutrition education ( 111591 ). In children aged 8-15 years with overweight or obesity, a moderate-size unblinded clinical study shows that practicing yoga for 1 hour approximately 3 times weekly for 18 weeks, in addition to diet and lifestyle counseling, modestly improves weight loss and body mass index when compared to baseline and no intervention, but not when compared with diet and lifestyle counseling alone ( 111594 ).

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with OCD.

Some clinical research in patients with OCD shows that practicing kundalini yoga for 3 months or 1 year improves symptoms of OCD and mood when compared with relaxation therapy ( 28415 , 105349 ). Additional clinical research in patients with OCD who are stabilized on fluoxetine shows that practicing the movement and breathing techniques of kundalini yoga for 5-6 months may decrease symptoms of OCD and the need for medication when compared with baseline ( 88992 ). However, the validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Opioid withdrawal.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with opioid use disorder receiving maintenance therapy.

Clinical research in patients with heroin use disorder who are receiving methadone therapy shows that practicing yoga improves recovery and rehabilitation as effectively as group psychotherapy ( 88996 ).

Osteoarthritis.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with osteoarthritis.

Clinical research in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee and hand shows that practicing yoga can improve functionality and decrease pain when compared to baseline or no treatment ( 16288 , 16503 ). A large clinical study in adults with knee osteoarthritis shows that practicing hatha yoga instructed by online videos for 30 minutes 3 times/week for 12 weeks modestly improves physical function but does not reduce knee pain during walking when compared with control. However, the improvement in physical function was not considered a clinically important difference and was not maintained at the 24 week follow up ( 111606 ). The validity of this study is limited by the use of patient-reported outcomes and actual participation in yoga of 2 sessions/week on average. The American College of Rheumatology (ACR) conditionally recommends the use of yoga for knee osteoarthritis ( 102114 ).

Osteoporosis.

Although there has been interest in using yoga for osteoporosis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of yoga for this purpose.

Pain (chronic).

Some clinical research in older adults shows that yoga reduces chronic pain when compared with non-exercise-based interventions but not more than other physical or mind-body exercises.

Clinical research in people with various types of chronic pain shows that practicing yoga 2 times weekly for 8 weeks improves some measures of pain, such as pain management, but not others, such as pain severity, when compared with usual care ( 101529 ). A network meta-analysis of clinical research in older adults with chronic pain shows that yoga alleviates pain when compared with non-exercise-based interventions, but not more than traditional physical exercise or other mind-body exercises. Additionally, yoga does not mitigate chronic pain-related disabilities ( 114878 ).

Pancreatitis.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with chronic pancreatitis.

Clinical research in patients with chronic pancreatitis shows that practicing yoga for 12 weeks improves several measures of quality of life when compared with a control group ( 16277 ).

Parkinson disease.

Small meta-analyses suggest that yoga may improve some motor and nonmotor symptoms in patients with Parkinson disease.

A meta-analysis of 3 small fair- to good-quality clinical studies in patients with Parkinson disease shows that practicing yoga twice weekly for 8-12 weeks moderately improves symptoms, but not balance or anxiety, when compared with usual care or waitlist control ( 107261 ). Another meta-analysis of 10 small low- to moderate-quality studies, including many in the above meta-analysis, shows that practicing yoga improves motor status and functional mobility, but not balance, when compared with usual care, waitlist control, or non-yoga exercise or education. Additionally, yoga appears to improve nonmotor symptoms including depression, anxiety, and quality of life ( 107262 ).

Physical performance.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for physical performance in older adults.

Meta-analyses of mostly small clinical studies in older adults show that practicing yoga 1-3 times weekly for 4-24 weeks is associated with improvement of physical fitness, including flexibility, balance, and muscle strength. ( 107266 , 114872 ). The validity of these findings is limited by heterogeneity and generally low quality of the included studies.

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with PCOS.

A small clinical study of patients with PCOS undergoing fertility treatment shows that practicing yoga for 6 weeks in addition to receiving routine care results in lower hirsutism scores, but does not affect incidence of acanthosis nigricans or alopecia, when compared with receiving routine care alone ( 107269 ). It is unclear if the improvement from baseline differed between groups.

Postoperative pain.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for reducing postoperative pain.

Clinical research in patients with recent cardiac surgery shows that practicing yoga reduces postoperative pain when compared with usual care ( 114874 ).

Postoperative recovery.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for postoperative recovery following hip surgery.

Clinical research in elderly adults who have undergone hip surgery following a fracture shows that practicing upper body yoga for 4 weeks modestly improves lung function and reduces impairments in activities of daily living, as measured by the Barthel Index, when compared with deep abdominal breathing ( 101531 ). The study was not adequately powered to detect a difference in rates of pneumonia, one of the primary outcomes, between groups.

Postpartum depression.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for patients with postpartum depression.

A meta-analysis of 3 small, low-quality clinical studies in patients with postpartum depression shows that practicing yoga 1-7 times weekly for 6-12 weeks improves depression symptoms when compared with standard care or no therapy  ( 114861 ). Similarly, a meta-analysis of up to 7 small clinical studies shows that practicing yoga helps  prevent and treat postpartum depression when compared with no exercise control ( 114858 ).

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for patients with PTSD.

A small clinical study in females with PTSD after a motor vehicle accident shows that practicing six 45-minute sessions of Kripalu yoga over 12 weeks improves scores related to PTSD symptoms at the end of the study but not at the 3 month follow up when compared with controls (exchanging daily life experiences and playing board games). However, practicing yoga improves scores related to depression, anxiety, and stress at both follow up time points ( 111597 ).

Pre-eclampsia.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial for preventing pre-eclampsia.

A small clinical study in healthy patients shows that practicing yoga daily from 18-22 weeks' gestation until delivery in addition to receiving routine care is associated with an 84% reduction in relative risk for pre-eclampsia when compared with routine care alone ( 107270 ).

Pregnancy-induced hypertension.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with third trimester pregnancy-induced mild hypertension.

Clinical research in patients with third trimester pregnancy-induced mild hypertension shows that practicing yoga 3 times weekly until delivery reduces systolic blood pressure by 7 mmHg, compared with only 2.5 mmHg in patients not practicing yoga. Diastolic blood pressure (DBP) reductions were similar between groups; however, mean DBP levels prior to delivery were modestly lower in those individuals practicing yoga. There were no differences in the number of patients requiring medications or developing pre-eclampsia or eclampsia ( 105374 ).

Premature ejaculation.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with premature ejaculation.

Clinical research shows that practicing yoga improves subjective measures of premature ejaculation when compared with baseline; effects were comparable to those observed with fluoxetine ( 16262 ).

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with PMS.

A meta-analysis that includes 11 mostly small studies in patients with PMS shows that practicing yoga for 2-16 weeks for 10-90 minutes per session improves global and affective PMS symptoms, but not physical symptoms of PMS when compared with no intervention ( 111596 ).  The interpretation of these results is limited by the inclusion of studies that employed variable yoga practices.

Preterm labor.

Small studies suggest yoga is beneficial for preventing preterm labor.

A small observational study has found that practicing yoga is associated with a reduced risk of preterm labor ( 16291 ). A small clinical study in healthy patients shows that practicing yoga daily from 18-22 weeks' gestation until delivery in addition to receiving routine care is associated with a 93% reduction in relative risk of preterm delivery when compared with routine care alone ( 107270 ).

Psychosis.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with psychosis.

A meta-analysis of 11 mostly small, lower-quality clinical studies in patients with non-affective psychosis shows that practicing yoga improves psychotic symptoms when compared with various controls. Practicing yoga may also improve quality of life and insight into illness and treatment ( 111598 ). However, the validity of this analysis is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which employed various yoga regimens that were often in combination with other interventions.

Quality of life.

Small clinical studies suggest that yoga improves quality of life.

Clinical research in a mixed population shows that practicing yoga helps improve well-being and quality of life ( 88994 , 101522 , 101523 , 101531 , 105365 , 105366 ). Additional research shows that practicing yoga improves quality of life in nurses working in the intensive care unit (ICU) when compared with control ( 101524 ). Some research also shows that yoga improves cardiovascular, respiratory, and general physical function, as well as constipation- and sleep-related quality of life in older adults when compared with a waitlist control ( 88995 , 107264 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with RA.

Clinical research in patients with RA shows that practicing yoga movements and yogic breathing for 2 hours, 5 days a week for 3 weeks, and then once a week for 3 months, does not improve morning stiffness, grip strength, disability, or other symptoms ( 88978 ). Another large clinical study in adults with RA treated with disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) shows that attending yoga sessions for 2 hours, 5 days a week for 8 weeks modestly improves disease activity scores (i.e., DAS28) when compared with patients who did not practice yoga ( 111605 ). However, it is unclear whether this improvement is clinically significant.

Scoliosis.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with scoliosis.

A meta-analysis of 2 small clinical studies in adolescents with idiopathic scoliosis shows that practicing yoga improves spinal curvature, as seen by a reduction in Cobb's angle by an average of 4.60 degrees, when compared with conventional treatment, such as education and awareness, brace therapy, and daily care ( 114863 ).

Seasonal affective disorder (SAD).

Although there has been interest in using yoga for SAD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of yoga for this purpose.

Stroke.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with stroke.

Clinical research in patients recovering from stroke shows that practicing yoga for 8-10 weeks does not improve quality of life, motor function, or pain when compared with usual treatment or waitlist control. However, yoga does seem to improve memory in these patients ( 97472 ). Clinical reports in patients with post-stroke hemiplegia show that practicing yoga might improve balance and movement ( 89002 ).

Substance use disorder.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with substance use disorder.

A nonrandomized small clinical study in females with various forms of substance use disorder undergoing inpatient treatment shows that practicing trauma-sensitive yoga 3 times weekly for a total of 14 sessions in addition to usual care modestly improves lack of premeditation, but not cravings, self-efficacy, anxiety, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) symptoms, or other forms of impulsivity, when compared with usual care alone. Most participants reported daily tobacco use and alcohol use, with varying use of other substances, such as cannabis, cocaine, and heroin ( 107272 ).

Syncope.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with syncope.

A meta-analysis of 4 mostly low-quality studies in 309 adults with vasovagal syncope shows that practicing yoga 3-5 times per week for 3-12 months reduces the frequency of syncopal attacks when compared with control ( 111595 ). However, the interpretation of these results is limited by the inclusion of observational and randomized studies, as well as studies that employed variable yoga practices.

Tension headache.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with tension headache.

Clinical research in patients with tension headaches shows that practicing yoga decreases headache frequency, duration, and intensity, and improves coping when compared with baseline ( 89011 ). Other clinical research shows that practicing shavasana, the supine non-moving pose in yoga, for 30 minutes 2 times weekly for 10 weeks decreases tension headaches to a similar degree as biofeedback ( 89012 ).

Tinnitus.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in patients with chronic idiopathic tinnitus.

Clinical research in patients with chronic idiopathic tinnitus shows that practicing yoga improves psychological impairment due to tinnitus; however, it is not as effective as cognitive behavioral therapy in improving tinnitus masking ( 88999 ).

Urinary incontinence.

It is unclear if yoga is beneficial in older females with stress or mixed urinary incontinence.

A small clinical study in older community-dwelling females with stress or mixed urinary incontinence shows that practicing supervised yoga once weekly for 4 weeks, followed by unsupervised CD-guided home practice for 8 weeks, improves the frequency and amount of urine leakage and the overall impact of urinary incontinence by a small to medium amount when compared with practicing Pilates. There was no difference in results of the 1-hour pad test for those practicing yoga when compared with those practicing Pilates or pelvic floor muscle training ( 107265 ).

More evidence is needed to rate yoga for these uses.

Yoga is comprised of a group of practices. There are "eight limbs" of yoga: pranayama (breathing exercises), asana (physical postures), yama (moral behavior), niyama (healthy habit), dharana (concentration), prathyahara (sensory withdrawal), dhyana (contemplation), and samadhi (higher consciousness). Many forms of yoga exist, most of which involve controlled breathing, meditation, and body posturing ( 99761 , 105362 , 105365 , 105368 , 105374 ). Yoga is often learned through schools, classes, or self-study. Once techniques are learned, individuals can practice yoga on their own. Yoga instructors generally are not licensed or credentialed through a formalized or recognized system. Many schools and teachers register with the Yoga Alliance. Registered teachers typically indicate the level of training they've received with RYT 200 (200 hours) or RYT 500 (500 hours). Hatha yoga is considered the basic form of yoga ( 105372 ). Iyengar yoga uses poses held for 20-30 seconds with props such as blankets, blocks, and straps as needed ( 105376 ). Kundalini, yoga is a large collection of techniques which are also used in some other traditions of yoga. It is perhaps one of the most comprehensive traditions in that it actively uses methods from all "eight limbs" of yoga. In kundalini yoga, movements are generally slower and the use of difficult stretching or challenging postures to build flexibility and strength, as emphasized in some other traditions of yoga, are not the main focus. Basic techniques might involve a seated posture with an erect spine, placing the hands in various positions on the body or the lap, meditating, and breathing in prescribed ways. Physical poses are combined with controlled breathing, and sometimes chanting (mantras), meditations, prayer, visualizations, and guided relaxation ( 99759 , 99760 , 99761 , 105348 , 105372 ).

#### ABDOMINAL SURGERY

"Kapalabhati pranayama" and other aggressive breathing techniques might place excessive pressure on the abdomen and result in an adverse outcome in patients with a recent abdominal surgery.

#### GLAUCOMA

In patients with glaucoma, intraocular pressure has been reported to increase during the practice of inversion asanas or head-down yoga poses such as headstand, downward facing dog, standing forward bend, plough pose, and legs up the wall pose ( 114869 ).

#### HYPERTENSION

"Kapalabhati pranayama" and other aggressive breathing techniques might temporarily increase blood pressure and potentially result in an adverse outcome in patients with existing, uncontrolled hypertension.

#### INTRAOCULAR LENS (IOL) IMPLANTS

Theoretically, prone or face-down positions in yoga might increase the risk of uveitis glaucoma hyphema (UGH) syndrome in patients with IOL implants. Malposition of an IOL occasionally causes UGH. In a case report, a 75-year-old female with IOL implants experienced pain and photophobia following yoga practice and a procedure was needed to fix the implant ( 105369 ).

Yoga is an ancient Indian practice and is a key part of traditional Ayurvedic medicine in India. Yoga uses several exercises involving controlled breathing, meditation, and body posturing ( 99761 , 105372 , 105374 ). Many different styles of yoga exist. These styles use techniques which range from relaxed motions and breathing to intense movements and breathing exercises. The controlled breathing can be rapid or slow. Ancient texts describe rapid breathing as being cleansing and stimulating, while the slow breathing, particularly through alternate nostrils, is thought to be calming ( 28417 ). Kundalini yoga incorporates multiple modalities (physical postures, chanting, meditation, breathing exercises, visualization). It is advocated as a way to keep the body in good condition and train the mind to be resilient and flexible in response to stress and change. Proponents claim that it increases oxygen capacity, boosts blood flow, balances the glandular system, strengthens the nervous system, and increases self-awareness, vitality, peace of mind, concentration, and self-confidence ( 28417 ). Yogoda is a type of meditation-based yoga, or Raja yoga, that is practiced in an effort to achieve self-realization and enlightenment. Raja yoga involves psycho-physical meditation techniques to experience what proponents refer to as "the truth." In essence, yogoda combines spirituality, meditation, and yoga in an attempt to help people reach higher levels of consciousness and live balanced lives. Ultimately, the purpose of yoga is to achieve self-realization or enlightenment. Today yoga is still used for this purpose but is also employed for a variety of medical conditions and to generally maintain good health. There are various proposed mechanisms of action for the health benefits of yoga. Kundalini and other forms of yoga are thought to alter excitation and inhibition in the brain, habituation of reflexes, and homeostasis ( 28418 ). Yoga has been shown to downregulate the sympathetic nervous system and hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis in response to stress ( 28419 , 105372 ). It has been found to reduce serum cortisol levels ( 114867 , 114874 ). It might also increase serum levels of brain-derived neurotrophic factor, increase oxygen flow to the brain, and stimulate brain activity ( 105372 , 114869 ). Yoga has also been shown to reduce pro-inflammatory cytokines and salivary cortisol ( 105365 ).
