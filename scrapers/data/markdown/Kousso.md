# Kousso
Scientific Name Brayera anthelmintica, Hagenia abyssinica (synonym: Banksia abyssinica) Family Rosaceae Other Common Names Agenia Abyssinica, Cossoo, Hagenia, Kooso, Kosso
|

---

## Overview

Kousso is a flowering tree native to the forests of Central and Eastern Africa ( 95928 ). It has been traditionally used as a medicine in Africa, but is now considered an endangered species ( 103942 ).

## History

The dried female flowers of kousso have been used as an anthelmintic in traditional Ethiopian medicine for centuries ( 95928 ). Kousso was brought to Europe in 1645 by a Portuguese priest and added to the European Pharmacopoeia ( 95928 ). The anthelmintic properties of kousso were studied by Merck in the 1870s, and a crystalline compound was isolated and named kosin. Although this was demonstrated to be an artifact of the extraction process, the name "kosins" was applied to active constituents identified later ( 95928 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, kousso is used for diabetes, tapeworm infestations, cancer, and as a homeopathic remedy for nausea. Topically, kousso is used for wound healing.

Possibly Unsafe when used orally. Kousso has been associated with adverse effects such as nausea, diarrhea, fainting, and vision problems ( 18 ).

PREGNANCY: Unsafe when used orally due to abortifacient activity ( 19 , 95928 ).

LACTATION: Unsafe when used orally due to its potential for toxicity ( 18 ).

#### General

Orally, kousso can cause gastrointestinal irritation, resulting in salivation, nausea, and diarrhea. It can also cause headache and general weakness ( 18 ). At high doses, vision disorders, spasms, and syncope can occur, progressing to acidosis, and shock ( 18 ).

#### Cardiovascular

Orally, kousso can cause syncope when used in high doses ( 18 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, kousso can cause gastrointestinal irritation, resulting in salivation, nausea, and diarrhea ( 18 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Orally, kousso can cause headache and general weakness ( 18 ). At high doses, kousso can cause spasms ( 18 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

Orally, vision disorders, possibly due to optic atrophy, have been reported with high doses of kousso ( 18 , 95928 ).

#### ANTIDIABETES DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Theoretically, kousso might have an additive effect with antidiabetes drugs and cause hypoglycemia. Animal research shows that kousso flower extract modestly decreases blood glucose levels in diabetic mice ( 103943 ).

Some antidiabetes drugs include glimepiride (Amaryl), glyburide (DiaBeta, Glynase PresTab, Micronase), insulin, metformin (Glucophage), pioglitazone (Actos), rosiglitazone (Avandia), and others.

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOGLYCEMIC POTENTIAL

Theoretically, kousso might have additive effects with herbs that decrease blood glucose levels. Animal research shows that kousso decreases blood glucose levels ( 103943 ). Herbs with hypoglycemic potential include devil's claw, fenugreek, guar gum, Panax ginseng, and Siberian ginseng.

#### GASTROINTESTINAL (GI) CONDITIONS

Kousso can irritate the GI tract. It is contraindicated in individuals with infectious or inflammatory GI conditions ( 19 ).

#### General

The applicable parts of kousso are the flowers, leaves, and bark. The main active constituents are thought to be phloroglucinol derivatives known as kosins ( 95928 ). Kousso also contains quercetin and derivatives, 3-O-beta-glucuronide, and rutin ( 95928 ). Kousso crude flower extract contains tannins, saponins, terpenoids, flavonoids, phenols, glycosides, plant steroids, and anthraquinones ( 103943 ).

#### Anthelmintic effects

In laboratory tests, non-polar fractions of an extract of dried female kousso flowers, in which the kosin constituents tend to concentrate, have demonstrated anthelmintic activity against blood, liver, and intestinal flatworms ( 95928 ).

#### Anti-cancer effects

The kousso constituents, kosins, have in vitro activity against murine adenocarcinoma cell lines ( 95928 ). Additional in vitro research shows that the ethyl acetate fraction of kousso root has cytotoxic activity against colon, liver, and gastric cancer cells ( 103942 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

In animal research, oral kousso flower extract decreases experimentally-induced paw swelling ( 103941 ).

#### Antidiabetic effects

Traditionally, kousso is used for diabetes. In diabetic mice models, oral kousso flower extract reduces blood glucose, but to a lesser extent than oral glyburide. Theoretically, kousso might work by inhibiting alpha-amylase ( 103943 ).

#### Antidiarrheal effects

In animal research, giving a single dose of an oral methanolic extract of kousso leaves 200 mg/kg or 400 mg/kg, or loperamide 3 mg/kg, before inducing diarrhea with castor oil is associated with decreased intestinal motility, and reduced volume and weight of intestinal contents, when compared with a water control. Defecation is reduced by 56% with kousso 200 mg/kg, 74% with kousso 400 mg/kg, and 88% with loperamide ( 107871 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

In vitro and animal research shows that kousso roots and flowers have antioxidant activity ( 103942 , 103943 ).

#### Wound healing

Traditionally, kousso is used for wound treatment. In animal research, topically applying ointment containing kousso flower extract 5% and 10% reduces wound size and improves the strength of new skin ( 103941 ).
