## Overview

Fasting involves a voluntary severe or complete restriction of energy intake for a period of time. It may be for therapeutic, spiritual, or political reasons. Intermittent fasting, including time-restricted feeding, is a type of fasting that involves periods of restricted energy intake followed by a period of eating ad libitum, and has become popular as a dietary strategy for improving health and losing weight ( 95492 , 102736 , 102741 , 102745 , 102755 ).

Possibly Safe when used appropriately, short-term. Intermittent fasting, involving severe energy restriction and/or time-restricted feeding for periods of up to 12 weeks, has been practiced for up to one year with no reported serious adverse outcomes ( 95486 , 95488 , 102731 , 102732 , 102734 , 102737 , 102739 , 102744 , 102745 , 102746 )( 102748 , 102753 , 105103 , 105106 , 105107 , 105110 , 105112 , 105114 , 108366 , 112987 )( 112990 , 116002 ). ...when water-only fasting is undertaken for up to 22 days with pre-fast energy reduction and refeeding protocols under medical supervision ( 102755 ). There is insufficient reliable information about the safety of intermittent fasting when used for longer than 12 months.

Likely Unsafe when prolonged water-only fasting is undertaken without medical supervision. Deaths have been reported ( 102755 ).

PREGNANCY: There is insufficient reliable information about the safety of intermittent fasting during pregnancy. While there is some evidence that fasting during Ramadan might be safe ( 102754 ), most fetal outcomes have not been adequately assessed. Also, no studies have evaluated the safety of other types of fasting or fasting for the duration of pregnancy. Stay on the safe side and avoid fasting.

LACTATION: Insufficient available evidence; avoid using.

#### General

Intermittent fasting has not been associated with serious adverse effects in clinical trials. Water-only fasting has been associated with a higher frequency and severity of adverse effects.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Orally : Bad breath, constipation, dizziness, fatigue, headache, hunger, lightheadedness, nausea, pain, reduced energy, and sleep disturbance.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Orally : Arrhythmia, dehydration, fever, hyponatremia, increased blood pressure, and palpitations.

#### Cardiovascular

Dehydration resulting in headache, increased blood pressure, and fever has occurred rarely with medically supervised water-only fasting. Palpitations have occurred rarely ( 102755 ). Medically supervised very low-calorie fasting has resulted rarely in mild transitory arrhythmia ( 102753 ).

#### Dermatologic

Medically supervised very low-calorie fasting has resulted rarely in skin rash ( 102753 ). Hair loss has been reported in clinical trials of intermittent fasting ( 102746 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Medically supervised water-only fasting has resulted in nausea in about 32% of patients. Dyspepsia, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and vomiting have occurred less commonly ( 102755 ). Vomiting, nausea, diarrhea, dyspepsia, bloating, bad breath, and dry mouth have also occurred rarely with medically supervised very low-calorie fasting ( 102753 ). The main adverse effect of the intermittent fasting diet is hunger, which generally resolves with time ( 102741 , 102748 , 108359 , 108366 , 108373 , 112990 ). Constipation, mild nausea, bloating, and bad breath have also been reported with intermittent fasting ( 102737 , 102741 , 102744 , 102745 , 102746 , 108366 , 108373 ).

#### Hematologic

Hyponatremia has occurred rarely with medically supervised water-only fasting ( 102755 ). Transitory hypoglycemia and bleeding gums have occurred rarely with medically supervised very low-calorie fasting ( 102753 ). Hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia are not more common with intermittent fasting when compared with continuous energy restriction ( 102736 , 108366 ).

#### Immunologic

Medically supervised very low-calorie fasting and intermittent fasting have resulted rarely in infections ( 102746 , 102753 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

Medically supervised water-only fasting has resulted in back pain in about 25% of patients. Other pain in extremities has also occurred ( 102755 ). Medically supervised very low-calorie fasting has resulted rarely in muscle cramps, restless legs, and back pain ( 102753 ). Muscle fatigue has been reported in clinical trials of intermittent fasting ( 102746 ). Clinical research in resistance-trained non-obese adults shows that muscle strength and endurance during intermittent fasting is no different than during continuous energy restriction ( 108364 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Medically supervised water-only fasting has resulted in fatigue in about 50% of patients. Insomnia, lightheadedness, and headache were also fairly common ( 102755 ). Medically supervised very low-calorie fasting has also resulted rarely in cold sensitivity, headache, dizziness, blurred vision, fatigue, and sleep disturbance ( 102753 ). Headache, reduced energy, sleep disturbance, cold sensitivity, dizziness, lightheadedness, irritability, fatigue, malaise, and lack of concentration have been reported in clinical trials of intermittent fasting ( 102737 , 102741 , 102742 , 102744 , 102745 , 108358 , 108366 , 108373 , 116002 ). Clinical research in resistance-trained non-obese adults shows that mood, sleep quality, and cognitive function during intermittent fasting is no different than during continuous energy restriction ( 108364 , 108365 ).

Possibly Effective
Metabolic syndrome.

Intermittent fasting for 2-4 months appears to improve body weight and waist circumference in patients with metabolic syndrome. It is unclear if fasting improves cardiometabolic parameters.

A small clinical study in adults with metabolic syndrome shows that intermittent fasting for 16 hours daily for 12 weeks reduces body weight by around 8 kg, body mass index (BMI) by 3 kg/m 2 , glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by 0.3%, fasting blood glucose by 15 mg/dL, systolic blood pressure (SBP) by 7 mmHg, diastolic blood pressure (DBP) by 5 mmHg, total cholesterol by 29 mg/dL, low density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol by 17 mg/dL, and triglycerides by 42 mg/dL when compared to baseline 105106). Another small clinical study in this population shows that alternate-day fasting for 8 weeks reduces body weight by 2.4 kg, waist circumference by 3 cm, fasting blood glucose by 5 mg/dL, and SBP by 12 mmHg, but does not improve BMI, lipid levels, or DBP, when compared with continuous energy restriction ( 102732 ). Neither study showed an improvement in high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels with intermittent or alternate-day fasting ( 102732 , 105106 ). A meta-analysis of six small clinical trials in patients with metabolic syndrome, including the studies described above, shows that intermittent fasting for 2-4 months reduces body weight by 2.5 kg, BMI by 1.56 kg/m 2 , and waist circumference by 3 cm when compared with various control diets, including continuous energy restriction, the DASH diet, and the Mediterranean diet. However, intermittent fasting did not improve glycemic control, blood pressure, or lipid levels in these patients ( 110467 ).

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

Several clinical studies suggest that intermittent fasting might improve body weight, liver enzyme levels, and hepatic steatosis and stiffness  in patients with NAFLD.

Meta-analyses of up to 10 small to large, moderate to high quality clinical studies in patients with NAFLD show that intermittent fasting for 4 to 52 weeks improves body weight, fat mass, waist circumference, waist-to-hip ratio, liver enzyme levels, hepatic steatosis and stiffness, serum triglycerides, low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, and insulin resistance when compared with control diets or baseline measures ( 112987 , 116000 ). However, meta-analyses also show that intermittent fasting does not seem to improve high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, fasting blood glucose, or blood pressure in patients with NAFLD ( 102731 , 110470 , 110535 , 110536 , 110537 , 112987 ). The specific intermittent fasting regimens followed in these studies include alternate-day fasting with or without exercise ( 102731 , 110470 , 112987 ), time-restricted fasting ( 102731 , 110535 , 112987 ), 5:2 periodic fasting ( 110536 , 112987 ), and Ramadan fasting ( 110537 , 112987 ). A network meta-analysis of these clinical studies shows that the 5:2 periodic fasting regimen is more effective for improving liver stiffness, time-restricted fasting is more effective for liver steatosis, and that the various regimens are similarly effective, overall, for improving liver enzymes, serum lipids, insulin resistance, and body composition ( 116000 ). Additionally, observational research has found that fasting for a mean of 8.5 days reduces the percentage of individuals in a high-risk fatty liver index category by 45%, with the greatest benefit in those with type 2 diabetes ( 102733 ).

Obesity.

Intermittent fasting seems to be beneficial for weight loss in adults with overweight or obesity.

Clinical research in adults with overweight or obesity shows that intermittent fasting for 1-24 months can reduce weight by about 2-14 kg or up to 14.7% and fat mass by about 2-4 kg when compared with control  ( 105105 , 108360 , 108362 , 108366 , 108369 , 115996 , 115998 ). The specific intermittent fasting regimens followed in most clinical studies include alternate-day fasting (ADF), time-restricted fasting (TRF), and 5:2 or 4:3 periodic fasting. The control diets were either usual diet or continuous energy restriction. An umbrella review and network meta-analysis of up to 10 meta-analyses of clinical studies shows that the reduction in body weight and fat mass was more pronounced when intermittent fasting was compared to usual diets than when compared with continuous energy restriction ( 115998 ). However, the majority of included clinical studies are graded low to very low quality of evidence. Intermittent fasting has also been evaluated in adolescents. A clinical study in adolescents with obesity shows that following an intermittent fasting diet reduces body mass index by about 0.3 kg/m 2 when compared to baseline, but not when compared to continuous energy restriction ( 116006 ). An umbrella review of meta-analyses of clinical studies mostly in adults with overweight or obesity shows that some types of intermittent fasting protocols improve metabolic outcomes; though to a small, potentially non-clinically relevant, degree. Specifically, pooling of up to 9 meta-analyses shows that ADF and 5:2/4:3 protocols reduce systolic blood pressure by about 4-6 mmHg and serum triglyceride levels by about 11-18 mg/dL, ADF reduces diastolic blood pressure by about 3.5 mmHg, total cholesterol by about 11 mg/dL, and low-density lipoprotein cholesterol by about 6 mg/dL when compared with usual diets, and TRE reduces fasting blood glucose by about 2.5 mg/dL and total cholesterol by about 6 mg/dL when compared with usual diets or continuous energy restriction ( 115998 ). Most clinical research shows that intermittent fasting does not increase high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels ( 112989 , 115996 , 115998 ). Intermittent fasting has also been evaluated in combination with exercise training. Meta-analyses of clinical studies in adults with normal weight, overweight, or obesity shows that intermittent fasting in combination with exercise training for 1-12 months reduces body weight by about 2-3 kg and fat mass by about 1 kg when compared with control diets in combination with exercise training ( 108361 , 115995 , 115997 , 115999 , 116001 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Acne.

Although there is interest in using fasting for various types of acne including hidradenitis suppurativa and acne vulgaris, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of fasting for this purpose.

Aging.

It is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for improving mood changes associated with aging.

Preliminary clinical research in healthy males over 50 years of age shows that fasting 2 days weekly and following a calorie-restricted diet on other days of the week for 12 weeks reduces self-reported feelings of anger, tension, and mood disturbances when compared with a regular diet ( 95487 ).

Athletic performance.

It is unclear if fasting is beneficial or detrimental for athletic performance. The effect of fasting on athletic performance seems to depend on the type of fasting, the overall daily energy intake, and/or the baseline performance level of the athlete.

Clinical research suggests that some types of fasting may worsen athletic performance while others may not have this effect. One clinical study in active healthy male adults shows that intermittent fasting from dawn to dusk for 3 days reduces sprint speed and power when compared with regular eating ( 95483 ). Preliminary clinical research in elite football athletes also shows that daily Ramadan intermittent fasting from sunrise to sunset increases muscle fatigue and decreases muscle power when compared with baseline ( 95484 ). However, time-restricted feeding, which allows for adequate daily energy intake, does not seem to worsen muscle strength in resistance trained males or females ( 95491 , 102737 ) or endurance capacity in trained males ( 105107 ). Also, a small clinical trial in active females shows that combining a time-restricted feeding intermittent diet with high-intensity interval training (HIIT) for 8 weeks increases the counter movement jump distance, with no effect on hand grip strength or measures of cycling power, when compared with HIIT alone ( 108363 ).

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

Although there is interest in using fasting for atopic dermatitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of fasting for this purpose.

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

It is unclear if fasting is beneficial for the prevention of CVD or CVD-related events.

Observational research has found that routine fasting for 24 hours once monthly is associated with a 35% reduced odds of coronary artery disease, which can lead to coronary heart disease, when compared with not fasting ( 95486 , 95490 , 95493 ). Fasting is also of interest for the prevention of cardiovascular events. However, clinical research investigating the effects of fasting on prevention of CVD mortality, stroke, heart failure, and myocardial infarction is lacking. Also, most research does not show that fasting improves markers of CVD risk ( 108360 ).

Chemotherapy-induced anemia.

It is unclear if therapeutic fasting is beneficial for reducing chemotherapy-induced anemia.

One very small clinical trial in patients undergoing chemotherapy who were not malnourished shows that short-term (48 hours) therapeutic fasting ameliorates chemotherapy-related reductions in erythrocyte counts when compared with a normal control diet ( 112988 ).

Chemotherapy-induced leukopenia.

It is unclear if therapeutic fasting is beneficial for reducing chemotherapy-induced leukopenia.

A meta-analysis of 3 clinical studies in patients undergoing chemotherapy who were not malnourished shows that short-term (48-96 hours) therapeutic fasting does not reduce chemotherapy-related neutropenia when compared with normal control diets ( 112988 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

It is unclear if therapeutic fasting is beneficial for reducing chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

A systematic review of clinical and observational studies in patients undergoing chemotherapy who were not malnourished suggests that short-term (48-96 hours) therapeutic fasting may help reduce nausea and vomiting when compared with normal control diets ( 112988 ). However, this benefit was only observed in one clinical trial and one observational study. The validity of this finding is limited by study heterogeneity and small sample sizes.

Chemotherapy-related fatigue.

It is unclear if therapeutic fasting is beneficial for reducing chemotherapy-related fatigue.

A systematic review of clinical and observational studies in patients undergoing chemotherapy who were not malnourished suggests that short-term (48-96 hours) therapeutic fasting may help improve chemotherapy-related fatigue when compared with normal control diets ( 112988 ). However, this benefit was only observed in one clinical trial and one observational study. The validity of this finding is limited by study heterogeneity and small sample sizes.

Chronic kidney disease (CKD).

It is unclear if Ramadan fasting or time-restricted feeding, a type of intermittent fasting, is beneficial for CKD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with type 2 diabetes and stable moderate CKD shows that Ramadan fasting, from sunrise to sunset, for 12-29 days does not affect weight, blood pressure, or glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels when compared with not fasting ( 102752 ). A small clinical trial in patients with moderate to severe CKD and overweight and obesity shows that following a time-restricted feeding diet where all daily calories are consumed over an 8-hour window daily for 12 weeks improves estimated glomerular filtration rate, serum uric acid levels and body weight when compared with a control diet ( 112990 ).

Cognitive impairment.

It is unclear if fasting is beneficial for cognitive impairment in older adults.

Observational research in adults at least 60 years of age with mild cognitive impairment shows that fasting intermittently twice weekly for 3 years is associated with improvements in mini mental state exam (MMSE) scores and overall cognitive status when compared with those who do not fast ( 105108 ).

Diabetes.

Some clinical research shows that some intermittent fasting diets may improve glycemic control and modestly reduce body weight in patients with type 2 diabetes. However, it is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for patients with gestational diabetes or for preventing diabetes.

A meta-analysis of up to 9 clinical studies in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that following an intermittent fasting diet for 3-12 months reduces glycated hemoglobin by about 1.2% and body weight by about 4 kg when compared with control. The specific intermittent fasting regimens followed in most of the studies included alternate-day fasting, time-restricted fasting, and 5:2 or 4:3 periodic fasting. The control diets were either usual diets or continuous energy restriction ( 115994 ). An open-label clinical study in adults with type 2 diabetes shows that following time-restricted fasting three days weekly for 3 months reduces fasting blood glucose by about 30 mg/dL, glycated hemoglobin by about 0.5%, and body weight by about 3-4 % when compared with usual diet control ( 116005 ). Additionally, a large, open-label clinical study in adults newly diagnosed with type 2 diabetes shows that the 5:2 intermittent fasting diet for 16 weeks reduces glycated hemoglobin by 1.9% when compared to baseline, but only by 0.3% when compared with metformin and by 0.4% when compared with empagliflozin. Approximately 80% of patients on the fasting diet achieved a glycated hemoglobin below 6.5% while only 55-60% on metformin and empagliflozin achieved this. Additionally, the fasting diet reduces body weight by about 4 kg when compared with metformin and empagliflozin ( 116003 ). The interpretation of results in the latter study is limited by the lack of a placebo or usual diet control. The effects of Ramadan fasting in patients with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease (CKD) have also been evaluated in a small clinical study, with Ramadan fasting from sunrise to sunset for 12-29 days showing no improvement in HbA1c when compared with not fasting ( 102752 ). Whether intermittent fasting might prevent diabetes has not been investigated in clinical research. However, observational research has found that fasting for 24 hours once monthly is associated with a 44% reduced odds of type 2 diabetes when compared with not fasting ( 95486 , 95490 ). One clinical study in overweight or obese adults with a history of gestational diabetes shows that intermittent fasting two days each week for 12 months modestly reduces body weight. Although there were some modest beneficial changes in glycemic indices after 3 months, beneficial effects on fasting serum glucose or insulin, HbA1c, or insulin resistance were not maintained ( 108367 ).

Epilepsy.

It is unclear if fasting is beneficial for reducing seizure frequency in patients with various types of epilepsy.

Observational research in patients with focal, general tonic, and absence seizure disorders has found that intermittent fasting from dawn to sunset for 30 days is associated with a reduction in self-reported seizure frequency when compared with the previous 30 days. The average fasting period was approximately 16 hours. Subgroup analyses suggest that intermittent fasting is not effective for reducing self-reported seizure frequency in patients with myoclonic seizures ( 105113 ).

Fibromyalgia.

Although there is interest in using fasting for fibromyalgia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of fasting for this condition.

Hyperlipidemia.

It is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for hyperlipidemia.

Although some limited research shows that intermittent fasting lowers levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and triglycerides by a small amount when compared with baseline, most research does not support this finding. Also, most clinical research shows that intermittent fasting does not increase high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels ( 102732 , 102744 , 102745 , 102748 , 102753 , 105105 , 105109 , 105116 , 108358 , 108360 )( 108368 , 110464 ). Most of this research has evaluated patients without hyperlipidemia; effects in patients with hyperlipidemia, specifically, are unclear.

Hypertension.

It is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for reducing blood pressure.

According to a recent scientific statement by the American Heart Association, some limited clinical research shows that intermittent fasting modestly reduces systolic and/or diastolic blood pressure in some populations. However, any benefit appears to be minimal and does not seem to be greater than in those experiencing weight loss from continuous energy restriction ( 108372 ). More research is needed to determine the benefits of intermittent fasting in patients with hypertension.

Impaired glucose tolerance (prediabetes).

It is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for prediabetes.

A meta-analysis of up to 5 clinical studies in patients with prediabetes shows that following an intermittent fasting diet for 1-6 months reduces body weight by about 5 kg but does not reduce glycated hemoglobin by a clinically significant degree, when compared with control. The specific intermittent fasting regimens followed in most of the studies included alternate-day fasting, time-restricted fasting, and 5:2 periodic fasting. The control diets were either usual diets or continuous energy restriction ( 115994 ).

Insomnia.

It is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for insomnia.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with obesity shows that time-restricted feeding over 4 or 6 hours does not reduce insomnia severity or improve sleep quality when compared with no dietary changes ( 108370 ).

Myocardial infarction (MI).

It is unclear if an intermittent fasting diet is beneficial for improving cardiac function in patients with recent MI.

A small, open-label clinical study in patients with a recent ST-segment-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) shows that following a time-restricted intermittent fasting eating plan for 3 months improves left ventricular ejection fraction and reduces diastolic, but not systolic, blood pressure when compared with usual diet control ( 116004 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Although there is interest in using fasting for osteoarthritis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of fasting for this condition.

Pain (chronic).

Although there is interest in using fasting for chronic pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of fasting for this purpose.

Physical performance.

It is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for improving physical performance in older adults.

Observational research in adults at least 64 years of age has found that intermittently fasting for long periods each day is associated with a reduction in standing balance and ability to rise from a chair when compared with intermittently fasting for 9 hours or less daily ( 105104 ).

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

It is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for PCOS.

A small clinical study in patients with anovulatory PCOS shows that time-restricted feeding for 5 weeks modestly reduces body weight by 1.3 kg, as well as body fat, when compared with baseline. There were also modest beneficial changes in measures of insulin resistance, but not fasting blood glucose or lipid levels. Menstrual cycle improvements occurred in 73% of participants ( 108359 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Psoriasis.

It is unclear if Ramadan fasting is beneficial for psoriasis.

Some research has found an association between Ramadan fasting and reduced severity of plaque psoriasis. In patients with stable disease, psoriasis severity was modestly reduced after Ramadan when compared with baseline. The level of benefit is possibly related to the specific treatment used at baseline and is not necessarily associated with weight loss ( 108373 , 108374 ).

Psoriatic arthritis.

It is unclear if Ramadan fasting is beneficial for psoriatic arthritis.

Some research has found an association between Ramadan fasting and reduced severity of psoriatic arthritis. In patients on stable treatment, the severity of psoriasis and arthritis symptoms were modestly reduced after Ramadan when compared with baseline. Benefits were not related to weight loss ( 108375 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if Ramadan fasting is beneficial in patients with RA.

A small clinical study in patients with RA shows that Ramadan fasting reduces disease activity scores and markers of inflammation, but not tender or swollen joint counts or pain, when compared to these measures 6 months before the beginning of Ramadan ( 110538 ). The validity of these findings is limited by a lack of control group.

Sleep apnea.

It is unclear if intermittent fasting is beneficial for sleep apnea.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with obesity shows that time-restricted feeding over 4 or 6 hours does not reduce sleep apnea occurrence when compared with no dietary changes ( 108370 ).

More evidence is needed to rate fasting for these uses.

Fasting involves a voluntary severe or complete restriction of energy intake for a period of time. It may be for therapeutic, spiritual, or political reasons. Medically supervised water-only or very low-calorie (up to 500 kcal daily) continuous fasts are sometimes used therapeutically. Well-designed clinical research is generally lacking. Some severe fasting protocols have pre-fasting periods of approximately 2 days where the diet is limited to fruits and vegetables, as well as a post-fast refeeding period to reintroduce solid food ( 102755 ). In clinical trials, the most commonly evaluated form of fasting is intermittent fasting or intermittent energy restriction. Intermittent fasting involves periods of restricted energy intake followed by a period of eating ad libitum. This type of fasting has become popular as a dietary strategy for improving health and losing weight and involves energy restriction on alternating days, for 1-4 days per week, or within a specific range of time ( 95492 , 102736 , 102741 , 102745 , 102755 , 108360 ). Three of the most common types of intermittent fasting are alternate-day fasting, periodic fasting, and time-restricted feeding. Alternate-day fasting : Alternate-day fasting involves fasting followed by "feasting", on alternate days. On fasting days, less than or equal to 25% of baseline energy is consumed during that 24-hour period. On feasting days, food is consumed as desired. This dietary pattern is repeated. Alternate-day fasting (zero calories on fasting day) is sometimes differentiated from modified alternate-day fasting (25% or less of maintenance calories) ( 95481 , 95490 , 108360 ). Periodic fasting : Periodic fasting involves very low-calorie intake for 1-6 days weekly, followed by consuming food as desired for the remainder of the week. The most common type of periodic fasting is the 5:2 diet, which includes two days each week with a very low caloric intake ( 95481 , 95490 , 95492 , 102736 , 102741 , 102745 , 108360 ). Occasionally, the phases of fasting last 2 weeks or more ( 102747 ). Periodic fasting is sometimes also known as full-day fasting as it consists of one or two days of complete fasting per week ( 108363 ). Time-restricted feeding : Time-restricted feeding involves consuming caloric needs by eating ad libitum during a certain time frame, while the remaining hours of the day are spent fasting. While time frames may vary, maximum fasting time frames of up to 16 hours may be considered ( 95491 , 108358 , 108359 ). Some types of time-restricted feeding are called early time-restricted eating due to the consumption of food only until early afternoon ( 108358 ). Religious fasts : The Ramadan fast is also a type of intermittent fasting. Ramadan fasts involve fasting for one month a year from sunrise to sunset with the number of hours dependent on season and location ( 108360 ). Each type of fasting generally results in an overall reduction in caloric intake. It is unknown what percentage of caloric reduction produces the most desirable results. However, some pooled research suggests that a 20% reduction in calories via intermittent fasting and/or calorie restriction results in greater loss of fat mass when compared with a 20% to 40% reduction in calorie consumption. The reason for this surprising finding is unclear. It could be simply because a smaller restriction in calories is easier to maintain over time than a larger restriction ( 95489 ).

Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Anecdotal evidence)

Fasting may increase the risk of bleeding if used with anticoagulant or antiplatelet drugs.

Fasting has been found to increase the international normalized ratio (INR) in patients stabilized on warfarin ( 102753 , 102795 , 102796 ). Closely monitor patients who are fasting while taking anticoagulant or antiplatelet drugs. Dose adjustment may be necessary.

#### ANTIDIABETES DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence B (Lower quality RCT)

Fasting might increase the risk of hypoglycemia if used with antidiabetes drugs.

Hypoglycemic events have been reported during intermittent fasting, including in patients using antidiabetes drugs. The risk of having a hypoglycemic event is largest for patients taking insulin or sulfonylureas. The risk of hypoglycemic events seems to be lower for patients taking other antidiabetes drugs such as glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonist (GLP- 1RA), metformin, thiazolidinediones, dipeptidyl peptidase-4 inhibitors (DPP4 inhibitors), and sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitors ( 102736 , 102741 , 102752 , 102797 , 102798 , 108362 ). Advise patients to monitor glucose levels closely. Dose or time adjustments may be necessary.

#### INSULIN
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence PROBABLE Level of Evidence B (Lower quality RCT)

Fasting might increase the risk of hypoglycemia if used with insulin.

Hypoglycemic events have been reported during intermittent fasting. The risk of a hypoglycemic event seems to be highest in patients using insulin or sulfonylureas ( 102736 , 102741 , 102752 , 102797 , 102798 ). Patients with type 2 diabetes and other comorbidities, or those with type 1 diabetes, should be strongly advised to avoid fasting while taking insulin. For patients taking insulin who insist on fasting, advise these patients to monitor glucose levels closely. Dose or time adjustments may be necessary.

#### General

Fasting involves the voluntary restriction of energy intake by varying amounts for predefined periods of time. With intermittent fasting, these periods of restricted energy intake are then followed by a period of eating ad libitum. There may or may not be restrictions placed on the type or amount of food eaten during the ad libitum period. By decreasing overall energy intake, fasting is thought to improve some aspects of health.

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials shows that intermittent fasting lowers C-reactive protein (CRP) levels, but not tumor necrosis factor-alpha or interleukin-6 levels, when compared with control diets. The reduction in CRP levels were small overall, but more pronounced in those with obesity ( 105117 ). Some other clinical research agrees with these findings ( 108359 , 108375 ). Observational research also shows that intermittent fasting twice a week is associated with lower levels of CRP when compared with people who do not fast ( 105108 ). However, not all research agrees ( 108360 ).

#### Anticancer effects

Observational research in adults with metabolic syndrome suggests that intermittent fasting for at least 14 hours per day for 4 weeks increases serum levels of anti-tumor proteomics when compared with baseline ( 105103 ).

#### Antiepileptic effects

There is interest in using intermittent fasting to reduce seizure frequency. Some researchers have suggested that intermittent fasting may cause fat catabolism and formation of ketone bodies, similar to a ketogenic diet, but this mechanism has not been well studied ( 105113 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

Preliminary clinical research in females with obesity shows that alternate-day fasting reduces skeletal muscle levels of reactive oxygen species. Researchers have suggested that fasting may directly reduce reactive oxygen species production, while others suggest that ketone formation from fat catabolism may scavenge reactive oxygen species ( 105110 ). In addition, observational research shows that intermittent fasting twice a week is associated with improved levels of oxidative stress markers, including lower malondialdehyde levels and higher superoxide dismutase levels, when compared with people who do not fast ( 105108 ).

#### Glucose effects

The effect of intermittent fasting on glucose control is not clear. Some research shows that intermittent fasting increases postprandial glucose and non-esterified fatty acid levels, with no effect on insulin levels. However, other research suggests that there is no effect on fasting blood glucose, postprandial glucose, non-esterified fatty acid levels, or insulin levels ( 95485 , 102749 , 105103 , 105106 ), while other research suggests that fasting insulin levels are reduced ( 102741 ). The effect of intermittent fasting on weight loss may lead to other health benefits, including reduced blood pressure, improved insulin resistance, and other improvements of cardiovascular and metabolic risk factors. However, results are not consistent and overall any benefit may be no more than what occurs with a continuous energy restriction diet ( 102741 , 102749 , 102751 , 102753 ).

#### Muscular effects

There is interest in using intermittent fasting strategies to alter body composition. Some clinical research shows that trained athletes fasting for 16 hours per day and consuming required daily calories as three meals during the remaining 8 hours of the day for a duration of 8 weeks reduced their fat mass by 16%, compared to a 3% reduction in the control group that consumed the required daily calories as three meals over 13 hours. There were no differences between groups in changes to non-fat mass. Additionally, the intermittent fasting group was found to have an increase in insulin resistance and adiponectin, and a decrease in leptin and insulin levels ( 95491 , 95494 ).

#### Weight loss effects

Intermittent fasting is thought to improve weight loss by reducing overall energy intake. Although fasting may lead to modest overconsumption of energy on non-fasting days, most research shows that the total net intake of calories is still lower during intermittent fasting when compared to a normal diet ( 95481 , 95485 ). However, preliminary clinical research in trained male athletes fasting for 16 hours and consuming 4800 kcal during the remaining 8 hours of the day for a duration of 4 weeks reduced their weight by 2% when compared with baseline. In contrast, trained male athletes consuming 4800 kcal without time-restricted feeding did not reduce their weight when compared with baseline ( 105107 ). A meta-analysis of clinical trials in adults shows that intermittent fasting reduces leptin levels when compared with control diets. Leptin is a hormone released from adipose tissue that regulates energy homeostasis and is associated with a higher body fat percentage and body mass index. The greatest reductions were seen in obese individuals and with intermittent fasting regimens lasting at least 12 weeks. However, a subgroup analysis suggests that the effects of intermittent fasting on leptin levels are largely due to a reduction in energy intake rather than a particular diet. This meta-analysis also shows that reduced energy intake, but not intermittent fasting, increases adiponectin levels. The validity of this meta-analysis is limited by high heterogeneity ( 105111 ). In contrast, some observational research in adults with metabolic syndrome suggests that intermittent fasting for at least 14 hours per day for 4 weeks does not affect serum levels of leptin or adiponectin when compared with baseline ( 105103 ). Intermittent fasting also seems to lower postprandial concentrations of acylated ghrelin, with no effect on glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1). Ghrelin and GLP-1 are gut hormones that change before and after eating and are theorized to influence appetite ( 95485 ). However, a meta-analysis of intermittent fasting in overweight and obese adults shows that fasting 14-18 hours per day for up to 12 weeks does not reduce serum ghrelin levels when compared with control ( 105116 ). In addition, fasting reduces levels of the anabolic hormone insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1) ( 102750 ). Clinical research in resistance-trained non-obese adults shows that intermittent fasting is associated with increased levels of peptide YY when compared with continuous energy restriction. Peptide YY is involved with appetite reduction ( 108364 ). There is some speculation that a possible mechanism of action related to intermittent fasting regimens include changes to the microbiome ( 108360 ).
