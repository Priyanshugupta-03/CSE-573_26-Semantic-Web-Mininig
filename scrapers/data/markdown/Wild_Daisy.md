## Overview

Wild daisy is the common species of daisy. It is native to Europe, northern Africa, and parts of Asia ( 98489 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of wild daisy.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

There is currently a limited amount of information on the adverse effects of wild daisy. A thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Bleeding.

Although there has been interest in using oral wild daisy for bleeding, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of wild daisy for this purpose.

Bronchitis.

Although there has been interest in using oral wild daisy for bronchitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of wild daisy for this purpose.

Cough.

Although there has been interest in using oral wild daisy for cough, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of wild daisy for this purpose.

Wounds . Although there has been interest in using topical wild daisy for wounds, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of wild daisy for this purpose.

More evidence is needed to rate wild daisy for these uses.

#### Adult
Oral:

Research is limited; typical dosing is unavailable. Traditionally, wild daisy tea, prepared by steeping 2 teaspoons of dried wild daisy in 300 mL of boiling water for 20 minutes, has been taken orally 2-4 times daily ( 18 ).

#### CROSS-ALLERGENICITY

Theoretically, wild daisy might cause an allergic reaction in individuals sensitive to the Asteraceae/Compositae family ( 12842 ). Members of this family include ragweed, chrysanthemums, marigolds, daisies, and many other herbs.

None known.

#### General

The applicable parts of wild daisy are the aerial parts, especially the flower. Constituents of wild daisy include saponins, including triterpene saponins such as perennisosides and bellissaponins, as well as flavonoids and alcohol glycosides ( 34183 , 34185 , 34195 , 98489 ).

#### Antifungal

In vitro and animal research suggests that wild daisy extract has antifungal activity ( 34189 ).

#### Dermatological effects

Topically, wild daisy is used for wounds and skin diseases. In vitro research shows that wild daisy extract and isolated triterpene saponins promote collagen synthesis in human dermal fibroblasts ( 98489 ).

#### Photoprotective effects

In vitro research in keratinocytes shows that incubation with wild daisy extract 1% reduces interleukin-6 expression and cytotoxicity after exposure to ultraviolet A radiation when compared with control ( 111443 ).
