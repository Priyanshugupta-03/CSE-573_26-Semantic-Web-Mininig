# Reiki Therapy
Other Common Names Bioenergy Therapy, Biofield Energy Therapy, Buddhist Reiki, Energy Healing, Energy Health, Energy Medicine, Energy Work, Healing Touch, Japanese Reiki, Ray-kee, Reiki, Reiki Therapie, Reiki Touch Therapy, Terapia Reiki, Therapeutic Touch, Touch Therapy, Universal Life Energy
|

---

Caution

Reiki therapy should not be confused with other Energy Therapies .

## Overview

Reiki therapy is a type of energy medicine that involves the practitioner channeling "universal life energy" to the patient through touch and positive intention of healing. Reiki is a Japanese word that derives from the individual terms "rei", meaning universal energy, and "ki", meaning life energy ( 96651 ). The Reiki practitioner lays hands on or above the recipient's body to transfer Reiki into the recipient's body ( 96653 ). Because practitioners intentionally transfer energy flow to the patient and so are, necessarily, aware of the treatment they are administering, most studies are only single blind.

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Reiki therapy has been safely used in numerous clinical studies ( 15797 , 15798 , 15799 , 15800 , 15801 , 15802 , 15803 , 88468 , 88469 , 88470 )( 96657 , 113527 , 113528 , 113529 , 113530 , 113531 , 113533 , 113534 , 113536 , 118046 ). No significant adverse effects have been identified.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no reason to expect adverse effects in lactating or pregnant women.

#### General

Reiki is generally well tolerated. No adverse effects have been reported in clinical research.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Alzheimer disease.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with Alzheimer disease.

A very small clinical study in patients with mild cognitive impairment or mild Alzheimer disease shows that receiving weekly Reiki therapy from master-level practitioners improves memory and decreases behavioral problems when compared with no treatment ( 15800 ).

Anxiety.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for reducing anxiety in hospitalized patients.

A small clinical study in females reporting to the hospital with pain, anxiety, and gynecological complaints shows that receiving a 25-minute Reiki therapy session from a master-level practitioner modestly reduces pain and anxiety when compared to baseline ( 104004 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a control group.

Cancer-related fatigue.

Small clinical studies suggest that Reiki therapy may modestly improve fatigue and quality of life in patients with cancer-related fatigue.

A small crossover study in patients undergoing cancer treatment shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 1 hour daily for 5 days reduces feelings of fatigue and improves quality of life when compared with resting for 1 hour daily ( 15798 ). Another small, low-quality clinical study in cancer patients shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 25-30 minutes once daily for 3 days modestly improves fatigue and pain when compared with no intervention or listening to a guided imagery recording ( 104005 ). Reiki therapy has also been given intermittently during chemotherapy treatment. A small clinical study in patients with breast cancer shows that receiving a 30-minute Reiki therapy session during four chemotherapy treatments improves fatigue when compared with usual care, but not when compared with the intervention of companionship ( 96657 ). Another small clinical study in patients with breast cancer shows that receiving a 42-minute Reiki therapy session during three consecutive chemotherapy sessions, plus daily Reiki therapy for 3 days following the first chemotherapy session, improves fatigue and quality of life when compared with no treatment ( 107424 ). Reiki therapy has also been evaluated during palliative care. A large clinical study in adults with stage III or IV cancer receiving palliative care shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 20 minutes twice weekly for 4 weeks reduces fatigue when compared with no intervention ( 113529 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study and the lack of a sham Reiki placebo group.

Cancer-related pain.

Small clinical studies suggest that Reiki therapy may modestly improve pain in patients with cancer, although it is unclear if it reduces analgesic use in these patients.

A small, low-quality clinical study in patients with cancer shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 25-30 minutes once daily for 3 days modestly improves pain and fatigue when compared with listening to a guided imagery recording, or when compared with no intervention ( 104005 ). Other small clinical studies in patients with cancer shows that Reiki therapy might produce short-term pain relief and improve quality of life when compared to baseline ( 15799 , 15803 , 96653 ); however, Reiki therapy does not seem to reduce the use of opioids in these patients. The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a control group. A large clinical study in adults with stage III or IV cancer receiving palliative care shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 20 minutes twice weekly for 4 weeks reduces pain by around 3 points on an 11-point visual analog scale and decreases analgesic use when compared with no intervention ( 113529 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study and the lack of a sham Reiki therapy placebo group.

Cerebral palsy.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for cerebral palsy.

A small, uncontrolled clinical study in 13 children with cerebral palsy shows that receiving Reiki therapy for approximately 30-45 minutes once weekly for 8 weeks decreases pain while lying down, but not while sitting, and may decrease anger frequency when compared to baseline. Qualitatively, when surveyed, most parents indicated that they perceived their children to be calmer and more relaxed after receiving Reiki therapy ( 113537 ). The validity of this study is limited by its small sample size, use of parent-reported outcomes, and the lack of a comparator group.

Cognitive impairment.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with mild cognitive impairment.

A very small clinical study in patients with mild cognitive impairment or mild Alzheimer disease shows that receiving weekly Reiki therapy from master-level practitioners improves memory and decreases behavioral problems when compared with no treatment ( 15800 ).

Depression.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with depression.

A small clinical study in patients with self-reported depression and anxiety shows that receiving hands-on Reiki therapy or distance Reiki therapy for 1-1.5 hours weekly for six weeks improves symptoms of depression and stress when compared with sham distance Reiki therapy ( 88469 ).

Dysmenorrhea.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with dysmenorrhea.

A small clinical study in adolescents with dysmenorrhea shows that receiving 30-45 minutes of Reiki therapy for a total of 7 sessions over 4 weeks reduces pain, but does not improve fatigue or quality of life, when compared with sham Reiki therapy ( 107425 ).

Fatigue.

Small clinical studies suggest that Reiki therapy may modestly improve fatigue.

A small clinical study in patients on hemodialysis in Turkey shows that receiving distance Reiki therapy for 36-40 minutes 3 times weekly for 4 weeks reduces fatigue when compared with a control group receiving no intervention ( 113527 ). Similarly, a small clinical study in nurses working in COVID-19 clinics in Turkey shows that receiving distance Reiki therapy 20 minutes daily for 4 consecutive days reduces fatigue when compared with a control group receiving no intervention ( 113536 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study and the lack of a sham Reiki therapy placebo group.

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with fibromyalgia.

A small clinical study in patients with fibromyalgia shows that receiving a direct touch Reiki or distance Reiki therapy for 30 minutes from a master-level provider twice weekly for 8 weeks does not improve pain when compared with baseline or sham Reiki therapy ( 96651 ). Another small clinical study in patients with fibromyalgia shows that receiving at least 3-5 minutes of Reiki therapy once weekly for 4 weeks improves self-reported state and trait anxiety scores by the fourth week and quality of life subdimension scores of energy, physical function, mental health, and pain when compared with sham Reiki therapy placebo. However, most overall pain scores decreased similarly in both groups when compared to baseline ( 118046 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study.

HIV/AIDS.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for improving anxiety and pain in patients with HIV/AIDS.

A small clinical study in patients with HIV/AIDS shows that receiving four 4-hour training sessions in Reiki therapy and subsequent self-practice modestly reduces anxiety and pain when compared to baseline ( 88470 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a control group.

Hypertension.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with hypertension.

A small clinical study in adults with hypertension shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 60 minutes three times weekly for 7 weeks decreases mean arterial pressure by around 13 mmHg when compared with a control group receiving guided meditations ( 113533 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study, the lack of a sham Reiki therapy placebo, and the fact that alcohol consumption, a study outcome, was also significantly reduced in the Reiki therapy group.

Insomnia.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for insomnia.

A small, uncontrolled study in nursing professionals with sleep disorders or poor sleep shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 30 minutes weekly for 6 weeks increases subjective sleep duration but does not improve other measures of sleep quality, including sleep latency, sleep disturbances, and use of sleep medications, when compared to baseline ( 113532 ). The validity of this study is limited by the small sample size and lack of a comparator group.

Pain (acute).

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with acute pain.

A small clinical study in adults reporting to the hospital with gynecological complaints, pain, and anxiety shows that receiving a 25-minute Reiki therapy session from a master-level practitioner modestly reduces pain and anxiety when compared to baseline ( 104004 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a control group.

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with chronic pain.

A large clinical study in adults with stage III or IV cancer receiving palliative care shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 20 minutes twice weekly for 4 weeks reduces pain by around 3 points on an 11-point visual analog scale and decreases analgesic use when compared with no intervention ( 113529 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study and the lack of a sham Reiki therapy placebo group. A small, uncontrolled study in children aged 1-5 years in palliative care primarily due to cancer, congenital or genetic conditions, or prematurity shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 17 minutes twice weekly for 3 weeks improves nurse-rated pain scores and heart rate variability when compared to baseline ( 113535 ). The validity of this study is limited by the small sample size, lack of a comparator group,  and lack of blinding.

Perioperative anxiety.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial in patients with perioperative anxiety.

A moderate-sized clinical study in adults about to undergo open abdominal surgery shows that receiving Reiki therapy pre-operatively and on days 1 and 2 postoperative reduces scores related to anxiety and surgical fear when compared with sham Reiki therapy and with no intervention control ( 113528 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single blind nature of the study.

Postoperative pain.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for preventing or treating postoperative pain; the available research is conflicting.

A small clinical study in post-hysterectomy patients shows that receiving three 30-minute sessions of Reiki therapy, in addition to usual care, reduces pain and anxiety when compared with usual care alone ( 15801 ). However, a small clinical study in children ages 9 months to 4 years shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 20-30 minutes prior to a surgical dental procedure does not reduce postoperative pain when compared with sham Reiki therapy ( 96656 ). A moderate-sized clinical study in patients about to undergo open abdominal surgery shows that receiving Reiki therapy pre-operatively and on days 1 and 2 postoperative reduces self-reported postoperative pain when compared with sham Reiki therapy and with no intervention control ( 113528 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study.

Pre-procedural anxiety.

Research on the use of Reiki therapy to improve pre-procedural anxiety is limited and conflicting.

One small clinical study in patients undergoing breast biopsy shows that receiving Reiki therapy for 45-50 minutes once within seven days prior to surgery, in addition to conventional therapy, does not reduce anxiety when compared with conventional therapy alone ( 88468 ). However, a small, low-quality clinical study in patients undergoing colonoscopy shows that receiving 15 minutes of Reiki therapy before the procedure reduces patient-reported anxiety, but does not reduce the use of sedative medications, when compared with usual care ( 96655 ). Another low-quality clinical trial in patients undergoing elective surgery shows that receiving Reiki therapy the night before surgery for 30 minutes modestly reduces anxiety when compared with no intervention ( 104006 ). The validity of this finding is limited because anxiety was only measured once immediately after the Reiki intervention. It is unclear whether the anxiety relief is maintained prior to surgery. A large clinical study in adults about to undergo an upper gastrointestinal endoscopy shows that receiving 15-20 minutes of Reiki therapy 60-90 minutes before the procedure decreases self-reported stress and anxiety levels when compared with sham Reiki and when compared with no intervention control ( 113530 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study.

Psychological well-being.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for improving psychological well-being in patients with cancer receiving chemotherapy.

A small clinical study in patients with cancer attending an outpatient clinic shows that receiving 20 minutes of Reiki therapy after receiving intravenous chemotherapy does not improve comfort or well-being when compared with sham Reiki therapy ( 96654 ). A smaller clinical study in patients with breast cancer shows that receiving a 30-minute Reiki therapy session during four chemotherapy treatments improves quality of life, distress, and mood when compared with usual care, but not when compared with the intervention of companionship ( 96657 ).

Quality of life.

Some small clinical studies suggest that Reiki therapy may improve quality of life in patients with cancer; however, the benefit of Reiki therapy in other patient populations remains unclear.

Several small clinical studies in patients with impaired quality of life due to cancer suggest that Reiki therapy may improve some measures of quality of life ( 15798 , 15803 , 96657 , 107424 ). However, it is unclear if Reiki therapy improves quality of life in other patient populations. One small clinical study in healthy individuals shows that receiving 10 minutes of Reiki therapy after a 20-minute massage twice weekly for one month does not improve quality of life, as measured using a modified version of the short form health survey, when compared with massage followed by rest ( 107422 ). Another small clinical study in adolescents with dysmenorrhea shows that receiving 30-45 minutes of Reiki therapy for a total of 7 sessions over 4 weeks does not improve quality of life when compared with sham Reiki therapy ( 107425 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for RA.

A moderate-sized clinical study in patients with RA shows that receiving 30 minutes of Reiki therapy 6 times over 4 weeks decreases self-reported pain and fatigue scores when compared to baseline ( 113534 ). Although the study enrolled a control group, the investigators did not provide a statistical comparison of outcomes between groups, limiting the validity of these findings.

Stress.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for reducing stress in healthy adults.

A small clinical study in healthy individuals shows that receiving a single treatment of Reiki therapy for 10 minutes does not improve stress levels, as measured on a 0-10 scale, or reduce heart rate or blood pressure when compared with sham Reiki therapy or no therapy ( 107423 ). Another small clinical study in healthy individuals shows that receiving 10 minutes of Reiki therapy after a 20-minute massage twice weekly for one month does not reduce stress levels, as measured using the Vasconcellos Stress Symptoms List, when compared with massage followed by rest ( 107422 ). The use of distance Reiki therapy, where the Reiki practitioner and the recipient are not in the same location, has also been evaluated.  A small clinical study in employees working remotely in Peru because of the COVID-19 pandemic shows that receiving distance Reiki therapy, 42 minutes twice weekly for 3 weeks, reduces anxiety and perceived stress when compared with a control group receiving no intervention. However, anxiety specifically related to COVID-19 was not improved ( 113531 ). The validity of this study is limited by poor methodology, including inadequate power and lack of randomization and blinding. Additionally, a small clinical study in nurses working in COVID-19 clinics in Turkey shows that receiving distance Reiki therapy for 20 minutes daily for 4 consecutive days reduces fatigue and stress when compared with a control group receiving no intervention ( 113536 ). The validity of these results is limited by the single-blind nature of the study and lack of a sham Reiki therapy placebo group.

Stroke.

It is unclear if Reiki therapy is beneficial for improving recovery in patients hospitalized with stroke.

A small clinical study in patients hospitalized with subacute ischemic stroke shows that receiving up to 10 treatments of Reiki therapy throughout 2.5 weeks, in addition to conventional rehabilitation, does not improve stroke recovery when compared with conventional rehabilitation alone ( 15802 ).

More evidence is needed to rate Reiki therapy for these uses.

Reiki involves the transfer of energy either through light hand touch or via distance and intention. Reiki can be administered by providers but can also be practiced as self-treatment ( 96653 ). Distance Reiki is typically only practiced by advanced Reiki providers ( 96651 ). Reiki practitioners are largely unregulated in North America. In some states, Reiki practitioners must also be licensed massage therapists ( 15796 ). There is no consistent standard for training in Reiki therapy; however, most schools recognize 3-4 levels of expertise ( 15796 ). Standard Reiki hand placement positions include: the eyes, back of head, crown of the head, chest or lung area, solar plexus or heart area, abdomen, scapula, sacrum, middle back, lower back, and the feet. The practitioner determines the duration of hand placement at each position, with an average of 2-5 minutes, though this is sometimes extended to 10-20 minutes for more problematic issues. Patients are typically supine or prone and can remain fully clothed. At the end of a Reiki therapy session, there is usually an air brushing motion performed from the head to the toes. This is said to brush off the energy of the session ( 15796 , 96651 , 118046 ). Reiki therapy sessions often last for 30, 60, or 90 minutes. The number of treatment sessions is individualized ( 15796 , 118046 ).

Reiki (pronounced Ray-Kee) therapy originated from Buddhist monks. Rei literally means "universal spirit" and ki means "life energy." Therefore, Reiki literally means "universal life energy" ( 15796 ). Reiki is referred to as an "energy medicine" therapy because practitioners believe that it can improve the flow and balance of energy. It is believed that energy imbalances or disturbances result in disease. It is also suggested that the rebalancing of energy may lead to a relaxation response which may then impact physiological responses that affect pain, immune system, and healing ( 113528 ). Reiki is also considered to be a "touch therapy" because it involves placing the hands on or very near to a patient's body. By applying the hands on or near the body, the Reiki practitioner attempts to transmit or deliver energy, or "ki." The Reiki practitioner uses 12-15 different hand positions and keeps them in place for 2-5 minutes ( 15796 ). Anecdotally, patients undergoing Reiki therapy sometimes can experience tingling sensations, sleepiness, or relaxation. Patients with pain, nausea, or fatigue sometimes experience relief of these symptoms. Currently, the beliefs and theories of Reiki therapy are not supported by scientific research.
