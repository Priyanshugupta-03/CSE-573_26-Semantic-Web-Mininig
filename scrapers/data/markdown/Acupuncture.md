Caution

Acupuncture should not be confused with Traditional Chinese Medicine or similar systems such as Kampo Medicine . Also, it should not be confused with similar treatments, such as Acupressure , Transcutaneous Electrical Acustimulation , Moxibustion , or Trigger Point Therapy .

## Overview

Acupuncture is a therapeutic modality procedure in which meridian points located on specific body areas are stimulated by piercing with fine needles or applying electric currents or heat. The practice of acupuncture originated in China over 2,000 years ago as a component of traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) ( 88514 , 102630 ).

Most states in the US regulate the practice of acupuncture, for which different degrees and certifications are available. The National Certification Commission for Acupuncture and Oriental Medicine (NCCAOM) works with most states to validate the competency of acupuncture practitioners prior to licensure ( 90052 , 90053 ).

Likely Safe when administered appropriately and performed with sterile needles. Acupuncture has been safely used in numerous clinical trials for up to 24 months ( 15990 , 15991 , 15992 , 15993 , 15994 , 15995 , 15996 , 15997 , 15998 , 15999 )( 16000 , 16001 , 16003 , 16004 , 29611 , 29619 , 29649 , 29653 , 29681 , 29692 )( 88549 , 88583 , 88586 , 88610 , 88611 , 88614 , 102626 , 102627 , 102628 , 102629 )( 102633 , 102635 , 102640 , 102642 , 102644 , 102645 , 103201 , 103423 , 103427 , 103429 ) ( 103430 , 103433 , 104691 , 105403 , 104720 , 104723 , 104725 , 107240 , 111778 , 111803 )( 111805 , 111812 , 111818 , 111819 , 111822 , 111827 , 111835 , 111838 , 111840 , 111841 )( 111842 , 111845 , 114203 , 114205 , 114206 , 114210 , 114212 , 114213 , 114222 , 114231 )( 114233 , 114235 , 114237 , 114238 , 114247 , 114248 , 114251 , 114255 , 114256 , 114261 ). Use of nonsterile needles during acupuncture can cause infection. Inappropriate acupuncture practices can cause trauma to the body and internal organs resulting in serious sequelae, such as pneumocranium, pneumothorax, hemothorax, kidney laceration, acute respiratory and circulatory failure, and death ( 101387 , 101399 , 101581 , 101589 , 105934 , 107241 , 109844 , 111813 , 111817 , 111830 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when used appropriately and performed with sterile needles. Some clinical research shows that acupuncture has been used in children without reports of significant adverse effects ( 29650 , 29679 , 29666 , 29667 , 88513 , 88514 , 88600 , 88601 , 104728 , 104729 )( 111837 , 114216 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Safe when used appropriately at most acupoints and performed with sterile needles. Some clinical research shows that acupuncture has been used during pregnancy and labor without reports of significant adverse effects ( 15991 , 82972 , 88592 , 88594 , 88595 , 88606 , 88611 , 88614 , 103419 , 111820 ).  POSSIBLY UNSAFE...when acupuncture at the SP6 acupoint is used. There is some concern that this acupoint can increase the risk of early contractions and miscarriage ( 108572 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Most evidence also suggests that acupuncture is safe for patients undergoing in vitro fertilization (IVF). One large clinical study and a meta-analysis show that receiving acupuncture one or more times between follicle stimulation and pregnancy testing does not affect the pregnancy or live birth rate when compared with sham acupuncture or no intervention ( 97562 , 109814 ). However, a smaller study shows that acupuncture performed off-site immediately before and after IVF embryo transfer reduces the pregnancy rate by 21% when compared to no additional intervention ( 97549 ). Use caution until more is known about the safety of acupuncture during IVF.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the use of electroacupuncture or laser acupuncture during pregnancy; avoid using.

#### General

Acupuncture, when used appropriately, is generally well-tolerated.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Bruising, pain, and swelling.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Some case reports raise concerns about acute respiratory failure, circulatory failure, hemothorax, infection at the needle site, pneumocranium, pneumothorax, kidney laceration, and death.

#### Dermatologic

Redness, blisters, and itching have been reported rarely ( 104731 , 109809 , 109814 , 111801 , 111820 , 111823 , 111838 , 114253 , 114261 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Nausea, gastrointestinal discomfort, and constipation have been reported rarely ( 103432 , 103435 , 105403 , 109836 , 111838 , 114228 , 114261 ).

#### Hematologic

Minor bleeding has been reported rarely with acupuncture in clinical trials ( 105403 , 104720 , 104723 , 104725 , 104729 , 109809 , 109820 , 109827 , 109832 , 109837 )( 111804 , 111808 , 111819 , 111823 , 114251 ).

#### Immunologic

Nonsterile conditions during acupuncture or the use of nonsterile acupuncture needles can lead to infection. There have been case reports of mycobacterial infection, hepatitis B, necrotizing fasciitis, fatal acute peritonitis, fungal spinal infection, bilateral facet joint septic arthritis, skin infections with gordonia species, and subcutaneous or paravertebral abscesses following treatments which included acupuncture ( 101374 , 101383 , 101389 , 114215 , 114257 , 114260 ). These cases may be related to nonsterile conditions during acupuncture and/or the immunocompetence of the patient, There is also a case report of pyoderma gangrenosum exacerbated by acupuncture in a 49-year-old female patient previously undiagnosed with this pre-existing condition ( 111807 ). Population research has found that acupuncture use is associated with an increased rate of hepatitis C transmission ( 114215 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Fainting and dizziness have been reported rarely ( 102639 , 102646 , 103432 , 103435 , 105403 , 104723 , 109820 , 109830 , 109836 , 114228 )( 114237 , 114251 , 114253 , 114261 ). Tiredness, transient sedation, and vasovagal shock have also been reported rarely ( 103434 , 109834 , 114261 ). Numbness has been reported rarely ( 109821 , 114256 ). Crying and irritability have been reported ( 104729 , 114210 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

Dyspnea has been reported rarely ( 111820 ). There is a case report of a fatal pulmonary fat embolism in a 72-year-old female thought to be related to stress caused by acupuncture ( 111817 ).

#### Other

Minor pain, swelling, and bruising have been reported with acupuncture in clinical trials ( 102626 , 102627 , 102628 , 102636 , 102638 , 102640 , 102642 , 102643 , 102644 , 102646 ) ( 103201 , 103419 , 103420 , 105403 , 104720 , 104723 , 104725 , 107240 , 109809 , 109820 )( 109821 , 109827 , 109833 , 109837 , 109845 , 111775 , 111801 , 111802 )( 111810 , 111819 , 111820 , 111823 , 111829 , 111834 , 111835 , 111836 , 111838 , 111840 )( 114210 , 114228 , 114233 , 114237 , 114247 , 114251 , 114254 , 114255 , 114256 , 114261 ). Mishandling acupuncture needles can cause traumatic damage to the body. There have been case reports of epidural and subdural hematomas from improper acupuncture, resulting in paresthesia, hemiparesis, quadriparesis, and sensory deficit ( 101379 , 101380 ). Inappropriate acupuncture practices have also been associated with trauma to internal organs, resulting in serious sequelae such as pneumocranium, pneumothorax, hemothorax, kidney laceration, liver injury, hematoma-mediated colon obstruction, acute respiratory and circulatory failure, and death ( 101387 , 101399 , 101581 , 101589 , 105934 , 107241 , 109844 , 111813 , 111817 , 111830 )( 114243 , 114245 , 114259 ). Acupuncture in the eyes can cause trauma to the eyes such as perforation and traumatic cataract ( 101585 ). Acupuncture needles that are not removed after treatment might embolize and cause damage to internal organs. An acupuncture needle that has embolized to the heart can cause hemothorax ( 101589 ). In one case, a 50-year-old female presented to the emergency department with chest pain due to a calcified 5 cm acupuncture needle in the heart from acupuncture performed 10 years prior ( 101583 ). In addition, there are case reports of lung abscess following migration of acupuncture needles into the lung, including one case involving a 38-year-old male ( 114244 ). In the case of a female in her early 50s, worsening lower back pain was determined to be caused by 3 acupuncture needles which had been embedded 32 years previously ( 114258 ).

Possibly Effective
Aromatase inhibitor-induced arthralgia.

Most research shows that acupuncture seems to reduce aromatase inhibitor-induced joint pain.

The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that acupuncture may be offered to patients with aromatase inhibitor-induced joint pain. This recommendation is based on moderate evidence from intermediate-quality research suggesting that benefits outweigh harms ( 109161 ). Although a meta-analysis of three small clinical trials shows that acupuncture does not reduce aromatase inhibitor-induced arthralgia when compared with sham acupuncture ( 102628 ), other research disagrees. The best evidence comes from a large clinical study in patients with breast cancer which shows that acupuncture over 6 weeks modestly reduces aromatase inhibitor-induced joint pain when compared with both sham acupuncture and wait list control. Benefits appear to last up to 12 months ( 97551 , 109841 ).

Back pain.

Most research shows that acupuncture seems to reduce back pain more than no treatment. However, it is unclear if acupuncture is more effective than sham acupuncture. Receiving more than 5 treatments with deep acupuncture seems to offer the most long-term benefit.

Meta-analyses of results from clinical research show that acupuncture reduces pain when compared with either sham acupuncture or no treatment in patients with either chronic or acute low back pain ( 15994 , 97564 , 105933 , 105935 , 111779 , 114209 ). For chronic low back pain, most clinical research suggests that after 3 months, acupuncture is no more effective than sham acupuncture, although it does seem to be beneficial when compared with no treatment ( 16905 , 29601 , 29602 , 29604 , 29608 , 29612 ). Despite the uncertain and short-term benefits, guidelines published by the American College of Physicians in 2017 recommend the use of nonpharmacological therapy, such as acupuncture, prior to using pharmacologic therapy in patients with chronic low back pain ( 95773 ). Other recommended nonpharmacologic options include superficial heat, massage, and spinal manipulation ( 95773 ). Sham acupuncture treatments include inserting needles into non-acupoints, using a device that masks the presence or absence of a needle (such as the Park Sham Device), or poking acupoints with a toothpick rather than inserting a needle. A meta-analysis of 10 studies that included over 4000 patients found that sham acupuncture which masks the absence of a needle is similarly effective to true acupuncture, whereas sham acupuncture which inserts needles at non acupoints is less effective than true acupuncture ( 114209 ). Other clinical evidence suggests that inserting acupuncture needles into trigger points, the most painful points on the lower back, instead of acupoints, reduces pain when compared with no needle insertion ( 29603 , 29610 ). The analgesic effect of acupuncture also seems to be influenced by several factors. The number of acupuncture sessions seems to influence the percentage of patients who experience pain relief, with a greater number of patients achieving relief of low back pain after 10 treatments compared to only 5 treatments ( 29609 ). In patients seeking care for acute, non-specific back pain, a single treatment with acupuncture in addition to advice about activity and analgesic medication is no more effective than the advice and analgesic medication alone ( 103427 ). The depth of needle insertion also appears to influence the analgesic effect of acupuncture on low back pain. One clinical study shows that superficial and deep acupuncture reduce low back pain similarly after 8 treatments. However, after a 3 month follow-up, deep acupuncture is more effective than superficial acupuncture ( 29613 ). Also, different acupuncture styles may have variable analgesic effects. One network meta-analysis suggests that motion style acupuncture improves acute back pain to a greater degree than either traditional acupuncture or electro-acupuncture ( 105935 ). A preliminary clinical study in patients experiencing pain from a herniated lumbar disc shows that receiving thread embedding acupuncture once weekly for 8 weeks might improve lower back pain at week 16, but does not impact lower back pain at week 8, nor does it affect radiating pain, disability scores, or quality of life at any time point, when compared with sham thread embedding acupuncture ( 108597 ). The validity of this finding is limited due to inappropriate sham design. Finally, there is some evidence that the analgesic effects of acupuncture may be delayed, with greatest benefit seen months after treatment discontinuation. Preliminary clinical research shows that giving 10 acupuncture treatments over 3 months does not decrease lower back pain at the end of 3 or 12 months when compared with standard treatment. However, patients who receive acupuncture treatment have greater pain relief at 24 months (21 months post-treatment) when compared with standard treatment ( 29611 ).

Cancer-related fatigue.

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may modestly improve symptoms in patients with cancer-related fatigue. Guidelines from multiple oncology associations include acupuncture and electroacupuncture as a potential treatment option for reducing post-cancer fatigue.

A meta-analysis of mainly preliminary clinical research shows that receiving acupuncture for a total of 6-10 sessions over 2-10 weeks improves feelings of cancer-related fatigue when compared with sham acupuncture or usual care alone ( 105403 ). Also, preliminary clinical research in breast cancer patients shows that receiving acupuncture in addition to usual care once weekly for 6 weeks modestly improves mental and physical fatigue when compared with usual care alone ( 101398 ). The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for cancer-related fatigue list acupuncture as a potential nonpharmacologic treatment option for patients undergoing cancer treatment or those who have completed treatment ( 112413 ). Furthermore, the American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) and the Society for Integrative Oncology recommend acupuncture for patients who have finished cancer treatment and for cancer survivors ( 97066 , 108673 ).

Cancer-related pain.

Acupuncture is conditionally recommended by The National Comprehensive Cancer Network for cancer-related pain in certain patient populations and The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that benefits of acupuncture outweigh harm. However, research is generally of low quality.

Meta-analyses and preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture has a small to moderate effect on malignancy-related and surgery-induced pain in patients with cancer when compared with baseline or a control group given sham acupuncture or usual care. Manual acupuncture, electroacupuncture, and auricular acupuncture have been studied ( 15993 , 29672 , 101373 , 102628 , 102643 , 114229 ). Furthermore, when used in combination with analgesics, meta-analyses of 2-3 studies show that acupuncture, usually electroacupuncture, decreases analgesic use by a small amount ( 102628 , 114229 ). The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for adult cancer pain highlight acupuncture and electroacupuncture as a potential integrative treatment for vulnerable populations, such as frail or elderly individuals who may not be able to tolerate pharmacological treatments ( 113680 ). The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that acupuncture may be offered to patients with general or musculoskeletal pain from cancer. This recommendation is based on moderate evidence from intermediate-quality research suggesting that benefits outweigh harms ( 109161 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

Most low-quality research shows that acupuncture reduces vomiting associated with chemotherapy when used with antiemetics. However, it is unclear whether acupuncture is beneficial for nausea associated with chemotherapy or whether acupuncture is more beneficial than sham acupuncture.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows with very low certainty that when used in combination with usual care, acupuncture increases the odds of complete control of acute and delayed vomiting by 13% and 47%, respectively, when compared with usual care alone. There was no effect of acupuncture on the complete control of acute or chronic nausea when compared with usual care alone, or for nausea or vomiting when compared with sham acupuncture in addition to usual care ( 111840 ). In a preliminary study in patients with chemotherapy-induced vomiting unresponsive to dexamethasone taken with ondansetron or granisetron, electroacupuncture reduced nausea and vomiting when compared to baseline ( 29653 ). Also, in patients with advanced cancer, a combination of manual acupuncture and electroacupuncture with ondansetron during 5 days of chemotherapy reduces the severity of nausea and vomiting over a total period of 21 days when compared with sham acupuncture. However, acupuncture does not increase the number of patients that experience no nausea or vomiting ( 103429 ). Clinical practice guidelines from the Society for Integrative Oncology (SIO) recommend that acupuncture or acupressure be considered as an adjunct to anti-emetic medications for CINV management ( 97066 ). Preliminary clinical research shows that manual acupuncture is comparable to giving ondansetron 8 mg intravenously for preventing acute nausea and vomiting in the first 24 hours after chemotherapy with paclitaxel and carboplatin ( 91770 ). However, this study is small and limited by a lack of blinding.

Chronic prostatitis and chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CP/CPPS).

When used alone or in combination with traditional Chinese medicine or conventional treatments, most research suggests manual acupuncture or electroacupuncture is beneficial in patients with CP/CPPS.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that acupuncture has modest beneficial effects on pain and overall symptom severity when compared with sham acupuncture or conventional medication ( 111775 ). A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research conducted in China shows that receiving acupuncture along with syndrome differentiation-based treatment with traditional Chinese medicine for 1-6 weeks improves quality of life, pain, and urinary symptoms, without increasing adverse events, when compared with acupuncture or traditional Chinese medicine alone ( 108590 ). The validity of these findings is limited due to the lack of follow-up and variety of treatments and acupoints used. Individual clinical trials support these findings. One large clinical study in patients with moderate to severe CP/CPPS shows that receiving 20 sessions of acupuncture over 8 weeks produces clinically important reductions in symptoms in 61% of patients, compared with 37% of patients receiving sham acupuncture. Such reductions were sustained for at least 24 weeks after treatment ended ( 107257 ). Also, a small preliminary clinical study in patients with CP/CPPS shows that receiving electroacupuncture twice weekly for 7 weeks improves overall symptoms and pain, but not urinary symptoms or quality of life, when compared with taking levofloxacin 500 mg daily and ibuprofen 200 mg twice daily for 6 weeks ( 101392 ). Another preliminary clinical study in patients with CP/CPPS shows that laser or filiform needle acupuncture may improve symptoms when compared to baseline ( 88608 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Additional preliminary clinical research in patients with CP/CPPS shows that acupuncture is similarly effective to extracorporeal shock wave therapy for improving response rates, urinary symptoms, pain, and quality of life when compared with sham acupuncture ( 108591 ). However, this study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups.

Depression.

Most research shows that acupuncture modestly reduces symptoms of depression when used alone or with conventional antidepressants.

Acupuncture may reduce symptoms of depression when used as an add-on to conventional therapy ( 15996 , 97559 , 101368 , 105931 , 105936 , 109816 , 109825 , 111818 ). The most compelling evidence comes from recent meta-analyses showing that using manual or electroacupuncture as an adjunct to antidepressants such as selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) has a moderate to large beneficial effect on depression severity when compared with antidepressants alone ( 97559 , 109816 , 109825 , 111818 ). One meta-analysis shows that remission rates and treatment responses were also modestly increased, and serious adverse effects were decreased, when acupuncture was used as an adjunct to antidepressants ( 109825 ). Acupuncture may also modestly reduce symptoms of depression when compared with sham acupuncture ( 97559 , 111818 ). The validity of these analyses is limited by the substantial heterogeneity and performance bias of the included studies, as well as the fact that most studies were conducted in one country and results may not be generalizable to other populations ( 97559 , 109816 , 109825 , 111818 ). It is unclear whether acupuncture is any better than conventional therapy in most patients with depression. At least one meta-analysis shows that acupuncture may be modestly more beneficial than conventional therapy. However, the clinical relevance is unclear and more information on the type and dosing of antidepressants is needed ( 15996 , 97559 , 101368 , 102633 , 104745 , 105931 , 105936 , 111818 ). There is also some evidence that acupuncture is more likely to alleviate symptoms of perimenopausal depression, post-stroke depression, or cancer-related depression when compared with antidepressants or hormone replacement therapy ( 102633 , 104745 , 105937 , 107256 , 108596 , 111827 ). More research is needed to determine the optimal number of acupuncture treatment sessions. One meta-analysis shows that when acupuncture is used alone or with antidepressants, optimal response occurred between approximately 28 and 36 sessions ( 109816 ). Since most of the available research has been conducted in China, it is unclear if these findings are generalizable to other geographic locations.

Dyspepsia.

Most research shows that acupuncture seems to reduce symptoms of dyspepsia.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture is beneficial for improving symptoms of dyspepsia when compared with various control treatments, including sham acupuncture and conventional treatments ( 97554 , 102640 , 109812 , 111832 ). Specifically, meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that manual and electroacupuncture are more effective than sham acupuncture for improving symptoms of dyspepsia and quality of life in patients with functional dyspepsia ( 97554 , 102640 , 109812 ). One meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research also shows that manual acupuncture is more effective than cisapride, domperidone, and itopride, and that electroacupuncture is more effective than itopride ( 102640 ). Individual preliminary clinical trials have shown that acupuncture is as effective as these agents ( 88511 , 97554 ). However, these studies might have not been powered to detect a difference between treatments. Further, some individual clinical studies in patients with functional dyspepsia have shown that acupuncture combined with Western medicine (including prokinetic agents, acid suppressants, and antidepressants) improves symptoms more than Western medicine alone ( 107246 ). Acupuncture has also been studied for improvement of mental health in patients with dyspepsia. Meta-analyses of generally low-quality clinical research show that acupuncture is modestly more effective than sham acupuncture for improving symptoms of anxiety and depression after 4 weeks on some, but not all, scales of anxiety and depression ( 114234 ). However, the number of studies included in each analysis was small.

Fibromyalgia.

Most research shows that acupuncture seems to reduce pain and stiffness in patients with fibromyalgia.

Most preliminary clinical research and meta-analyses of clinical research show that acupuncture improves pain and stiffness when compared with no treatment and standard therapy ( 29648 , 29649 , 29650 , 29652 , 97550 , 97570 , 101594 ). However, although one meta-analysis disagrees, most meta-analyses show that acupuncture is no different than sham acupuncture or various alternative therapies for improving pain, sleep, or quality of life ( 97550 , 97570 , 101594 , 109826 ). The discrepancies in results might be due to the type of sham acupuncture used and the integrity of blinding procedures. For example, acupuncture was more effective at relieving pain when compared with the sham acupuncture method that involved placing a needle in the acupoint with a bandage, without piercing the skin ( 29649 ). In another preliminary clinical trial in which the skin was pierced for both sham and real acupuncture, there was no difference in pain relief between groups ( 29650 ).

Insomnia.

Most research shows that manual acupuncture seems to improve sleep quality in patients with insomnia. It is unclear if electroacupuncture is beneficial.

Clinical research shows that manual acupuncture modestly improves sleep quality when used as an adjunct to sleep hygiene instruction, Chinese medicine, and/or conventional treatment such as estazolam or cognitive behavior therapy (CBT) ( 88568 , 88570 , 88571 , 114207 , 114228 , 114247 ). Also, individual studies and meta-analyses of generally low-quality research show that acupuncture used alone improves sleep quality and reduces insomnia severity when compared with sham acupuncture and/or waitlist control ( 88568 , 88569 , 102643 , 102646 , 103419 , 104724 , 104744 , 105939 , 109820 ). Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture has a moderate effect on sleep quality when compared with sham acupuncture or fluoxetine ( 102643 , 104745 ). However, other research shows that manual acupuncture used alone is no better than conventional insomnia treatment or cognitive behavioral therapy ( 88568 , 102643 , 109820 ). Electroacupuncture has also been examined in generally low quality research with mixed evidence of benefit when compared with usual treatment or sham acupuncture ( 88568 , 91764 , 108128 , 109824 ). Acupuncture is also of interest for improving sleep quality in specific populations. Generally low-quality clinical research from systematic reviews and meta-analyses show that acupuncture modestly reduces symptoms of insomnia and improves sleep quality in patients with cancer-related insomnia or with reduced sleep quality related to Parkinson disease or stroke. In these studies, acupuncture is usually used as an add-on to conventional medicine and compared with conventional medication, usual care, and/or sham acupuncture ( 108128 , 114225 , 114247 , 114226 ). In patients with cancer-related insomnia, acupuncture was used alone or as an add-on with moxibustion ( 108128 ).

Labor pain.

Most research shows that acupuncture seems to reduce labor pain and the need for epidural analgesia.

Clinical research shows that pregnant patients receiving acupuncture during labor experience less pain and use less epidural analgesia when compared to usual care or sham acupuncture ( 15991 , 88610 , 88611 , 102641 ). However, high quality research is still needed to confirm these results ( 102641 ).

Migraine headache.

Most research shows that acupuncture seems to prevent migraine headaches. It is unclear if acupuncture is as effective as conventional prophylactic strategies or as a migraine treatment.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture modestly reduces frequency of migraine headache when compared with no treatment ( 88527 , 88528 , 97555 , 102631 ). Individual clinical studies also show that acupuncture helps prevent migraine headache when compared to baseline or no treatment, but not always when compared to sham acupuncture ( 88530 , 88531 , 88534 , 88535 , 88537 , 88539 , 88550 , 97571 ). However, pooled analyses of up to 11 clinical trials did find a small decrease in migraine frequency and pain when acupuncture was compared to sham acupuncture ( 97555 , 103435 , 111828 ). It is unclear how acupuncture compares with conventional preventive medications for migraine. A meta-analysis of a few small studies shows that acupuncture has a moderate effect on the reduction of migraine episodes when compared with propranolol over 4 weeks ( 102631 ). In addition, one small clinical trial shows that receiving acupuncture for 12 weeks is as effective as metoprolol 100-200 mg daily in preventing migraines ( 88549 ). Another small clinical trial shows that receiving acupuncture for 12 weeks modestly reduces the number of monthly migraines and days of acute medication use when compared with topiramate 50-100 mg daily plus sham acupuncture. These benefits were sustained for at least an additional 12 weeks ( 114256 ). Additionally, three pooled analyses of preliminary clinical studies show that acupuncture might reduce migraine pain and frequency when compared to drug treatment. However, the included studies evaluated various drug treatments, including flunarizine, metoprolol, valproate, or unspecified guideline-recommended drugs ( 97555 , 103435 , 114261 ). Acupuncture may also be beneficial when used as an add-on to conventional therapy. Preliminary clinical research in patients treated with propranolol 20 mg twice daily shows that auricular acupuncture treatment over 4 weeks modestly reduces the pain, duration, and frequency of migraine headaches when compared with propranolol alone. There was no difference in the severity of nausea and vomiting ( 109806 ). Acupuncture has also been studied for treatment of acute migraine, in comparison with sham acupuncture or pharmaceutical agents ( 97565 , 111845 ). A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that acupuncture increases the rate of complete headache freedom and the rate of a reduction in pain of at least 50% within 2 hours when compared with sham acupuncture. There was also some evidence of a small reduction in headache intensity within 2 hours of treatment; however, the clinical relevance is unclear ( 111845 ). This meta-analysis also shows that acupuncture alone may be as effective as pharmaceutical interventions for improving the rate of headache freedom or relief or reducing headache intensity. However, there is no evidence that acupuncture has additional beneficial effects when used with pharmaceutical interventions ( 111845 ). The number of studies included in most of these individual meta-analyses was small and the certainty of evidence was generally very low to low. Acupuncture has also been studied for improvement of mental health in patients with migraines. A meta-analysis of a small number of lower quality studies shows that acupuncture is no more effective than sham acupuncture for improving symptoms of anxiety, depression, and overall mental health, However, it may be modestly more beneficial than pharmaceutical agents ( 111828 ). There is also interest as to whether use of acupuncture by patients with migraines is associated with a reduced incidence of stroke. Observational retrospective research in patients with migraines suggests that acupuncture use is associated with a 60% reduced incidence of stroke, including ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke, over a 19-year follow-up period ( 111814 ).

Neck pain.

When used alone or in addition to routine treatment, most research shows that acupuncture seems to modestly reduce neck pain.

A meta-analysis of 7 clinical trials in patients with whiplash injury shows that receiving acupuncture modestly reduces neck pain and increases range of motion when compared with control groups receiving sham treatment and/or usual care ( 114235 ). Also, some clinical research shows that short-term acupuncture modestly decreases general neck pain when compared with sham and inactive acupuncture ( 88630 , 101385 ). Adding acupuncture to routine treatment appears to provide additional relief and improvement for patients with neck pain ( 29616 ). Research regarding effects of traditional acupuncture compared to trigger point acupuncture is conflicting. Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic neck pain shows that traditional acupuncture decreases neck pain compared to acupuncture performed on trigger points and sham acupuncture ( 29605 ). However, another small study shows that trigger point acupuncture reduces pain when compared with traditional acupuncture and sham treatment in patients with chronic neck pain ( 29614 ). Acupuncture appears to decrease chronic neck pain to a greater degree than transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) ( 29617 ). However, laser acupuncture does not improve neck pain and other symptoms of whiplash injury ( 88662 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Most research shows that acupuncture seems to reduce pain and improve function in patients with osteoarthritis. Acupuncture is conditionally recommended by The American College of Rheumatology (ACR) for any form of osteoarthritis.

Acupuncture appears to improve symptoms of osteoarthritis when used short-term. Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that electrical, laser, and manual acupuncture modestly reduce pain and modestly improve function in patients with hip or knee osteoarthritis when compared with no treatment, sham treatment, or standard treatment, and when used as an adjunct to standard treatment or massage ( 16004 , 29606 , 29618 , 29619 , 91769 , 97558 , 97564 , 102632 , 111810 , 111819 )( 111842 , 114217 ). However, there is significant heterogeneity amongst the acupuncture protocols used in the existing research ( 111842 ). A network meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that silver needle or conventional acupuncture are more effective for reducing pain in adults with knee osteoarthritis than other types of acupuncture, including electroacupuncture. However, the number of studies investigating each type of acupuncture is small ( 114220 ). A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical studies suggests that acupuncture in combination with Chinese herbal medicines modestly improves function, knee pain, and stiffness in patients with knee osteoarthritis when compared with standard treatments, such as glucosamine, topical or oral diclofenac, or intra-articular sodium hyaluronate ( 108588 ). Furthermore, most individual preliminary clinical trials show that acupuncture is more effective than control treatments, such as advice or sham acupuncture, for reducing pain in knee and hip osteoarthritis for up to 12 weeks ( 29620 , 29621 , 29622 , 29625 , 29626 , 29627 , 91767 , 104725 ). One large clinical trial shows that electroacupuncture three times weekly for 8 weeks improves pain and function when compared to sham acupuncture and benefits last for up to an additional 18 weeks ( 104725 ). Although a large number of clinical trials show some evidence for the use of acupuncture for osteoarthritis, available research is limited by a lack of appropriate blinding or sham controls, overall small sample sizes, and prior expectations of outcomes ( 102114 ). Additionally, conflicting evidence exists. Some small meta-analyses and preliminary clinical trials did not find a difference between acupuncture and sham acupuncture for improving pain in hip or knee osteoarthritis ( 29624 , 91767 , 97558 , 104725 , 114249 ). Also, a large clinical trial did not find a difference between manual acupuncture and sham acupuncture administered three times weekly for 8 weeks for improving knee osteoarthritis pain and function, although it did show benefit on pain during 18 weeks of follow-up ( 104725 ). One clinical trial shows that traditional acupuncture reduces osteoarthritis knee pain to the same degree as sham acupuncture; however, both acupuncture methods are more effective in reducing pain when compared with physiotherapy and as needed anti-inflammatory drugs, such as diclofenac or rofecoxib ( 29624 ). Therefore, it is possible that the benefits of acupuncture are due to a placebo effect.

Postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV).

Most research shows that acupuncture, usually at the P6 acupoint, reduces PONV in adults and children.

Some meta-analyses of clinical research show that acupuncture can reduce the incidence of postoperative nausea and vomiting, as well as the need for antiemetics, by up to 48% ( 29628 , 107251 ). However, results from more recent clinical research suggests that acupuncture might reduce the incidence of nausea, but not vomiting. One meta-analysis shows that acupuncture reduces the risk of postoperative nausea by 29% but does not reduce the risk of postoperative vomiting ( 111822 ). Also, individual clinical trials show that acupuncture reduces the incidence of postoperative nausea, and in some cases, the need for antiemetics 6-hours postoperatively. However, there was no benefit for vomiting or the severity of nausea or vomiting in these studies ( 102649 , 109842 , 111835 ). Two meta-analyses found that the effect of acupuncture changes with time. In the first 6 hours after surgery, acupuncture reduces acute vomiting but not acute nausea. At 24 hours or more after surgery, acupuncture was shown to decrease delayed nausea but not delayed vomiting ( 91763 ). The available trials are generally small and lower quality with control groups receiving only usual care and conventional treatments. The effects of acupuncture have also been studied in children. A meta-analysis of six small clinical trials suggests that acupuncture reduces postoperative vomiting in children when compared with control acupuncture. Surgeries in this analysis included trabismus surgery, tonsillectomy, general surgery, hernia repair, circumcision, or dental surgery ( 88601 ). Another meta-analysis suggests that acupuncture is not effective for reducing nausea and vomiting in children after tonsillectomy ( 88600 ). However, a separate small clinical study in children post-tonsillectomy, published after the meta-analysis, shows that receiving acupuncture on the P6 acupuncture point is as effective as sham acupuncture and dexamethasone ( 101397 ).

Tension headache.

Most research shows that acupuncture is beneficial for tension headache prevention when compared with no treatment. However, it is unclear if acupuncture is more effective than sham acupuncture.

One meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with frequent tension headaches shows that acupuncture modestly prevents tension headache when compared with sham acupuncture ( 97556 ). A separate meta-analysis that pooled data from studies of acupuncture against studies of tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) in the treatment of tension headaches found that both treatments offered similar efficacy for reducing the frequency of headache attacks ( 114241 ). However, the findings have low certainty as no studies involved a head-to-head comparison of acupuncture and TCAs. Additionally, most individual clinical trials are small and show that acupuncture prevents tension headaches when compared with baseline or no treatment, but not when compared with sham acupuncture. This suggests a possible placebo effect ( 88528 , 88533 , 88536 , 88540 , 88541 , 88543 , 88544 , 88548 ). Acupuncture has also been compared with other modalities. Some preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture is less effective than relaxation training for chronic-type tension headache ( 89615 ). Additional preliminary clinical research shows that physical therapy is more effective than acupuncture in decreasing muscle tenderness in tension headache ( 88544 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Asthma.

Most research shows that acupuncture does not improve symptoms of asthma.

Although some research disagrees ( 29679 , 89073 , 111838 ), most evidence from clinical research shows that acupuncture does not improve asthma symptoms or asthma parameters such as forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV1) and forced vital capacity ( 29673 , 29674 , 29675 , 29677 , 111838 ). Meta-analyses of clinical trials show that acupuncture does not improve most measures of lung function, including forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV1) and forced vital capacity. Some research disagrees with these findings,  ( 29673 , 29674 , 111838 ). Clinical research supports that acupuncture has no effect on asthma ( 29675 , 29677 ). However some preliminary clinical research suggests that acupuncture or laser acupuncture and probiotics may marginally improve asthma symptoms and pulmonary function ( 29679 , 89073 ).

HIV/AIDS-related peripheral neuropathy.

Some research shows that acupuncture does not improve peripheral neuropathy in patients with this condition.

Clinical research shows that acupuncture may not be effective for reducing peripheral neuropathy associated with HIV when compared with sham acupuncture at control sites ( 88596 ).

Smoking cessation.

Most research shows that acupuncture does not improve rates of smoking cessation.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that although acupuncture might have short-term benefits, it does not improve rates of long-term smoking cessation when compared with sham acupuncture ( 16000 , 88676 , 97566 ). Additional clinical research supports this finding ( 88677 , 88679 , 88680 , 88681 ). However, some other preliminary clinical research suggests that acupuncture with education might improve smoking quit rates ( 88678 ), and that acupuncture might help to decrease the number of cigarettes smoked per day ( 88682 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Age-related macular degeneration (AMD).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for AMD.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research shows that acupuncture, used alone or in addition to traditional Chinese medicine, modestly increases the proportion of patients with improvement in visual acuity and the absorption of fundus bleeding and exudate when compared with antioxidant vitamins or traditional Chinese medicine alone. There were also modest improvements in the best-corrected visual acuity and central macula thickness ( 111808 ). This meta-analysis is limited by the availability of a small number of low-quality studies without appropriate sham controls.

Alcohol use disorder.

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may be modestly beneficial in adults with alcohol use disorder.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with alcohol use disorder shows that acupuncture may decrease drinking episodes, tremor, depression, and cravings when compared with sham acupuncture at non-acupoints ( 88647 , 88648 , 88649 ).

Allergic rhinitis (hay fever).

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may modestly reduce symptoms of allergic rhinitis.

Low quality research suggests that acupuncture is at least as effective as conventional medicine for reducing symptoms of allergic rhinitis. One meta-analysis of 11 preliminary clinical trials shows that manual acupuncture over a 2- to 8-week period reduces symptoms of allergic rhinitis, including nasal obstruction, sneezing, rhinorrhea, and nasal itch, by a small amount when compared to various types of conventional medicine. Benefits might last up to 12 months ( 103424 ). Subgroup analyses of a more recent meta-analysis show that acupuncture is modestly more beneficial than cetirizine for reducing nasal symptoms after 8 weeks of intervention, with no differences between groups when compared with cetirizine after 4 weeks of intervention or with loratadine over 2 to 6 weeks of intervention ( 109809 ). Also, this meta-analysis shows that using acupuncture as adjunct to conventional medication is modestly more beneficial than conventional medication alone. However, more research is needed ( 109809 ). Acupuncture has also been compared with sham acupuncture or no treatment in preliminary clinical research. Although some conflicting evidence exists from individual trials, most meta-analyses and individual preliminary clinical trials show that acupuncture modestly improves symptoms of allergic rhinitis when compared with sham acupuncture ( 88625 , 88626 , 91766 , 109809 ). Also, another preliminary clinical study shows that acupuncture used in combination with a Chinese herbal medicine improves symptoms of seasonal allergic rhinitis when compared to sham acupuncture and a non-specific herbal formula ( 88627 ).

Alzheimer disease.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for this condition.

Meta-analyses of generally small or preliminary clinical trials in patients with Alzheimer disease show that acupuncture, in combination with conventional medicine for up to 12 weeks, modestly improves cognition, as measured by the Mini Mental State Examination (MMSE), and activities of daily living when compared with conventional medicine such as donepezil. However, a more recent meta-analysis does not support this finding ( 100785 , 103428 ). In addition, when using measures of cognition other than the MMSE, acupuncture in combination with conventional medicine is no more effective than conventional medicine alone ( 103428 ). A moderate-sized clinical trial in patients with mild to moderate Alzheimer disease which was not included in either meta-analysis also shows that receiving acupuncture three times weekly for 12 weeks might improve cognitive function and clinical status, but not activities of daily living or behavioral measures, when compared with donepezil 10 mg daily ( 100772 ).

Angina.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with angina.

A meta-analysis of small preliminary clinical studies shows that acupuncture alone or with conventional medicine seems to further relieve angina and reduce nitroglycerin use when compared with medicine alone ( 100776 ). However, as all of the studies in the analysis were conducted in China and outcomes measured did not comply with international standards, the generalizability of these results is unclear. A larger clinical study, also conducted in China in patients with chronic stable angina using conventional medicine, shows that receiving acupuncture on the disease-affected meridian reduces angina attacks when compared with receiving acupuncture on the wrong meridian, sham acupuncture, or being on the waitlist control ( 100784 ). A few small studies conducted in other countries have yielded conflicting findings. Some preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture may decrease the occurrence of angina and increase exercise tolerance in patients with stable and exercise-induced angina when compared to sham acupuncture or no treatment ( 29659 , 29661 ). However, other preliminary clinical research shows that there is no difference in incidence of angina and exercise tolerance between acupuncture and sham acupuncture in patients with treatment-resistant and stable angina pectoris ( 29660 ).

Ankylosing spondylitis.

Although there has been interest in using acupuncture for ankylosing spondylitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acupuncture for this condition.

Antidepressant-induced sexual dysfunction.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with sexual dysfunction due to antidepressant use.

Observational research has found that administering acupuncture to patients with sexual dysfunction due to antidepressant use is associated with an improvement in drive, erection, and ability to orgasm in males and an improvement in desire, libido, and lubrication in females ( 101388 ).

Anxiety.

Some research suggests that acupuncture or electroacupuncture, alone or in combination with other treatments, is beneficial in patients with anxiety.

A small clinical study in patients with various anxiety disorders, including panic disorder and generalized anxiety disorder, shows that receiving acupuncture in combination with a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) for 4 weeks improves state and trait anxiety scores by 53%, compared with 46% in those receiving sham acupuncture in combination with an SSRI and 42% in those receiving an SSRI alone ( 107254 ). Another clinical study in patients with various anxiety disorders shows that receiving 10 weekly sessions of acupuncture or electroacupuncture, either alone or with other anti-anxiolytic treatment, improves scores of Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI), Generalized Anxiety Disorder questionnaire (GAD-7), and Overall Anxiety Severity and Impairment Scale (OASIS) as early as week 5, with continued improvement observed at week 10, when compared with standard treatment alone ( 108594 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of blinding and sham control.

Arrhythmia.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with arrhythmia.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that acupuncture plus traditional Chinese medicine modestly increases the proportion of patients with at least significantly improved symptoms of arrhythmia when compared with traditional Chinese medicine alone. There were also modest improvements in the daily number of premature beats and the left ventricular ejection fraction ( 111803 ). This meta-analysis is limited by the availability of a small number of low-quality studies without appropriate sham controls.

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with eczema.

Preliminary clinical research shows that receiving eight acupuncture treatments does not reduce eczema severity when compared with no intervention. However, there was a modest reduction in itch ( 109833 ).

Atrial fibrillation.

Early research suggests that acupuncture might be beneficial for atrial fibrillation. However, some research suggests acupuncture does not improve persistent atrial fibrillation.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture may be beneficial for patients with atrial fibrillation ( 104718 , 104722 , 109818 ). A meta-analysis of mainly low-quality clinical research shows that acupuncture at the NeiGuan (PC6) point, either alone or with other treatments, results in a 40% increased rate of conversion to normal sinus rhythm when compared to control ( 104718 ). In addition, another meta-analysis shows that acupuncture alone may be as effective as conventional medications for patients with atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter ( 104722 ). However, most of the studies in these analyses were of unclear methodological quality and only 2-3 studies could be included in each meta-analysis due to heterogeneity of design. It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with specific types of atrial fibrillation. A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that the benefits of acupuncture when used as adjunct to pharmacological resuscitation may be specific to paroxysmal atrial fibrillation, and not persistent atrial fibrillation ( 109818 ). However, the majority of research is conducted in patients with paroxysmal atrial fibrillation and only two studies in patients with persistent atrial fibrillation were available. An additional small clinical study in patients with persistent, treatment-refractory atrial fibrillation undergoing electrical cardioversion shows that receiving weekly acupuncture at the PC5, PC6, ST36, and ST37 points, starting 2 weeks prior to cardioversion and continuing for 10 weeks, does not improve recurrence rate or cardiac function when compared with sham acupuncture ( 108589 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the short duration of treatment and follow-up.

Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with ADHD.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture treatment 2-7 times weekly for 4-12 weeks is approximately 2.2-fold more likely to have at least some effect on symptoms of ADHD when compared with standard medications (specific drugs and doses not provided) ( 104728 ). Another meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture treatment for 4-24 weeks modestly improves overall efficacy, as well as symptoms such as impulsivity and hyperactivity, when compared with unknown doses of methylphenidate hydrochloride or venlafaxine, or when used as adjunct with these medications when compared with medications alone ( 111790 ). However, the number of studies included in each analysis was small and the overall quality of evidence was low.

Autism spectrum disorder.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for children with autism.

Meta-analyses of mainly low-quality clinical research show that acupuncture, when used for 2-10 months in addition to behavioral and educational intervention, has a moderate effect on overall symptoms of autism in children. The addition of acupuncture increases effectiveness by up to 50% when compared to behavioral and educational interventions alone. However, studies included in the analysis were heterogeneous with respect to treatment methods and there is little information provided regarding specific symptoms ( 104729 , 111801 ). Another meta-analysis of low-quality research in children with autism shows that acupuncture, when used in addition to behavioral intervention for up to 6 months, improves childhood autism rating scale scores by 5 points and autism behavioral checklist scores by 7 points when compared with behavioral intervention alone ( 107244 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies and the risk for publication bias.

Autoimmune thyroiditis.

Small clinical studies suggest that acupuncture may modestly improve thyroid function in adults with Hashimoto thyroiditis.

A meta-analysis of small clinical trials in adults with Hashimoto thyroiditis with or without hypothyroidism shows that adding acupuncture to levothyroxine treatment for 1-3 months modestly improves thyroid hormone and autoantibody levels when compared with levothyroxine alone ( 114230 ). These findings are limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies and the short treatment duration.

Bell palsy.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for this condition.

Preliminary clinical research and a meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture improves symptoms of Bell palsy when compared with standard care ( 88519 , 88520 , 88521 , 100783 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that starting acupuncture, alone or in combination with moxibustion, within 7 days of onset may shorten symptoms and reduce facial disability when compared with various controls or placebo, but does not improve symptoms in patients beyond the 7-day acute stage ( 108115 , 108587 ). One large meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research suggests that starting acupuncture within 7 days of onset modestly improves symptoms of Bell palsy when compared with placebo, but not when compared with receiving prednisone ( 108587 ). The validity of this finding is limited due to unclear reporting and the variety of treatment protocols included. All studies discussed were conducted in China; therefore, it is unclear if the benefits of acupuncture are generalizable to other geographic locations.

Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH).

Electroacupuncture has only been evaluated in combination with electromoxibustion; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A small preliminary clinical trial in patients with BPH treated with alpha blockers shows that electroacupuncture treatment in combination with electromoxibustion 3 times weekly for 6 weeks results in clinically beneficial effects on symptom severity in 77% of patients compared with 20% of those continuing alpha blocker treatment only. These benefits lasted for 6 weeks following study completion. There was no benefit on urinary flow rate, prostate volume, or life quality ( 111770 ).

Bipolar disorder.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with bipolar disorder.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture at targeted acupoints is no more effective than acupuncture at placebo points for improving symptoms of bipolar disorder in patients on stable psychotropic medications ( 104730 ).

Breast cancer-related hot flashes.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with hot flashes related to breast cancer.

In patients undergoing treatment for breast cancer, a meta-analysis and most preliminary clinical research show that although acupuncture and electroacupuncture might decrease hot flashes when compared with baseline, neither treatment affects the frequency or severity of hot flashes when compared with sham acupuncture or relaxation techniques ( 29669 , 29670 , 101372 ). One clinical study shows that acupuncture decreases hot flashes when compared with sham acupuncture in patients with breast cancer ( 104744 ).

Breast engorgement.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with breast engorgement.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture does not improve pain and swelling of breasts in patients during lactation when compared with a control group ( 88578 ).

Carpal tunnel syndrome.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research and a meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome shows that acupuncture and laser acupuncture have little to no effect on carpal tunnel symptoms when compared with placebo or sham acupuncture ( 29683 , 100770 ). However, other preliminary clinical research in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome shows that receiving acupuncture in combination with other treatments might be beneficial. One small clinical trial shows that receiving 10 sessions of acupuncture treatment in addition to physical therapy modestly reduces pain and disability when compared with physical therapy alone. There was no effect on grip strength ( 111846 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that low-level laser acupuncture and transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) can reduce pain in patients who previously failed standard medical or surgical treatments for carpal tunnel syndrome ( 29684 ). Also, when used twice weekly in addition to nightly hand splinting, acupuncture reduces pain by a small amount when compared to hand splinting without acupuncture. There was no effect on function ( 102645 ).

Cerebral palsy.

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may modestly improve symptoms in children with cerebral palsy.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in children with cerebral palsy shows that acupuncture treatment in combination with routine treatment modestly improves muscle tone and muscle tension when compared with routine treatment alone. Also, some evidence of effectiveness occurred in 91.5% of patients given acupuncture treatment compared with 74.2% of those given only routine treatment ( 111839 ).

Chemotherapy-induced leukopenia.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with leukopenia following treatment with chemotherapy.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture can increase leukocyte counts in patients undergoing chemotherapy and experiencing chemotherapy-induced leukopenia ( 88577 ). However, poor study methodology and the small number of patients enrolled in the trials limits the reliability of these findings.

Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy.

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture modestly improves pain in patients with neuropathy following treatment with chemotherapy.

The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that acupuncture may be offered to patients with chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy. This recommendation is based on weak evidence from low-quality research suggesting that benefits outweigh harms ( 109161 ). Meta-analyses of available low-quality clinical trials show that receiving acupuncture modestly reduces chemotherapy-induced neuropathic pain and improves quality of life when compared with control groups receiving usual care, medication, and/or specific B vitamins ( 114203 , 114232 , 114251 ). In addition, two small pooled analyses of preliminary clinical trials show that acupuncture is more effective than injection with B vitamins for reducing chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy ( 102636 ). Other clinical research shows that adding acupuncture to standard care for 10 weeks almost doubles the number of patients with a modest improvement in the most troublesome patient-reported outcome when compared to standard care alone. The most common troublesome symptoms were numbness, pain or burning, tingling, and mobility issues. There were also improvements in quality of life ( 109837 ). A small clinical trial in patients treated with chemotherapy for colorectal cancer has compared single acupuncture sessions with sham acupuncture sessions on peripheral neuropathy pain severity. Pain and unpleasant sensations in the face were modestly reduced after each acupuncture session. However, there were no benefits on pain in the hands or feet ( 111836 ).

Chemotherapy-related fatigue.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with chemotherapy-related fatigue.

A small clinical study in patients with chemotherapy-related fatigue shows that receiving once weekly acupuncture for 6 weeks is no different than sham acupuncture for reducing fatigue ( 101377 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with CFS.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical studies shows that acupuncture may have a moderate effect on symptoms of CFS when compared to control. There was a 55% increase in the proportion of patients with complete or partially improved symptoms of fatigue when compared with control groups treated with sham acupuncture, Chinese herbal medicine, conventional medicine, or usual care ( 104731 ). However, another meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research shows that while acupuncture modestly increases the overall response rate when compared with no intervention, benefits were reduced when compared with acupuncture in combination with moxibustion or traditional Chinese herbal medicine, or when compared with either treatment alone ( 109831 ). Higher quality research is needed to confirm these results.

Chronic kidney disease (CKD).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in adults with CKD.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical trials in adults with CKD shows that treatment with acupuncture therapy reduces serum creatinine levels by a modest amount when compared with controls including sham acupuncture or usual care. Acupuncture does not seem to be beneficial for reducing blood urea nitrogen levels. The effects of acupuncture treatment on pruritus or other physical symptoms are unclear due to limited or contradictory findings ( 114239 ).

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with COPD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with COPD shows that receiving acupuncture for up to 6 months can decrease dyspnea and improve 6-minute walking distance by up to 80 meters when compared with sham acupuncture. When used in combination with other treatments such as medications or traditional Chinese medicine, a meta-analysis shows that 6-minute walking distance improves by 38 meters ( 29685 , 97563 , 101382 , 111809 ). However, a separate meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with COPD experiencing acute exacerbations shows that receiving acupuncture in combination with medications does not improve dyspnea, duration of hospital stay, or exercise capacity when compared with medications alone ( 114238 ). However, only 2-3 clinical trials were included in each analysis. Small clinical studies have also evaluated the impact of acupuncture on lung function. One meta-analysis of mostly small, low-quality studies in patients with stable COPD shows that receiving acupuncture treatment can improve pulmonary function when compared with sham acupuncture or when used in addition to other treatments ( 111809 ). A separate meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with COPD experiencing acute exacerbations shows that receiving acupuncture in combination with medications improves some, but not all, measures of pulmonary function when compared with medications alone ( 114238 ).

Cocaine dependence.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for cocaine dependence.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with cocaine dependence who are maintained on methadone shows that acupuncture is no more effective than sham acupuncture for reducing cravings ( 88651 ).

Cognitive impairment.

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may modestly improve cognitive function in patients with cognitive impairment.

A meta-analysis of mainly preliminary clinical research shows that receiving acupuncture with conventional or Chinese medications for up to 12 weeks results in 3-fold higher odds of clinical efficacy when compared with conventional or Chinese medications alone. A meta-analysis of two studies shows that receiving acupuncture alone results in a 2-fold higher odds of clinical efficacy when compared to conventional medications. However, clinical efficacy was not specifically defined. Limited information provided in the meta-analysis suggests there were also improvements in most of the specific assessment scores, including the mini-mental state examination scores (MMSE) ( 105404 ). Additional clinical research in patients with cognitive impairment shows that acupuncture treatment twice a week for 8 weeks, alone or in combination with an oral traditional Chinese compound Yishen Granule, modestly improves some measures of cognitive function as well as the proportion of patients with at least a 12% improvement in scores, when compared with sham acupuncture plus placebo ( 111834 ).

Colic.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in infants with colic.

A small clinical study in infants with colic shows that giving acupuncture for 30 seconds daily for 3 days does not reduce crying when compared with no treatment ( 101586 ).

Constipation.

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may increase bowel movements by a small amount in patients with constipation.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research, as well as individual clinical trials, in patients with functional constipation show that receiving manual acupuncture or electroacupuncture for 2-10 weeks increases bowel movements by approximately 0.8 per week when compared with sham acupuncture and by approximately 0.5 per week when compared with various conventional medications. Stool formation and related symptoms were also improved when compared with sham acupuncture. However, acupuncture was no more effective than various conventional medications, including prucalopride, mosapride, and lactulose ( 97557 , 103430 , 111815 ). Also, a meta-analysis of 5 small studies in patients with cancer and opioid-induced constipation shows that patients receiving acupuncture with or without medication have a remission rate of 87%, compared with a 79% remission rate in the control group. Symptom scores and quality of life were improved in those receiving acupuncture ( 107242 ).

Diabetes.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with type 2 diabetes.

A meta-analysis of 21 low- and moderate-quality studies in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that acupuncture in combination with antidiabetic agents has no effect on glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels, although it does reduce fasting blood glucose levels by 7 mg/dL, when compared with antidiabetic agents alone ( 107250 ). Another meta-analysis of generally low-quality studies in patients with type 2 diabetes and obesity also shows that acupuncture has modest beneficial effects on HbA1c levels, as well as fasting plasma glucose, triglycerides, body mass index (BMI), body fat, and waist circumference. Most patients in the included studies were also treated with metformin or another antidiabetes medication ( 109843 ). A meta-analysis of clinical trials in patients with diabetic kidney disease shows that acupuncture in combination with antidiabetic agents also has modest beneficial effects on HbA1c, fasting plasma glucose, triglycerides, total cholesterol, and high-density lipoprotein cholesterol ( 114233 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies.

Diabetic nephropathy.

It is unclear whether acupuncture is beneficial in adults with diabetes and kidney disease.

A meta-analysis of small clinical trials in adults with diabetic kidney disease in China shows that acupuncture treatment is modestly more beneficial than conventional medications alone for relieving overall signs and symptoms of kidney disease and improving renal function indices. However, there was high heterogeneity in the conventional treatment regimens employed across each study; most patients were not taking conventional renoprotective medications, such as renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitors ( 114233 ). The validity of these findings is limited by this high heterogeneity and small study sizes.

Diabetic neuropathy.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with diabetic neuropathy.

A meta-analysis of mainly small and non-blinded clinical trials in patients with diabetic neuropathy shows that acupuncture treatment modestly reduces pain intensity and improves quality of life when compared with control groups receiving usual care, other treatments, or sham acupuncture ( 114222 ).

Dry eye.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for improving symptoms of dry eye; the available research is conflicting.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies shows that acupuncture, used in combination with artificial tears, is modestly more beneficial than artificial tears alone for improving symptoms of dry eye ( 88576 , 114237 ). Conversely, a small clinical trial shows that acupuncture does not improve symptoms of dry eye in patients using artificial tears ( 88575 ). However, this study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups.

Dry mouth.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with dry mouth.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture improves symptoms of dry mouth or salivary flow rates when compared to baseline ( 88669 , 88670 , 88671 ). However, when compared with sham acupuncture, acupuncture does not appear to be more beneficial for increasing salivary flow rates ( 88669 , 88670 ). In patients undergoing radiation therapy for head or neck cancer, a large sham-controlled study shows that treatment with acupuncture during radiation therapy has a moderate effect over sham acupuncture at one of the clinical sites, located in China, for the extent of subjective symptoms of dry mouth after 12 months. However, this benefit was not observed at the second clinical site located in the U.S. or in the overall trial population ( 102644 ).

Dysmenorrhea.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for dysmenorrhea.

Preliminary clinical research and a meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture decreases pain associated with dysmenorrhea when compared with sham acupuncture or no treatment ( 88507 , 88510 , 97567 ). However, higher quality research did not find a difference between acupuncture and sham acupuncture for dysmenorrhea ( 97560 ). Acupuncture has also been compared to non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) for dysmenorrhea. Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical studies show that acupuncture modestly reduces pain and discomfort associated with dysmenorrhea when compared to therapy with NSAIDs ( 97560 , 97567 ). However, poor study quality limits the reliability of these findings.

Emergence delirium.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of emergence delirium in children.

Some preliminary clinical research in children ages 1-6 years shows that intraoperative acupuncture reduces emergence delirium before discharge by a small amount when compared with no acupuncture ( 102652 ). However, most clinical research shows that intraoperative acupuncture does not prevent emergence delirium in children ( 102650 , 111837 ). A meta-analysis of very low-quality clinical research shows that intraoperative acupuncture treatment does not prevent emergence delirium when compared with control treatments including standard care with no acupuncture treatment, sham acupuncture, or no electrical stimulus ( 111837 ). The studies included in this meta-analysis were small and may not have been adequately powered to detect differences between groups. Also, the individual clinical trials used different scales to assess effectiveness, as well as different acupuncture points.

Endometriosis.

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may reduce pain associated with endometriosis.

A meta-analysis of low-quality research shows that acupuncture has a small effect on pain when compared to control groups receiving conventional medications or traditional Chinese medications. In addition, the odds of having at least some reduction in pain and symptoms was approximately 2-fold higher than in the control groups ( 104732 ). Higher quality research is needed to confirm these findings.

Epilepsy.

Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may not reduce seizures.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical trials in patients with epilepsy show that adding acupuncture to treatment with traditional Chinese medicine or valproate does not further reduce seizure duration or frequency. Acupuncture also does not seem to be better than sham acupuncture in reducing seizure frequency ( 88514 , 97548 ).

Erectile dysfunction (ED).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with ED.

Preliminary clinical research shows that traditional acupuncture is more effective than sham acupuncture for improving symptoms of ED ( 88515 ). Another preliminary clinical study shows that acupuncture can improve the quality of erection and restore sexual activity when compared to baseline ( 88517 ). However, another preliminary clinical study shows that acupuncture does not improve symptoms of ED better than control ( 88516 ).

Exercise-induced muscle soreness.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for exercise-induced muscle soreness.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture does not inhibit exercise-induced delayed-onset muscle pain when compared with sham acupuncture. When compared with no treatment, acupuncture had a small effect the next day, but not 2-3 days later ( 103422 ).

Fecal incontinence.

Although there has been interest in using acupuncture for fecal incontinence, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acupuncture for this condition.

Gastroparesis.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for gastroparesis.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research in patients with diabetic gastroparesis show that acupuncture, alone or combined with conventional therapy, modestly improves some symptoms, short-term ( 100773 , 111774 ). One meta-analysis of 16 studies shows that receiving acupuncture treatment along with usual care or medications results in a 26% increased odds of effectiveness when compared to usual care or medications alone. However, effectiveness was not specifically defined ( 111774 ). These analyses are limited by poor quality of the included trials and non-validated outcome measures. The use of acupuncture for the treatment of postsurgical gastroparesis has also been investigated. One meta-analysis in patients with postoperative gastroparesis following abdominal surgery shows with moderate certainty that acupuncture treatment in combination with conventional treatment increases clinical effectiveness by 20% and the rate of recovery by 61% when compared with conventional medications alone. It is unclear if acupuncture treatment is beneficial for patients undergoing other types of surgeries due to the limited available research. In this analysis the efficacy and recovery rates are based on gastric juice volume, radiography, and subjective symptoms ( 111831 ).

Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with GAD.

In patients with GAD, meta-analyses of mainly preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture moderately reduces anxiety symptoms more than control groups receiving conventional medications, traditional Chinese treatments or medications, psychotherapy, or sham acupuncture ( 104723 , 111778 ). One analysis shows that the greatest benefit occurred when anxiety symptoms were measured by self-assessment, when results were compared with conventional medications, and when the study period was less than 6 weeks ( 104723 ).

Glaucoma.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with glaucoma.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with glaucoma shows that a single session of manual acupuncture or electroacupuncture reduces intraocular pressure by a small amount 60 minutes later when compared with sham acupuncture. After four interventions over two weeks, the decrease in intraocular pressure was evident immediately after treatment ( 103423 ).

Hearing loss.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for hearing loss.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture does not improve hearing in adults or children with hearing loss when compared with sham acupuncture or baseline ( 88667 , 88668 ).

Hemiplegic shoulder pain.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for hemiplegic shoulder pain.

A meta-analysis of mainly preliminary clinical trials shows that 10-30 sessions of acupuncture treatment is as effective as rehabilitation therapy involving exercise and other treatments for the improvement of post-stroke shoulder pain and function ( 111829 ). This analysis is limited by the availability of small clinical trials and the heterogeneity of acupuncture and control treatments used in the available research.

Herpes zoster (shingles).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with this condition.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in adults with herpes zoster shows that treatment with acupuncture or moxibustion reduces posterior neuralgia and might modestly improve blister healing and pain scores when compared with standard treatments such as topical or oral acyclovir, gabapentin, and vitamin B1 ( 108593 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the variety of treatment protocols used and the small size of available clinical trials.

HIV/AIDS.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with this condition.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture might improve psychological symptoms, especially those related to stress, and quality of life in patients with HIV when compared to baseline ( 88597 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the small study size and lack of statistical comparison to the control group.

Hypertension.

Most research suggests that acupuncture may reduce blood pressure by a small amount in patients with hypertension. However, it is unclear if changes are sustainable or clinically relevant.

Most clinical research and a meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with hypertension shows that acupuncture has a slight blood pressure lowering effect when compared with no treatment or with sham acupuncture ( 88557 , 88558 , 88559 , 88560 , 100781 ). However, it is unclear if this effect is sustainable or clinically beneficial.

Infertility.

Most research shows that acupuncture does not improve fertility in patients undergoing intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI) or in vitro fertilization (IVF). It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for improving fertility in patients not undergoing IVF or ICSI, or if patients with certain forms of infertility might be more likely to benefit.

Most research shows that acupuncture does not improve fertility in patients undergoing IVF or ICSI. Although one meta-analysis in patients receiving IVF shows that traditional acupuncture modestly improves pregnancy rate when compared with sham or no acupuncture ( 114223 ), most meta-analyses show that acupuncture does not improve ongoing pregnancy or live birth rates when compared with control ( 100780 , 108595 , 111806 , 114223 ). Also, although some individual clinical studies show that there is a difference in pregnancy rates, most available clinical trials show that there is no difference in live birth rates between patients receiving acupuncture and those receiving sham acupuncture prior to and during IVF or ICSI ( 88562 , 88563 , 88564 , 88565 , 97549 , 97562 , 114204 ). Acupuncture has been evaluated in specific subgroups of patients with infertility, with mixed results. A meta-analysis specific to studies of patients undergoing frozen-thawed embryo transfer following IVF or ICSI shows that acupuncture increases the clinical pregnancy rate by about 54%, but does not affect the live birth rate, when compared with sham acupuncture or no adjuvant treatment ( 109836 ). Also, another meta-analysis of studies in patients with poor endometrial receptivity shows that acupuncture is more beneficial than sham acupuncture or no adjuvant treatment for improving clinical pregnancy rate ( 109811 ). A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in a specific group of patients not undergoing assisted reproductive techniques shows that acupuncture improves pregnancy rate by 84% when compared with control ( 100782 ). Live birth rate was not reported. Furthermore, when used alone, a meta-analysis of clinical research shows that acupuncture is about twice as effective as clomiphene alone for increasing pregnancy rate ( 102634 ). However, in a meta-analysis of two studies in patients with anovulatory infertility comparing the use of acupuncture plus clomiphene with clomiphene alone, there is no beneficial effect of acupuncture on pregnancy rate or live birth rate ( 102634 ). Reasons for any discrepancies are unclear. The available meta-analyses suggest that acupuncture may be beneficial in specific groups of patients not undergoing IVF. However, the overall quality of evidence is low, control groups were not limited to sham acupuncture, and many studies did not report live birth rate. Also, some of the meta-analyses included transcutaneous electrical acustimulation (TEAS) ( 102634 , 109811 , 109836 , 111806 ). Dosing may also play a role. Some research limits the use of acupuncture to a few days in the cycle of interest. However, subgroup analyses from one meta-analysis show that the beneficial effect of acupuncture on pregnancy rate is limited to studies in which acupuncture is conducted over one or more menstrual cycles ( 109811 ). More research is needed to determine whether acupuncture is beneficial for patients with infertility.

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with IBS.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with IBS shows with moderate certainty that acupuncture treatment, most commonly 3-5 times per week for 4-8 weeks, reduces symptom severity and abdominal pain modestly more than conventional medication ( 111000 ). The evidence related to acupuncture treatment when compared with sham acupuncture is mixed. A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows with low certainty that acupuncture is no more effective than sham acupuncture for overall symptom severity reduction. However, abdominal pain is reduced by a small amount ( 111000 ). Other clinical research shows that acupuncture is no more effective than sham acupuncture or taking Bifidobacterium by mouth for improving symptoms of IBS, quality of life, or response to treatment. However, acupuncture does appear to modestly improve symptoms when compared with no treatment ( 88572 , 88573 , 91768 , 101396 , 109845 ). Some preliminary research suggests that acupuncture might be beneficial in patients with diarrhea-predominant IBS (IBS-D) and not constipation-predominant IBS (IBS-D). Preliminary clinical research shows that 18 acupuncture sessions over a 6-week period reduces overall symptoms of IBS by a small amount more than taking pinaverium bromide or polyethylene glycol, with benefits lasting for up to an additional 12 weeks. Patients with IBS-D took pinaverium bromide, whereas patients with IBS-C took polyethylene glycol. A sub-analysis suggests that patients with IBS-D benefited more from acupuncture than those with IBS-C ( 103420 ). More research is needed to determine if some patients with IBS benefit more than others.

Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis).

A single session of acupuncture may be beneficial for acute pain from kidney stones. It is unclear if acupuncture improves chronic pain from kidney stones.

A clinical study in patients with pain due to kidney stones shows that a single session of acupuncture at the Sanyinjiao (SP6) and Yinlingquan (SP9) acupoints improves pain during a 40-minute, post-treatment observation period when compared with receiving one intravenous dose of lornoxicam 8 mg ( 108592 ). Another clinical study in patients with moderate to severe pain related to kidney stones given intramuscular diclofenac shows that a single session of acupuncture at Yaotongdian (EX-UE 7) acupoints results in a pain decrease of at least 50% in 78% of patients within 10 minutes, compared with 10% of patients given sham acupuncture. Benefits persisted for up to 30 minutes, but there was no difference between groups in use of rescue analgesia ( 109828 ). Although this research suggests that acupuncture may be beneficial in the initial emergency treatment of pain related to kidney stones, the effects of continued acupuncture are unclear.

Lactation.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for postpartum hypogalactia.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research in postpartum adults with low milk production shows that acupuncture treatment modestly increases milk volume, the rate of exclusive breastfeeding, and levels of serum prolactin when compared with control groups, most of which received Chinese herbs ( 114253 ). Due to a lack of available research, it is unclear whether acupuncture is more beneficial than sham acupuncture or galactagogues for improving lactation.

Lung cancer.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for improving quality of life or treatment response in adults receiving chemotherapy for lung cancer.

A meta-analysis of two small clinical trials in adults with non-small cell lung cancer receiving chemotherapy shows that acupuncture with or without moxibustion modestly improves the rate of treatment response and quality of life when compared with chemotherapy alone ( 114213 ). However, these two small studies were considered low- to moderate-quality, limiting the validity of these findings.

Lymphedema.

Early research suggests that acupuncture may reduce pain and swelling in patients with lymphedema.

Meta-analyses of low-quality clinical research shows that acupuncture over 4-9 weeks is over four times more likely to be effective for reducing pain and swelling associated with breast cancer-related lymphedema when compared with control. However, indicators of effectiveness are not consistent between studies and the control treatments included the use of various medications, other conventional treatments, and/or functional exercise ( 102626 , 102627 ). Additional meta-analyses show that acupuncture, alone or with moxibustion, might have modest beneficial effects on some, but not all, measures of lymphedema related to breast cancer when compared with conventional treatments such as diosmin or pneumatic circulation ( 108116 ). However, each meta-analysis included only two clinical trials of generally low- to moderate-quality, limiting the validity of these findings.

Male infertility.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for improving male infertility.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in males with oligospermia and asthenozoospermia shows that acupuncture does not improve sperm motility, sperm concentration, or semen volume when compared with placebo acupuncture or other treatments such as clomiphene or vitamins E and C. Various types of acupuncture were included in this analysis, such as manual, scalp, warm, auricular, and three-edged needle, in addition to other forms of acustimulation, including transcutaneous electrical acustimulation and moxibustion ( 108586 ). All studies were low-quality and conducted in China; it is unclear if these effects are generalizable to other geographic locations.

Malnutrition.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for improving appetite in children with malnutrition related to inadequate or selective food intake.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that manual acupuncture alone has a modest effect on improving food intake in children with malnutrition due to inadequate and/or selective food intake when compared with conventional treatment and a large effect when compared with no treatment. However, when used as adjunct to conventional medications, manual acupuncture is no more effective than conventional medication alone ( 109830 ).

Melasma.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for melasma.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that acupuncture is better than various oral or topical treatments for improving melasma severity ( 104733 ). However, higher quality research is needed to confirm these results.

Menopausal symptoms.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for menopause-associated hot flashes.

Some clinical research in adults with moderately severe hot flashes shows that acupuncture does not reduce hot flash severity when compared with sham acupuncture ( 91765 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that needle acupuncture can reduces hot flash severity by up to 22%, but does not reduce hot flash frequency, when compared with sham acupuncture ( 88580 , 88581 , 88582 , 88583 , 88586 ). Conversely, one study shows that acupuncture decreases frequency of hot flashes when compared with no acupuncture after 4 and 12 weeks of treatment ( 88584 ). Reasons for these conflicting results are not clear but may relate to the small sample sizes and differing control groups. Electroacupuncture has also been tried for menopausal symptoms. A large clinical study during peri-menopause shows that 24 electroacupuncture treatments, given over 8 weeks, do not relieve menopausal symptoms, but seem to improve quality of life, when compared with electroacupuncture given at sham points ( 100777 ). Acupuncture has also been compared with hormonal therapy for the treatment of menopausal symptoms. A meta-analysis of 15 small but mostly high-quality studies shows that acupuncture, particularly manual acupuncture, is more likely to have a favorable effect on clinical effective rate when compared with hormonal therapy. Acupuncture also improves the severity of menopause symptoms, with reductions of almost 3 points in the Kupperman index, when compared with hormonal therapy ( 107249 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the risk for publication bias.

Myasthenia gravis.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with myasthenia gravis.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical trials shows that, when acupuncture is used in combination with medications such as immunosuppressants and/or steroids, symptoms are reduced by an additional 42% when compared with medications alone. These improvements occurred after 12 weeks of treatment, but not 8 weeks ( 102629 ). Another meta-analysis of 13 small clinical trials shows that acupuncture, usually used in combination with Chinese herbs or the medication pyridostigmine, increases the odds of improving clinical symptoms when compared with pyridostigmine alone ( 114231 ).

Myofascial pain syndrome.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with myofascial pain syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research shows that 5-14 sessions of acupuncture are effective for myofascial head and neck pain. A meta-analysis of four preliminary clinical trials shows that acupuncture is moderately more effective than no treatment or sham acupuncture ( 102635 ). Some individual studies agree ( 88587 , 88589 ).

Neurodermatitis.

Although there has been interest in using acupuncture for neurodermatitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acupuncture for this condition.

Neurogenic bladder.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with neurogenic bladder.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with spinal nerve injury shows that acupuncture treatment, usually in combination with treatments including bladder function training or intermittent catheterization, increases the odds of bladder function improvement by 4-fold when compared with these treatments alone ( 111811 ). Additional preliminary clinical research shows that electroacupuncture improves symptoms of a neurogenic bladder due to spinal cord injury, such as incontinence, dysuria, and urgency, when compared with standard therapy alone ( 88525 ).

Neuropathic pain.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with neuropathic pain.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with trigeminal neuralgia shows that acupuncture treatment, most commonly 5-7 times weekly for 4 weeks, modestly improves pain severity when compared with carbamazepine 0.2 to 1.5 grams daily. Also, the proportion of patients experiencing an improvement in symptoms was improved by 20% ( 111841 ).

Nocturnal enuresis.

Early research suggests that acupuncture may reduce nocturnal enuresis in children.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture, alone or along with desmopressin, might help decrease nocturnal enuresis episodes in children when compared with sham acupuncture or no treatment ( 15997 , 88512 , 88513 ).

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with NAFLD.

A meta-analysis of 10 mostly low-quality studies in patients with NAFLD shows that acupuncture, with or without conventional medicine, is at least 3 times more likely to have a favorable effect on clinical efficacy when compared with conventional medicine alone ( 107252 ).

Obesity.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for weight loss.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture might decrease weight by a small amount; however, there is significant heterogeneity related to treatment methods and controls ( 88672 , 103421 ). The effect of acupuncture on weight loss in individual clinical studies is inconsistent and unclear ( 88673 , 88674 , 88675 , 111805 ). Acupuncture has also been studied as an adjunctive treatment for weight loss. Meta-analyses of clinical research in children and adults and a recent clinical trial show that adding acupuncture to lifestyle interventions is more effective than sham acupuncture or lifestyle interventions alone for weight loss. However, additional weight loss is minimal ( 100774 , 103421 , 109838 , 111821 , 114214 ).

Ophthalmoplegia.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with ophthalmoplegia.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture of the eye improves eye movement and other symptoms in patients with ophthalmoplegia when compared with standard therapy alone ( 88518 ).

Opioid withdrawal.

Early research suggests that acupuncture is beneficial in patients receiving treatment for opioid withdrawal or opioid use disorder.

One clinical trial shows that receiving acupuncture for 8 weeks as an adjunct to methadone treatment modestly reduces opioid cravings for up to 12 weeks when compared with sham acupuncture and methadone treatment. This study also shows that a methadone dose reduction of at least 20% was achieved by 37% of patients receiving acupuncture treatment for 8 weeks compared with only 16% of patients receiving sham acupuncture ( 114255 ). Some very small studies utilizing other forms of treatment for opioid withdrawal, including rapid opiate detoxification, have found that acupuncture may attenuate the severity of withdrawal symptoms ( 88654 , 88657 ).

Oral mucositis.

Although there has been interest in using acupuncture for radiation-induced oral mucositis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acupuncture for this purpose.

Osteoporosis.

It is unclear if electroacupuncture is beneficial in patients with osteoporosis.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical studies conducted in China in patients with osteoporosis or osteopenia shows that receiving electroacupuncture, alone or in combination with other treatments, improves back pain but does not affect bone mineral density or markers of bone metabolism when compared with other treatments, such as calcitriol, calcium carbonate, or Xianlingubao capsules ( 108598 ). All studies included in this analysis were small, low-quality, and nonblinded, limiting the validity of this finding.

Overactive bladder.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with overactive bladder.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture is as least as effective as medications such as tolterodine tartrate and solifenacin, but is no more effective than sham acupuncture, for improving overall symptoms ( 103201 , 109834 ). Poor methodology and a small number of available studies limits the reliability of these findings.

Pain (acute).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with acute pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture treatment for 15 minutes prior to insertion of an intrauterine device (IUD) reduces pain in the 10 minutes after insertion by at least 50% when compared with no acupuncture treatment ( 111844 ).

Parkinson disease.

Some research shows that acupuncture might improve motor function when used as an adjunct to levodopa.

Pairwise and network meta-analyses of 17 relatively low-quality studies in patients with Parkinson disease show that acupuncture, including bee venom acupuncture, manual acupuncture, and electroacupuncture, in combination with levodopa therapy, improves motor function when compared with levodopa therapy alone ( 107258 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies. Another meta-analysis of 2-3 clinical trials shows that receiving acupuncture in combination with various antiparkinsonian medications modestly improves overall disease rating when compared with medication alone; however, acupuncture does not seem to be more beneficial than sham acupuncture ( 114226 ). Poor methodology and the small number of studies limits the reliability of these findings. Acupuncture is also of interest for improving mood in patients with Parkinson disease. Meta-analyses of clinical research in patients with insomnia related to Parkinson disease show that acupuncture treatment modestly improves sleep when compared with control (usually medication alone) ( 114226 , 114228 ). One of these meta-analyses also shows that acupuncture reduces feelings of anxiety and depression when compared with control ( 114228 ). However, the other meta-analysis suggests that acupuncture does not have beneficial effects on feelings of anxiety or depression in patients with Parkinson disease ( 114226 ). Acupuncture has also been investigated in combination with tuina massage and qi gong. A small clinical study shows that this combination improves subjective symptoms of Parkinson disease, such as quality of life, when compared with baseline ( 88871 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Acupuncture is also of interest for improving symptoms of dysphagia in adults with Parkinson disease. One meta-analysis of 3 small and generally low-quality clinical trials shows that acupuncture, when used as an adjunct to swallowing training, is modestly more beneficial than swallowing training alone for improving swallowing function ( 114227 ). Some clinical research suggests that acupuncture may be modestly beneficial for constipation in people with Parkinson disease. A meta-analysis of small clinical studies shows that receiving acupuncture in combination with conventional treatment modestly improves the rate of complete spontaneous bowel movements and constipation-related quality of life when compared with conventional treatment alone ( 114250 ). These findings are limited by small study sizes and high heterogeneity.

Paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia.

Preliminary clinical research shows that the addition of wrist-ankle acupuncture may improve paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia when compared with standard therapy alone ( 29682 ).

Parturition.

Early research suggests that acupuncture is not beneficial for preventing caesarean section.

A pooled analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture does not affect rates of caesarean section when compared with sham acupuncture, usual care, or sweeping of fetal membranes ( 97561 ). In addition, the majority of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture alone is not effective for reducing the risk of breech presentations and the incidence of caesarean section births; however, it might be beneficial when used along with moxibustion ( 88618 , 88619 , 88620 , 88621 ).

Pelvic pain.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with pelvic pain.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in females with chronic pelvic pain shows that treatment with acupuncture combined with pelvic floor rehabilitation therapy modestly reduces pain and improves pelvic floor dysfunction when compared with acupuncture or pelvic floor rehabilitation therapy alone. Also, there was a large difference in the overall effective rate ( 109815 ). This analysis is limited by the poor quality of available research.

Peptic ulcers.

Although there has been interest in using acupuncture for peptic ulcers, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acupuncture for this condition.

Peripheral neuropathy.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with peripheral neuropathy.

A pooled analysis of a small number of preliminary clinical studies shows that acupuncture is no different than sham acupuncture for reducing neuropathic pain. However, adding acupuncture to oral treatments, such as mecobalamin and Xiaoke bitong capsule, reduces pain intensity and improves quality of life when compared with oral treatments alone ( 97553 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the small size and high heterogeneity of the included studies. Also, one small, preliminary clinical study shows that acupuncture might improve nerve signaling when compared with no acupuncture in patients with peripheral neuropathy ( 88598 ).

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

It is unclear if acupuncture improves fertility or metabolic indices in patients with PCOS; the available research is conflicting.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research in patients with PCOS who are taking metformin shows that acupuncture treatment over 2-6 months increases pregnancy rate by 35% and improves the ovulation rate when compared with metformin alone ( 109840 ). An additional meta-analysis of low- to moderate-quality clinical research in patients with PCOS shows that using acupuncture combined with moxibustion in addition to basic treatment for 3-6 cycles increases pregnancy rate by about 81% and ovulation rate by 31%, and reduces miscarriage rate by 55%, when compared with basic treatment alone ( 108129 ). However, one large clinical study shows that receiving acupuncture for 30 minutes twice weekly for up to 16 weeks, with or without clomiphene, does not increase the rate of live births when compared with sham acupuncture ( 97568 ). Acupuncture has also been evaluated for improving hormonal indices in adults with PCOS. A meta-analysis of low- to moderate-quality clinical research in patients with PCOS shows that using acupuncture combined with moxibustion in addition to basic treatment for 3-6 months is associated with improvements in levels of luteinizing hormone (LH), testosterone, and insulin, but not follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) or estradiol ( 108129 ). A small clinical study shows that receiving low-frequency electroacupuncture twice weekly for up to 16 weeks has no effect on human chorionic gonadotrophin (HCG) stimulated-androgen secretion when compared with sham acupuncture ( 107253 ). Acupuncture has also been evaluated for improving metabolic indices in adults with PCOS. A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients mostly living in China shows that receiving manual or electroacupuncture does not have clinically meaningful effects on glycemic indices or lipid levels, although it may modestly reduce body mass index, when compared with control treatments such as sham acupuncture. It is unclear if the patients in these studies were also receiving treatment with conventional medications ( 105941 ). Conversely, a meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research in patients with PCOS who are taking metformin shows that acupuncture treatment over 2-6 months modestly improves insulin resistance when compared with metformin alone ( 109840 ). These meta-analyses do not include a secondary analysis of a large clinical trial ( 97568 ) conducted in patients in China. This secondary analysis found that receiving acupuncture for 30 minutes twice weekly for up to 16 weeks, with or without clomiphene, improves acne, as well as levels of homocysteine and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, when compared with sham acupuncture. However, there was no effect on insulin resistance or on levels of fasting plasma glucose, high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, or triglycerides ( 114208 ). The validity of findings in this population is limited by unclear reporting and lack of blinding. Also, most studies discussed were conducted in China; it is unclear if these effects are generalizable to other geographic locations.

Postherpetic neuralgia.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with postherpetic neuralgia.

A meta-analysis of two small, low-quality clinical studies shows that acupuncture improves pain a little better than pharmacologic therapy ( 100779 ). Studies in this analysis are limited by small size and short duration.

Postoperative bowel function.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for improving postoperative bowel function.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing colorectal cancer resection shows with low certainty that acupuncture treatment, usually postoperatively and for up to 2 weeks, reduces times to first flatus by approximately 46 minutes and time to first bowel movement or defecation by up to 1.4 hours when compared with usual care and sham acupuncture ( 111822 ).

Postoperative ileus.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with postoperative ileus.

A meta-analysis of low- to moderate-quality clinical research shows that both manual and electroacupuncture, usually given postoperatively, modestly reduce the time to first postoperative flatus, defecation, and bowel sounds when compared with usual care or sham acupuncture ( 109817 ). However, only a small number of trials have compared acupuncture with sham acupuncture and individual studies have yielded mixed findings ( 109817 ). One study included in the analysis in patients with colorectal cancer receiving laparoscopic surgery shows that electroacupuncture does not affect time to first well-tolerated solid food or flatus, but might reduce time to first defecation. However, the sham acupuncture group took more opioid doses when compared with the electroacupuncture group, which may have confounded results by increasing the risk of opioid-induced constipation in the sham acupuncture group ( 101400 ). Another small clinical trial in patients with colon cancer undergoing colectomy shows that receiving electroacupuncture is no different than sham acupuncture for reducing postoperative ileus or improving time to resolution of postoperative ileus ( 101378 ). Most studies discussed were conducted in China; it is unclear if these effects are generalizable to other geographic locations.

Postoperative pain.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with postoperative pain.

Most meta-analyses and individual preliminary clinical studies show that manual acupuncture, laser acupuncture, and electroacupuncture might modestly decrease postoperative pain and/or analgesic requirements ( 29597 , 29629 , 29630 , 29631 , 29634 , 29636 , 29639 , 29640 , 29641 , 29642 )( 29658,101591,103431,109823,111777,111804,111824). However, not all research has been positive ( 29633 , 29638 , 103426 , 111822 ). Overall, poor study methodology and the small number of patients enrolled in the trials limit the reliability of these findings. The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that acupuncture may be offered to patients with cancer-related postoperative or post-procedural pain. This recommendation is based on weak evidence from low-quality research suggesting that benefits outweigh harms ( 109161 ).

Postoperative recovery.

Early research suggests that acupuncture is beneficial for preventing postoperative cognitive dysfunction and bladder dysfunction.

A meta-analysis of mainly preliminary clinical research shows that receiving acupuncture, usually starting prior to anesthesia and lasting through the surgery, moderately reduces the odds of having postoperative cognitive dysfunction one and three days after surgery when compared with no treatment. Mini-mental state examination (MMSE) scores were also improved. The benefit was no longer evident after seven days ( 104726 ). There is also interest in whether acupuncture prevents postoperative bladder dysfunction. A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients after hysterectomy shows that, in addition to bladder exercises, receiving mostly electro-acupuncture once daily for 5-30 days seems to reduce post-void residual urine, increase maximal bladder capacity, and increase urine flow rate when compared with bladder exercises alone ( 105940 ). All research has been conducted in China; it is unclear if these findings are generalizable to other geographic locations.

Postoperative sore throat.

Although there has been interest in using acupuncture for postoperative sore throat prevention, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acupuncture for this purpose.

Postpartum depression.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with postpartum depression.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical studies in patients with postpartum depression shows that acupuncture might be slightly better than antidepressants for reducing depression scores measured on the Hamilton Rating Scale for Depression (HAMD) ( 100775 ). This analysis was limited by the inclusion of an older trial suggesting that acupuncture has a large benefit when compared with an antidepressant agent that is not approved in U.S. or Canada.

Post-stroke fatigue.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for improving post-stroke fatigue.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that manual acupuncture treatment over 2-8 weeks is 15% more effective than routine treatment, including medication and rehabilitation, for improving post-stroke fatigue. Fatigue severity was also modestly reduced ( 111802 ). Studies in this analysis are limited by small size and poor methodological quality.

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with PTSD.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that acupuncture may improve subjective, self-reported symptoms of PTSD and may be as effective as cognitive-behavioral therapy ( 88605 ).

Pregnancy-induced nausea and vomiting.

Research on the use of acupuncture for pregnancy-induced nausea and vomiting is conflicting. Some research suggests possible benefit in patients with hyperemesis gravidarum.

Evidence from some preliminary clinical research, including a meta-analysis of clinical data, suggests that acupuncture is not effective in reducing nausea and vomiting during pregnancy ( 88592 , 82972 ). However, other clinical research in patients with hyperemesis gravidarum shows that acupuncture treatment improves nausea and vomiting. Two small clinical trials show that deep acupuncture at the PC6 acupoint might improve nausea and vomiting ( 88594 , 88595 ). A large clinical trial in patients with moderate to severe symptoms shows that daily acupuncture treatment alone or with doxylamine-pyridoxine (Diclegis, Duchesnay, Inc) 20 to 40 mg each for 14 days modestly improves symptoms of nausea and vomiting when compared with sham acupuncture ( 111820 ). Additionally, one meta-analysis of 16 small studies in patients with hyperemesis gravidarum shows that acupuncture is 8 times more likely to have a favorable effective rate when compared with control ( 107243 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by unclear clinical relevance and reporting.

Pregnancy-related pain.

Early research suggests that acupuncture is modestly beneficial for pregnancy-related pain.

A small clinical study in patients with pelvic and low back pain during pregnancy shows that acupuncture modestly improves pain and ability to perform general activities such as walking when compared with no treatment ( 88606 ). Other small studies show that acupuncture reduces pelvic pain or pelvic girdle pain during pregnancy ( 88612 , 88613 ).

Premature ejaculation.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with premature ejaculation.

A small clinical study in patients with premature ejaculation shows that receiving two acupuncture sessions weekly for 4 weeks increases time to ejaculation when compared with sham acupuncture, but not when compared with taking dapoxetine ( 101582 ).

Premature rupture of membranes (PROM).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with PROM.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with PROM shows that acupuncture does not affect labor induction when compared with no treatment, but may decrease the need for oxytocin for labor augmentation and decrease labor duration ( 88614 , 88615 , 88616 ).

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with PMS.

A small clinical trial shows that acupuncture reduces psychological and physical PMS symptoms when compared with sham acupuncture ( 100769 ).

Pre-procedural anxiety.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for reducing pre-procedural anxiety.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that auricular and body acupuncture may reduce anxiety and pain in patients with kidney stones undergoing lithotripsy procedures such as extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL) ( 29632 , 29637 ). Acupuncture has also been tried for anxiety and pain in endoscopic procedures. Some preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture improves anxiety and results in a nonsignificant trend to less pain during a colonoscopy when compared with sham acupuncture ( 29645 ). However, other preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture does not alleviate pain during colonoscopy when compared with receiving meperidine ( 29647 ). A meta-analysis of generally low-quality clinical research in adults undergoing in vitro fertilization (IVF) shows that acupuncture treatment, including one or more sessions between oocyte retrieval and pregnancy testing, may modestly reduce anxiety when compared with control, including sham acupuncture, no intervention, or medication. Although the number of studies was small, acupuncture seems to have the most benefit when performed on the day of embryo transfer ( 109814 ). Acupuncture has also been evaluated for pre-operative anxiety. Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture may reduce pre- and post-operative anxiety when compared with no treatment in patients undergoing cataract surgery; however, acupuncture is no more beneficial than sham acupuncture, which suggests a possible placebo effect ( 29688 ). Other clinical research in patients undergoing a Cesarean section shows that auricular acupuncture at three points results in a clinically significant reduction in anxiety at the time of entry into the operating room, but not immediately prior to the incision, in 43% of patients, compared with 13% of those given sham auricular acupuncture. However, this appears to be related to an increase in anxiety in the group given sham acupuncture and there was no beneficial effect of auricular acupuncture when compared with usual care alone ( 109813 ).

Pruritus.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with pruritus.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that acupuncture improves itching in patients with hemodialysis-associated uremia when compared to baseline ( 88609 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a control group.

Psoriasis.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with psoriasis.

A small clinical trial shows that electroacupuncture plus ear acupuncture for 10 weeks is no different than sham acupuncture, or "minimal" acupuncture, in improving severity of the skin lesions in patients with longstanding plaque psoriasis ( 88634 ).

Radiation-induced nausea and vomiting.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with radiation-induced nausea and vomiting (RINV).

A small clinical trial in patients with various types of cancer shows that receiving acupuncture for 30 minutes 2-3 times weekly for the duration of radiotherapy does not reduce nausea when compared with sham non-penetrating acupuncture ( 101381 ). A post-hoc analysis of this study also found that acupuncture is not associated with reduced nausea or vomiting when compared with sham acupuncture in a subgroup of patients that received chemotherapy in addition to radiotherapy ( 101588 ).

Raynaud syndrome.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with Raynaud syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture modestly improves symptoms and reduces attack frequency in patients with Raynaud syndrome when compared with no treatment ( 88622 ).

Restless legs syndrome (RLS).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with RLS.

A meta-analysis of observational studies in patients with RLS shows that acupuncture, either alone or in combination with other related treatments, is associated with a 91% total effective rate, compared with a 66% total effective rate in those treated with non-acupuncture therapies ( 107247 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies and the lack of statistical comparison for the pooled outcomes.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with RA.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture does not improve pain, tender joints, and general health in patients with RA when compared with sham acupuncture ( 88624 ). A small preliminary clinical study, in patients with RA of the hand and an additional traditional Chinese medicine diagnosis called "pivot syndrome", shows that receiving 2-3 acupuncture sessions for 4 weeks improves pain, arm strength, and hand grip strength when compared with sham acupuncture or no treatment ( 101584 ).

Rhinosinusitis.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with rhinosinusitis.

A small clinical trial shows that acupuncture does not improve symptoms of chronic sinusitis such as mucus production, stuffy nose, headache, and decreased sense of smell when compared with sham acupuncture ( 88633 ).

Schizophrenia.

Early research suggests that acupuncture is beneficial for reducing symptoms of schizophrenia.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that acupuncture, with or without conventional antipsychotic drug therapy, modestly improves some symptoms of schizophrenia and prevents relapse ( 15999 , 17220 , 91771 , 111800 ). However, poor study methodology and the small number of patients enrolled in the trials limits the reliability of these findings.

Sciatica.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with sciatica.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with sciatica shows that acupuncture alone or with moxibustion results in a 20% increase in the proportion of patients with at least a 30% decrease in pain severity when compared with analgesics such as acetaminophen or NSAIDs, as well as corticosteroids used as analgesic adjuvants. Acupuncture was most often given over 2-6 weeks ( 109832 ). In addition, a small clinical trial in patients with chronic sciatica caused by lumbar disc herniation shows that twelve sessions of acupuncture over 4 weeks has a clinically significant benefit on leg pain when compared with sham acupuncture. However, benefits were not present at 28 weeks and there was no effect on low back pain, overall disability, or quality of life ( 102642 ).

Sexual dysfunction.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in females with sexual dysfunction.

A meta-analysis of 4 small clinical trials including a total of 178 females with sexual dysfunction shows that acupuncture may improve some domains of female sexual dysfunction when compared with unspecified control groups; however, no change was seen in most domains of sexual dysfunction ( 114218 ). Poor study methodology and the small number of patients enrolled in the trials limits the reliability of these findings.

Shoulder pain.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with shoulder pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture reduces shoulder pain in spinal cord injury patients who use wheelchairs; however, there was a high placebo response and acupuncture was not more effective than sham treatment ( 15990 , 15998 ). Other preliminary clinical research suggests that acupuncture reduces shoulder pain in patients that have had an arthroscopic acromioplasty compared to sham acupuncture ( 88629 ). Finally, some preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture improves pain and function by a small amount in patients with periarthritis of the shoulder, or "frozen shoulder" ( 88631 , 88632 , 104721 ).

Sleep apnea.

Early research suggests that acupuncture is beneficial for improving symptoms of sleep apnea.

Preliminary clinical trials show that acupuncture improves symptoms of sleep apnea when compared with sham acupuncture or no treatment ( 88638 , 102638 ). Meta-analyses show that acupuncture reduces the number of apnea and hypopnea events by approximately 4-6 per hour, and improves oxygen saturation and daytime sleepiness, when compared with a control consisting of sham acupuncture, no treatment, continuous positive airway pressure, or medications ( 102638 , 111816 ). In patients with 16 or more events per hour, the number was reduced by an average of 9-10 events per hour. There was also a small benefit in daytime sleepiness in patients with at least 16 or more events per hour at baseline ( 102638 ). A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with post-stroke apnea-hypopnea also shows that acupuncture treatment modestly reduces the severity of apnea and daytime sleepiness, and improves oxygen saturation, when compared with control including no treatment, rehabilitation or biofeedback training, and/or continuous positive airway pressure. All patients were given unspecified conventional medical treatments ( 111799 ).

Stroke.

When used in combination with conventional physiotherapy or rehabilitative measures, early research shows that acupuncture might help to improve recovery from stroke. However, it is unclear if acupuncture is more effective than sham acupuncture.

There is interest in using acupuncture for functional recovery following a stroke. A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture following an ischemic stroke is associated with a modest reduction in the risk of death, dependency, and institutional care after 3 months when compared with sham acupuncture ( 15995 ). Also, although some research from individual clinical trials disagrees, most meta-analyses show that acupuncture is beneficial for various measures of stroke rehabilitation ( 16003 , 88639 , 88640 , 88641 , 88642 , 88643 , 88644 , 88645 , 88646 , 91772 )( 97569 , 102651 , 107255 , 114236 , 114252 ). A meta-analysis of 21 studies shows that receiving a specific type of abdominal acupuncture in combination with rehabilitation training, body acupuncture, or conventional acupuncture, improves overall, upper limb, and lower limb motor function scores and activities of daily living scores when compared with rehabilitation training, body acupuncture, or conventional acupuncture alone ( 107255 ). Other meta-analyses show that acupuncture is beneficial for improving motor function, including upper and lower limb function, when compared with control groups receiving sham acupuncture, rehabilitation, or usual care alone ( 114236 , 114252 ). One pooled analysis of low-quality clinical trials in patients with stroke in the convalescent phase shows that there is no difference between acupuncture and sham acupuncture for improving motor function. However, this analysis found that adding acupuncture to standard stroke treatments modestly improves the ability to perform activities of daily living, neurological and cognitive function, depression, swallowing function, and pain when compared with standard treatment alone ( 97569 ). There is also interest in using acupuncture for post-stroke symptoms, including dysphagia, sleep apnea, and pain. One meta-analysis shows that receiving acupuncture following an ischemic or hemorrhagic stroke results in a small to moderate improvement in swallowing ability in patients with dysphagia when compared with not receiving acupuncture ( 104720 ). Another meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture treatment, alone or in combination with conventional medication or traditional Chinese medicine, modestly reduces the severity and intensity of post-stroke thalamic pain when compared with conventional medication, usually carbamazepine or pregabalin ( 111780 ). An additional meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with post-stroke apnea-hypopnea shows that acupuncture treatment modestly reduces the severity of apnea and daytime sleepiness, and improves oxygen saturation, when compared with control including no treatment, rehabilitation or biofeedback training, and/or continuous positive airway pressure. All patients were given unspecified conventional medical treatments ( 111799 ). The effect of acupuncture treatment on post-stroke hiccups has also been investigated. One meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with post-stroke hiccups shows that acupuncture treatment increases the proportion of patients with a cessation of or only occasional hiccups by 27% when compared with taking metoclopramide. The severity of hiccups was also modestly reduced ( 111823 ). Poor study methodology and the small number of patients enrolled in the trials limits the reliability of these findings. There is interest in using acupuncture for hemiplegia and unilateral spatial neglect after stroke. Preliminary clinical research in patients with post-stroke hemiplegia shows that manual or electroacupuncture might improve spastic state and motor function. Most studies have used body, scalp, and/or abdominal acupuncture. When used in combination with rehabilitation training, acupuncture seems to be more effective than rehabilitation training alone ( 88551 , 88552 , 88553 , 88554 , 102639 , 109835 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture may improve joint mobility in stroke patients with hemiplegic shoulder subluxation ( 88555 ). A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture, usually to the scalp, for 4-8 weeks modestly improves cognitive and motor function and unilateral spatial neglect symptoms when compared with rehabilitation alone. However, only a limited number of studies were available for each endpoint and any long-term effects are unclear ( 109822 ). Finally, the use of acupuncture to improve post-stroke cognitive impairment and speech problems is also of interest. Meta-analyses and individual clinical studies show that receiving manual or scalp acupuncture with various conventional or traditional treatments improves cognitive function scores and modestly reduces aphasia when compared with using conventional or traditional treatments alone. Control treatments included cognitive function training, speech and language therapy, and medications ( 101367 , 101587 , 104719 , 105938 , 108596 , 109829 , 111843 , 114211 , 114246 ). Additionally, preliminary clinical research in patients hospitalized for stroke in China shows that receiving simultaneous scalp acupuncture and interactive cognitive function training 6 times weekly for 8 weeks improves cognitive function, depression, anxiety, sleep, and self-care abilities when compared with standard scalp acupuncture, with or without non-simultaneous cognitive function training ( 108596 ). The validity of this study is limited by the lack of follow-up.

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for SLE.

A meta-analysis evaluating up to 5 small clinical trials in adults with SLE shows that acupuncture treatment for 5-34 weeks, in combination with conventional medication, modestly improves overall symptoms, some markers of disease activity, and measures of kidney function when compared with conventional medication alone ( 114205 ). The validity of these findings is limited by poor study methodology and the small total number of patients.

Tendinopathy.

Early research suggests that acupuncture is beneficial for improving symptoms of tennis elbow.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with tennis elbow, a tendinopathy of the elbow, shows that manual acupuncture modestly reduces pain in the short-term when compared with sham acupuncture. However, laser acupuncture does not seem to work ( 101369 ).

Tinnitus.

Evidence on the use of acupuncture for tinnitus is conflicting.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture does not improve tinnitus loudness or intensity when compared with sham acupuncture ( 88663 , 97552 , 103433 , 103434 ). However, there is a small benefit of acupuncture for tinnitus severity when a different scale is used ( 103434 ). When compared with usual treatment alone in people with early tinnitus symptoms, the addition of acupuncture treatment may improve the subjective change in tinnitus intensity by a small amount ( 103433 ). In addition, other preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture might improve symptoms of tinnitus and quality of life when compared with no treatment, sham acupuncture, or oral traditional Chinese medicine. However, any improvements diminish over time ( 47050 , 88666 , 88667 , 102647 , 111805 ). A meta-analysis of these and other generally small clinical trials shows that acupuncture alone or in combination with medications is modestly more beneficial than medication alone for improving overall response, as well as function and mood ( 114224 ). More research is needed to determine the effects of specific acupuncture points and/or treatment duration.

Tourette syndrome.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with this condition.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture has a small, short-term benefit on symptoms of Tourette syndrome when compared with conventional medication. It is unclear if adding acupuncture to conventional medication is more beneficial than conventional medication alone due to the limited available research ( 104734 ). Well-designed clinical research is needed.

Traumatic brain injury (TBI).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with TBI.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical studies in patients with TBI shows that manual or electroacupuncture is slightly more effective than standard treatment for overall recovery and improved function related to TBI. Use of acupuncture also seems to reduce the risk of mortality by about 50% ( 102637 ). However, poor study methodology limits the reliability of these findings.

Ulcerative colitis.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with ulcerative colitis.

Meta-analyses of low- to moderate-quality preliminary clinical trials show that receiving acupuncture for 14-60 days is at least as effective as conventional medications for improving symptoms of ulcerative colitis. In addition, when taken along with conventional medicines, acupuncture may be moderately more effective than conventional medicine alone. Conventional medications were most commonly mesalazine or metronidazole with sulfasalazine ( 104691 ). The studies included in the meta-analyses were heterogeneous, resulting in no more than 3 studies used in each analysis.

Urinary incontinence.

Early research suggests that acupuncture is beneficial for improving symptoms of urinary incontinence.

A meta-analysis of mainly low-quality clinical studies in middle-aged and elderly patients with urinary incontinence shows that receiving acupuncture results in 5.5-fold higher odds of a clinical effect when compared with rehabilitation exercise or medication. However, of the seven studies included in the analysis, two used moxibustion ( 104727 ). A meta-analysis of clinical research in females shows that receiving 3 sessions of acupuncture a week for at least 6 weeks modestly reduces urinary leakage and episodes of incontinence, and improves self-evaluation, when compared with sham acupuncture. However, only 2 or 3 studies were included in these analyses ( 111812 ). An additional preliminary clinical trial in females with stress, urgency, or mixed urinary incontinence also performing pelvic floor exercise shows that weekly acupuncture treatment for 6 weeks modestly improves objective and subjective symptoms when compared with pelvic floor exercise alone. There was some evidence of benefit for up to 24 weeks ( 109821 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture may improve urge- or mixed-type incontinence in elderly patients who do not achieve sufficient efficacy with other pharmacological and nonpharmacological treatments ( 29668 ). Also, receiving acupuncture for 20 minutes twice weekly for 4 weeks reduces incontinence symptoms and improves quality of life when compared with sham acupuncture in older patients with urinary incontinence and/or overactive bladder ( 97547 ).

Urinary retention.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for urinary retention.

Meta-analyses of low-quality research in patients with urinary retention show that electroacupuncture or manual acupuncture may be beneficial for urinary retention ( 107245 , 114248 ). One meta-analysis shows that electroacupuncture or manual acupuncture improves the likelihood of spontaneous voiding and decreases residual urinary volume when compared with control ( 107245 ). Another meta-analysis shows that electroacupuncture used alone or in combination with conventional treatments such as intermittent catheterization, routine care, or bladder training, modestly reduces residual urine volume and increases bladder capacity when compared with conventional treatments alone ( 114248 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies and the risk for publication bias.

Urinary tract infections (UTIs).

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for UTI prevention or recurrence.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture reduces the risk of UTI recurrence by approximately 61% when compared with no treatment. In addition, a single study included in a meta-analysis shows that acupuncture reduces the risk of UTI recurrence by 55% when compared with sham acupuncture ( 88661 , 103432 ).

Urticaria.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with urticaria.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture may improve symptoms of resistant urticaria compared to antihistamines such as acrivastine, cinnarizine, or ranitidine used off-label for urticaria ( 88635 ).

Vascular dementia.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with vascular dementia.

One large meta-analysis of low-quality studies in patients with vascular cognitive impairment shows that acupuncture improves global cognitive function, with increases of 2 points on the Mini Mental State Examination (MMSE) and Hasegawa's Dementia Scale scores, when compared with Western medicine alone. Results were similar when acupuncture was combined with Western medicine or usual care and when compared with Western medicine or usual care alone ( 107248 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which persisted throughout subgroup analyses. Other preliminary clinical research suggests that acupuncture is not better than piracetam for improving symptoms of senile vascular dementia ( 88501 ).

Vertigo.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with vertigo.

Preliminary research shows that acupuncture improves vertigo but is less effective than moxibustion for cervical vertigo ( 88684 , 88685 ).

Vocal cord dysfunction.

It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial for vocal nodules.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in adults with vocal nodules shows, with very low to moderate certainty, that acupuncture treatment modestly improves overall symptoms when compared with control groups receiving conventional medicine or traditional Chinese medicine. The evidence was inadequate to conduct a comparison between acupuncture and vocal training ( 114212 ).

Cervical spondylosis . Low quality studies suggest that acupuncture may modestly improve pain in patients with cervical spondylosis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture modestly reduces pain in patients with cervical spondylosis ( 29693 , 29694 , 29695 , 29696 , 29697 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the lack of adequate control groups.

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . It is unclear if acupuncture is beneficial in patients with TMD.

Meta-analyses and small clinical studies show that traditional and laser acupuncture reduce pain and improve jaw function in patients with TMD when compared to control groups receiving no treatment and sham treatment ( 15992 , 29686 , 29687 , 88588 , 109819 , 114210 , 114240 ). Conversely, one small clinical study shows that acupuncture at specific points once weekly for 4 weeks is no more effective than acupuncture at non-specific points for pain or mouth-opening ability ( 102648 ). Research comparing acupuncture with active controls, including medication and physical therapy, or evaluating acupuncture in conjunction with these treatment options, is limited. One small preliminary clinical trial shows that auricular acupuncture is as effective as occlusal sprints for improving pain and feelings of depression ( 109819 ). However, poor study methodology limits the reliability of these findings. One small clinical study in patients with myofascial TMD shows that receiving a 20-minute traditional acupuncture session weekly for one month seems to reduce pain to a similar degree as receiving one-time bilateral injections with botulinum toxin type-A into the anterior and masseter muscles ( 115722 ).

More evidence is needed to rate acupuncture for these uses.

Traditional acupuncturists take a comprehensive medical history and examination similar to Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) practitioners. They may examine the tongue, the face, and the quality of the pulses. Both traditional and Western acupuncturists may palpate to identify points at which pressure causes tenderness or pain. Traditional acupuncturists can add other modalities from TCM into the acupuncture therapy session. These modalities include but are not limited to moxibustion, cupping, massage, and herbal products ( 95101 ). Many different styles of acupuncture exist: Traditional acupuncture : This form of acupuncture originated in China and is now commonly used throughout Eastern countries. Needles are inserted into over 350 key points along "meridians" in the body. Stimulating these points through a needle or pressure (e.g., acupressure) is thought to stimulate the body to correct energy flow and balance ( 15309 , 102630 ). An acupuncture session may involve up to 20 very fine needles inserted into acupoints and allowed to remain in place for different durations, usually about 10-30 minutes. The needles can be inserted to a depth of 5 to 40 mm. They can be stimulated by winding by hand ( 29602 ). Electroacupuncture : In this practice, acupuncture needles are stimulated by a weak electrical current ( 29602 ). Laser acupuncture : In this practice, lasers are used to stimulate acupuncture points. A typical schedule of acupuncture administration is 6-12 sessions over 3 months ( 95101 ). Thread embedding acupuncture : This procedure inserts medical threads, such as catgut or polydioxanone (PDO) sutures, into subcutaneous tissue or muscle at specific points, thus providing long-term chemical stimulation in addition to the mechanical stimulation provided by traditional acupuncture. Typically, PDO threads take approximately 4 weeks to reach 50% tensile strength and 6 months to be completely absorbed ( 108597 ).

Acupuncture is a primary treatment modality of traditional Chinese medicine (TCM). Acupuncture treatment involves inserting fine needles into specific parts or points on the body along pathways called "meridians." The purpose is to stimulate points that correspond to specific organs, emotions, or sensory feelings. For example, acupuncture around the ear, feet, and hands targets the pain of labor ( 15991 , 2630 ). In traditional Chinese medicine, it is thought that disease is caused by an imbalanced or blocked flow of energy or "qi." Therefore, acupuncture is thought to stimulate energy flow, unblock energy, and rebalance energy, which results in healing ( 15996 , 102630 ). Most acupuncture points are located near nervous tissue or structures. Researchers suggest that insertion of an acupuncture needle at these points may block transmission of pain signals through certain neural gates ( 15991 ). Some experts also theorize that acupuncture might result in the release of endorphins and/or opioids, which naturally reduce pain ( 15991 ). However, because some research shows acupuncture to be no more effective than sham acupuncture, it is unclear if the effectiveness is due to physiologic stimulation ( 16905 ). Some human research shows that acupuncture reduces levels of the inflammatory compounds, interleukin-6 and tumor necrosis factor-alpha ( 111816 ). For depression and other psychiatric conditions, acupuncture is thought to stimulate neurotransmitters. It is thought that acupuncture might increase synthesis and release of serotonin and norepinephrine ( 15996 ). For anxiety, acupuncture has been shown to decrease levels of adrenocorticotropic hormone. It is thought that acupuncture inhibits hyperactivity of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis ( 109827 ). For insomnia, acupuncture seems to increase levels of 6-sulfatoxymelatonin, a marker for overnight melatonin secretion ( 103419 ). For overactive bladder, acupuncture is thought to inhibit the peripheral nerves related to the lower urinary tract or affect signalling from the central nervous system to the bladder ( 109834 ). Acupuncture treatment is of interest for infertility. Although some clinical research shows that acupuncture treatment improves levels of follicle-stimulating hormone, estrogen, and anti-Müllerian hormone, and also improves oocyte retrieval and implantation, most research shows that acupuncture is unlikely to increase live birth rates ( 100780 , 108595 , 109836 , 111806 , 111826 , 114223 , 114254 ).
