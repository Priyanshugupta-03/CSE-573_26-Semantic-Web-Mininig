## Overview

Krebiozen was the commercial name for a compound that was claimed to be an effective treatment for cancer. The compound was purportedly prepared from horses that had been inoculated with the bacteria Actinomyces bovis ( 27147 ). The compound was brought to the US by Dr. Stevan Durovic in 1949 ( 27148 ).

The claims that krebiozen could treat cancer could not be verified by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) or the National Cancer Institute (NCI) ( 27148 ). In fact, these federal agencies determined that krebiozen was actually nothing more than mineral oil containing a form of creatine. Eventually, multiple practitioners were brought to trial for fraud and violations of various FDA regulations, including violations of the Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, mail fraud, mislabeling, making false statements to the government, and conspiracy related to the production and distribution of krebiozen. However, these individuals were eventually acquitted ( 27148 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of krebiozen. However, use of Krebiozen in lieu of proven medical treatments has caused serious harm and death.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information is available; avoid using.
