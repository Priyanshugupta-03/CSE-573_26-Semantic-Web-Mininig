## Overview

Healing touch is a type of biofield or energy-based therapy. It involves the practitioner's light touch, hand placement, and mental intention to balance the recipient's energy field ( 98742 ).

## History

Healing touch was established by Janet Mentgen, founder of Healing Touch International, in the early 1980s as a nursing continuing education program ( 27762 ).

## People Use This For

Healing touch is used for addictions, anxiety, arthritis, fractures, cancer-related pain, cancer-related quality of life, cardiovascular disease (CVD), carpal tunnel syndrome, childbirth, chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), depression, dystonia, epilepsy, hypertension, multiple sclerosis (MS), Parkinson disease, phantom limb pain, physical trauma or injury, osteoarthritis, postoperative recovery, promoting flow of breast milk, psychological well-being, sarcoidosis, rhinosinusitis, sleep enhancement, wound healing, spasticity, and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Likely Safe when used appropriately, short-term. Healing touch has been used with apparent safety in several studies ( 98742 , 98743 , 98746 , 98747 , 98748 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no reason to expect safety issues.

#### General

Healing touch is well tolerated when used appropriately, short term. Adverse effects attributed to healing touch have not been reported in clinical trials ( 98742 , 98743 , 98746 , 98747 , 98748 ). Although these studies suggest safety, a high quality assessment of safety has not been conducted.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Back pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic lower back pain shows that receiving eight healing touch treatments may reduce pain and improve range of motion when compared with baseline ( 27747 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Cancer-related pain.

Observational research in cancer patients has found that a single session of healing touch is associated with reduced pain when compared to baseline ( 98743 ). A retrospective analysis of this observational research in a subgroup of patients with breast cancer shows that a single 45-minute session of healing touch is associated with similar clinically meaningful improvements in pain when compared with oncology massage ( 106039 ).

Dementia.

Some preliminary clinical research in patients with Alzheimer disease or dementia shows that administering healing touch treatments 2-3 times weekly for up to 4 weeks may decrease agitation and restlessness when compared with baseline ( 27744 , 27746 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Osteoarthritis.

A small clinical study in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that receiving 20 minutes of healing touch three times weekly for 6 weeks reduces pain and improves function compared to receiving a weekly friendly conversation visit ( 98746 ). It is unclear how healing touch compares to conventional treatments for osteoarthritis such as analgesics or exercise.

Pain (chronic).

Most observational research in patients with chronic pain has found that healing touch is associated with reduced pain and anxiety when compared with baseline ( 27751 , 27752 , 98750 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group. In older adults with chronic pain living in long-term care facilities, healing touch has also been associated with reductions in chronic pain; however, there is a large variability in response between patients. It is unclear which patient-specific factors contribute to the response variability ( 98750 ).

Postoperative pain.

Results regarding the effect of healing touch postoperative pain seem to differ depending on the type of surgery. Some preliminary clinical research in patients after knee replacement surgery shows that receiving healing touch for 30 minutes once between two daily physical therapy sessions for 2 days, or until discharge, reduces pain compared to receiving physical therapy only ( 98744 ). However, research in patients recovering from coronary artery bypass surgery shows that healing touch does not reduce pain medication use compared to control ( 98748 ).

Postoperative recovery.

Preliminary clinical research in patients after coronary artery bypass surgery or hematopoietic stem cell transplant shows that giving healing touch marginally reduces the length of hospital stay compared to control treatment ( 98745 , 98748 ).

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

A small clinical study in combat veterans shows that healing touch, given for 1 hour for 10 weekly sessions, along with standard care appears to improve self-reported PTSD symptom severity scores by about 18 points from baseline, compared with reductions of about 6 points from baseline in those receiving standard care alone. Effects persisted for up to 3 months following the last session ( 106040 ).

Pre-procedural anxiety.

A small clinical study in children undergoing elective reconstructive surgery shows that healing touch, performed in a single 1-hour session on the night prior to the surgery has no effect on preoperative sleep and anxiety or postoperative pain when compared with sham healing touch or controls ( 106041 ).

Psychological well-being.

Overall, it seems that healing touch improves psychological measures in postoperative patients, but results are generally low-quality. Preliminary clinical research in patients recovering from coronary artery bypass surgery or knee replacement surgery shows that healing touch reduces anxiety compared to control ( 98744 , 98748 ). Other preliminary clinical research in patients after hematopoietic stem cell transplant or after hysterectomy shows that healing touch improves psychosomatic outcomes such as stress and emotional wellbeing compared to control interventions ( 27755 , 98745 ).

Quality of life.

Research on the effects of healing touch on quality of life and well-being in cancer patients is conflicting. Preliminary clinical research in cancer patients shows that receiving 20-45 minutes of healing touch once weekly for 4 weeks reduces mood disturbance, depression and/or fatigue compared to control treatment with usual care only, rest, or relaxation therapy ( 29505 , 98747 ). Additionally, one small study shows that receiving healing touch seems to improve pain, function, mood, and vitality when compared to no treatment in females receiving radiation therapy for gynecological or breast cancer ( 27750 ). However, other studies show no benefit. Some observational research found no benefit in outcomes such as quality of life, depressive symptoms, mood, or sleep ( 98749 ). Additionally, some preliminary clinical research in patients with end-stage cancer or colorectal cancer shows that receiving healing touch is no different than usual care for improving outcomes such as quality of life and mood ( 27749 , 98749 ). Another preliminary clinical study in breast cancer patients undergoing radiation therapy shows that receiving healing touch for 45 minutes once weekly does not reduce fatigue or improve quality of life compared to a sham treatment ( 98742 ). The conflicting results might be due to differences in patient preference and belief. One study showing no benefit from healing touch overall did find a benefit on mood in a subgroup of cancer patients with positive views about alternative therapy and the belief that mood is an important outcome ( 98749 ).

Stress.

A small clinical study in university students with self-reported stress shows that a single 20-minute session of healing touch (Chakra Connection) with soft background music reduces self-reported stress scores and blood pressure 30 minutes later, but not salivary cortisol levels, when compared with watching a healing touch video ( 113599 ). Clinical research in nurses completing shifts at acute tertiary care hospitals shows that a 4- to 7-minute session of healing touch reduces respiratory rate and subjective stress scores 1-4 hours after the intervention when compared with reading about self-care and performing deep breathing exercises for the same period of time ( 113600 ).

More evidence is needed to rate healing touch for these uses.

Healing touch involves the use of the practitioner's hands above and on the patient's body, without the physical pressure and palpation of massage. First, a light touch is used to scan for irregularities in temperature, texture, or vibration, which purportedly indicates areas with energy imbalance on which the practitioner will focus the healing touch modality ( 29505 , 98742 ). Healing touch practitioners usually begin the session by centering themselves and scanning the patient's body. After scanning the patient's energy field, the practitioner begins applying healing touch techniques ( 98742 ). Many clinical studies employ practitioners of healing touch that have completed a certificate program in energy-based therapy from Healing Touch International (HTI). HTI has a standardized curriculum involving a didactic and experiential training and a one year mentorship. After the student completes the HTI curriculum and passes the review process, they are awarded the title of certified Healing Touch practitioner (CHTP) ( 98742 , 98746 , 98748 , 98750 ).

None known.

None known.

The mechanism of action of healing touch is not known. Theoretically, healing touch helps to restore balance in the individual's energy and induce the relaxation response to allow them to better self-heal ( 29505 ).
