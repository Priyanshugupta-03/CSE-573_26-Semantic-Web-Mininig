# Fish Therapy
Other Common Names Fish Pedicure, Fish Spa, Ichthyotherapy, Ichtyothérapie, Ictioterapia
|

---

## Overview

During fish therapy, or ichthyotherapy, a specific species of fish (Garra rufa) feeds on dead skin cells ( 103107 ). Garra rufa, commonly called doctor fish, are toothless bottom dwellers. In their natural habitat, they feed on small organisms that live on rocks. Garra rufa is typically placed in pools or containers of warm water; people can immerse themselves in the pools for whole body treatment, or immerse only specific parts of the body, such as the hands or feet ( 103108 ). Fish therapy originated in Turkey, but has become a popular mode of relaxation and pedicure in parts of Asia, especially in Japan, Singapore, and Malaysia ( 103109 ). However, there have been multiple case reports of infection in patients who have received fish therapy. This is likely due to an inability to sufficiently sterilize the bath water without killing the Garra rufa. Additionally, there is concern that the transmission of pathogens may occur between patients exposed to the same fish ( 103111 ). Due to these concerns, at least 10 states in the US have banned the use of fish therapy ( 103113 ).The Garra rufa fish are native to Kanga, Turkey and the practice of fish therapy originated in Turkey's highland hot springs ( 103107 ).

## People Use This For

Fish therapy has been used for dermatitis, eczema, psoriasis, and to improve overall skin health and appearance.

Possibly Unsafe when fish therapy is administered in a non-medical setting. Although a thorough safety evaluation has not been conducted, there have been multiple case reports of infection in healthy patients receiving fish therapy in a spa or salon. The water used in fish therapy, as well as the fish themselves, can transmit pathogenic organisms ( 103108 , 103109 , 103111 ). Due to these concerns, at least 10 states in the US have banned the use of fish therapy ( 103113 ). There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of fish therapy when administered in a medical setting. No safety concerns have been reported in this setting, possibly due to the use of more stringent sanitation practices. However, the risk for infection in this setting cannot be ruled out.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

There is currently a limited amount of information available about the adverse effects of fish therapy. Most people report a tickling sensation during treatment ( 103107 ). There have also been case reports of severe skin infection after fish therapy administered in non-medical settings ( 103109 , 103110 , 103111 ). In one patient, toenail onychomadesis occurred after receiving a fish therapy pedicure ( 103108 ).

#### Dermatologic

In one case report, a young woman experienced toenail onychomadesis, or a reduction in nail plate production. She had received a fish therapy pedicure a few months prior to the start of these nail abnormalities. The authors hypothesize that the onychomadesis was caused by the direct trauma of the fish biting multiple nail units. However, this could not be confirmed ( 103108 ).

#### Other

Multiple case reports suggest that fish therapy can increase the risk for bacterial infection ( 103109 , 103110 , 103111 , 103112 ). Studies of fish therapy water have identified multiple bacteria capable of causing disease ( 103108 , 103109 ). It is currently not possible to adequately sterilize the water without killing the fish. Additionally, when performed in a spa or salon setting, pedicure tubs cannot be adequately cleaned when fish are present. There is also a concern that the fish themselves can transmit pathogens between patients exposed to the same fish ( 103111 ). Due to the high cost of the fish, spas and salons are more likely to re-use fish between clients. The risk for infection may be reduced when fish therapy is administered in a medical setting that maintains stringent sanitation practices ( 103113 ). In one case, a 64-year-old man developed superficial necrotic bullous dermatitis, found to be due to Aeromonas sobria, after undergoing a fish therapy pedicure. The patient had previously undiagnosed diabetes, likely increasing his risk for infection by this opportunistic pathogen ( 103109 ). Also, a 21-year-old male developed a mycobacteriosis infection of his left foot that developed after receiving a fish therapy pedicure in Thailand ( 103112 ). In another report, a 47-year-old male developed painful swelling of his right foot found to be due to infection with methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA). He had received a fish therapy pedicure 6 days prior to presenting to the emergency department ( 103111 ). A 39-year-old woman developed bilateral dermatitis of the feet caused by Staphylococcus aureus. She had undergone a fish therapy pedicure 3 days prior to the development of dermatitis ( 103110 ). Hand infections have also been reported, including a hardened, erythematous periungual plaque on the thumb of a 41-year-old male which developed 4 weeks after a fish manicure. Mycobacterium marinum was isolated from the lesion which resolved with 4 months of treatment with doxycycline 200mg daily orally ( 110691 ). Some experts caution about the potential for infection with bloodborne pathogens, such as HIV and hepatitis. However, there have been no reported cases of bloodborne pathogen transmission during fish therapy ( 103113 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Psoriasis.

One small, retrospective study in adults with psoriasis shows that undergoing whole body fish therapy for 2 hours each day for 3 weeks improves the Psoriasis Area Severity Index (PASI) score by 72% when compared with baseline. Patients also reported subjective satisfaction with the treatment ( 103107 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the use of retrospective chart review and the lack of a comparator group.

More evidence is needed to rate fish therapy for this use.

The Garra rufa, or doctor fish, are placed in warm pools of water, where clients can immerse their bodies for treatment. Full body therapy for skin conditions typically lasts for about 2 hours and is repeated daily for a few weeks ( 103107 ). Salons may offer treatment for the hands, feet, or the entire body. Some facilities use individual containers of warm water and fish instead of pools. Spa treatments generally last 15-30 minutes ( 103109 , 103110 ).

#### IMMUNOLOGIC DYSFUNCTION

There have been multiple case reports of skin infection after fish therapy ( 103109 , 103110 , 103111 , 103112 ). Patients with immunologic disorders or reduced immune function may be at increased risk for serious infection. Use fish therapy with caution in patients with compromised immune function.

None known.

Fish therapy uses Garra rufa, or doctor fish. These fish feed on dead skin, removing dead skin cells that are softened by warm water. Proponents of fish therapy claim that, in addition to providing smoother skin and relaxation, the fish also provide a micro-massage as they nibble, which may theoretically improve blood circulation ( 103109 ). In patients who have received therapy in Kangal, Turkey, where fish therapy originated, it is hypothesized that the natural ultraviolet (UV) radiation at this high altitude (5500 feet) might also contribute to the benefits of fish therapy. It is possible that the removal of dead tissue by the fish facilitates the penetration of UV rays into the dermis ( 103107 ).
