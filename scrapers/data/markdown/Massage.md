## Overview

Massage is a broad term encompassing a variety of approaches that involve the manual or mechanical manipulation of soft tissue to achieve health benefits. It is a holistic therapy that is thought to affect the musculoskeletal, circulatory, lymphatic, and nervous systems ( 29100 , 91817 , 104543 , 107038 ).

Likely Safe when most forms of massage are administered appropriately ( 91817 , 91829 ). Massage has been evaluated in clinical trials without severe adverse effects ( 29533 , 29534 , 29535 , 29536 , 29537 , 29546 , 29547 , 29548 , 29748 , 98478 , 98486 , 101166 , 101167 , 101170 )( 107021 , 107025 , 107045 ). Aggressive forms of massage, such as deep tissue massage, may be more likely to cause serious adverse effects. Although deep tissue massage has been used safely in clinical research ( 107032 ), it has been associated rarely with hematomas, neuropathies, and thromboembolisms ( 29098 , 29099 , 98485 , 107038 , 107039 , 107040 , 107042 ). The use of massage should not delay the time to diagnosis or treatment with more proven therapies.

CHILDREN: Likely Safe when most types of massage are administered appropriately. Many types of massage have been studied in clinical trials without evidence of severe adverse effects ( 29561 , 29713 , 29714 , 29716 , 29717 , 29719 , 29743 , 98477 , 98479 , 98484 , 101169 )( 107014 ). Some heat loss has been reported in premature infants ( 107047 ). There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of more aggressive forms of massage, including deep tissue massage. The use of massage should not delay the time to diagnosis or treatment with more proven therapies.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Likely Safe when most forms of massage are administered to enhance mood and physical well-being during pregnancy and labor ( 29709 , 29710 , 29711 , 101168 ). There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of more aggressive forms of massage, such as deep tissue massage. The use of massage should not delay the time to diagnosis or treatment with more proven therapies.

#### General

In general, massage is well tolerated, but it is important to choose a licensed massage practitioner.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Soreness.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Bone fracture, hematoma, neuropathy, swelling, and venous thromboembolism.

#### Cardiovascular

There are case reports of venous thromboembolism (VTE) thought to be caused by deep tissue massage. In one case report of a 67-year-old male, a deep muscle massage session was thought to be responsible for the development of a deep vein thrombosis in the calf, which eventually led to the development of pulmonary emboli ( 107039 ). In a second case, a 53-year-old female underwent a ten-minute vigorous leg massage that was thought to be responsible for the development of pulmonary emboli. The patient later received a diagnosis of endometrial adenocarcinoma, which may have contributed to an increased risk of VTE ( 29099 ).

#### Hematologic

There are case reports of hematomas associated with massage. In one , a 59-year-old male developed a large hematoma on the buttock caused by a 30-minute deep tissue massage involving the hand, wrist, and elbow. The area around the hematoma was protruding and swollen and resulted in numbness down the back of the thigh to the foot. The patient was prescribed bed rest with pressure on the buttocks; however, bleeding occurred the next day, requiring embolization of a small artery ( 107038 ). In another case, a 31-year-old female developed a kidney laceration and large hematoma after receiving combination treatment with acupuncture and traditional Chinese massage ( 111813 ). Researchers theorize that the acupuncture caused the initial kidney laceration which left her vulnerable to hematoma development due to massage.

#### Hepatic

There is one case report of a hepatic hematoma developing in an otherwise healthy 39-year-old female following deep tissue massage of the upper abdomen. Nausea and low-grade fevers lasted approximately 6 months ( 29098 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

While rare, cases of bone fracture, as well as discomfort, soreness, bruising, and swelling of massaged tissues have been associated with massage ( 29097 , 101164 , 101168 , 107021 , 113446 ). Approximately 10% of patients may experience minor musculoskeletal discomfort following massage ( 29097 ). In one case report, a patient developed subcutaneous and paravertebral abscesses requiring drainage and intravenous antibiotics after massage, acupuncture, and cupping ( 114260 ). However, it is unclear whether this adverse effect is due to massage, acupuncture, cupping, or the combination of complementary therapies.

#### Neurologic/CNS

There are multiple case reports of neuropathy, including radial neuropathy, spinal accessory neuropathy, and acute cervical radiculopathy, which were thought to be caused by deep tissue massage ( 98485 , 107042 , 113442 ). There is also a case of posterior interosseous nerve compression syndrome after deep tissue massage administration ( 107040 ). In another case, a 69-year-old male developed small watershed strokes after presenting with hemianopsia (i.e., loss of half the visual field), aphasia, and vertigo immediately following bilateral self-massage of his carotid arteries. The patient was successfully treated with thrombolysis and had no neurological sequalae ( 113450 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

At least four patients have reported the development of acute hearing deterioration likely associated with conventional massage ( 107047 ).

Likely Effective
Back pain.

Various forms of massage seem to reduce back pain and disability temporarily. More information is needed to determine which forms of massage may be most beneficial.

Most clinical research shows that various massage types, alone or along with other types of manual therapies, provide at least temporary relief in patients with back pain. Types of massage include Ayurvedic massage, deep tissue massage, Iranian traditional massage, tender point deep massage, therapeutic massage, stroking massage, Swedish massage, underwater massage, and others ( 29527 , 29528 , 29530 , 29531 , 29532 , 29533 , 29534 , 29535 , 29536 )( 29537 , 29538 , 29540 , 96285 , 107033 , 107037 , 107041 , 113461 ). Some types of massage may be better than others. For example, deep tissue massage seems to be more effective than therapeutic massage for reducing pain and disability associated with low back pain ( 107037 ). Massage has also been compared to and used in combination with other treatment modalities. When evaluated immediately after treatment, some types of massage appear to be less effective than spinal manipulation ( 29534 , 29536 ), but more effective than acupuncture ( 29530 ). One small clinical study in adults with chronic low back pain shows that traditional Iranian massage reduces pain, disability, and analgesic requirements when compared to baseline and similarly to physiotherapy and acupuncture ( 113461 ). Massage also seems to be beneficial when used together with napratherapy, transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS), exercise, and stretching ( 29528 , 91826 ). However, the addition of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) to treatment with almost daily deep tissue massage for 2 weeks is no more effective than deep tissue massage alone ( 107033 ). The 2017 guidelines of the American College of Physicians recommend massage and other nonpharmacological therapies as first line treatment for acute low back pain. The guidelines also recommend massage as a potential nonpharmacological therapy for achieving short-term improvement in pain and function in patients with chronic low back pain ( 29527 , 95773 ).

Cancer-related pain.

Massage seems to reduce cancer-related pain and associated symptoms. More information is needed to determine which forms of massage may be most beneficial.

Meta-analyses of clinical research show that massage therapy reduces cancer pain and anxiety when compared with no treatment or conventional care. Foot reflexology massage seems to be more effective than body massage or aromatherapy massage ( 91827 , 96286 ). Clinical research also shows that massage can improve sleep quality and mood and reduce fatigue and nausea in cancer patients ( 29502 , 29504 , 29506 , 29509 , 29510 , 29512 , 113446 , 113460 ). Furthermore, numerous clinical trials and case reports suggest that massage can decrease pain and anxiety in patients with cancer. Various types of massage therapy, and combinations of these types, have been used, including acupressure massage, aromatherapy massage, foot massage, Swedish massage, and therapeutic massage ( 29501 , 29502 , 29503 , 29505 , 29506 , 29507 , 29508 , 29510 , 29511 , 29512 )( 29513 , 29514 , 29515 , 91833 , 113446 ). The 2024 National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for adult cancer pain list massage as a potential nonpharmacologic integrative treatment option for patients to use in conjunction with pharmacologic interventions as needed. The guidelines note that integrative interventions may be particularly important in vulnerable populations, such as frail or elderly individuals who may not be able to tolerate pharmacological treatments ( 113099 ). Furthermore, the 2022 guidelines for the treatment of cancer pain from the American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) and the Society for Integrative Oncology moderately recommend massage for patients experiencing pain during palliative or hospice care based on intermediate quality evidence and moderately recommend massage for general cancer pain or musculoskeletal pain based on low quality evidence ( 108673 ).

Possibly Effective
Burns.

Massage seems to be beneficial for reducing pain and anticipatory anxiety in patients with burns.

Clinical research shows that Swedish massage on healthy skin for 20 minutes prior to burn wound care reduces pain and anticipatory anxiety when compared with usual care. Combining this Swedish massage with preferred background music appears to be more beneficial than either intervention alone ( 98481 ). Preliminary clinical research also shows that Swedish massage therapy decreases anxiety, depression, and pain during debridement in burn patients ( 29543 ). Other preliminary clinical research in patients with second-degree burns shows that receiving a single 30-minute aromatherapy massage treatment with almond oil or lavender oil on unburned skin lowers anxiety and pain after treatment when compared with standard treatment ( 95774 ).

Cancer-related fatigue.

Massage seems to be beneficial for reducing cancer-related fatigue.

A small clinical study in patients with breast cancer who had undergone surgery plus radiation and/or chemotherapy shows that once weekly Swedish massage for 6 weeks modestly improves feelings of cancer-related fatigue when compared with light touch or wait-list control ( 107044 ). A moderate-sized study in adults with cancer on palliative care shows that administering massage for 15, 30, or 60 minutes 2 or 3 times weekly for 4 weeks improves fatigue when compared with a control, with the greatest effects seen at the greater frequency and duration of massage ( 113460 ). The 2024 National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for cancer-related fatigue recommend massage as a nonpharmacologic treatment option for patients who are undergoing active cancer treatment based on high-level evidence resulting in uniform consensus that the intervention is appropriate ( 112413 ).

Constipation.

Most clinical research shows that massage, particularly abdominal massage, improves bowel function and frequency in adults and children with constipation from various causes.

A large body of preliminary clinical research, most of which has evaluated abdominal massage or infant massage as part of traditional Chinese medicine (TCM), shows that massage improves bowel function, increases bowel movement frequency, and reduces constipation severity in patients with constipation from various causes ( 29549 , 29550 , 98486 , 101162 , 104541 , 107014 , 107024 , 113445 ). A meta-analysis of mainly small clinical trials in infants shows that massage based on TCM modestly improves defecation frequency and most symptoms of constipation when compared with medications or other treatments ( 107014 ). In adults in intensive care being fed via a nasogastric tube, abdominal massage decreases the frequency of constipation by an additional 70% when compared with patients receiving standard care ( 98486 ). In patients with opioid-induced constipation, abdominal massage reduces the incidence of constipation and improves stool consistency, incomplete evacuation, and the number of defecations when compared with no abdominal massage. The severity of constipation was reduced by 23% ( 101162 ). Massage has also been evaluated for reducing constipation in older adults. One clinical study shows that administering a gentle abdominal massage for 15 minutes daily, five days a week for 8 weeks, 30 minutes after a meal has a large effect on reducing constipation when compared to no abdominal massage ( 104541 ). Another small clinical study conducted in Iran in older adults with mild to moderate constipation shows that administering abdominal massage for 15-20 minutes daily for 5 days reduces the severity of constipation when compared to baseline and similarly to acupressure ( 113451 ). Preliminary clinical research in older adults who have had a stroke also shows that abdominal massage twice daily for 10 days in addition to lifestyle recommendations improves defecation frequency when compared with lifestyle recommendations alone ( 107024 ).

Dementia.

Massage seems to be beneficial for reducing pain and anxiety in patients with dementia.

Clinical research in institutionalized elderly patients with dementia shows that a 20-minute relaxing massage on 5 days each week for 3 months improves pain, anxiety, and depression when compared with a control ( 98482 ). Also, preliminary clinical research shows that a 10-minute hand massage reduces agitation when compared with no intervention ( 29555 ). Additional early research shows that hand and lower arm massage, with or without aromatherapy, subjectively improves behavior in patients with severe dementia and disturbed behavior ( 29556 ).

Fibromyalgia.

Massage seems to be beneficial for reducing pain in patients with fibromyalgia.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that massage therapy modestly improves pain, anxiety, and depression in people with fibromyalgia ( 91828 ). Some studies included in the analysis show that massage might also reduce the use of analgesics and improve quality of life ( 29574 , 29575 ). Clinical research shows that combining connective tissue massage with exercise 2 days each week is more effective than exercise alone for improving pain, fatigue, sleep problems, and physical limitations ( 98478 ). Another clinical study shows that both myofascial release massage and Swedish massage improve symptoms of fibromyalgia ( 91829 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

Massage seems to be beneficial for improving some symptoms of MS.

A meta-analysis of small- to moderate-sized preliminary clinical trials in patients with MS shows that massage treatment modestly reduces fatigue when compared with various control groups. The types of massage most commonly used in the individual studies were slow-stroke back massage, effleurage, or a combination of types ( 107019 ). Preliminary clinical research also shows that receiving massage twice weekly for 5 weeks improves anxiety and depression scores in patients with MS ( 29583 ). Massage may also be preferable to exercise therapy. Preliminary clinical research shows that receiving 15 massage therapy sessions reduces pain, improves balance, and increases walking speed in patients with MS when compared with exercise therapy ( 91830 ).

Neonatal jaundice.

When used along with phototherapy, massage seems to be beneficial for reducing bilirubin levels in neonates with jaundice.

Clinical research in infants with neonatal jaundice shows that infant massage twice daily, in conjunction with phototherapy, reduces bilirubin levels by a small amount when compared with phototherapy alone. In addition, there was a moderate increase in the frequency of urination and the number of bowel movements in these infants after receiving massage ( 98477 , 103593 , 113444 ).

Pain (chronic).

Massage seems to have a short-term beneficial effect on chronic pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic pain shows that massage reduces pain, depression, and anxiety and improves overall mental health when compared with relaxation techniques or standard therapy ( 29546 , 29547 , 29548 , 29748 ). However, the benefits of massage appear to be short-lived, declining a few weeks after treatment ( 29546 , 29547 ). Various types of massage have been used in clinical research, including deep tissue, neuromuscular, Swedish, and pressure-point massage techniques.

Postoperative pain.

Massage seems to have a short-term beneficial effect on postoperative pain.

Most clinical research shows that postoperative massage, in conjunction with routine care, can decrease pain and anxiety during the first several days after major surgery when compared with routine care alone. Clinical research has evaluated various forms of massage, including deep tissue massage, effleurage massage, foot massage, hand massage, and Swedish massage ( 29597 , 29600 , 29701 , 91817 , 91820 , 95776 , 98483 , 101166 , 107020 ). Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical studies show that single or multiple massage treatments starting 1-5 days after surgery improves postoperative pain and anxiety ( 98483 , 104543 ). However, massage does not seem to decrease length of hospitalization or use of opioid analgesics ( 29599 , 29701 ). Most research suggests that the benefits of massage do not seem to last long-term ( 91817 , 91820 , 101166 ), but one meta-analysis suggests that the pain-reducing benefits of massage therapy endure 4-6 weeks after surgery. Subgroup analysis suggests that massage is more effective for adults than children and has superior analgesic effects for cardiac surgery and cesarian section than orthopedic surgery ( 113455 ). Furthermore, some clinical research shows that massage decreases only the unpleasantness of pain, but not the intensity of pain ( 29599 ).

Prematurity.

Massage seems to increase body weight and improve feeding tolerance in preterm infants.

Most clinical research shows that light to moderate pressure massage therapy might help to increase body weight in preterm infants with low birth weight when compared with infants receiving no massage and the same caloric intake ( 29713 , 29714 , 29716 , 29717 , 29719 , 29743 , 98479 , 98484 , 103599 ). Moderate pressure massage appears to result in greater weight gain, improved sleep, and reduced fussing when compared with light pressure massage ( 29716 , 103599 ). Moderate pressure massage also seems to reduce the time to full enteral feeding when compared with a sham procedure ( 98484 , 103597 ). In addition, abdominal massage, usually twice daily for 5-7 days, improves feeding tolerance when compared to routine care ( 103597 , 107022 ). Massage might also help decrease postnatal complications and stress in premature infants ( 29714 , 98479 ).

Stress.

Massage seems to reduce feelings of stress in various patients and circumstances.

One preliminary clinical study in acute care nurses shows that a 15-minute Swedish massage once weekly for 5 weeks reduces perceived stress when compared with no intervention ( 29702 ). Also, therapeutic back or effleurage massage appears to reduce stress and anxiety in spouses of cancer patients and in elderly residents in long-term care facilities ( 29703 , 29707 ). Finally, some research shows that 6 sessions of therapeutic massage improves perceived stress in patients undergoing surgery; however, it is not better than listening to relaxation tapes ( 29704 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Alcohol use disorder.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for reducing symptoms of alcohol withdrawal.

Preliminary clinical research shows that massage, as an adjunct to standard medical detoxification, might reduce early stage withdrawal symptoms in patients undergoing alcohol detoxification when compared with no massage ( 29517 ).

Ankylosing spondylitis.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for pain and function associated with ankylosing spondylitis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that receiving almost daily sessions of deep tissue massage for two weeks is modestly more effective than therapeutic massage for reducing pain and overall disease activity in patients with ankylosing spondylitis. There was no difference in ability to function ( 107035 ).

Aromatase inhibitor-induced arthralgia.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for joint pain associated with aromatase inhibitor treatment.

Preliminary clinical research in postmenopausal adults with breast cancer shows that a home-based massage treatment for 6 weeks is less effective for reducing aromatase inhibitor-associated knee pain than practicing yoga for 6 weeks ( 107259 ).

Asthma.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving pulmonary function and reducing anxiety in children with asthma.

A small clinical study in children with asthma suggests that parent-administered massage therapy, given 20 minutes before bedtime for 30 days, might reduce anxiety and improve pulmonary function when compared with baseline ( 29520 ). The lack of statistical comparison to the control group limits the validity of these findings.

Athletic performance.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving athletic performance.

Although some individual studies disagree, a meta-analysis of mainly small clinical studies shows that massage, as either manual sports massage, superficial massage, or deep massage, does not improve measures of strength, speed, endurance, performance, or perceived fatigue. There was a small beneficial effect on flexibility, perceived tiredness, and perceived well-being, but one very small clinical study showed that massage may worsen perceived heaviness and pain when compared with a control ( 98480 , 103594 , 113434 ).

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving symptoms of eczema in children treated with standard topical care.

A small clinical study in children with eczema suggests that parent-administered massage, 20 minutes daily, in conjunction with standard topical care, might improve redness, lichenification, excoriation, and pruritus when compared with baseline ( 29521 ). The lack of statistical comparison to the control group limits the validity of these findings.

Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving behavior in children and adolescents with ADHD.

Preliminary clinical research shows that massage therapy at least once weekly for 2-12 weeks can improve mood and behavior in children with ADHD when compared with exercise or a waitlist control ( 29522 , 29523 , 101169 ).

Autism spectrum disorder.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for autism spectrum disorder.

A small clinical study in children 3-6 years of age with autism spectrum disorder suggests that parent-administered massage, given 15 minutes prior to bedtime nightly for 30 days, might reduce stereotypy, off-task behavior, and sleep problems when compared with baseline ( 29524 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for BPH.

A small clinical study conducted in Thailand in adults with BPH shows that administering traditional Thai abdominal massage for 30 minutes twice weekly for 4 weeks is non-inferior to tamsulosin 0.4 mg daily for reducing the severity of lower urinary tract symptoms, improving uroflowmetry parameters, and reducing post-residual urinary volume ( 113468 ).

Carpal tunnel syndrome.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for carpal tunnel syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research shows that 30 minutes of massage and trigger point therapy given twice weekly for 6 weeks modestly improves symptoms and functional status in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome when compared to baseline ( 91821 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Cerebral palsy.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial in children with cerebral palsy.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that massage might reduce spasticity and improve mobility in children with spastic diplegia, a form of cerebral palsy. Adding 30 minutes of gentle whole-body massage to routine physical therapy five times weekly for 3 months reduces spasticity in the right side when compared to physical therapy alone. However, there was no benefit on the left side ( 103600 ). In addition, administering Swedish massage to the calf muscle for 14 minutes twice weekly for 5 weeks seems to improve mobility in these children ( 29544 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for the treatment or prevention of CINV; the available research is conflicting.

A small clinical study in breast cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy shows that effleurage-type massage given to the hand/lower arm or foot/lower leg for 20 minutes during five chemotherapy sessions reduces nausea when compared with a control treatment of unstructured conversation ( 29545 ). Additional preliminary clinical research shows that three 20-minute sessions of Swedish foot massage before, during, and after the first chemotherapy treatment reduces levels of nausea and vomiting when compared with control treatment in newly diagnosed cancer patients. Approximately 32% of patients had no nausea and vomiting, compared to only 15% of patients not given massage ( 103598 ). In addition, Swedish massage to the abdomen with sage essential oil for 15 minutes twice daily for three days modestly reduces nausea severity immediately after treatment when compared with routine care. However, this benefit did not last for an additional week, and there was no effect on vomiting severity. Also, there was no effect of abdominal massage without sage oil on nausea or vomiting severity at any time point ( 107023 ).

Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for reducing peripheral neuropathy associated with chemotherapy.

A network meta-analysis in patients with chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy shows that massage has the highest probability of alleviating pain, improving quality of life, and relieving neuropathological, motor, and sensory symptoms when compared with a variety of other non-pharmacological therapies including acupuncture, education, electrostimulation, endurance training, hydroelectric baths, multimodal exercise, photobiomodulation, resistance training, sensorimotor training, vitamins and gabapentin, whole body vibration, and yoga ( 113479 ). Preliminary clinical research in females with breast cancer shows that weekly Swedish massage for 12 weeks while receiving treatment with paclitaxel prevents paclitaxel-induced peripheral neuropathic pain and improves nerve conduction when compared with no massage ( 101167 ). Another very small clinical study in adults with breast or gastrointestinal cancer and lower-extremity chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy shows that administering oncology massage for 10-12.5 minutes per leg twice weekly for 6 weeks or thrice weekly for 4 weeks does not reduce pain more than a shoulder and neck massage control group. However, those administered massage thrice weekly for 4 weeks had greater reductions in pain than twice weekly for 6 weeks, independent of treatment site, suggesting that massage reduces pain associated with chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy regardless of the area massaged ( 113458 ).

Chemotherapy-related fatigue.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for preventing chemotherapy-related fatigue.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with breast cancer shows that slow-stroke back massage ten minutes before and after each chemotherapy infusion modestly reduces fatigue when compared with routine care alone ( 107030 ). Additional preliminary clinical research in patients with gastrointestinal cancer shows that foot massage modestly reduces fatigue immediately after and 24 hours after chemotherapy when compared with routine care ( 107029 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

Although there is interest in using massage for CFS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Chronic prostatitis and chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CP/CPPS).

Although there is interest in using prostatic massage for CP/CPPS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Colic.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for colic in infants.

A small clinical study in infants with colic shows that parent-administered light massage, given 2-3 times daily for 3 weeks, is similarly effective to a crib vibrator for reducing total crying time ( 29745 ).

Congestive heart failure (CHF).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial symptoms of CHF.

A small clinical study conducted in Turkey in patients with CHF shows that administering a 10-minute foot massage per foot daily for 7 days improves peripheral edema and sleep quality but does not reduce the need for sleep medication when compared with a control ( 113469 ).

Coronary heart disease (CHD).

Although there is interest in using massage for CHD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Critical illness (trauma).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for patients in critical care.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that massage might improve sleep quality and relaxation in critical care patients ( 29551 , 29552 ). However, other research suggests that massage is not more effective than aromatherapy or a period of rest for improving stress, anxiety, mood, or ability to cope in an intensive-care setting ( 29553 ). Two meta-analyses of clinical studies, many of which overlap, and a small clinical study in critically ill adults receiving enteral nutrition shows that administering abdominal massage for 15-20 minutes twice daily improves feeding intolerance as shown by reduced abdominal and gastric distention, abdominal circumference, constipation, gastric residual volume, and the incidence of ventilator-associated pneumonia when compared with a control. However, evidence is conflicting on whether abdominal massage improves vomiting and diarrhea in these patients ( 113471 , 113472 , 113478 ).

Cystic fibrosis.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for cystic fibrosis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that a parent-administered 20-minute light massage nightly for one month while positioned to allow for postural drainage might improve mood and peak air flow in children with cystic fibrosis when compared with no massage ( 29554 ).

Delirium.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for reducing the occurrence of delirium in patients in intensive care.

Although some previous preliminary clinical research shows that massage might improve the level of consciousness or delirium in patients in intensive care, other research shows that Swedish foot massage ten minutes daily for six days by a nurse or family member does not improve these outcomes ( 107017 ).

Depression.

Some small clinical studies show that massage may be beneficial for self-reported symptoms of depression. More research is needed in patients diagnosed with clinical depression.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that back massage therapy using moderate pressure improves depression, anxiety, and restlessness in hospitalized adolescents with depression and adjustment disorder ( 29561 ). Preliminary clinical research also shows that massage therapy with aromatherapy oils twice weekly for 8 weeks seems to improve symptoms of depression in community-dwelling older adults ( 98474 ). In addition, a meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in pregnant adults shows that massage, usually whole-body with moderate pressure, 1-2 times weekly for up to 16 weeks, moderately reduces anxiety and depression when compared with usual care ( 104542 ). However, preliminary clinical research in bedridden elderly patients shows that a 30-minute therapeutic or nursing-type massage given 2-3 times weekly for 3 months does not improve depression, satisfaction, or apathy when compared with usual care ( 29708 ).

Diabetes.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving glycemic control in adults and children with diabetes.

Preliminary clinical research shows that massaging insulin injection sites 15 minutes after the injection might increase free insulin levels and reduce serum glucose 30 minutes after injection in some patients; effects over a longer term were not reported ( 29093 ). Additionally, preliminary clinical reports show that parent-administered full body massage at night improves serum glucose levels in children with diabetes and reduces anxiety and depression in both children and parents ( 29094 ). Another very small clinical study in adults with type 2 diabetes shows that abdominal massage for 30 minutes every other day for a total of 3 times weekly for 8 weeks reduces glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by about 0.7% but does not improve fasting blood glucose or postprandial blood glucose when compared with a control ( 113475 ).

Diabetic neuropathy.

Although there is interest in using massage for diabetic neuropathy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Diarrhea.

Massage seems to be more beneficial than clay for the treatment of diarrhea in children. It is unclear how massage compares to other treatments.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research, including 2644 children under 5 years of age with acute diarrhea, shows that massage is more effective than oral clay treatment for improving symptoms of diarrhea. At 72 hours after initiating treatment, the number of children with a stool frequency reduction of at least 50% was 20% higher in those receiving massage than in those receiving clay, and normalization of diarrhea occurred in 37% more children receiving massage than those receiving clay ( 98475 ).

Dysmenorrhea.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for dysmenorrhea.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with dysmenorrhea who have received lifestyle advice such as exercise, stretching, and small dietary modifications shows that daily connective tissue manipulation, starting around the time of ovulation and continuing until the start of menses, reduces pain and the use of analgesics when compared with no treatment ( 101170 ).

Endotracheal intubation-associated adverse effects.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for reducing pain associated with endotracheal intubation.

Preliminary clinical research shows that Swedish foot massage once daily for 6 days by a family member or nurse moderately reduces pain when compared to no foot massage in patients in the intensive care unit (ICU) with a tracheal tube or tracheostomy. Benefits lasted for at least a week following intervention ( 104540 ). Another small clinical study conducted in China in mechanically ventilated patients in the ICU receiving enteral nutrition shows that abdominal Swedish massage for 15 minutes twice daily for 3 days reduces gastric retention by 25%, abdominal distension by 18%, aspiration by 21%, abdominal circumference, and gastric residual volume but does not improve vomiting, diarrhea, or reflexes when compared with a control ( 113478 ).

Exercise-induced muscle soreness.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for reducing muscle soreness after exercise; the available research is conflicting.

Some clinical research shows that massage reduces exercise-induced delayed-onset muscle soreness in addition to reducing lactate and creatine kinase levels ( 29564 , 29565 , 29566 , 29567 , 29569 , 29570 , 103594 ). However, other clinical research and a meta-analysis suggest that massage does not improve muscle soreness or stiffness ( 29568 , 29571 , 29572 , 29573 , 101165 , 113459 ). Any benefit seems to be small ( 103594 ). Types of massage studied in clinical research have included athletic, effleurage, foam roller, retrograde, stick, Swedish, therapeutic, and others.

Gastritis.

Although there is interest in using massage for gastritis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for reducing symptoms of GERD in infants.

Clinical research in infants with GERD shows that massage therapy for 10 minutes twice weekly for 6 weeks does not improve symptoms of GERD, but does decrease salivary cortisol levels, when compared with the control treatment of being held ( 91831 ).

HIV/AIDS.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving immune function or quality of life in adults with HIV/AIDS.

Some, but not all, preliminary clinical research shows that various types of massage therapies might enhance immune function in patients with HIV ( 29578 , 29579 , 29580 , 29581 ). Massages also seems to reduce anxiety, depression, and hyperventilation in these patients ( 29579 , 29581 , 96284 ).

Hypertension.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for hypertension.

A very small clinical study in 14 patients with hypertension suggests that light massage therapy given for 10 minutes three times weekly for 10 weeks might lower blood pressure when compared with relaxing on the same schedule ( 29582 ).

Hypertrophic scars.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for hypertrophic scars.

One preliminary clinical study shows that 10 minutes of friction massage daily for 3 months in addition to standard therapy does not help improve the vascularity, pliability, or size of hypertrophic scars in children, although it might decrease pruritus in some of these patients ( 29593 ). Also, another small clinical study in adults shows that although there were improvements in redness and thickness of postburn scar tissue immediately following massage, there was no benefit when compared with usual care alone after a 12-week treatment period ( 101164 ). However, another clinical study shows that burn rehabilitation massage given for 30 minutes 3 times weekly for 5 weeks in addition to standard therapy improves pain, itchiness, scar thickness, and elasticity when compared with standard therapy alone ( 91818 ).

Infant development.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for neonatal sleep.

A moderate-sized clinical study in neonates aged 15 to 20 days shows that administering massage for 15 minutes at bedtime for two weeks reduces sleep latency and the number of nighttime awakenings and lengthens the longest sleep period but does not change the duration of nighttime awakenings, total nighttime sleep duration, number or duration of naps, or total time asleep per day when compared with a control ( 113456 ).

Infertility.

It is unclear if bedtime massage is beneficial for infertility.

A moderate-sized clinical study conducted in China in adults undergoing frozen thawed embryo transfer after in-vitro fertilization (IVF) failure shows that pelvic floor massage with a medical massager for 15-20 minutes daily from the end of menstruation until embryo transfer on cycle day 20 improves endometrial thickness and blood flow and reduces the incidence of biochemical pregnancy but does not impact other birth outcomes such as the incidence of clinical pregnancy, ectopic pregnancy, or embryo loss in the first 12 weeks of pregnancy when compared with a control ( 113467 ).

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).

Although there is interest in using massage for IBD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for diarrhea-predominant IBS (IBS-D).

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that Tuina massage treatment is as effective as routine treatment for increasing the overall rate of symptom improvement to at least 30% in patients with IBS-D. When used in combination with routine treatment, Tuina massage is more effective than routine treatment alone ( 107045 ). However, the low quality of the studies and inconsistency of Tuina methods used limits the validity of these findings.

Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA).

It is unclear if massage improves symptoms in children with JIA.

Preliminary clinical research in children with mild-to-moderate JIA shows that parent-administered massage therapy, 15 minutes daily for 30 days, decreases pain, anxiety, and cortisol levels when compared to baseline ( 29519 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Kidney failure.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for fatigue associated with hemodialysis.

Preliminary clinical research in adults undergoing hemodialysis shows that foot massage three times weekly for 2 months with no oil, chamomile oil, or almond oil reduces fatigue when compared with no massage. Use of the oils seems to increase the benefits ( 104539 ). Additional preliminary clinical research in adults undergoing hemodialysis shows that either hand or foot massage three times weekly for 4 weeks during dialysis modestly reduces fatigue when compared with standard care alone. However, neither form of massage affected measures of kidney function ( 107015 ).

Labor pain.

Most small clinical studies suggest that massage might modestly reduce labor pain.

A meta-analysis of six low-quality clinical trials shows that massage modestly decreases pain when compared to usual care during the first stage of labor, but not during the second or third stages of labor. Any pain relief did not result in a reduction in analgesic use or the duration of labor ( 98476 ). A large clinical study conducted in China shows that massage combined with controlled breathing and visualization during labor does not reduce the number of subjects who used strong analgesics when compared with a control. Amongst patients who used strong analgesics during labor, massage did not lengthen the time to initial pain relief requests or reduce pain scores, but those in the massage group did request pain relief at a greater cervical dilation when compared with a control. Furthermore, in the massage group, there was a shift from requesting stronger to weaker analgesics and an increase in the number of subjects who requested no analgesics at all when compared with a control ( 113453 ). Some preliminary clinical research also shows that massage, administered alone or in combination with guided breathing exercises, might decrease anxiety, depression, and agitation during labor ( 29710 , 29711 , 98476 ). Also, preliminary clinical research shows that mechanical massage on the lumbosacral region modestly reduces labor pain and labor duration when compared with routine care alone. Use of warm mechanical massage is no more effective than mechanical massage ( 107018 ).

Low birth weight.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for increasing weight in low birth weight infants.

Preliminary clinical research shows that infant massage twice weekly for four weeks modestly increases weight gain in low birth weight infants when compared with routine care ( 107027 ).

Menopausal symptoms.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for menopausal symptoms.

A small clinical study conducted in Turkey in postmenopausal adults shows that administering a foot massage for 20 minutes daily for 7 days reduces fatigue and anxiety and increases sleep duration when compared with no intervention. However, given different baseline anxiety scores in the study groups, the effect of foot massage on symptoms of anxiety in these individuals is unclear ( 113448 ).

Mental alertness.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for increasing mental alertness.

Preliminary clinical research in healthy adults shows that receiving a chair massage for 15 minutes two times weekly for 5 weeks enhances computational ability and alertness when compared with a control treatment of just relaxing in a massage chair ( 29749 ).

Muscle strength.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for increasing muscle strength.

Preliminary clinical research in healthy volunteers shows that receiving 5 minutes of forearm and hand massage after maximal exercise improves grip strength on subsequent exercises when compared with 5 minutes of rest ( 29576 ).

Myofascial pain syndrome.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for myofascial pain syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research shows that massage, in combination with exercise, may reduce the number and intensity of painful trigger points in individuals with myofascial pain ( 29584 ). The effect of massage alone on myofascial pain is unclear.

Neck pain.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for neck pain.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that upper body massage is as effective as physical therapy for reducing pain and disability in patients with neck pain ( 29590 ). Clinical research in adults with neck arthritis also shows that receiving massage once weekly for 4 weeks and being taught how to administer self-massage decreases pain and increases range of motion when compared with no intervention ( 91823 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that six deep tissue massage treatments around the upper back, chest, and neck, with or without supervised strengthening and stretching, over a six-week period modestly reduces pain intensity for up to 26 weeks when compared with advice to stay active ( 107032 ). However, some research shows that massage is inferior to acupuncture for improving pain during motion in patients with neck pain ( 29588 , 29589 ).

Obesity.

Although there is interest in using massage for obesity, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Oral mucositis.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for preventing radiotherapy-induced oral mucositis.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with nasopharyngeal carcinoma undergoing radiotherapy shows that maxillofacial and oral massage reduces the incidence of severe oral mucositis to 30%, compared with 62% in those given only routine oral care and medication. There were no differences between groups in overall incidence of oral mucositis. However, the time to any or severe oral mucositis was delayed by 2 days and the mean duration of mucositis was shortened by 4-5 days ( 107021 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Low-quality clinical studies suggest that massage may modestly reduce pain and stiffness in patients with knee osteoarthritis.

A meta-analysis of low quality clinical studies along with other preliminary clinical research in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee shows that receiving Swedish, acupressure, soft tissue, or aromatherapy massage once or twice weekly for 1-8 weeks improves pain, stiffness, range of motion, functionality, and walking speed when compared with usual care ( 29518 , 91832 , 113474 ). However, receiving treatment with massage for more than 12 weeks does not seem to improve pain, stiffness, or functionality when compared with a control ( 113474 ). Due to the poor quality of the available evidence, the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) conditionally recommends against the use of massage for improving symptoms of knee and hip osteoarthritis ( 102114 ). Other research has evaluated the use of massage with oils and/or aromatherapy for knee osteoarthritis. Preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing physiotherapy shows that classical massage treatment with almond oil three times weekly modestly reduces pain when compared with aromatherapy or no other treatment ( 107026 ). Another small clinical study shows that performing self-massage with lavender oil or almond oil for 20 minutes nine times over 3 weeks reduces pain when compared with no massage ( 95600 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that conventional massage with olive oil, as well as aromatherapy massage with lavender, eucalyptus, ginger, sweet almond, and apricot kernel oils, three times weekly for 3 weeks, reduces pain and stiffness and improves function when compared with no massage. Aromatherapy massage seems to be more effective than conventional massage ( 101171 ).

Pain (acute).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for acute pain.

A large clinical study in adults undergoing intravenous cannulation prior to minor surgery shows that 15 seconds of massage at the intravenous access site reduces perceived pain intensity when compared with a control ( 113432 ). A meta-analysis of clinical studies in newborns under 1 month of age undergoing puncture shows that administering massage for 1-30 minutes prior to the puncture procedure reduces the duration of crying but does not change pain scores when compared with routine care ( 113443 ).

Parturition.

It is unclear if massage prevents perineal tears or episiotomy or reduces the duration of childbirth.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical studies along with a very large clinical study in adults undergoing childbirth shows that perineal massage during the second stage of labor increases the incidence of an intact perineum, reduces the rates of second- and third-degree perineal tears, and reduces episiotomy rates when compared with routine care. Additionally, perineal massage during either the first or second stage of labor reduces intrapartum hemorrhage when compared with routine care. However, there was no benefit of perineal massage on the rates of perineal laceration or first-degree tears ( 113454 , 113463 ). Sacral massage also seems to help some aspects of childbirth. A large clinical study in Turkey in primiparous pregnant adults giving birth without an epidural shows that sacral massage for 10 minutes starting upon contraction onset until fully dilated reduces the incidence of perineal injuries, duration of time from fully dilated to birth, and pain but does not improve the length of the first stage of labor or total delivery duration when compared with a control ( 113470 ). Perineal massage might prevent perineal tears, especially more severe tears, and episiotomy when performed prior to labor initiation. A meta-analysis and small clinical studies in pregnant adults at 34-36 weeks' gestation shows that prenatal perineal massage weekly until birth reduces the incidence of perineal tear and episiotomy but does not increase the likelihood of vaginal delivery when compared with a control ( 98487 , 103595 , 113429 , 113441 ). However, one small clinical study shows that prenatal perineal massage for 10 minutes every other day from week 36 until delivery does not reduce the incidence of perineal injury during childbirth when compared with a control ( 113452 ).

Pelvic pain.

Although there is interest in using massage for pelvic pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Physical performance.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving physical performance in older females.

Preliminary clinical research in recreationally trained older females shows that a single session of either stick massage or manual massage is beneficial for physical performance when compared with no massage. Functional capability, based on outcomes such as walking speed, standing from a chair, agility, and others, was modestly improved with the use of both massage types. Balance was only improved following the use of manual massage ( 107031 ).

Plantar heel pain.

It is unclear if calf massage is beneficial for reducing plantar heel pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with plantar heel pain syndrome shows that eight treatments of deep tissue massage to the calf over 4-6 weeks modestly improves functional status more than a self-calf stretch regimen. However, there was no difference between groups in relation to first morning pain ( 107046 ).

Postpartum complications.

Evidence from low-quality studies suggests that perineal massage modestly reduces the risk of perineal complications during delivery. Perineal massage does not seem to prevent postpartum incontinence.

Preliminary clinical research shows that administering perineal massage from 34-36 weeks' gestation reduces perineal pain and increases wound healing when compared with no massage ( 98487 , 103595 ). A meta-analysis of clinical studies in pregnant adults at 34-36 weeks' gestation shows that prenatal perineal massage reduces perineal pain at 3 months postpartum but does not change the incidence of postpartum urinary or fecal incontinence or pain at 3 days postpartum when compared with a control ( 113441 ). A small clinical study in pregnant adults shows that perineal self-massage twice weekly for 10 minutes or physiotherapist-performed perineal massage for 30 minutes weekly from 34 weeks' gestation until delivery does not reduce the incidence or severity of postpartum urinary incontinence when compared with a control ( 113429 ). Another small clinical study conducted in China in primiparous pregnant adults shows that perineal massage administered for 8-10 minutes every other day from 34 weeks' gestation until delivery does not improve pelvic floor function when compared with a control ( 113452 ).

Postpartum depression.

Evidence from low-quality studies suggests that receiving massage, or being involved in the massage of infants, reduces symptoms of postpartum depression.

Preliminary clinical research shows that receiving 10 sessions of massage therapy for 30 minutes each appears to improve anxiety in adolescent mothers with postpartum depression ( 29560 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that receiving massage therapy during labor decreases the risk for postpartum depression ( 29711 ). Some preliminary clinical research also shows that massaging their newborns or watching a massage of their newborns reduces depression and anxiety in those with postpartum depression ( 29558 , 29559 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that receiving classes about infant massage in addition to attending a support group improves mother-infant interactions and symptoms of depression in parents with postpartum depression ( 29559 ).

Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for PMDD.

Preliminary clinical research shows that massage decreases anxiety, depression, and pain in adults with PMDD immediately after each massage session. Massage also reduces menstrual distress up to 5 weeks after the last treatment. However, it does not appear to provide long-term benefit for mood or activity level ( 29591 ).

Pre-procedural anxiety.

Evidence from most preliminary clinical research shows that massage reduces preoperative and pre-procedural anxiety.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that 1-5 sessions of massage moderately reduces preoperative anxiety when compared to no or usual treatments ( 104543 ). In addition, preliminary clinical research shows that massage therapy reduces nausea and psychological symptoms, such as distress, fatigue, and anxiety, in patients scheduled to undergo bone marrow transplantation ( 29541 , 29542 ). Preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing cataract surgery shows that slow-stroke back massage for 15 minutes the morning before surgery reduces anxiety by a small amount when compared to baseline, compared with no change in patients who did not receive a massage ( 101163 ). Preliminary clinical research in patients about to undergo venipuncture shows that a 5-6 minute hand massage reduces anxiety when compared with not receiving hand massage ( 107013 ). However, other preliminary clinical research shows that massage does not reduce anxiety or blood pressure prior to cardiac catheterization procedures or intravenous cannulation ( 29594 , 29595 , 113432 ). The effect of massage on pre-procedural anxiety in children has also been evaluated. A small clinical study in children aged 9-12 years shows that hand massage for 16 minutes prior to surgery reduces state anxiety but does not change hemodynamic markers of anxiety when compared with no massage ( 113430 ). The validity of this effect is limited by a lack of blinding.

Pressure ulcers.

Although there is interest in using massage for pressure ulcers, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Preterm labor.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for prevention of preterm labor.

Preliminary clinical research shows that massage therapy 20 minutes twice weekly throughout pregnancy reduces the chance of having a preterm birth when compared with relaxation ( 29709 ).

Prostatitis.

Although there is interest in using prostate massage for prostatitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Quality of life.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving quality of life in hospitalized patients receiving palliative care or nursing home residents.

A moderate-sized observational study in hospitalized patients receiving palliative care suggests that administering therapeutic massage for 10 or 20 minutes daily for 3 consecutive days or a single 20-minute session improves quality of life, physical and psychological well-being, and distress levels when compared to baseline. It appears that consecutive days of massage show the greatest improvements in distress at one day's follow-up ( 113449 ). Preliminary clinical research shows that hand massage does not improve comfort levels or satisfaction in nursing home residents when compared with a control group ( 29592 ).

Radiation-induced fatigue.

Although there is interest in using massage for radiation-induced fatigue, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Respiratory tract infections.

Although there is interest in using massage for respiratory tract infections, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for these conditions.

Restless legs syndrome (RLS).

Although there is interest in using massage for RLS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if massage is beneficial in adults with RA.

A small clinical trial in adults with RA shows that a moderate pressure massage given for 15 minutes once weekly for 4 weeks decreases pain, increases hand grip strength, and decreases anxiety when compared with light pressure massage ( 91822 ). Another small clinical study in adults with RA shows that hand massage for 15 minutes per hand for 6 sessions over 4 weeks reduces pain and fatigue when compared with a control, but not as effectively as Reiki ( 113465 ). Another small clinical study in adults with RA shows that administering Swedish massage for 30 minutes two to three times weekly for 8 weeks decreases pain and analgesic use when compared with a control. These effects were noted both immediately after and one month after treatment completion, suggesting immediate and enduring benefits of Swedish massage ( 113464 ).

Seizures.

Although there is interest in using massage for seizures, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Shoulder pain.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving shoulder pain and range of motion.

One small clinical study in patients referred to physiotherapy for shoulder pain shows that those receiving 6 sessions of soft tissue massage around the shoulder over 2 weeks experienced greater improvements in range of motion and pain when compared with no massage ( 29587 ). Also, a clinical study in older adults with post-stroke shoulder pain shows that receiving slow-stroke back massage for 10 minutes each evening for one week reduces pain and anxiety levels when compared with a control group ( 29586 ).

Smoking cessation.

Although there is interest in using massage for smoking cessation, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Spinal cord injury.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving function in patients with a spinal cord injury.

A very small clinical study in patients with C5 through C7 spinal cord injury shows that moderate pressure massage given for 40 minutes twice weekly for 5 weeks improves anxiety, depression, muscle strength, and wrist range of motion when compared to baseline ( 29705 ). Another very small clinical study in patients with spinal cord injury shows that abdominal massage improves bowel function when compared to baseline ( 29706 ). The validity of the findings from these studies is limited by the lack of comparator groups.

Sprains.

Although there is interest in using massage for sprains, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Stroke.

It is unclear if massage is beneficial for improving motor function following a stroke.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that Tuina massage plus conventional physiotherapy improves upper limb motor function and spasticity, as well as lower limb spasticity, to a greater extent than conventional physiotherapy alone ( 107025 ). Another meta-analysis of clinical studies conducted in Asia in patients post-stroke shows that meridian massage improves motor function, lower limb function, functional independence, balance, and limb spasticity but does not improve upper limb function when compared with conventional rehabilitation. Subgroup analysis suggests that short-term meridian massage for less than 8 weeks is more effective when compared with conventional rehabilitation, but not when treatment duration exceeds 8 weeks ( 113477 ). More research is needed to determine whether there are benefits associated with other types of massage.

Tendinopathy.

Although there is interest in using massage for tendinopathy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of massage for this condition.

Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP).

It is unclear if abdominal massage is beneficial for the preventions of VAP.

A meta-analysis of critically ill adults receiving enteral nutrition shows that administering abdominal massage for 15-20 minutes twice daily reduces the incidence of VAP when compared with a control ( 113472 ). Additionally, a small clinical study conducted in Turkey in adults in the intensive care unit (ICU) on mechanical ventilation and enteral nutrition shows that 3% of patients given abdominal massage for 15 minutes twice daily for 3 days developed VAP compared with 31% of patients given standard treatment. Research also shows that abdominal massage reduces adverse effects of enteral feeding which are a risk factor for developing VAP ( 113447 , 113471 , 113472 ).

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . It is unclear if massage is beneficial for TMD.

Preliminary clinical research in females with TMD shows that receiving a 30-minute face massage three times weekly for 4 weeks is slightly less effective for improving range of motion of the jaw when compared with wearing an occlusal splint ( 91825 ).

More evidence is needed to rate massage for these uses.

The following assessment techniques are often employed prior to beginning a massage therapy session: - A patient interview to determine medical history and any injuries. - Observation of posture and movement (e.g., walking) to assess patterns of musculoskeletal function or tension. - Range of motion techniques to determine musculoskeletal flexibility and restrictions. - Palpation to determine areas of tension or pain. The following therapeutic techniques may be utilized during massage therapy sessions: - Abdominal massage : Conducted around the intestinal region. In infants the massage can be conducted in an 'I LOVE YOU' (heart) shape ( 107022 ). - Anchor and stretch : Involves anchoring at an area that is tight and then stretching away from that spot ( 107035 , 107037 ). - Articulation : Repetitive passive movements of muscles attached to joints. - Connective tissue manipulation : A type of massage that targets the superficial connective tissue in order to restore autonomic balance and improve organ function ( 101170 ). - Cross-fiber strokes : Involves rolling the fingers back and forth over a tendon or muscle in the direction perpendicular to the fiber ( 107035 , 107037 ). - Deep tissue massage : Focuses on deep tissues, especially the muscle, fascia, and connective tissue ( 107020 ) using a combination of lengthening strokes, cross-fiber strokes, anchor and stretch, and freeing muscle from entrapment ( 107035 , 107037 ). - Effleurage : Long stroking or gliding with a smooth continuous motion ( 104543 , 107037 ), which may be toward or away from the heart if superficial, but always done in the direction of the heart if deep. - Freeing muscle from entrapment : Involves using all fingers to apply force along the muscle border to slowly mobilize it to the other side ( 107035 , 107037 ). - Friction : Rapid rubbing movements of the hand back and forth that may be deep or superficial, or penetrating pressure applied through the fingertips ( 107037 ). - Infant massage : Moderate pressure stroking with an oil, with or without passive kinesthetic stimulation to the extremities ( 98479 ). - Inhibition/ischemic compression : Applying pressure for prolonged periods of time. - Jostling/shaking : Jostling or shaking muscles, believed to increase synovial activity and circulation. - Lengthening strokes : Involves extending a joint while working the muscle in the direction of the lengthening ( 107035 , 107037 ). - Maxillofacial massage : Includes the cheeks, upper and lower jaw, and lips, as well as the tongue, palate, and gums ( 107021 ). - Perineal massage : Insertion of the lubricated thumb and index finger 3-5 cm into the vagina, by the patient or another person, then sweeping the fingers downwards and sideways to produce a burning, tingling, or stinging feeling and holding in place until numb ( 98487 ). - Petrissage : Compression or kneading motion with lifting, wringing, or squeezing of soft tissue ( 104543 , 107037 ) administered in a circular pattern with the fingers and thumbs, believed to enhance circulation and venous flow, as well as stimulate muscle tissue. - Pincement : Thumb and fingers are used to lightly pick up, or pinch, tissue ( 103594 ). - Positional release methods : A technique "moving away from restriction barriers, allowing spontaneous normalization to occur." - Rhythmic traction : Repetitive attempts to stretch the inter- and periarticular structures. - Springing : Repetitive pressure of a gradual nature. - Stretching : Attempting to separate muscular fibers and stretch ligaments, fascia, and membranes by short and long movements. - Swedish gymnastics : Designed to stretch muscles and ligaments, for the purpose of increasing circulation and waste elimination. - Tapotement : Rhythmic, percussive, pounding movements such as lightly striking, slapping, beating, and/or tapping vigorously to stimulate deeper muscles ( 103594 , 104543 , 107037 ). This is the most common technique used for athletes before competition. - Vibration : A shaking technique for relaxation done by the therapist's hands or an electric vibrator. - Wringing : Superficial tissue is twisted in opposite directions ( 103594 ). Note : Many other variations and styles of massage or touch exist, practiced and developed in various regions across the world. Most massage approaches involve the patient lying face down on a platform or table with a sheet covering the lower body. Depending on the technique, sessions may last from 15 to 90 minutes. Environment is considered integral to massage therapy and often consists of a comfortable, quiet location. Soothing, repetitive, low-volume music or sounds may be played in the background. Massage therapy practices may be based in a therapist's home, a private practice office, a hospital, spa, athletic club, hair salon, hotel, airport, or out-of-doors. Some practitioners will travel to a patient's home or office. Sports massage may be administered in a gym or locker-room setting. In the United States, licensure requirements for administering massage therapies vary from state to state. Some practitioners are licensed as nurses, physical therapists, massage therapists, or other types of health care professionals. Many have attended extensive training programs that grant professional degrees. However, a number of massage practitioners are unlicensed. Internationally, though standards are not universally agreed upon, a number of licensing bodies grant qualifications in nations throughout the globe. Swedish massage or classical massage is the most commonly practiced form of massage and is the core of most professional training programs ( 107019 ). Practitioner licensing and certification is generally based on training in this approach. Other types of massage, such as sports massage, Esalen massage, and neuromuscular massage, are adaptations of the core Swedish massage methodology. Another variation, aromatherapy massage, involves the application of essential oils during massage ( 91835 , 98474 ).

#### BROKEN, DAMAGED SKIN

Massage may worsen broken or damaged skin; avoid use on those with skin diseases, rashes, or open wounds.

#### CLOTTING DISORDERS

Theoretically, deep tissue massage may increase the risk of venous thromboembolism ( 29099 , 107039 ); avoid use on patients at increased risk for blood clots.

#### OSTEOCHONDROMA

Use cautiously in patients with an osteochondroma. There is a case report of a pseudoaneurysm likely related to massage conducted by a practitioner of traditional Chinese medicine in a 16-year-old male with a pre-existing osteochondroma ( 107043 ).

#### LYMPHATIC MAPPING AND BIOPSY IN BREAST CANCER

A targeted 5-minute massage post-injection may enhance the sensitivity and diagnostic ability of lymphatic mapping and sentinel lymph node biopsy procedures in breast cancer patients. Preliminary clinical research suggests that massage aids in the spread of injected isosulfan blue dye through the tissues being examined ( 29562 ).

Although numerous theories as to massage's mechanism of action have been proposed, there is, as of yet, limited research on the topic. It has been suggested that massage may have local effects on muscles and soft tissues, reduce inflammation, stimulate oxygenation of tissues, soften or stretch scar tissue, break up adhesions, reduce buildup of lactic acid in muscles, induce muscle fiber relaxation, and stimulate healing of connective tissues or damaged muscles ( 98480 ). Other proposed effects include immune system enhancement ( 29100 , 29509 ), reduction of blood pressure ( 29505 , 29582 , 29746 , 107012 , 107013 , 107036 ), central nervous system relaxation and sedation ( 98476 ), parasympathetic stimulation ( 98478 , 98479 ), blockage of sensations from nerves that sense pain (the "gate theory") ( 98476 ), stimulation of blood and lymphatic circulation ( 98476 , 107045 ), decreases in heart rate ( 29505 , 29746 , 98476 , 107012 , 107036 ), increases in skin temperature ( 29595 , 98476 ), endorphin release ( 98476 ), alteration of hormones such as cortisol, oxytocin, gastrin, cholecystokinin, and insulin ( 98476 , 98479 ), stimulation of substance P release, increased serotonin levels ( 98474 ), stimulation of somatostatin release, sleep enhancement, or removal of blood toxins. Practitioners suggest that Swedish massage may assist the body in delivering nutrients and removing waste products from various tissues. Despite these proposed theories, clinical research in patients in intensive care found that blood pressure and heart rate were not reduced after Swedish leg massage by a nurse or family member ( 107012 ). Also, in patients with muscle strain, deep tissue massage had only a small effect on diastolic blood pressure, with no significant effect on systolic blood pressure ( 107036 ). In patients undergoing radiation therapy, maxillofacial and oral massage increased levels of anti-inflammatory lipids, but had no effect on inflammatory mediators ( 107021 ). Proponents have stated that massage therapy is said to transform nervous energy into a more steady state and that rhythm is important to establishing balance. Furthermore, the nervous system is thought to benefit from repetition and tempo. Rhythms are felt to have a meditative quality that is refreshing both to the therapist and patient. Abdominal massage is thought to increase peristalsis, improving gastrointestinal function ( 98486 ). Perineal massage is thought to stretch out the vaginal tissues, promoting relaxation and blood circulation ( 98487 ). Some of the proposed tenets of massage follow: Circulation of blood: The most basic tenet of traditional massage therapy is the belief that improved blood circulation is beneficial for most health conditions. Tension in the muscles and other soft tissues can impair circulation, resulting in deficient supply of nutrients and inadequate removal of wastes or toxins from the tissues of the body. This can lead to illness, structural and functional problems, or slower healing. Energy: Massage is believed to influence the flow of energy through the body as a means to promote healing. Massage is sometimes used in concert with energy-based modalities such as acupuncture, reiki, therapeutic touch, healing touch, and polarity therapy. Enhancement of all bodily systems: Traditional massage theory teaches that all bodily systems are affected by better circulation and more harmonious functioning of the soft tissue and musculature. Internal organ systems as well as the nervous system, the immune system, and other systems can benefit. Mind/body integration: In traditional massage practice, the mind and body are seen as having a reciprocal relationship. Soma (body) affects psyche (mind) and vice versa. Hence, there can be somatopsychic effects, in which the conditions of the body affect the mind and emotions, and there can be psychosomatic effects, in which psychological or emotional conditions affect the body. A habit or fixed pattern in one may impede change in the other and require special attention. Psychotherapy and massage are believed to complement each other. Movement of lymphatic fluid: Further teachings of traditional massage note that the lymph system is almost as extensive as that of the blood. The circulation of lymphatic fluid plays a key role in ridding the body of wastes, toxins, and pathogens. The lymph system is believed to benefit from specialized massage, particularly in conditions where lymphatic flow is impaired by injury or surgery (e.g., in postmastectomy women). Reduction of stress: The stress response is thought to affect health status, and massage therapy is thought to reduce stress and promote relaxation. Release of tension: Proponents of massage have further suggested that chronic muscular tension, as a result of high-stress lifestyles, trauma, or injury can accumulate and impair the body's structure and function as well as affect psychological well-being. Release of tension through massage is believed to have important physiological and psychological benefits. Release of toxins: Another tenet of traditional massage is that massage helps remove toxins that have been accumulated in the body. Structure and function are interdependent: Massage teachings suggest that the musculoskeletal structure and function affect the other. Massage is thought to help restore healthy structure and function, allowing for greater ease and wider range of movement, increased flexibility, and the release of chronic patterns of tension.
