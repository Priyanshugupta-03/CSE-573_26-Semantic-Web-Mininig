# Trigger Point Therapy
Other Common Names Deep Dry Needling, Dry Needling, Myofascial Trigger Point Therapy, Neuromuscular Therapy, NMT, Positional Release Therapy, PRT, Terapia de puntos gatillo, Thérapie des points de déclenchement, TPT, Trigger Point Injection, Trigger Point Management

## Overview

Trigger point therapy involves the stimulation of myofascial trigger points in muscles to relieve pain and dysfunction. Trigger points are hypersensitive points in taut bands of skeletal muscle that are painful with mechanical stimulation, elicit referred pain, and generate motor dysfunction ( 112965 ). They can be caused by injuries, poor posture, and overexertion ( 28494 ). Most trigger point therapy uses the insertion of dry needles at trigger points. However, compounds might sometimes be injected ( 97830 , 97835 ). Various other therapies target trigger points. Acupuncture, chiropractic, and massage, are described in detail in their respective monographs. This monograph covers only trigger point dry needling or injection techniques.

## History

Janet Travell, MD, (1901-1997) is credited with the concept of myofascial trigger points. Between 1942 and 1990, Travell published more than 40 papers on myofascial trigger points. Janet Travell, MD, and David Simons, MD, published Myofascial Pain and Dysfunction: The Trigger Point Manual. The 1999 edition is considered the definitive study of myofascial pain and dysfunction ( 28494 , 97840 ).

## People Use This For

Trigger point therapy has been used for back pain, brachial plexus entrapment, bursitis, carpal tunnel syndrome, pain (chronic), dental conditions, fibromyalgia, joint pain, joint immobility, herniated disk, migraine headache, muscle cramps, neck pain, osteoarthritis, pain following arthroplasty, plantar heel pain, postural stability, repetitive strain injuries, sciatica, scoliosis, shoulder pain, skeletal problems, sports injuries, sprains and strains, stress, stroke, temporomandibular disorders (TMD), and tension headache. It has been also used for myofascial pain syndrome, kidney stones (nephrolithiasis), abdominal pain, relaxation, and to improve muscle strength.

Possibly Safe when used appropriately. Trigger point therapy has been used in clinical trials without reports of significant adverse effects ( 28479 , 28480 , 28481 , 28482 , 28483 , 28484 , 28486 , 28487 , 28488 , 28490 )( 28491 , 28494 , 88590 , 88591 , 97828 , 97831 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no reason to expect safety issues if used appropriately.

#### General

Trigger point therapy seems to be generally well tolerated. The most common adverse effect from trigger point injection is post-injection soreness that generally lasts three to four days ( 28494 ). Rarely, incorrect insertion of the needle into the intercostal or intrathecal space has resulted in pneumothorax and pneumocephalus ( 28803 , 28804 , 103558 ). Injection of pharmacologic agents into the trigger point can cause drug-related adverse effects. Trigger point injections are capable of producing systemic pharmacologic effects, and should be used with caution.

#### Gastrointestinal

In women with pelvic pain, trigger point therapy with botulinum toxin or saline was reported to cause constipation and, rarely, fecal incontinence. However, as constipation and fecal incontinence are not unusual in women with pelvic pain, the relationship to trigger point therapy is unclear ( 103556 ).

#### Genitourinary

In women with pelvic pain, trigger point therapy with botulinum toxin or saline was reported to cause urinary incontinence and, rarely, urinary retention and urinary tract infection. However, as urinary incontinence, urinary retention, and urinary tract infections are not unusual in women with pelvic pain, the relationship to trigger point therapy is unclear ( 103556 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

The most common adverse effect from trigger point injection is post-injection soreness that generally lasts three to four days ( 28494 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

A case report describes a 37-year-old male who developed quadriplegia and severe hypokalemia within 12 hours following trigger point injections of bupivacaine and methylprednisolone in the trapezius muscles for neck pain associated with myofascial pain syndrome. He recovered with potassium repletion ( 112970 ). Another case report describes a 24-year-old female who received 8 trigger point injections of lidocaine into the right trapezius muscle for neck and arm pain associated with myofascial pain syndrome, and developed a right-sided ischemic stroke with blurred vision and loss of left arm strength. She made a full recovery over 3 months with aspirin therapy. Ultrasound guidance was not used for the injections and it was hypothesized that an injection made too deeply caused vertebral artery damage ( 112971 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

Incorrect placement of the needle during trigger point therapy has been reported to cause pneumothorax or hemopneumothorax ( 28803 , 103558 ). This serious complication can be avoided by refraining from insertion of the needle into an intercostal space. Accidental intrathecal injection has also been reported during an attempt to give a local injection in a superficial trapezius trigger point. In a case of a hemopneumothorax, the hematoma was aspirated, and the bleeding stopped. Video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery was used to treat the pneumothorax and partially collapsed lung ( 103558 ). In another case, a patient immediately experienced respiratory depression and hemiplegia and required emergency tracheal intubation for ventilatory support. Computed tomography revealed pneumocephalus ( 28804 ).

Possibly Effective
Back pain.

A meta-analysis of the available clinical research in adults with low back pain shows that trigger point dry needling reduces pain and improves functionality by a moderate to large amount when compared with other treatments such as sham needling, other types of needling, and laser therapy. Improvements are maintained for up to 9 weeks ( 97854 ). Some clinical research shows that giving neuroreflexotherapeutic therapy, or inserting devices into trigger points, results in immediate and long term pain relief when compared to sham treatment in patients with chronic low back pain ( 28479 ). Another clinical study shows that trigger point therapy with injections of lidocaine (with or without a steroid), acupuncture, and acupressure, all produce similar improvements in low back pain ( 28480 ). One clinical study shows that both trigger point dry needling and acupuncture administered for 12 weeks reduce pain in elderly patients with low back pain ( 28481 ). A single session of dry needling at a latent myofascial trigger point in the gluteus medius muscle of patients with non-specific low back pain is less effective than ischemic compression for reducing pain intensity and increasing the pressure pain threshold immediately after the procedure, but more effective between 48 hours and 1 week after the procedure ( 112958 ).

Myofascial pain syndrome.

Most research on the use of trigger point dry needling shows that it can reduce myofascial pain by moderate to large amounts when compared with sham needling ( 97835 , 97852 ). Some research also shows that adding dry needling to active or passive stretching improves myofascial pain more than stretching alone ( 88590 , 97833 ). Some preliminary clinical research on the use of trigger point injections shows that the injection of a local anesthetic reduces myofascial pain to a greater extent than saline or botulinum toxin A ( 28491 , 28486 , 28487 , 28488 ).

A meta-analysis of the available clinical research involving 123 patients with upper quadrant myofascial pain shows that trigger point dry needling reduces pain by a moderate to large amount when compared with sham needling. This improvement lasts for the duration of follow-up, or 4 weeks ( 97852 ). One small clinical study in adults with myofascial pain shows that 6 sessions of trigger point dry needling administered over 4 weeks increases pain reduction by about 3.9-fold more than sham dry needling ( 97835 ). When combined with passive stretching, trigger point dry needling 1-3 times over 2 weeks reduces pain about three times more than passive stretching alone in patients with chronic neck pain due to myofascial pain syndrome ( 97833 ). Other research also shows that combining trigger point dry needling with active stretching for 3 weeks improves pain when compared with stretching alone in patients with various types of musculoskeletal pain ( 88590 ). Some research shows that trigger point dry needling is as effective as physiotherapy for myofascial pain ( 97839 ). However, not all research has shown benefit. One clinical study shows that laser therapy for 4 weeks is more effective than trigger point dry needling in patients with myofascial pain syndrome ( 28490 ), and another study found no benefit of dry needling over sham needling into trigger points in patients with myofascial pain in the jaw ( 88591 ). In patients with myofascial pain syndrome and postherpetic neuralgia, dry needling once weekly for 4 weeks is associated with greater pain relief and lower recurrence rates 3 months after treatment than pharmacotherapy ( 112961 ). Some research has examined the use of trigger point injections (wet needling) for myofascial pain. A retrospective observational study of 100 patients with chronic abdominal myofascial pain syndrome, treated with 2 or more trigger point injections of lidocaine every 2-4 weeks, showed that 77% of patients were moderate to good responders ( 112969 ). One small study in adults with myofascial pain syndrome shows that trigger point injection with lidocaine decreases pain scores more than dry needling or injection with botulinum A toxin ( 28491 ). A study in patients with cervicothoracic myofascial pain shows that injection of botulinum A toxin into trigger points is no different than injecting saline ( 28486 ). When used in combination with physical therapy starting four weeks after the injection, a study in women with pelvic myofascial pain also shows that injection of botulinum A toxin into trigger points is generally no more effective than saline for muscle pain ( 103556 ). One clinical study shows that injecting an anesthetic such as lidocaine, mepivacaine, bupivacaine, or etidocaine into trigger points is rated as subjectively superior to saline in patients with myofascial pain ( 28487 , 28488 ). However, other research shows that anesthetic injection into trigger points is no better than sham injection in patients with myofascial pain in the jaw ( 88591 ). Injection of lidocaine into trigger points on the upper trapezius muscle is more effective immediately after the procedure than interfascial injection of saline, but is no better 2 and 4 weeks after the procedure ( 113391 ). Trigger point injections with bupivacaine 0.25%, up to 5 mL per site and 2 mg/kg total, in patients with back or neck pain associated with myofascial pain syndrome seen in the emergency department, reduces pain intensity and the need for rescue medications more than rescue medications alone within 15-30 minutes of the procedure, but not by the time of discharge ( 112959 ). Trigger point injections of 1 mL lidocaine 2% at up to 3 sites in the upper trapezius muscle of patients with neck pain due to myofascial pain syndrome reduces pain and disability 1 month after the procedure similarly to injections of granisetron 1 mg, but to a greater extent after 3 months ( 112968 ).

Tension headache.

Some clinical research suggests that trigger point dry needling improves symptoms of tension headache. Clinical research shows that six sessions of dry needling in head and neck muscle trigger points over a 2-week period reduces headache intensity by a large amount when compared with sham therapy, with benefits lasting for up to one month after treatment ( 103552 ). In addition, preliminary clinical research shows that trigger point dry needling three times over one week reduces headache frequency and intensity, increases the pain threshold at trigger points, and improves neck extension when compared with baseline. However, dry needling does not seem to be more beneficial than friction massage ( 103552 ). An additional preliminary clinical study shows that dry needling improves neck tenderness and range of motion when compared with sham needling; although both groups experienced a similar reduction in headache frequency ( 28478 ). However, a meta-analysis of 9 clinical studies suggests that dry needling does not improve tension headache pain intensity in the short-term, but might improve short-term disability, headache frequency, trigger point tenderness, and cervical range of motion, although the evidence is of very low quality, and some outcomes are based on only 1 or 2 studies ( 112967 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Abdominal pain.

A retrospective evaluation of patients with abdominal wall pain suggests that trigger point injections are associated with pain relief in 77% of patients, and no relief in 23% of patients ( 28483 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the retrospective design and the lack of a comparator group.

Fibromyalgia.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with fibromyalgia shows that four weekly sessions of trigger point dry needling improves myofascial pain when compared with cross-tape therapy. However, dry needling and cross-tape therapy resulted in similar improvements in spinal mobility ( 97829 ).

Joint pain.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with greater trochanteric pain syndrome shows that dry needling into trigger points over a 6-week period is as effective as a cortisone shot for reducing pain ( 97828 ).

Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis).

Preliminary clinical research in adults with kidney stones shows that a single trigger point injection of lidocaine improves pain when compared to intravenous injection of butylscopolamine and sulpyrine ( 28482 ).

Migraine headache.

In patients with migraine headache related to a trigger point within the sternocleidomastoid muscle, a small clinical trial shows that receiving three sessions of dry needling over one week at those trigger points reduces the number of days with a headache by 2.1 days and headache duration by about 45% when compared with placebo dry needling. In addition, average headache intensity was reduced from moderate-high to low-moderate and range of motion was improved, with benefits lasting for up to one month ( 103559 ). Trigger point therapy has also been compared with conventional treatments. Some preliminary clinical research shows that trigger point dry needling for 17 weeks is as effective as metoprolol 100 mg daily prophylaxis for reducing the frequency of migraine attacks in patients with a history of migraines ( 28477 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that a specific type of trigger point therapy, called positional release therapy, given five times over 2 weeks to patients receiving routine medical therapy for migraine headaches provides more relief than routine therapy alone. Headache frequency, intensity, and duration were reduced by 18%, 29%, and 50%, respectively, over routine therapy alone ( 97837 ).

Muscle strength.

A small clinical study shows that receiving dry needling into trigger points in combination with water pressure massage once weekly for 4 weeks increases quadricep strength and hip flexibility when compared with baseline or no treatment in elite soccer players. However, the effects were small and no different than treatment with a placebo laser and water massage treatment, suggesting that any benefits might be related to a placebo effect ( 97832 ).

Neck pain.

In patients with non-specific neck pain, preliminary clinical research shows that dry needling at trigger points on the upper trapezius muscle once weekly for 3 weeks reduces pain and improves disability by moderate amounts when compared with baseline. Effects are similar to those following treatment with extracorporeal shock wave therapy ( 103555 ). Preliminary clinical research also shows that trigger point dry needling of the neck muscles in 10 treatments over 6 weeks is no more effective for improving neck pain than sham dry needling in patients with chronic whiplash-associated disorders who are also participating in an exercise program. Those receiving dry needling treatment did have a higher rate of pain reduction maintenance at 6 weeks when compared to exercise alone ( 97838 ). Preliminary clinical research in patients with acute or chronic mechanical neck pain and receiving physical therapy, shows that trigger point dry needling in various muscles of the cervical and thoracic spine for 7 sessions over 4 weeks is no more effective than sham needling for reducing pain and disability at any time of follow-up, up to one year ( 112960 ). A meta-analysis of 6 studies comparing dry needling and lidocaine wet needling of cervical muscles in patients with musculoskeletal neck pain associated with myofascial pain syndrome, shows a greater reduction in pain intensity after 2-4 weeks of follow-up with wet needling than with dry needling, but no difference in pain-related disability, pressure pain thresholds, or cervical lateral flexion between the groups ( 112966 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Preliminary clinical research in older adults with knee osteoarthritis shows that receiving trigger point dry needling while participating in an exercise program over a 12-week period does not improve pain or functionality when compared with exercise alone ( 97827 ). A meta-analysis of low-quality studies using myofascial trigger point dry needling, periosteal stimulation, or intramuscular electrical stimulation for knee osteoarthritis reports conflicting results ( 113392 ).

Plantar heel pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that trigger point dry needling improves plantar heel pain by 12% to 25% more than sham dry needling when used weekly for 6 weeks ( 97834 ). A meta-analysis of 6 studies shows that at least 3 sessions of trigger point dry needling reduces pain intensity in the short-term, and pain and related disability in the long-term, when compared with various control groups. However, it is unclear if this is clinically significant given the heterogeneity of the methods and results, and low to moderate quality of the evidence ( 112964 ).

Postoperative pain.

Preliminary observational research in females with chest wall pain after mastectomy shows that trigger point injection of a 1:1 mixture of bupivacaine 0.5% and dexamethasone 4 mg/mL produces significant or complete relief of pain for at least 3 months in about 91% of patients after 1 or 2 injections ( 112963 ).

Shoulder pain.

A large preliminary clinical trial shows that receiving trigger point dry needling three times during personalized physical therapy visits over 5 weeks does not improve pain or function when compared with physical therapy alone in patients with shoulder pain due to rotator cuff tendinopathy or subacromial impingement syndrome ( 97830 ). Other preliminary research in patients with subacromial pain syndrome shows that adding trigger point dry needling to an exercise program over 5 weeks does not improve pain when compared with exercise alone ( 97831 ). A small, uncontrolled clinical study in patients with adhesive capsulitis of the shoulder joints shows that receiving trigger point dry needling of the shoulder muscles, 6 sessions over 12 days in addition to daily physical therapy, reduces pain intensity and shoulder disability, and increases range of motion and pressure pain threshold when compared with baseline ( 112962 ). A meta-analysis of 6 clinical trials of trigger point dry needling for non-traumatic musculoskeletal shoulder pain shows that short-term reductions in pain intensity are not clinically significant, but there might be some improvement in related disability. However, the evidence is low-quality, with heterogeneous methods and results ( 112965 ).

Stroke.

One small clinical study in adults receiving post-stroke rehabilitation shows that deep trigger point dry needling of the shoulder muscles once weekly for 3 weeks improves shoulder range of motion by a small to moderate amount, but not muscle spasticity, when compared with no dry needling ( 97836 ). Another small study in adults receiving post-stroke rehabilitation shows that dry needling at a myofascial trigger point in the hand once weekly for 4 weeks reduces spasticity and improves resting flexion angles of the wrist, thumb, and fingers immediately after the injections, when compared with sham dry needling, but the difference is not significant 4 weeks after needling ( 113393 ).

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research shows that short-term trigger point dry needling is no better than sham therapy for pain, although it may modestly increase the pressure pain threshold. Short-term dry needling also has a modest effect on pain intensity when compared with other therapies, such as analgesics or lidocaine injection. However, there was no effect on the pressure pain threshold ( 103554 ). When added to exercise and stretching, dry needling or trigger point injection with prilocaine weekly for 3 weeks is no more effective for reducing pain than exercise and stretching alone ( 103553 ). More research with long-term follow up is needed.

More evidence is needed to rate trigger point therapy for these uses.

Trigger points are small spots in muscle or connective tissue ( 97837 , 97852 ). Detection and localization of a trigger point is based on palpation by physician, expression of patient, and observations of local twitch responses. Although no laboratory test or imaging technique has been established for finding trigger points, the use of ultrasonography, electromyography, thermography, and muscle biopsy are being evaluated ( 28495 ). Dry needling : Trigger point dry needling involves needle insertion with a sterile, thin, dry needle into the trigger point. Insertion of the needle causes an initial local twitch response and then the needle is removed, resulting in deactivation of the trigger point ( 97852 ). In some cases, the needle is inserted and withdrawn almost immediately. In others, it is partially withdrawn and reinserted until one or more fast twitch responses are achieved. Occasionally, it is left in place for up to 7 minutes ( 97828 , 97830 , 97832 , 97833 , 97834 , 103559 ). The Hong method involves a repeated fast-in, fast-out approach until a local twitch response occurs ( 97829 , 97830 , 97836 ). Compression after needling results in hypoxia at the site for approximately 15 seconds ( 97829 ). Trigger point therapy is often followed by stretching ( 97852 ). Injection : Trigger point injection involves needling directly into the trigger point and injecting an agent, such as saline, local anesthetic, corticosteroid, or botulinum A toxin ( 97835 , 97852 ). The needle is inserted into the trigger point at an acute angle of 30 degrees. This may require an initial insertion at 1 to 2 cm away from the trigger point ( 28494 ). Sometimes a small amount of drug is injected once the needle is inside the trigger point. The needle is then withdrawn to the level of the subcutaneous tissue, then redirected, repeating the needling and injection process in each direction until the local twitch response is no longer elicited or resisting muscle tautness is no longer perceived ( 28494 ). Re-injection of the trigger points is not recommended until the post-injection soreness resolves, usually after three to four days ( 28494 ). Neuroreflexotherapy : Neuroreflexotherapy is a type of trigger point therapy involving the temporary implantation of epidermal devices into trigger points and referred tender points ( 28479 ).

None known.

The precise mechanism by which trigger point injection inactivates the trigger point is currently unknown. However, several mechanisms have been proposed, including mechanical disruption of the abnormal muscle fibers and nerve endings, increased blood flow to the muscle, depolarization of nerve fibers by the intracellular potassium released from disrupted muscle fibers, interruption of the pain feedback mechanism, local dilution of nociceptive substances, increased metabolite removal caused by the vasodilatory effect of the local anesthetic, focal necrosis of the trigger point by the injected substance, or counter-stimulation analogous to the effect of acupuncture ( 28493 , 28495 , 28496 , 28497 , 28498 , 97852 , 103552 ). Trigger points are theorized to reduce pain by overstimulating pain receptors in a specific trigger point and disrupting the regular chronic pain cycle. Additionally, trigger point injection of an anesthetic is theorized to normalize nerve function, which helps to prevent abnormal neural activity that causes pain ( 28500 ). Some research suggests that myofascial trigger-point massage therapy decreases heart rate, blood pressure, and muscle tension, increases parasympathetic activity, and improves emotional state in healthy subjects ( 28484 ). Dry needling has been shown to increase levels of met-enkephalin in the blood following the stimulation of alpha-delta nerve fibers. This can cause an opioid-mediated pain suppression ( 97829 , 97852 ). Some animal research suggests that trigger point injections with phentolamine and dry needling reduce spontaneous electrical activity in the muscle when compared to control ( 28801 , 28802 ).
