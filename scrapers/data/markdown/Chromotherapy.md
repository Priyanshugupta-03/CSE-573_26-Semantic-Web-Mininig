Caution

This monograph focuses on chromotherapy which includes color therapy and color light therapy. For a more comprehensive review of light therapy, see the Light Therapy monograph.

## Overview

Chromotherapy, or color therapy, is the use of color for therapeutic purposes ( 15988 ). There is no standard education or training for practitioners of chromotherapy. Chromotherapy is not a licensed health practice in North America.

## History

The first recorded use of chromotherapy occurred in ancient Greece, China, and Egypt around 4000 B.C. A physician of the renaissance period, Paracelsus, described light and colors as essential to human health and used them in his practice. In 1940, the Russian scientist Dr. Krakov generated the theory that colors affect sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous system. In 1958, the American scientist Dr. Gerard discovered the psychoactive effects of colors on mood and behavior ( 100488 ).

## People Use This For

Chromotherapy is used for depression, anxiety, stress, fatigue, pain, cramps, headache, migraine headache, diabetes, hypertension, asthma, cough, and many other conditions.

Possibly Safe when used appropriately, short-term. Chromotherapy has been used with apparent safety in several preliminary clinical studies with no reports of adverse effects ( 100487 , 100489 , 100490 , 100491 , 100492 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; there is no reason to expect problems with safety.

#### General

Chromotherapy seems to be well tolerated. It has been used with apparent safety in limited clinical research with no reports of adverse effects ( 100487 , 100489 , 100490 , 100491 , 100492 ).

#### General

Chromotherapy practitioners believe that color can be used to correct energy imbalances in the body's "chakras". These imbalances are thought to cause disease. Different colors have different effects. For example, red is thought to increase pulse rate, blood pressure, and breathing rate. Therefore, it is often used for circulatory conditions. Blue is believed to balance the autonomic nervous system resulting in relaxation. Therefore, blue is used for headaches, pain, cramping, stress, and other conditions ( 15988 ). Another chromotherapy theory is that color light generates electrical and magnetic energy that activate human biochemical and hormonal processes. It is thought that the human body absorbs energy from the colored light which then impacts automatic processes such as breathing, heart rate, digestion, and the stress response. Some proponents of light therapy believe that when light energy is absorbed, it can catalyze reactions with glucose oxidase, cholesterol oxidase, lipase and other enzymes affecting DNA/RNA synthesis, protein and collagen synthesis and cellular proliferation ( 100485 ). Although there is some evidence in vitro that colored light impacts different enzymes, it is not clear how this reaction translates to humans or animals. Different colored light is also thought to have indirect psychological effects on the body by sending signals through the visual centers of the brain to the hypothalamus. The hypothalamus has effects on numerous bodily functions such as the stress response, immune function, thirst, hunger, temperature, emotions, and sleep ( 100485 , 100489 ). There is no reliable scientific support for these beliefs.

#### Cardiovascular effects

Preliminary clinical research suggests that 10 minute exposure to red, green, and blue-colored fluorescent light can increase heart rate variability (HRV) and decrease heart rate ( 100489 , 100490 ). Green, blue, and red also seem to impact mood by decreasing anger, fatigue, and depression ( 100489 ). These studies are limited by small size and a lack of statistical comparison to a control group. It is unclear how these findings translate into therapeutic benefits. There is some interest in determining whether exposure to colored light affects blood volume and tissue oxygenation; however research has also been inconclusive. One small study in healthy volunteers found that a 10 minute exposure to red and blue light might slightly increase blood volume measured by total hemoglobin concentration, and that blue light might slightly increase tissue oxygen saturation. However, another study in healthy volunteers found no effect of blue or red light on these parameters ( 100491 , 100492 ).
