# Mantra Meditation
Other Common Names Concentrative Meditation, Meditación, Meditación mantra, Méditation, Méditation Concentrative, Méditation de Pleine Conscience, Méditation mantra, Méditation Transcendantale, TM, Transcendental Meditation

## Overview

Meditation is a practice involving various techniques to train the mind and achieve different modes of consciousness ( 91855 ). Mantra meditation involves the repetition of a sound, word, or phrase to attain a meditative state. One popular form of mantra meditation is called transcendental meditation ( 97546 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Mantra meditation has been safely used in clinical trials and there is no known reason to expect any adverse effects ( 16211 , 16216 , 16219 , 88689 , 88690 , 88811 , 91853 , 107277 , 110756 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no known reason to expect any adverse effects.

#### General

Mantra meditation is generally well tolerated.

Possibly Effective
Anxiety.

Practicing transcendental meditation or other types of mantra-based meditation may modestly improve anxiety. It is unclear if transcendental meditation is beneficial in children without a diagnosis of anxiety.

A meta-analysis of 16 clinical studies in people with trait anxiety shows that transcendental meditation moderately decreases perceived anxiety when compared with attention control or a waitlist control. Transcendental meditation also appears to be modestly more effective for reducing anxiety scores when compared with other active alternative treatments, including psychotherapy, group therapy, progressive relaxation, and biofeedback ( 91853 ).  Another meta-analysis of 25 mostly small, low-quality clinical studies that includes 1825 patients with self-reported anxiety shows that practicing transcendental or other mantra-based meditation modestly reduces anxiety when compared with various controls. However, practicing mantra meditation did not improve anxiety when compared with relaxation therapy ( 110756 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which enrolled various patient populations, and employed variable interventions with a broad range of treatment durations and follow up periods. The effect of transcendental meditation on anxiety has also been evaluated in children without a diagnosis of anxiety. In a clinical study of children 9-16 years of age, practicing transcendental meditation for 12 weeks, with an average frequency of 1-3 times per week, did not reduce anxiety scores when compared with no intervention ( 107275 ).

Hypertension.

Practicing transcendental meditation, a type of mantra meditation, may slightly reduce blood pressure in patients with hypertension. It is unclear if mantra meditation is more effective than health education in these patients.

Several small clinical trials and meta-analyses of these studies addressing the effect of transcendental meditation on blood pressure have been published ( 28991 , 28992 , 69701 , 91843 , 103740 , 103742 , 107276 , 110757 ). Three meta-analyses of clinical research show that practicing transcendental meditation decreases systolic blood pressure (SBP) by about 2-3 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) by about 2-5 mmHg when compared with control in patients with normal blood pressure, prehypertension, or hypertension ( 28991 , 91843 , 110757 ). However, these effects may wane after 3 months ( 110757 ). Transcendental meditation seems to have greater effects in patients with uncontrolled hypertension. A subgroup analysis in this population shows that transcendental meditation reduces SBP and DBP by up to 7 mmHg and 6 mmHg, respectively, when compared with control ( 91843 ). Similarly, transcendental meditation seems to have greater effects in older patients. A subgroup analysis in this population shows that transcendental meditation reduces SBP by about 10 mmHg in patients over age 65 compared to a reduction of about 1 mmHg in patients under age 65. However, the reduction in DBP was similar between groups ( 110757 ). A modestly-sized clinical study in Black patients with prehypertension shows that practicing transcendental meditation 20 minutes twice daily for 3-36 months reduces SBP, but not DBP, by around 3 mmHg when compared with health education; however, meditation was no more effective than health education in normotensive patients ( 107276 ). Conversely, a meta-analysis and a clinical study in patients with hypertension shows that practicing transcendental meditation reduces blood pressure when compared to baseline, but not when compared with receiving health education ( 103740 , 103742 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Alcohol use disorder.

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for alcohol use disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Angina.

It is unclear if practicing transcendental meditation, a type of mantra meditation, improves angina in females with cardiac syndrome X.

One very small clinical study in females with cardiac syndrome X, or microvascular angina, shows that practicing transcendental meditation twice daily for 3 months modestly improves exercise tolerance, angina, and quality of life when compared to baseline ( 88816 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Attention.

It is unclear if practicing mantra meditation can improve the ability to pay attention.

One small clinical study in healthy adults shows that practicing meditation, such as transcendental mantra meditation, seems to improve measures of attentiveness when compared to baseline ( 16211 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for ADHD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

It is unclear if practicing transcendental meditation, a type of mantra meditation, improves exercise capacity in patients with established CVD or recovery after a CV event.

One small clinical study in adults with coronary artery disease shows that practicing transcendental meditation for 20 minutes daily for 6-8 months improves exercise tolerance by around 15% and maximum workload by around 12% when compared with no additional intervention ( 88814 ). A very small clinical study in adults with cardiovascular disease undergoing cardiac rehabilitation shows that practicing transcendental meditation for 20 minutes twice daily for 4 weeks does not improve hemodynamic parameters (i.e blood pressure, heart rate, cardiac index) or skeletal muscle strength when compared with usual care ( 110759 ). However, the duration of this study may have been too short and it may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups due to its small size.

Cocaine dependence.

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for cocaine dependence, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Cognitive function.

It is unclear if practicing transcendental meditation, a type of mantra meditation, improves cognitive function in elderly nursing home patients.

One small study in elderly nursing home patients shows that practicing transcendental meditation 20 minutes twice daily for 3 months improves certain aspects of cognitive function, such as learning and word fluency, when compared with no treatment ( 88804 ).

Congestive heart failure (CHF).

It is unclear if practicing transcendental meditation, a type of mantra meditation, is beneficial in patients with CHF.

One small clinical study in Black patients with class II or III NYHA heart failure shows that practicing transcendental meditation for 15-20 minutes twice daily for 6 months improves exercise capacity, quality of life, and re-hospitalization rates when compared with health education alone ( 16219 ).

Depression.

It is unclear if practicing mantra meditation, is beneficial in patients with depression.

A meta-analysis of 22 mostly small, low-quality clinical studies that includes 1616 patients with self-reported depression shows that practicing transcendental or other mantra-based meditation modestly reduces depression when compared with various controls. However, practicing mantra meditation did not improve anxiety when compared with psychotherapy ( 110756 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which enrolled various patient populations, and employed variable interventions with a broad range of treatment durations and follow up periods.

Diabetes.

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for diabetes, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Epilepsy.

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for epilepsy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Fatigue.

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for fatigue, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Headache.

It is unclear if practicing transcendental meditation, a type of mantra meditation, is beneficial in children with primary headache.

One small clinical study in children 9-18 years of age with primary headache shows that practicing transcendental meditation over five 60-90 minute training sessions, followed by 10 minutes twice daily for 3 months, reduces headache frequency by at least 50% at 3 and 9 months when compared to baseline. However, the impact of transcendental meditation on headache frequency was no different than with hypnotherapy or progressive muscle relaxation ( 100746 ).

HIV/AIDS.

It is unclear if practicing Metta meditation, a type of mantra meditation, is beneficial in patients with HIV or AIDS.

One small clinical study in patients with late-stage AIDS shows that practicing Metta meditation, which includes repeating loving-kindness phrases for 15 minutes daily for 1 month, along with receiving Swedish massage daily for 5 days each week for 1 month, improves function and well-being after 2 months when compared with no intervention. These benefits were reported to persist at the 17-month follow-up ( 88828 ).

Insomnia.

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for insomnia and other sleep disorders, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if practicing mantra meditation is beneficial in patients with migraine headaches.

One small clinical study in adults with migraines shows that practicing spiritual mantra meditation for 20 minutes daily for 30 days decreases the frequency of migraine headache and the need for migraine medication when compared with secular mantra meditation or progressive muscle relaxation. Participants practicing spiritual meditation chanted a spiritual phrase such as "God is peace", while participants practicing secular meditation chanted internal phrases such as "I am content" or external phrases such as "Grass is green" ( 97545 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for MS-related symptoms, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Neck pain.

It is unclear if practicing Jyoti mantra meditation is beneficial in patients with chronic neck pain.

One small clinical study in patients with chronic neck pain shows that adding the practice of Jyoti mantra meditation for 75-90 minutes weekly for 8 weeks to an exercise program decreases pain scores by an additional 39% when compared with an 8-week exercise program alone ( 91849 ).

Pain (chronic).

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for chronic pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Practicing transcendental meditation or other types of mantra-based meditation may modestly improve PTSD symptoms.

A meta-analysis of 2 small clinical studies in veterans with PTSD from combat-associated trauma shows that practicing mantra meditation for 90 minutes weekly for 6 weeks in addition to usual care does not improve anxiety, depression, or symptoms of PTSD when compared with usual care alone ( 97544 ). Both studies were small and might not have been adequately powered to detect a difference. These 2 studies were also included in another meta-analysis with 4 other mostly small, low-quality clinical studies. This analysis of 513 veterans or prison inmates with PTSD shows that practicing transcendental or other mantra-based meditation modestly improves PTSD symptom scores when compared with various controls. Practicing mantra meditation also seems to improve PTSD symptom scores when compared with psychotherapy ( 110756 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which employed variable interventions with a broad range of treatment durations and follow up periods. Small clinical studies have also tested the effects of transcendental meditation on PTSD-related symptoms. One small clinical study in South African college students with possible PTSD shows that practicing transcendental meditation for 15-20 minutes twice daily for 15 weeks reduces PTSD and depressive symptoms when compared with no intervention ( 103739 ). A small clinical study in veterans with PTSD shows that practicing transcendental meditation in group sessions and individually with the guidance of certified teachers for 8 weeks reduces PTSD symptoms when compared to baseline for up to a two month follow-up period ( 100747 ). Since these studies included several group meditation sessions, it is unclear if these findings are due to transcendental meditation itself, regular social interaction, or the combination.

Psoriasis.

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation for psoriasis, including psoriatic arthritis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Psychological well-being.

It is unclear if practicing transcendental meditation, a type of mantra meditation, improves psychological well-being.

Two clinical studies in impoverished young adult females in Uganda show that learning transcendental meditation for five sessions and then practicing at home for 20-25 minutes twice daily for 3-5 months improves feelings of self-esteem and gratitude, quality of life, and the perceived ability to cope with life challenges, and reduces stress, anxiety, tiredness, and excessive alcohol use, when compared with baseline or waitlisted controls ( 100745 , 115347 ). Additionally, clinical research in nurses working during the COVID-19 pandemic shows that practicing transcendental meditation for 20 minutes twice daily for 3 months improves well-being, and reduces anxiety, burnout, and posttraumatic stress symptoms when compared with controls who do not practice meditation ( 115348 ). The specificity of the studied patient populations limits the generalizability of these findings.

Quality of life.

Although there has been interest in the practice of mantra meditation to improve quality of life, particularly in patients with cancer, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mantra meditation for this purpose.

Smoking cessation.

It is unclear if practicing transcendental meditation, a type of mantra meditation, is beneficial in college students attempting to quit smoking.

One small clinical study in college students who are trying to quit smoking shows that receiving training in transcendental meditation is comparable to participating in a self-control discussion group for decreasing the number of cigarettes smoked ( 88845 ).

Stress.

While some conflicting evidence exists, a meta-analysis and several small clinical studies suggest that practicing mantra meditation may modestly reduce stress.

A meta-analysis of 14 mostly small, low-quality clinical studies that includes 1078 patients with self-reported stress shows that practicing transcendental or other mantra-based meditation modestly reduces depression when compared with various controls ( 110756 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies, which enrolled various patient populations, and employed variable interventions with a broad range of treatment durations and follow up periods. Several small clinical studies in healthy adults also show that practicing mantra meditation, including transcendental meditation, for 15-20 minutes twice daily for 4 months modestly improves stress and negative mood when compared to baseline or waitlist control ( 16216 , 88811 , 100749 ). Clinical research in nurses working during the COVID-19 pandemic shows that practicing transcendental meditation for 20 minutes twice daily for 3 months improves well-being, and reduces anxiety, burnout, and posttraumatic stress symptoms when compared with controls who do not practice meditation ( 115348 ). However, some conflicting evidence exists ( 91848 ). A small open-label clinical study in healthcare workers shows that practicing transcendental meditation for 20 minutes twice daily for 3 months does not reduce acute psychological distress but may improve burnout, insomnia, and anxiety when compared with wellness resources ( 110758 ).

More evidence is needed to rate mantra meditation for these uses.

Mantra meditation involves the repetition of a sound, word or a phrase to attain a meditative state ( 97546 ). There are different types of mantra meditations. Some examples include transcendental meditation, Jyoti mantra meditation, Metta meditation, and others. Transcendental meditation uses a mantra to transcend the practitioner to an effortless state involving a period of consciousness that is without any content ( 88804 , 91848 ). The practice of transcendental meditation is said not to require any changes in lifestyle or belief system ( 88804 ). Jyoti mantra meditation requires visual concentration involving repeating a mantra and sitting motionless with closed eyes ( 91849 ). Metta meditation involves repeating phrases of loving-kindness ( 88828 ). Mantra meditation can invoke the repetition or chanting of various types of phrases. These include spiritual phrases, such as "God is peace", secular internal phrases, such as "I am content", and secular external phrases, such as "Grass is green" ( 97545 ).

Meditation is a practice used to free the mind of cluttered thoughts and achieve a relaxed mental and physical state. Mantra meditation is a type of meditation practice that involves the repetition of a sound, word, or phrase to attain a meditative state. Mantra meditation is a concentrative meditation because it involves giving attention to a chant or a repeated mantra. One popular form of mantra meditation is transcendental meditation. Transcendental meditation involves deep physical relaxation while maintaining a highly alert mind ( 91855 , 97546 ). Through meditation, the brain is thought to switch from resting brain waves (alpha) to relaxing brain waves (theta). This switch is thought to increase release of endorphins, slow breathing and heart rate, decrease blood pressure, and decrease metabolism. Meditation has been shown to decrease the amplitude of low-frequency fluctuations in the bilateral frontal pole which are thought to be correlated with increased relaxation and reduced rumination and production of complex thought. Meditation has also been linked with increased connectivity within the different prefrontal cortexes, which is thought to be associated with improved regulation of emotions ( 91846 ). Some research shows that meditation alleviates emotional and relationship problems which can precipitate stress and fatigue. Researchers theorize that psychological stress and fatigue significantly contribute to disease processes. Therefore, it is thought that by reducing stress and fatigue, meditation might be beneficial for a variety of conditions ( 91855 ). Cortisol is a hormone released in response to stress. Practicing transcendental meditation for 4 weeks resulted in lower salivary cortisol levels in healthy adults when compared with a waitlist control. However, this effect was no longer seen after 8 weeks of practice ( 103741 ).
