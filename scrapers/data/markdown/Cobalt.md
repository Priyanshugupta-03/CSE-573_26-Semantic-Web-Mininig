## Overview

Cobalt is a metal element. It is magnetic, ductile, and silvery gray in appearance ( 102070 , 102079 ). Cobalt is also the metal component of vitamin B12 (cyanocobalamin) ( 102079 , 102080 ). Cobalt is commonly used as an alloy in steel products, including prosthetic orthopedic joints and other medical devices. It is also found in ceramics, cement, certain pigments, leather tanning materials, and other items ( 102070 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, cobalt is used for treating anemia of chronic disease. In manufacturing, cobalt is used in the ceramic, pigment, heavy metal, electronic, and leather tanning industries.

Possibly Safe when used orally and appropriately, short-term. Cobalt chloride 1 mg daily has been used with apparent safety for up to 90 days in two small clinical studies ( 102081 , 102082 ).

Possibly Unsafe when used orally, long-term or in large amounts. ...when inhaled due to occupational exposure. Cobalt exposure has been linked to reports of cardiomyopathy, dermatitis, hypothyroidism, interstitial lung disease, and neurotoxicity, including vision and hearing loss ( 102066 , 102067 , 102068 , 102070 , 102071 , 102072 , 102073 , 102074 , 102076 , 102077 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Orally, cobalt seems to be well-tolerated when taken for up to 90 days in small amounts. Taking cobalt orally long-term or in high doses may cause cardiomyopathy, hearing loss, and vision loss ( 102066 , 102068 , 102077 ). Topically, cobalt-containing products can cause allergic contact dermatitis in some patients ( 102070 , 102071 , 102072 , 102073 ). Occupational exposure to cobalt dust may cause cardiomyopathy, interstitial lung disease, and hearing and vision loss in some people ( 102066 , 102067 , 102068 , 102077 ). Exposure to cobalt from cobalt-containing prosthetic joints has been linked to cardiomyopathy, dermatitis, hypothyroidism, hearing loss, and vision loss ( 102066 , 102068 , 102070 , 102074 , 102076 , 102077 ).

#### Cardiovascular

Oral, occupational, and systemic exposure to cobalt may cause cardiomyopathy. While rare, this adverse effect has been reported in people taking cobalt for anemia, those with cobalt-containing prosthetic joints, those consuming cobalt-fortified beer, and those working in industries with exposure to cobalt dust ( 102066 , 102076 ). Patients who consume a low protein diet, are thiamine deficient, or who have hypothyroidism appear to be at a greater risk of cobalt-induced cardiomyopathy. Cobalt-related cardiomyopathy can be distinguished from non-cobalt-related cardiomyopathy by its rapid onset and progression ( 102066 ). A case report describes a 25-year-old female with a cobalt-containing hip arthroplasty who developed severely elevated cobalt levels and cardiomyopathy, with dyspnea on light exertion, sinus tachycardia, left atrial enlargement, left ventricular and septal hypertrophy, a left ventricular ejection fraction of 44%, and pericardial effusion. The condition resolved with removal of the hip prosthesis, extensive tissue debridement, and the use of chelating agents ( 114033 ).

#### Dermatologic

Topically, cobalt can cause allergic contact dermatitis, with an estimated incidence of 1% to 4% in the general population ( 114036 , 114578 ). Cobalt is the second most common metal contact allergen after nickel ( 114036 ). Numerous cases of allergic skin reactions have been reported after contact with cobalt in leather goods, jewelry, nail polishes, cobalt blue tattoo ink, and cement ( 102069 , 102070 , 102071 , 102072 ). Hand dermatitis can occur in workers handling metal items containing cobalt ( 114036 ). Dermatitis has also been reported in patients with occupations that involve the production or handling of ceramics, enamel, glass, pigments, or electrical components, as cobalt is extensively used in these industries ( 102070 , 102073 ). Exposure to cobalt found in prosthetic joints and other metallic surgical implants has also been reported to cause generalized dermatitis in some patients ( 102070 ). Dermatologic allergic reactions have also been reported to the cobalt-containing molecule cyanocobalamin (vitamin B12). A case report describes a 69-year-old female who took oral vitamin B12 supplements weekly for 10 years following bariatric surgery and developed an erythematous-squamous rash in the inguinal, axillary, and submammary flexural areas, with a strong positive patch test for cobalt. The condition resolved slowly when vitamin B12 supplements were discontinued. and the rash was treated with topical corticosteroids ( 114578 ).

#### Endocrine

Exposure to cobalt-containing prosthetic joints may cause hypothyroidism in some patients ( 102074 , 102076 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Exposure to cobalt may cause reversible neurologic adverse effects in some patients ( 102074 , 102076 , 102077 ). Demyelinating neuropathy, cognitive decline, headaches, depression, convulsions, and forgetfulness have been reported in patients with cobalt-containing hip joint prostheses ( 102074 , 102077 ). Vertigo and impairment in memory and attention have been reported after occupational exposure to cobalt ( 102077 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

Oral, occupational, and systemic exposure to cobalt may cause adverse ocular and otic effects. While rare, reversible and irreversible vision loss, optic neuropathy and atrophy, signs of abnormal retinal functioning, hearing loss, bilateral deafness, and tinnitus have been reported in patients taking cobalt for anemia, working in industries with exposure to cobalt dust, and those with cobalt-containing prosthetic joints ( 102068 , 102074 , 102077 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

Occupational exposure to cobalt can cause interstitial lung disease (ILD). Patients with subacute cobalt-related ILD may present with dyspnea, cough, fevers, chills, and weight loss a few months to years after exposure, and symptoms typically resolve after removal of the exposure. Patients with chronic cobalt-related ILD experience a more gradual development of dyspnea and cough which generally do not improve after removal of the exposure. Chronic cobalt-related ILD can progress to end-stage fibrosis and death. While removal of exposure is the mainstay of treatment, some case reports show that inhaled or systemic corticosteroids can improve symptoms and radiographic signs of cobalt-related ILD ( 102067 ).

#### Other

Exposure to cobalt-containing prosthetic joints may cause pseudotumor formation near the replaced joints ( 102074 , 102075 , 102076 ). In one study, around 54% of patients with metal-on-metal total hip arthroplasty developed pseudotumors, while 22% with conventional metal-on-polyethylene articulations developed pseudotumors. Women with metal-on-metal articulations and elevated serum cobalt levels were at the greatest risk of developing pseudotumors ( 102075 ). Two case reports describe males in their late 50s who developed severe cobalt toxicity from eroded hip prostheses, presenting with dyspnea on exertion, hypothyroidism, weight loss, vision and hearing loss, muscle weakness, hyporeflexia, and peripheral neuropathy. In one case the patient developed severe hemodynamic instability and died despite removal of the prosthesis and treatment with chelating agents ( 114034 ). In the other case, the patient received chelating agents but developed heart failure with an ejection fraction of 9% and was unable to tolerate surgery to remove the prosthesis. His condition worsened to multiorgan failure, severe cardiovascular compromise, and death ( 114037 ).

#### Absorption

When ingested orally, cobalt bioavailability is 5% to 45% ( 114034 ). Systemic uptake and average serum levels of cobalt after oral intake appear to be higher in women than men ( 102081 , 102082 ). In one pharmacokinetic study, taking cobalt chloride 1 mg daily for 90 days resulted in greater serum and whole blood cobalt levels in women when compared with men. The average serum cobalt levels were 71 mcg/L and 25 mcg/L in women and men, respectively ( 102081 ). Steady-state concentrations of cobalt are achieved after 14-24 days of once daily dosing ( 102082 ).

#### Distribution

Cobalt circulates in the bloodstream bound to albumin and transferrin ( 114034 ).

#### Excretion

After oral ingestion, 85% of a dose of cobalt is excreted via the kidneys, with the remainder in the bile and feces ( 114034 ). Cobalt appears to follow a two-phase elimination curve. This is likely explained by the sequestering of cobalt by red blood cells, which have a 120-day life span. Cobalt's elimination half-life in both serum and whole blood is around 3 days in the first phase of elimination. In the second phase, cobalt has a half-life of 16-22 days and 36-39 days in serum and whole blood, respectively ( 102081 , 102082 ).

#### General

Cobalt is a metal element. The cobalt ions cobaltus (Co2+) and cobaltic (Co3+) are the predominant valence states of cobalt. Cobaltus is found most often in nature and is commonly used commercially ( 102079 ). Cobalt is an essential trace element found throughout the body, including the heart, liver, kidneys, pancreas, and brain ( 114037 ). Normal plasma levels of cobalt are below 0.2 mcg/L. Toxicity can develop at levels of 7-10 mcg/L, with systemic cobaltism developing at levels above 50 mcg/L ( 114034 , 114037 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

In vitro and animal research suggests that cobalt has both beneficial and harmful cardiovascular effects. Cobalt appears to modulate the cardiovascular system's ability to respond to hypoxic stress by increasing the synthesis of erythropoietin, increasing the expression of hypoxia-induced factor 1, inducing vascular endothelial growth factor, and increasing the activity of enzymes involved in glycolysis. However, cobalt also appears to depress cardiac function and change the structure of cardiac cells by interfering with the binding and activity of calcium in heart tissue, increasing generation of reactive oxygen species, interrupting the citric acid cycle, and inhibiting the production of adenosine triphosphate ( 102066 ).

#### Erythropoietic effects

In hypoxic conditions, cobalt stabilizes hypoxia-induced factor 1-alpha and 2-alpha. This seems to increase the production of erythropoietin (EPO) in the kidney and liver, suggesting that cobalt may be useful for enhancing athletic performance and treating anemia ( 102083 ). However, clinical research on the erythropoietic effects of cobalt is mixed ( 102081 , 102082 , 102083 ). Taking cobalt chloride 1 mg daily for up to 90 days does not appear to increase hemoglobin, hematocrit, or red blood cell (RBC) counts ( 102081 , 102082 ). Additionally, while one study shows that taking a single dose of cobalt 5 mg increases plasma EPO by 20.5% and that a 10 mg dose increases EPO by nearly 53% when compared to baseline, improvements in hematocrit, hemoglobin, and RBC counts were lacking. In another study, taking cobalt 10 mg, but not 5 mg, daily for 5 days increased EPO by 27% when compared to baseline, with no improvement in hemoglobin, hematocrit, and RBC counts. Earlier research shows that higher doses of cobalt chloride 25-150 mg can increase hemoglobin levels and RBC counts; however, these doses may be unsafe ( 102083 ).

#### Pulmonary effects

The adverse pulmonary effects of cobalt are thought to be due to immunologic and oxidant injury-related mechanisms ( 102067 ).

#### Toxic effects

Cobalt blocks mitochondrial metabolism and can damage various organs ( 114034 ). Elevated serum levels and toxicity can occur from eroded joint prostheses, manifesting as local pain, edema, malaise, fatigue, and anorexia, and progressing to mixed polyneuropathy, visual and hearing loss, hypothyroidism, congestive cardiomyopathy, lactic acidosis, and death ( 114034 ).
