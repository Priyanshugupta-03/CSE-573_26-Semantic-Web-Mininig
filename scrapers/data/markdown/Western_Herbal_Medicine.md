## Overview

Western herbal medicine is a system of medicine that involves the use of plants and plant materials to treat disease and to promote health and well-being ( 102302 ). The term "Western herbal medicine" is used to differentiate this practice from other systems of herbal medicine, such as Ayurveda or Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM). It is commonly practiced in Australia, Canada, New Zealand, the United Kingdom, the United States, and Western Europe ( 102302 ). The theory and practice of Western herbal medicine is based in traditional models of health. However, it is increasingly informed by advances in modern medicine. Additionally, the herbs used in Western herbal medicine are the subject of increasing interest in the Western medical community. While both the United States and Europe have a long history of Western herbal medicine practitioners, this form of treatment has not always been considered widely available or accepted ( 102302 , 102303 ). Western herbal medicine practitioners typically take a holistic, or whole person, approach to treatment. This results in highly individualized treatment recommendations that might blend Western medicine with the use of herbal formulations. These herbal formulations might use the whole herb, isolated active chemicals, or specific parts of the plant. They also often use traditional preparations in lieu of herbal products available in the retail market ( 102303 ).

Western herbal medicine is based on the use of proposed synergistic and curative properties of plants to treat disease or to enhance overall wellness. Western herbalism is also based upon pharmacognosy, the study of natural products, including the identification, extraction methods, and applications of specific plant constituents responsible for therapeutic actions ( 102302 ). Practitioners of Western herbal medicine focus on holistic care, in which a person must be treated physically, mentally, socially, and spiritually to achieve health. This involves a person-centered approach, with the practitioner focusing on the patient rather than the disease. Diagnosis typically includes an interview that lasts for an hour or longer. It may also include a routine physical examination, depending on the profession of the practitioner, and can also include conventional laboratory tests ( 102303 ). Practitioners may also provide emotional support to the patient and inquire about quality of life. Herbal treatments are intended to treat the underlying disorder causing the observed symptoms. The goal is to move the patient towards an overall state of wellness, rather than to simply treat one medical condition ( 102302 , 102303 ). Western herbal medicine sometimes uses single herbs, rather than formulas of multiple herbs, to treat conditions. These herbs are known as "simples." However, herbs may also be given in combination with one another. When herbs are combined, they are known as a "formula." Practitioners may choose to use medicinal plants in fresh or dried form or as simple extracts, standardized extracts, teas, poultices, liquid extracts, and capsules. Herbal remedies can be given internally or externally ( 102302 , 102303 ).
