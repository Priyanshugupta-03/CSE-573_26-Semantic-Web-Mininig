## Overview

Red soapwort is a plant native to Europe and Asia. It is sometimes used in medicine ( 102772 ). In the Middle Ages, Franciscan and Dominican monks viewed soapwort as a divine gift that was meant to keep them clean ( 6 ). It is often used as an ingredient in soaps, herbal shampoos, and detergents. As a medicine, it has traditionally been used both orally and topically as an anti-inflammatory.

Likely Safe when used topically. Red soapwort is widely used in soaps and shampoos ( 6 ) without reports of adverse effects.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of red soapwort when used orally.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Orally, there is limited reliable information available about the safety of red soapwort. Topically, red soapwort is well tolerated.

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, red soapwort has been reported to cause stomach irritation ( 2 ), nausea, and vomiting ( 7 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Acne.

Oral red soapwort has only been evaluated in combination with other ingredients; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A small, unblinded study in young adults with facial acne suggests that application of a cream containing red soapwort root extract 0.32%, elecampane flower and leaf extract 0.8%, and black nightshade leaf extract 0.8%, 2 to 3 times daily for 6 weeks, reduces acne severity index scores by 55%, 85%, and 91% from baseline at 2, 5, and 6 weeks, respectively, compared with no change in the placebo group ( 105733 ). It is unclear if this effect is due to red soapwort, other ingredients, or the combination. The poor study methodology and reporting of results, and the lack of statistical comparison to the placebo group, limit the validity of these findings.

More evidence is needed to rate red soapwort for this use.

#### GI MUCOSAL IRRITATION (e.g. ulcers, etc.)

Red soapwort is contraindicated, because it can exacerbate existing GI mucosal irritation due to its saponin content ( 6 ).

#### General

The applicable parts of red soapwort are the leaves or root. The root contains saponin constituents ( 7 , 102772 ).

#### Dermatologic effects

In vitro, a red soapwort root extract inhibits sebum production in isolated sebaceous glands. It also inhibits growth of Cutibacterium acnes and inhibits lipopolysaccharide-induced interleukin-6 and tumor necrosis factor-alpha production in human monocytes ( 105733 ).

#### Expectorant effects

The saponin constituents of red soapwort have expectorant effects. They irritate the gastric mucosa, which then stimulates bronchial mucous secretion via the parasympathetic sensory pathways ( 7 ).
