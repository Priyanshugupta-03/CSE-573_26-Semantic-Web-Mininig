## Overview

Therapeutic touch is intended to treat medical conditions by manipulating the "human energy field" with the practitioner's hands just above the skin. It was developed by Dolores Krieger, PhD, RN, and Dora Kunz in the 1970s. Therapeutic touch is primarily practiced by nurses ( 15933 ); however, there is no universal training standard or licensing requirement for practitioners. The outcomes of therapeutic touch may vary between practitioners ( 107366 ). A limitation to much of the clinical research on therapeutic touch is the difficulty in assuring blinding. Because a component of therapeutic touch involves the practitioner's intention, trials can generally only be single-blind at most ( 113102 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Therapeutic touch has been used safely in clinical studies without adverse effects ( 15797 , 15799 , 15935 , 15936 , 15937 , 15938 , 15939 , 15940 , 113010 , 113101 )( 113102 , 113103 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when used appropriately. Therapeutic touch has been used with apparent safety in clinical studies in children and infants with no reported adverse effects ( 89275 , 89294 , 89302 , 89303 , 113104 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Safe when used appropriately. Therapeutic touch has been used with apparent safety in clinical studies in pregnant and lactating patients with no reported adverse effects ( 89276 , 89301 ).

#### General

Therapeutic touch is well tolerated. Serious adverse events have not been reported in clinical research.

Possibly Effective
Anxiety.

Most clinical research shows that therapeutic touch reduces anxiety in adults and children with various underlying conditions.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that therapeutic touch helps to reduce anxiety in patients with various disease states ( 89271 ). Some preliminary clinical studies show that one session of therapeutic touch decreases anxiety in HIV-infected children and patients on chemotherapy ( 89274 , 89275 ). Research in patients hospitalized for cardiovascular conditions shows that therapeutic touch reduces anxiety better than casual touch or no touch ( 89279 , 89280 ). Research in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) shows that therapeutic touch for 10 minutes on 3 consecutive days reduces anxiety progressively more each day, when compared with no touch ( 107366 ). Preliminary clinical research in hospitalized burn patients shows that therapeutic touch may decrease anxiety, but not pain, when compared with nursing presence alone ( 89273 ). However, a small clinical study in older adults living in a nursing home shows similar improvement in anxiety and comfort levels over time in those receiving therapeutic touch or no touch, when compared with baseline levels ( 106002 ).

Stress.

Limited research suggests that therapeutic touch may be beneficial in people with stress.

Preliminary clinical research shows that therapeutic touch can reduce subjective measures of stress in college students and healthy females when compared with control ( 15797 , 89277 , 89278 ). Additional preliminary clinical research in children shows that therapeutic touch reduces time to relaxation after a stressful event, as determined by pulse, temperature, and other autonomic responses, when compared to baseline ( 89294 ). Therapeutic touch has also been shown to decrease stress when compared to baseline in patients who have faced a natural disaster ( 89295 ). It is unclear how therapeutic touch compares with other interventions.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Asthma.

Although there is interest in using therapeutic touch for asthma, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of therapeutic touch for this condition.

Autism spectrum disorder.

Although there is interest in using therapeutic touch for autism spectrum disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of therapeutic touch for this condition.

Cancer-related pain.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is useful for cancer-related pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with cancer shows that therapeutic touch might produce short-term pain relief ( 15799 , 89304 ); however, it does not seem to reduce the use of analgesics ( 15799 , 15933 ).

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

Although there is interest in using therapeutic touch for CVD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of therapeutic touch for this condition.

Carpal tunnel syndrome.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial for carpal tunnel syndrome.

Preliminary clinical research shows that therapeutic touch does not improve symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome when compared with sham therapeutic touch ( 15939 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with CINV.

Preliminary clinical research in breast cancer patients receiving chemotherapy shows that therapeutic touch modestly reduces acute nausea when compared with control ( 99596 ).

Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy.

A scoping review of small clinical studies in patients with chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy shows that therapeutic touch improves well-being and reduces pain and fatigue when compared with rest or usual care ( 113010 ).

Dementia.

Limited clinical research suggests that therapeutic touch may reduce responsive behaviors associated with dementia.

Several small clinical studies in adults with dementia show that therapeutic touch can improve aggressive or agitated responsive behaviors when compared to baseline or when compared with sham therapeutic touch or routine care. However, behaviors typically revert to baseline levels once no longer receiving therapeutic touch ( 15937 , 15938 , 89296 , 89297 , 89298 , 113101 ). Other clinical studies shows that therapeutic touch can decrease discomfort levels and increase relaxation in patients with dementia when compared to baseline, but not when compared with hand massage ( 89299 , 89300 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the small sample sizes, short duration of therapy, and single-blind design of most studies.

Diabetes.

Therapeutic touch has only been evaluated in combination with other modalities; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with type 1 diabetes shows that therapeutic touch for 15 minutes on even days, and intercessory prayer for 15 minutes on odd days, both for two weeks, does not affect blood glucose or insulin requirements when compared with control ( 89287 ).

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with fibromyalgia.

Preliminary clinical research shows that therapeutic touch might decrease pain and improve functional ability in patients with fibromyalgia when compared to baseline ( 89284 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

HIV/AIDS.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with HIV/AIDS.

Preliminary clinical research shows that therapeutic touch does not improve CD4 cell counts or coping ability in patients with HIV when compared to baseline ( 89286 ).

Insomnia.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with insomnia; the available research is conflicting.

A small clinical study in older adults with poor sleep quality living in a nursing home shows that therapeutic touch does not improve sleep quality when compared with sham therapeutic touch or no touch ( 106003 ). However, preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) shows that therapeutic touch given for 10 minutes daily on 3 consecutive days progressively improves sleep quality when compared with no touch. This occurs in parallel with improvements in anxiety levels ( 107366 ). Additionally, clinical studies in adults with menopausal symptoms show that therapeutic touch 15 minutes once weekly for 4 weeks or 10 minutes daily for 5 consecutive days improves sleep quality when compared with a control group and to a similar degree when compared with music therapy ( 113102 , 113103 ). Likewise, a small clinical study in children with liver transplantation shows that therapeutic touch 20 minutes daily for 3 consecutive days increases total sleep time, time in bed, and sleep efficiency when compared to baseline and as well as music therapy ( 113104 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of double-blinding due to the conscious intention of the practitioner being a core component of therapeutic touch treatment.

Lactation.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial for increasing lactation.

Preliminary clinical research shows that therapeutic touch does not increase lactation in parents of premature infants when compared with sham therapeutic touch ( 89301 ).

Menopausal symptoms.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial for menopausal symptoms.

A small clinical trial in adults with menopausal symptoms shows that therapeutic touch 10 minutes daily for five consecutive days improves sleep quality, but not fatigue, when compared with a control group receiving sham therapeutic touch ( 113102 ). Similarly, a moderate-sized clinical trial in adults with menopausal symptoms shows that therapeutic touch 15 minutes once weekly for 4 weeks improves sleep quality, menopause-specific quality of life, and somatic, psychological and sexual symptoms, including vasomotor symptoms, when compared with a control group receiving no treatment, with improvements similar to those reported with music therapy ( 113103 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of double-blinding due to the conscious intention of the practitioner being a core component of therapeutic touch treatment.

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

Although there is interest in using therapeutic touch for MS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of therapeutic touch for this condition.

Neuropathic pain.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with neuropathic pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with neuropathic pain related to spinal cord injury shows that therapeutic touch might decrease pain and improve feelings of well-being when compared with guided progressive relaxation ( 15936 ).

Osteoarthritis.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with knee osteoarthritis.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee shows that therapeutic touch can reduce pain and improve functionality when compared with sham treatment ( 15940 , 89283 ). Another small clinical study in patients with osteoarthritis shows that therapeutic touch given in weekly sessions lasting 15-20 minutes for 6 weeks may decrease pain when compared with progressive muscle relaxation treatment. However, progressive muscle relaxation is associated with greater improvements in walking and bending when compared with therapeutic touch ( 89288 ).

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with chronic pain.

A preliminary clinical study in patients with chronic non-oncologic pain shows that receiving a 25-minute therapeutic touch session twice weekly for 1 month may decrease pain, improve depressive symptoms, and improve sleep when compared to baseline ( 89292 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Postoperative pain.

Evidence for the efficacy of therapeutic touch for relieving postoperative pain is conflicting.

Some unblinded, clinical research in patients undergoing vascular or orthopedic surgery shows that therapeutic touch decreases postoperative pain ( 89291 , 89293 ). However, not all research agrees. Some shows that therapeutic touch does not reduce postoperative pain after abdominal or pelvic surgery, but might prolong the time before analgesic medication is requested ( 89289 , 89290 ). Other preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing breast biopsy shows that therapeutic touch does not reduce pain, discomfort, or fearfulness when compared with sham therapeutic touch ( 15935 ).

Prematurity.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch can improve outcomes in premature infants.

Preliminary clinical research shows that therapeutic touch given for 10 minutes three times daily until discharge may decrease hospital stay and postnatal complications in premature infants when compared with conventional treatment ( 89303 ).

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

Although there is interest in using therapeutic touch for PMS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of therapeutic touch for this condition.

Pre-procedural anxiety.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial in patients with anxiety prior to a procedure or surgery; the available research is conflicting.

Preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing surgery for breast cancer shows that 10 minutes of therapeutic touch reduces anxiety preoperatively, but not postoperatively ( 89272 ). However, conflicting evidence exists. Preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing heart surgery shows that one 5-minute session of therapeutic touch does not decrease anxiety ( 89281 ). Additionally, a clinical trial in preterm infants shows that therapeutic touch immediately before and after a 2-minute heel lance procedure does not reduce stress or pain responses when compared with control ( 99594 ).

Quality of life.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch improves quality of life.

Clinical research in adults with colorectal cancer shows that receiving therapeutic touch does not improve quality of life, depressive symptoms, or mood when compared with control ( 98749 ). However, a moderate-sized clinical trial in adults with menopausal symptoms shows that therapeutic touch 15 minutes once weekly for 4 weeks improves menopause-specific quality of life when compared with a control group receiving no treatment ( 113103 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of double-blinding due to the conscious intention of the practitioner being a core component of therapeutic touch treatment.

Stroke.

Although there is interest in using therapeutic touch for stroke, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of therapeutic touch for this condition.

Tension headache.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is beneficial for tension headache.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with tension headache shows that receiving therapeutic touch for 5 minutes can reduce pain for up to 4 hours when compared with sham therapeutic touch ( 89285 ).

Wound healing.

It is unclear if therapeutic touch is helpful in wound healing.

A small clinical trial in patients with full-thickness dermal wounds shows that therapeutic touch alone and in combination with Reiki, LeShan, and intercessory prayer does not improve wound healing when compared with a control group. In fact, patients in the control group experienced better wound healing than those receiving the interventions ( 89307 ).

More evidence is needed to rate therapeutic touch for these uses.

Therapeutic touch involves three steps. The first step is called "centering", and involves the therapeutic touch practitioner focusing or meditating on their intent to treat the patient or improve the patient's condition. The second step is "assessment", during which the practitioner moves their hands over the patient's body. The hands are kept within a few centimeters of the patient's body and are never intended to touch the body. The third and final step is "intervention." During this phase, the practitioner continues to move their hands over the patient's body, intending to "unblock areas of congested energy" ( 15933 , 15934 ).

Therapeutic touch practitioners believe that they can heal medical conditions by manipulating a "human energy field" that emanates just beyond the patient's skin ( 15933 ). The practitioners believe humans are dynamic energy fields and that the world is an energy system ( 15934 ). They identify sensory cues to problematic issues in the patient's energy field and then utilize their hands to unblock areas of congested energy or otherwise restore the proper energy balance to the patient ( 15933 , 15934 ). However, there is no reliable scientific evidence to support these beliefs.
