# Prenatal Vitamins
Other Common Names Prenatal vitamins, Prenatals, Vitaminas Prenatales, Vitamines prénatales
|

---

## Overview

In the US, up to 92% of pregnant patients use prenatal vitamins ( 94792 ). Prenatal vitamins are multivitamins that are specifically formulated for use during pregnancy. The main differences between multivitamins and prenatal vitamins are the ingredient type and quantity. Nutrient requirements increase during pregnancy. Prenatal vitamins usually contain nutrient quantities to address those unique dietary requirements. For instance, prenatal vitamins generally contain higher amounts of folic acid than multivitamins to prevent neural tube defects in the infant ( 94792 ). In the US, prenatal vitamins are available as a dietary supplement or by prescription. There are no mandated differences between these categories ( 94792 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, prenatal vitamins are used to prevent pregnancy complications such as stillbirth, miscarriage, early fetal death, and low fetal birth weight. Prenatal vitamins are also used by the mother to prevent adverse outcomes in the child such as autism spectrum disorder, attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), cancer, rickets, obesity, and birth defects such as neural tube defects. Prenatal vitamins are also taken to prevent anemia in the mother.

Possibly Safe when used orally. Prenatal vitamins contain many different minerals, vitamins, and sometimes other dietary supplement ingredients. Each individual ingredient has a unique safety profile. Prenatal vitamins usually do not contain ingredients in amounts that exceed the tolerable upper intake level (UL). The UL is the highest level of nutrient intake that is unlikely to have a risk of adverse effects for most people, as determined by the Food and Nutrition Board and the National Academies of Science. Therefore, prenatal vitamins have apparent safety in those who are not pregnant ( 94595 , 94596 , 94598 , 94793 ). However, high quality research assessing the safety of prenatal vitamins in those who are not pregnant is lacking. Prenatal vitamins do contain more iron than what is recommended for males and might therefore increase the risk of side effects.

PREGNANCY: Likely Safe when used orally. Prenatal vitamins contain many different minerals, vitamins, and sometimes other dietary supplement ingredients. Each individual ingredient has a unique safety profile. Clinical studies evaluating prenatal vitamins show that prenatal vitamins are safe when used appropriately during pregnancy ( 94595 , 94596 , 94598 , 94793 , 94820 ).

LACTATION: Possibly Safe when used orally. Prenatal vitamins usually do not contain ingredients in amounts that exceed the tolerable upper intake level (UL). The UL is the highest level of nutrient intake that is unlikely to have a risk of adverse effects for most people, as determined by the Food and Nutrition Board and the National Academies of Science. Therefore, prenatal vitamins have apparent safety during lactation ( 94089 ). However, high quality research assessing the safety of prenatal vitamins during lactation is lacking.

#### General

Prenatal vitamins contain many different minerals, vitamins, and other dietary supplement ingredients. See specific ingredient monographs for adverse effect information. Prenatal vitamins should be used conscientiously along with other nutrient sources to avoid over-supplementation and potential toxicity ( 94599 ).

Likely Effective
Neural tube birth defects.

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recommends prenatal vitamins containing folic acid 600-800 mcg be taken daily during pregnancy to prevent neural tube defects, such as spina bifida and anencephaly, in infants ( 94811 ). Prospective and retrospective research has found that taking a folic acid-containing prenatal vitamin is associated with a lower risk of neural tube defects ( 94812 , 94813 , 94816 , 101353 ).

Rickets.

Deficiency of vitamin D during pregnancy can lead to rickets in the infant. The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) and Institute of Medicine of the National Academies recommend prenatal vitamins that contain at least 600 IU of vitamin D be taken daily during pregnancy to prevent rickets in infants. A greater dose of vitamin D is needed if the pregnant patient is determined to be vitamin D deficient ( 94811 ).

Possibly Effective
Anemia.

There is a higher requirement of dietary iron during pregnancy. The recommended dietary allowance (RDA) of iron during pregnancy is 27 mg daily. This daily requirement was calculated by accounting for the increased hemoglobin mass during pregnancy, increased iron requirement for deposition into the placenta and fetus, and other factors. If sufficient iron is not acquired through dietary intake, most prenatal vitamins contain 27 mg elemental iron to prevent iron-deficiency anemia. Note that gummy formulations do not contain iron; therefore, a separate iron supplement may be needed in those who are taking gummy formulations ( 94811 , 94595 ).

Congenital heart disease.

The exact cause of congenital heart disease is unknown but is thought to be a combination of genetic and environmental factors. Meta-analyses of population research have found that maternal intake of folic acid-containing prenatal vitamins prior to and during the first trimester of pregnancy is associated with a 22% lower odds of congenital heart defects in newborn infants ( 94816 , 95466 ). Clinical research in this area is limited. One clinical study found 10 cases of congenital heart defects in the group taking prenatal vitamins prior to and during pregnancy, compared to 17 cases in the control group. Although the difference between groups was not statistically significant, it is possible that the study was underpowered ( 94813 ). Although the evidence from clinical research is limited, the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recommends folic acid-containing prenatal vitamins be taken daily during pregnancy to prevent certain birth defects such as congenital heart disease ( 94811 ).

Oral clefts.

The exact cause of oral clefts is unknown. However, the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recommends folic acid-containing prenatal vitamins be taken daily during pregnancy to prevent birth defects such as cleft palate ( 94811 ). Meta-analyses of population research have found that intake of a folic acid-containing prenatal vitamin before conception and during the first trimester of pregnancy is associated with a 26% lower odds of cleft palate in newborn infants ( 94816 , 95466 ). Although one clinical study suggests that periconceptional use of folic acid-containing prenatal vitamins does not prevent oral clefts compared to control ( 94813 ), this study did not systematically assess included patients for potential confounding risk factors of oral clefts such as chronic use of anti-epileptic drugs, smoking, etc.

Stillbirth.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows an 8% reduction in the risk for stillbirth in pregnant patients taking prenatal vitamins containing iron and folic acid when compared with those taking only iron and folic acid. This analysis is limited by the variability of the interventions used ( 94820 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Fetal and premature infant mortality.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that there is no significant difference in the risk of total fetal loss in patients taking a prenatal vitamin containing iron and folic acid when compared with patients taking only iron and folic acid. This analysis was limited by the variability of the interventions used ( 94820 ).

Miscarriage.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that there is no significant difference in risk of miscarriage in patients taking prenatal vitamins containing iron and folic acid when compared with patients taking only iron and folic acid. This analysis is limited by the variability of the interventions used ( 94820 ).

---

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

A small observational study found that use of prenatal vitamins is associated with a 21% lower risk of ADHD medication use and a 30% lower risk of hyperkinetic disorders, a severe form of ADHD ( 101352 ).

Autism spectrum disorder.

Some observational research has found that prenatal vitamin use is associated with a reduction in the risk of autism spectrum disorder. This research suggests that the dose of folic acid and the consistency and duration of prenatal vitamin use may impact outcomes. However, not all research agrees, and these findings are limited by the high heterogeneity and observational nature of the studies, which may have been confounded by parental attributes associated with voluntary prenatal vitamin use.

A meta-analysis of 5 observational studies, including 231,163 children, shows that use of prenatal vitamins during pregnancy reduces the risk of autism spectrum disorder in children by 38% when compared with non-use. Subgroup analyses based on study location, study design, and timing of prenatal vitamin supplementation also reported a lower risk of developing autism spectrum disorder in the prenatal vitamin group ( 103812 ). Conversely, a larger meta-analysis of observational studies, including 904,947 children, has found that use of prenatal vitamins during pregnancy is not associated with a reduced risk of autism spectrum disorder when compared with low- or non-use. However, a subgroup analysis of only high-quality observational and prospective studies found a reduced risk of autism with prenatal vitamin use. Additionally, studies conducted in regions without mandatory folic acid fortification suggest a stronger association between prenatal vitamin use and reduced risk of autism when compared with those conducted in regions with mandatory fortification ( 106662 ). Individual studies included in these meta-analyses have reported relative risk reductions sometimes exceeding 60% in patients using prenatal vitamins ( 101350 , 101351 , 103862 ). These meta-analyses are limited by significant heterogeneity, as well as their observational design and variability of study quality and prenatal dosing strategies. Some observational studies have evaluated prenatal vitamin use specifically in people who already had a child diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder, with mixed findings. A small observational study has found that maternal use of prenatal vitamins is associated with a 50% lower risk of the child developing autism spectrum disorder by 36 months of age. A sensitivity analysis shows that the risk was lower for those taking prenatal vitamins with an average folic acid dose of at least 600 mcg per day during the first month of pregnancy ( 103813 ). However, early supplementation with prenatal vitamins beginning in the first month of pregnancy has not been found to be significantly associated with the child's odds of developing autism spectrum disorder by 36 months of age, even after adjustment for confounding variables ( 106663 ). Frequency of prenatal vitamin use might also play a role on its effect on autism spectrum disorder. A small observational study in pregnant patients has found that more frequent use of prenatal vitamins is associated with a 76% lower odds of offspring developing autism spectrum disorder ( 94596 ). Another observational study found that using prenatal vitamins 3-5 times weekly has been associated with reduced risk of autism, while using prenatal vitamins more than 5 times weekly or 2 times or less weekly, has been associated with increased risk ( 101351 ).

Cancer.

Population research has found that maternal use of prenatal vitamins during pregnancy is associated with 39% lower odds of the offspring developing leukemia, 27% lower odds of the offspring developing a brain tumor, and 47% lower odds of the offspring developing neuroblastoma by the age of 20 years when compared with the offspring of those that did not use prenatal vitamins ( 94815 ).

Cognitive function.

Moderate to severe maternal prenatal psychological distress (PPD) at 12-16 weeks gestation has been linked to suboptimal child neurodevelopment and reduced scores on the NIH Early Childhood Cognition Battery at age 4. Daily use of prenatal vitamins in people with PPD does not improve cognitive outcomes ( 110689 ).

Low birth weight.

Population research has found that prenatal vitamin use in low income patients is associated with 41% lower odds of infants having a low birth weight when compared with those not using a prenatal vitamin ( 94814 ). Other research in low- to middle-income patients in Tanzania shows that use of multivitamins from early pregnancy through delivery improves the percentage of patients with adequate gestational weight gain from about 94% to about 97%, when compared to placebo. The relative risk for inadequate or severely inadequate weight gain is 0.95 and 0.9 respectively ( 110690 ).

Obesity.

Retrospective research suggests  that maternal intake of prenatal vitamins during pregnancy has very little effect on the risk of childhood obesity ( 94598 , 112766 ). In a large retrospective study of children aged 3 to 6.5 years who had been born small for gestational age, prenatal use of a multivitamin product alone, as reported by the mother, did not influence the risk of obesity, but did add to the benefit of prenatal folic acid in girls, but not boys. It improved the odds ratio for obesity from 0.72 with folic acid alone, to 0.67 with the combination ( 112766 ).

Pre-eclampsia.

A large observational study has found that daily use of prenatal vitamins does not reduce the odds of pre-eclampsia when compared with non-use. The results were not different between those who self-reported use of prenatal vitamins around the time of conception and those who self-reported use of prenatal vitamins beginning in early pregnancy. A subgroup analysis of overweight subjects showed prenatal vitamin use reduced the odds of pre-eclampsia; however, this subgroup analysis was limited by small size with few cases of pre-eclampsia ( 103811 ).

Preterm labor.

One observational study found that prenatal vitamin use in low income patients is associated with a 34% to 45% reduced odds of preterm delivery when compared with those that did not use a prenatal vitamin ( 94814 ). However, when this study was pooled with three other observational studies in high income countries, prenatal vitamin use was no longer associated with a reduced risk of preterm birth ( 101353 ).

Small for gestational age (SGA).

Observational research from high income countries found that prenatal vitamin use is associated with a 13% lower risk of having children that are SGA ( 101353 ).

More evidence is needed to rate prenatal vitamins for these uses.

Prenatal vitamins are typically taken orally once daily. They are intended to supplement the diet to aid pregnant patients in meeting the unique recommended dietary allowances (RDA) for this population. Deficiencies of iron, folic acid, vitamin A, and other nutrients can have serious adverse health repercussions, so these ingredients are typically included in RDA levels or higher. Other nutrients such as calcium and choline are typically included in amounts below the RDA because it is expected that patients will obtain the remainder through the diet ( 94089 , 94792 , 94811 ). There is no single standard or requirement that dictates the content of prenatal vitamins. As a result, there is wide variability among prenatal vitamin products. Not all prenatal vitamin products have all of the necessary components. For example, gummy prenatal vitamins do not contain iron. Many patients prefer gummy products because they have difficulty swallowing large prenatal vitamin pills. This can be especially problematic for patients with morning sickness. If a patient prefers to take a gummy formulation, be sure to recommend adequate intake of iron through other supplements or dietary sources. Some prenatal vitamins contain unique ingredients that might not be typically found in prenatal vitamins. In some cases, these ingredients might not be safe or safety might not be known. It is important to examine the contents of prenatal vitamin products to make sure they are appropriate for each individual patient. Up to 6% of prescription and 33% of nonprescription prenatal vitamins contain botanical ingredients, and 2% to 8% of nonprescription prenatal vitamin products contain probiotics. Up to 17% of prescription prenatal vitamins contain the stool softener drug docusate sodium 50 mg per serving. Prenatal vitamins are taken to prevent nutrient deficiencies prior to and during pregnancy, and to prevent conditions caused by nutrient deficiencies in the infant, such as neural tube defects ( 94792 ). During pregnancy, higher amounts of most nutrients are required because, in addition to the nutrients required to support normal body processes, additional nutrition is needed for the growing fetus. Taking a prenatal vitamin helps to ensure adequate nutrition to prevent complications in pregnancy and prevent adverse outcomes in the child ( 94810 ). In the U.S., pregnant patients seem to be most at risk for having a deficiency in calcium, iron, folate and vitamin D ( 94792 ). Some have a higher risk of deficiency than others. For example, vegetarians, people with limited sun exposure, and those with darker skin are at a higher risk for being deficient in vitamin D ( 94811 ). Severe deficiency in vitamin D during pregnancy can lead to rickets in the infant ( 94811 ). Prenatal vitamins contain folic acid to prevent the infant from developing neural tube defects ( 94818 ). The National Academy of Sciences recommends taking iron 27 mg daily during pregnancy, an amount present in most prenatal vitamins, to prevent iron-deficiency anemia ( 94811 ). Iron deficiency in pregnancy has been associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes and increased perinatal maternal mortality. It is important to prevent iron deficiency with adequate iron intake ( 7135 ). Miscarriage has been associated with poor diet and nutrient deficiencies. Increased oxidative stress and inadequate homocysteine metabolism are two theoretical mechanisms by which nutrient deficiencies can lead to miscarriage, pre-eclampsia, or other adverse outcomes in pregnancy ( 94820 , 103811 ). Some population research suggests that oxidative stress can lead to spontaneous miscarriage. Certain nutrients such as vitamins C and E have antioxidant activity. Therefore, supplementing with these vitamins may reduce the amount of oxidative stress in the body and therefore decrease the risk of miscarriage. Insufficient maternal and fetal homocysteine metabolism have also been associated with increased miscarriage risk. Homocysteine metabolism is regulated by folate, vitamin B6 and vitamin B12. Therefore, supplementing with folate and the B vitamins in theory can improve homocysteine metabolism and prevent miscarriage ( 94820 ).
