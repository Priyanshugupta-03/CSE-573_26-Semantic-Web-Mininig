# Juice Fasting
Other Common Names Ayuno de jugos, Juice Cleanse, Juice Detox, Juice Therapy, Juicing, Régime jus
|

---

Caution

Juice fasting is also known as juice detox and should not be confused with Detoxification .

## Overview

Juice fasting is a type of diet including only juices made from fresh fruits and vegetables. It has been popularized by many sources, including naturopathic physicians, practitioners of Ayurveda, and various books, including the 2007 book, The Juice Fasting Bible ( 103137 ).

Possibly Unsafe when followed as directed. Juice fasts are not intended to provide adequate nutrition and involve the consumption of large amounts of fluids. This can result in electrolyte imbalances when used short-term, as well as malnutrition when used long-term ( 103181 ). There are also multiple reports of serious renal injury due to juice fasting in previously healthy adults ( 103134 , 103135 , 103136 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Unsafe during pregnancy and lactation. Juice fasting is not intended to provide adequate nutrition and can result in electrolyte imbalances when used short-term, as well as malnutrition when used long-term ( 103181 ).

#### General

Juice fasts do not provide adequate nutrition and involve the consumption of large quantities of water, which can lead to electrolyte imbalances when used short-term and malnutrition when used long-term.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Orally : Renal injury.

#### Renal

There are multiple case reports of oxalate-induced kidney injury in adults who are participating in juice fasts. It is likely that this kidney injury is due in part to the significant increase in oxalate consumption, as well as the reduction in calcium intake, that occurs during a juice fast ( 103135 , 103136 ). In one case report, a 65-year-old female developed acute oxalate nephropathy that progressed to end-stage renal disease after consuming a green smoothie juice "cleanse" that was prepared by juicing oxalate-rich green leafy vegetables and fruits. Prior to the cleanse, she had normal renal function ( 103134 ). In another case report, a male developed acute renal failure after consuming a daily average of 1260 mg of oxalate from juicing for over 6 weeks. He presented to the emergency department with a high oxalate level that responded to temporary hemodialysis. Although he recovered most of his kidney function, he had a permanent reduction in glomerular filtration rate ( 103135 ). In a third case report, a 68-year-old male developed acute oxalate nephropathy and dehydration, progressing to irreversible dialysis-dependent end-stage renal disease after consuming a juice regimen prepared from multiple vegetables. Daily oxalate intake was around 1500 mg daily (10 times the normal dietary intake), with spinach, Swiss chard and beets providing the highest amounts. Renal biopsy showed severe tubular injury, with intratubular oxalate crystal deposition ( 112307 , 112308 ).

#### Other

Juice fasts do not provide adequate nutrition and involve the consumption of large amounts of fluids. This can result in electrolyte imbalances when used short-term, as well as malnutrition when followed long-term ( 103181 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Obesity.

It is unclear if juice fasting is beneficial in adults with obesity.

Although juice fasting is often promoted for weight loss, it has not been evaluated specifically for obesity. However, it has been evaluated for weight loss in healthy, non-obese adults. Preliminary clinical research in healthy adults under the age of 30 shows that following a lemon honey juice fast for 4 days reduces weight, body mass index, fat mass, and muscle mass when compared with baseline. Juice fasting also reduces serum triglycerides, but has no effect on serum cholesterol levels ( 103132 ). An exploratory study in 5 healthy male volunteers shows that following a juice fast for 8 days temporarily reduces serum total cholesterol, phospholipid, and insulin levels when compared with baseline. However, any changes in blood parameters disappear within 1 week after discontinuing the juice fast ( 103133 ). The validity of these studies is limited by the lack of comparator groups.

More evidence is needed to rate juice fasting for this use.

In general, juice fasting usually involves the consumption of a limited number of carbohydrate-derived calories, typically 150-300 daily, obtained solely from vegetable and fruit drinks. Most often, juice fasting lasts for 3-10 days ( 103135 ). These fasts may or may not limit physical activity ( 103133 ). There are many variations of the juice fast. For instance, the lemon honey juice fast involves the consumption of 300 mL of water containing half a lemon and a teaspoon of honey four times daily. Water can be consumed as desired, but physical activity is limited. This diet is typically followed for a total of 4-7 days ( 103132 ). Juice fasting can also involve the consumption of smoothies and home-made juices that contain a wide range of fruits and vegetables ( 103135 ). Proponents of juice fasting suggest that specific juices may be consumed for certain ailments. They also recommend using organic products and identifying fruit and vegetable combinations that suit each person's personal tastes ( 103137 ).

#### DIABETES

Juice fasting can affect blood glucose levels and glucose management in patients with diabetes. Juice fasts are not intended to provide balanced nutrition, and instead provide a calorie-restrictive diet comprised almost entirely of carbohydrates ( 103181 ).

#### RENAL DYSFUNCTION

People with existing renal dysfunction may be at increased risk for renal injury when juice fasting. There are multiple reports of serious oxalate-induced renal injury in otherwise healthy patients who were following a juice fast ( 103134 , 103135 , 103136 ).

None known.

Juice fasting is based on the theory that juices can enhance the body's natural detoxification process. Proponents of juice fasting believe that the body has more energy to devote to healing and repairing if it does not have to digest a meal that is high in fat, protein, or highly processed foods ( 103137 ). Proponents also believe that juice can help repair the body since fruits and vegetables are high in vitamins, minerals, trace elements, enzymes, and natural sugars. Theoretically, specific combinations of juices may alleviate specific ailments. In addition, juices contain an abundance of alkaline elements, which are purported to help normalize the acid-base balance in the blood and tissues ( 103137 ).
