## Overview

In 1919, William F. Koch MD, PhD, developed a substance he named "synthetic anti-toxin," which later became known as "glyoxylide." The treatment was marketed under the label of the "Christian Medical Research League." Koch proposed that a single toxin caused cancer, and that cancer could be prevented or reversed by removing that toxin with his anti-toxin ( 102821 ).

In 1941, the Ontario Commission in Canada stated that "a careful review of all the evidence presented at this date fails utterly, in the opinion of the Commission, to support the claim made on behalf of the Koch treatment that it is either a remedy or cure for cancer" ( 102822 ). In 1943, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) prosecuted Koch for misbranding his glyoxylide product, but he was not convicted. In 1948, after an FDA inspection of his laboratories, Koch announced that he was retiring in Brazil. Although his glyoxylide product was no longer sold in the US after this point, it continued to be sold in Mexico for an extended period of time ( 102821 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of Koch's glycoside. Use of Koch's glycoside in lieu of proven medical treatments can cause serious harm and death ( 102821 , 102822 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of Koch's glycoside in pregnancy and lactation. Avoid use.

#### General

A thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted. However, use of Koch's glycoside in lieu of proven medical treatments can cause serious harm and death.

There is insufficient reliable evidence about the effectiveness of Koch's glycoside.

Koch's glycoside has been administered intramuscularly in one or two total doses, and sometimes prophylactically every 6-12 months. The injections are reported to have been made up of one part glyoxylide to one trillion parts water ( 102821 ).

Koch believed that cancer is caused by a germ resembling the spirochete of syphilis, but this has never been validated by any scientific evidence. He claimed that use of his glyoxylate anti-toxin was the only way to remove this toxin. Additionally, he claimed that it had been shown to be effective in tuberculosis, psoriasis, leprosy, poliomyelitis, and syphilis ( 102821 ). Although Koch described glyoxylate as a "synthetic chemical compound of very definite molecular arrangement" and a "difficultly prepared synthetic structure, worked down on a recrystallization process", the chemical composition of the compound remains unknown ( 102821 ). The only evidence that Koch and his colleagues ever provided in support of Koch's glycoside were case reports and testimonies. However, upon further examination by regulatory bodies and medical associations, it became apparent that many of these cases had progressed to death or represented cases of cancer misdiagnosis ( 102821 , 102822 ).
