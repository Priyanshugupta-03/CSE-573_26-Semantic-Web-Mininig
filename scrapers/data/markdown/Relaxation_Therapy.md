# Relaxation Therapy
Other Common Names Abbreviated Muscle Relaxation Therapy, Applied Relaxation, Progressive Muscle Relaxation, Relaxation Appliquée, Relaxation Training, Relaxation Treatment, RT, Terapia de Relajación, Terapia de relajación, Therapeutic Relaxation, Thérapie de Relaxation, Thérapie de relaxation
|

---

Caution

Relaxation therapy should not be confused with Guided Imagery , Mantra Meditation , Mindfulness , Tai Chi , or Yoga .

## Overview

Relaxation therapy is a behavioral therapy used to relieve psychological stress and fatigue. It can be guided by a therapist or trainer or performed alone ( 16250 , 16251 , 16252 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Relaxation therapy has been safely used in several clinical trials ( 16249 , 16250 , 16251 , 16253 , 16254 , 16255 , 16256 , 16257 , 16258 , 98318 , 115556 , 115564 ).

PREGNANCY: Likely Safe when used appropriately. Relaxation therapy has been used in several clinical trials with apparent safety in pregnant women ( 29709 , 89634 , 89635 , 94590 , 98320 ).

LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there are no known reasons to expect adverse effects in lactating women.

#### General

Relaxation therapy is generally well tolerated; no adverse effects have been reported in clinical research.

|
Possibly Effective
Angina.

Practicing relaxation therapy may modestly improve angina frequency and exercise tolerance in patients with angina.

A meta-analysis of 27 clinical studies in patients with myocardial ischemia shows that relaxation therapy reduces the frequency of angina and improves resting heart rate, exercise tolerance, and anxiety ( 88755 ). Also, a small clinical study in females with cardiac syndrome X shows that relaxation therapy improves health-related quality of life and tolerated exertion during six minutes of walking when compared to baseline ( 88756 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Anxiety.

Practicing relaxation therapy may modestly improve symptoms of anxiety, although it might be less effective than cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) or meditation.

Meta-analyses and individual clinical studies show that relaxation therapy improves anxiety in patients with anxiety disorders and patients experiencing situational anxiety due to medical conditions such as cancer and COVID-19, medical procedures, scholastic tests, pregnancy, or other events ( 88762 , 88757 , 98318 , 103682 , 107421 , 115553 , 115555 , 115557 , 115560 , 115565 )( 115569 ). Evaluated relaxation therapies include the Benson relaxation technique, progressive muscle relaxation, and applied relaxation ( 98318 , 103682 , 115553 , 115555 , 115557 , 115560 , 115569 ). However, relaxation therapy might be less effective than meditation or CBT for certain anxiety disorders. One meta-analysis of 5 clinical studies shows that relaxation therapy is less effective than CBT in patients with panic anxiety disorder ( 88758 ). Another meta-analysis of 14 small clinical studies shows that meditation is slightly more effective than relaxation therapy for reducing anxiety ( 103686 ).

Depression.

Practicing relaxation therapy may modestly improve symptoms of depression in patients with depression, although it might be less effective than cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).

A meta-analysis of 11 clinical trials in patients with depression shows that relaxation therapy modestly reduces self-rated depressive symptoms when compared with minimal or no treatment; however, relaxation therapy does not appear to be as effective as psychological treatment approaches such as CBT ( 89603 ).

Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD).

Practicing relaxation therapy may modestly improve symptoms of anxiety in patients with GAD. However, it is unclear if it is as effective as cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).

A meta-analysis of 5 clinical studies shows that relaxation therapy is as effective as cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) for GAD ( 88758 ). However, a more recent network meta-analysis of clinical research in adults with GAD shows that relaxation therapy and CBT reduce GAD symptoms to a similar extent when compared with treatment as usual. After sensitivity analyses excluding studies with a high risk of bias, relaxation therapy was no longer associated with an improvement in GAD symptoms ( 115551 ).

Hypertension.

Practicing relaxation therapy may modestly reduce blood pressure in patients with hypertension.

A meta-analysis of 25 clinical trials in patients with hypertension shows that relaxation therapy reduces systolic blood pressure by 5.5 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by 3.5 mmHg when compared with a control ( 88773 ). While some evidence shows no benefit with relaxation therapy ( 88789 ), most clinical research confirms that relaxation therapy modestly reduces blood pressure in patients with hypertension ( 16258 , 69666 , 88774 , 88775 , 88776 , 88790 , 88791 , 88792 , 88793 , 88794 )( 88795 , 103683 , 115552 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Asthma.

Some small, low-quality clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy may modestly improve asthma symptom management; however, conflicting evidence exists.

A meta-analysis of small, low-quality clinical studies in adults with asthma shows that relaxation therapy might reduce the need for rescue inhalers but does not improve FEV1 ( 88764 , 88765 ). Another meta-analysis shows that relaxation therapy might improve peak expiratory flow rate in children with asthma; however, most of the studies included in this analysis enrolled a small number of patients and had other methodological flaws ( 88766 , 88767 ). One small crossover trial in patients with asthma shows that relaxation therapy improves airway resistance when compared with placebo but is less effective than terbutaline ( 88768 ). Another small clinical study in children with asthma shows that relaxation therapy plus a specific asthma self-management program reduces the duration of asthma attacks and improves peak expiratory flow rate when compared with pharmacological treatment alone ( 88770 , 88771 ). However, one small clinical trial in children with asthma shows that practicing relaxation therapy 20 minutes nightly before bed for 30 days is less effective for improving lung function when compared with massage ( 29520 ).

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

Although there has been interest in the practice of relaxation therapy for eczema, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of relaxation therapy for this purpose.

Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in children and adolescents with ADHD.

A small, open-label clinical study in children aged 6-12 years with ADHD and sleep problems shows that practicing progressive muscle relaxation therapy in combination with sleep hygiene training for 8 weeks does not reduce ADHD symptoms and sleep problems when compared with sleep hygiene training alone ( 115568 ). A separate small, open-label clinical study in adolescents with ADHD shows that practicing relaxation therapy daily for 10 days is less effective than massage therapy for improving hyperactivity and mood ( 29523 ). The validity of these studies is limited by the lack of blinding.

Breast cancer.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy might improve medication adherence and reduce hot flash frequency in patients with breast cancer.

One small clinical study in patients with breast cancer shows that practicing relaxation therapy for 1.5 hours weekly for 5 weeks in a group setting along with individual relaxation therapy sessions improves long-term adherence to endocrine therapy ( 107300 ). Another small clinical trial in patients with breast cancer who experience menopausal symptoms due to chemotherapy or ovarian suppression shows that practicing relaxation therapy for 10 weeks reduces hot flash frequency when compared with placebo and is as effective as escitalopram 5-10 mg daily ( 107299 ).

Burns.

It is unclear if relaxation therapy is beneficial for reducing anxiety related to pain in adults with burns.

A small clinical study in adults admitted to the burn intensive care unit shows that practicing the Bensen relaxation technique daily for 7 days in the morning before wound dressing changes reduces anxiety scores related to pain and also reduces body image dissatisfaction when compared with routine care. However, listening to nature sounds seems to reduce pain-related anxiety more than relaxation therapy ( 115565 ).

Cancer-related fatigue.

Some, but not all, clinical research suggests that relaxation therapy may reduce cancer-related fatigue.

A meta-analysis of 5 clinical studies in adults with cancer shows that practicing progressive muscle relaxation therapy for 20-30 minutes, 1-2 times daily for 1-3 months in combination with routine nursing care, modestly reduces cancer-related fatigue when compared with routine nursing care alone or combined with exercise. However, the quality of the evidence was overall low ( 115553 ). Additionally, two clinical studies in adults undergoing chemotherapy show that practicing progressive muscle relaxation twice daily for 8 weeks improves patient-reported overall sleep quality when compared with routine care ( 115570 , 115571 ). Conversely, another meta-analysis of 4 clinical studies in adults with cancer shows that progressive muscle relaxation therapy practiced daily or three times weekly for 5 days to 3 months does not reduce fatigue when compared with routine care or aerobic exercise ( 115557 ). The validity of this analysis is limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies. Relaxation therapy has also been evaluated in combination with other interventions. A small uncontrolled clinical study in adults receiving chemotherapy for breast cancer shows that use of a sleep promotion plan including relaxation therapy, sleep hygiene, stimulus control, and sleep restriction does not appear to promote sleep, but might modestly improve fatigue, when compared to baseline ( 89607 , 89608 ). It is unclear if these findings are due to relaxation therapy, other interventions, or the combination.

Cancer-related pain.

Some clinical research shows that relaxation therapy reduces pain in adults with cancer.

Two meta-analyses including a total of 6 low-quality clinical studies in adults with cancer show that progressive muscle relaxation therapy, in combination with routine nursing care, improves sleep quality and reduces pain when compared with routine nursing care alone. However, there was no improvement in quality of life ( 115553 , 115557 ). Additionally, two clinical studies in adults undergoing chemotherapy show that practicing progressive muscle relaxation twice daily for 8 weeks improves patient-reported overall sleep quality, pain severity, and dyspnea when compared with routine care ( 115570 , 115571 ). Similarly, a small clinical study in adults with cancer receiving aromatase inhibitors shows that practicing progressive muscle relaxation four times each week for 6 weeks reduces pain when compared with receiving advice on relaxation ( 115562 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

It is unclear if relaxation therapy is beneficial for reducing CINV.

A clinical study in adults with gastric cancer shows that practicing the Benson relaxation technique 10-15 minutes twice daily for 1 week before starting chemotherapy reduces acute CINV by about 29%, but does not affect delayed CINV, when compared with routine care ( 115559 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in patients with CFS.

A small clinical study in patients with CFS shows that relaxation therapy might reduce symptoms of fatigue when compared to baseline; however, relaxation therapy appears to be substantially less effective than cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) ( 16249 , 88800 ).

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in patients with COPD.

A small clinical study in patients with COPD shows that four weekly sessions of relaxation therapy improve dyspnea, anxiety, and airway obstruction when compared with sitting quietly ( 89601 ). A larger clinical study in patients with COPD shows that relaxation therapy combined with exercise training, patient education, nutritional counseling, and smoking cessation support for 3 weeks improves exercise capacity, dyspnea, and health-related quality of life when compared to baseline ( 89602 ). However, it is unclear if these findings are due to relaxation therapy, other treatments, or the combination, and the validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Dysmenorrhea.

It is unclear if relaxation therapy is beneficial for dysmenorrhea.

A small clinical study in adults with primary dysmenorrhea shows that practicing progressive muscle relaxation therapy under supervision for 20-30 minutes three times weekly, starting on the estimated date of ovulation and continuing through the next menstruation, reduces the intensity of menstrual pain and menstrual symptoms when compared with a control group receiving no intervention ( 115564 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of blinding.

Epilepsy.

Although there has been interest in the practice of relaxation therapy for epilepsy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of relaxation therapy for this purpose.

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in adults with fibromyalgia.

A small clinical study in adults with fibromyalgia shows that practicing progressive muscle relaxation therapy for 40 minutes twice weekly under the supervision of a therapist, in conjunction with twice daily home practice 5 days each week for 8 weeks, reduces pain, fatigue, perceived stress, and blood pressure when compared with conventional therapy ( 115563 ). Relaxation therapy has also been evaluated in combination with other therapies. Some small clinical studies in adults with fibromyalgia show that relaxation therapy in combination with other therapies, such as biofeedback, yoga, or stretching, modestly reduces pain and tender points when compared to baseline ( 89609 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Additionally, it is unclear if these findings are due to relaxation therapy, other interventions, or the combination.

Headache.

While some conflicting evidence exists, several small, low-quality clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy may modestly reduce the frequency and intensity of recurrent headaches in children and adults.

Meta-analyses on the effectiveness of relaxation therapy for improving headache frequency and severity in children have shown inconsistent results, with most studies having significant methodological flaws ( 89610 , 89611 ). Some small clinical studies in children with recurrent headaches shows that relaxation therapy might improve headache activity, reduce headache intensity, increase headache-free days, and reduce the need for medication when compared to baseline or attention-control approaches ( 89613 , 89614 ). Another small clinical study in children with recurrent headaches shows that relaxation therapy is as effective as amitriptyline for improving headache-related symptoms ( 89616 ). The effect of relaxation therapy on headache has also been studied in adults. One small clinical study in adults shows that relaxation therapy twice weekly for 2 weeks decreases headache frequency and disability in patients with tension headaches and decreases headache severity in patients with migraine headaches when compared with performing writing exercises ( 89612 ). Another small clinical study in adults with chronic tension headaches shows that relaxation therapy increases the number of headache-free days when compared with acupuncture ( 89615 ).

Infertility.

Relaxation therapy has only been evaluated in combination with other interventions; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A small clinical study in patients undergoing in vitro fertilization (IVF) shows that practicing relaxation therapy along with laughter therapy for 40 minutes for a total of 3-4 sessions reduces symptoms of anxiety and depression, but does not improve pregnancy rate or the number of oocytes, when compared with placebo ( 107419 ). It is unclear if these findings are due to relaxation therapy, laughter therapy, or the combination.

Insomnia.

Although there has been interest in the practice of relaxation therapy for insomnia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of relaxation therapy for this purpose.

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in adults or children with IBS.

One small clinical study in adults with IBS shows that practicing relaxation therapy for six months reduces the number of IBS-related consultations required when compared with a control ( 89619 ). However, another small clinical study in adults with IBS shows that practicing relaxation therapy plus standard care for eight weeks does not improve bowel symptoms, anxiety, or depression when compared with cognitive behavioral therapy plus standard care or standard care alone ( 89618 ). Relaxation therapy has also been evaluated in children with IBS. A small clinical study in children aged 10-15 years shows that practicing the Benson relaxation technique twice daily for 3 weeks reduces the severity of IBS symptoms by about 40% and improves quality of life ratings by about 16% when compared to baseline. A control group receiving only standard care experienced no changes in these measures ( 115567 ). The validity of this study is limited by the lack of a statistical comparison between groups.

Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in children with JIA.

A small clinical study in children with mild to moderate JIA shows that massage is more effective than relaxation therapy for improving pain, anxiety, and stress hormone levels ( 29519 ).

Kidney failure.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial for reducing pain in adults undergoing hemodialysis.

A small clinical study in adults who had been undergoing hemodialysis for at least 6 months at the time of enrollment shows that practicing the Bowen relaxation technique for 10 minutes twice daily for 8 weeks reduces pain and perceived stress scores when compared with a control group educated on progressive muscle relaxation techniques. These improvements were maintained for up to 1 month ( 115566 ). Unlike the group participating in Bowen relaxation, the group educated on progressive muscle relaxation was not encouraged to perform the technique during hemodialysis sessions; adherence to this technique during the study is unclear.

Labor pain.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy may slightly improve pain during the latent phase of labor, but not during active labor.

A meta-analysis of five, low-quality small clinical studies shows that relaxation therapy reduces pain during the latent part of labor, but it does not seem to reduce pain during the active part of labor, when compared with standard treatment alone ( 98320 ).

Meniere disease.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in patients with Meniere disease.

A small clinical study in patients with Meniere disease who are experiencing tinnitus and dizziness shows that relaxation therapy might improve hearing in the ear not primarily affected by Meniere disease, but does not improve hearing in the affected ear, when compared with baseline. Relaxation therapy seemed to be similarly effective to transcutaneous nerve stimulation ( 89643 ).

Menopausal symptoms.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy may modestly reduce hot flash frequency and improve other symptoms of menopause.

Several small clinical studies in patients with menopausal symptoms show that relaxation therapy decreases hot flash frequency and intensity and improves anxiety, depression, and sexual dysfunction when compared with attention control or baseline ( 89620 , 89622 , 89623 , 107299 ). One of these studies also shows that relaxation therapy is as effective as escitalopram 5-10 mg daily for 10 weeks for reducing hot flash frequency in patients with breast cancer whose menopausal symptoms are caused by chemotherapy or ovarian suppression ( 107299 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if relaxation therapy is beneficial for patients with MS.

A meta-analysis of 5 small clinical studies in adults with MS shows that practicing relaxation therapy at varying intervals and durations, sometimes in combination with other modalities, does not reduce self-reported pain intensity when compared with routine care, no intervention, or other therapies ( 115558 ). The validity of this analysis is limited by the heterogeneity of the relaxation techniques used and the wide variation in disease severity amongst the patient populations enrolled in the included studies. A more recent clinical study in adults with relapsing-remitting MS shows that practicing progressive muscle relaxation therapy or the Benson relaxation technique at home for one hour daily for 12 weeks reduces pain and fatigue scores at week 12, but not weeks 4 and 8, when compared with usual care ( 115561 ).

Myocardial infarction (MI).

While some conflicting evidence exists, several small clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy may modestly improve outcomes in patients recovering from MI.

Some small clinical studies in patients recovering from MI show that relaxation therapy plus exercise improves ST abnormalities, anxiety, well-being, and feelings of invalidity, leads to more pronounced training bradycardia, and reduces the risk for cardiovascular events when compared with exercise alone ( 88798 , 89626 , 89627 ). Another small clinical study in patients recovering from MI shows that participating in three sessions of relaxation therapy over two days decreases apical heart rate and cardiac complications in some patients ( 28878 ). Additionally, one small clinical study in patients with risk factors for heart disease shows that relaxation therapy might reduce the risk for ischemic heart disease and fatal MI ( 69666 ). However, some conflicting evidence exists. One small clinical study in patients recovering from MI shows that relaxation therapy does not improve the rate of cardiovascular death, re-infarction, or medical consumption when compared with control ( 89625 ). Also, a larger clinical study shows that listening to a relaxation audiotape is no more effective for improving outcomes after an acute MI than listening to music of choice in some patients ( 89628 ).

Postoperative pain.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy reduces postoperative pain. Clinical studies are conflicting and may vary depending on the type of surgery and the type of relaxation technique used.

A meta-analysis of seven small clinical studies shows that practicing various relaxation techniques modestly reduces pain after a wide range of abdominal surgeries. Relaxation techniques used in these studies included jaw relaxation, systematic relaxation, progressive muscle relaxation, and Benson's relaxation ( 103684 ). This analysis is limited by the heterogeneity of the techniques and the surgeries performed. It is unclear which technique is most effective for reducing postoperative pain, as well as whether the added pain relief would be considered clinically significant. Relaxation therapy has also been evaluated for pain after orthopedic surgeries. A clinical study in adults undergoing hip surgery shows that practicing progressive muscle relaxation, 30 minutes nightly starting the night following surgery and continuing for 3 days, does not reduce pain at a 1-month follow-up when compared with no intervention ( 115554 ). The clinical relevance of a pain score one month after hip surgery is unclear. A clinical study in adults with recent arthroscopic rotator cuff repair shows that practicing relaxation breathing exercises at home post-discharge for 2 minutes every 4 hours daily for 5 days does not reduce pain scores or opioid use when compared with no intervention ( 115556 ). The validity of this study is limited by the use of a per-protocol analysis, limited training in the relaxation technique, and variability in surgical techniques between groups.

Postpartum depression.

Relaxation therapy has only been evaluated in combination with other interventions; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A small clinical study in patients with postpartum depression shows that using relaxation therapy, music mood induction, massage therapy, and mother-infant interaction coaching along with social, educational, and vocational rehabilitation improves symptoms of depression when compared to baseline ( 89604 ). It is unclear if these findings are due to relaxation therapy, other interventions, or the combination.

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Small clinical studies suggest that relaxation therapy may modestly improve anxiety, depression, and feelings of anger or guilt in patients with PTSD.

A small clinical study in Puerto Rican patients with PTSD shows that practicing relaxation therapy for 60-90 minutes weekly for 12-15 weeks reduces PTSD-related symptoms, including anxiety and depression, when compared to baseline. Improvements in PTSD symptoms were similar to those reported in patients undergoing prolonged exposure therapy ( 107420 ). Another small clinical study in patients with PTSD shows that relaxation therapy significantly reduces feelings of anger and guilt when compared to baseline ( 16255 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Pregnancy-induced hypertension.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in patients with pregnancy-induced hypertension.

A small clinical study in pregnant patients with hypertension shows that systematic relaxation training with or without biofeedback weekly for 6 weeks lowers blood pressure and reduces hospital admission rates when compared with control ( 89635 ).

Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in patients with PMDD.

A small clinical study in patients with PMDD shows that, while massage therapy might improve PMDD-related symptoms in some patients, relaxation therapy does not improve physical or emotional symptoms when compared to baseline ( 29591 ).

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in patients with PMS.

A small clinical study in patients with PMS shows that practicing relaxation therapy for five months significantly improves physical symptoms of PMS when compared with charting and reading controls ( 83040 ).

Pre-procedural anxiety.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial for reducing pre-procedural anxiety.

A small clinical study in females undergoing breast-core needle biopsy (CNB) shows that relaxation therapy, which included playing classical music and ocean sounds during CNB, does not improve anxiety when compared with taking alprazolam 0.5 mg ( 88797 ).

Preterm labor.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy may not reduce the risk of preterm birth, although it might reduce maternal anxiety during preterm labor.

Small clinical studies in patients experiencing preterm labor show that, while relaxation therapy seems to reduce maternal anxiety, adding it to usual care does not reduce the risk of preterm birth or increase infant birth weight when compared with usual care alone ( 89634 , 94590 ).

Psychological well-being.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial for improving psychological well-being.

A small clinical study in aggressive adolescents shows that 20-minute relaxation therapy sessions twice weekly for 5 weeks does not improve feelings of anxiety or hostility when compared to baseline ( 29516 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in children or adults with RA.

A small clinical study in adults with RA shows that adding the Benson Relaxation Technique to conventional medication improves anxiety, depression, and well-being, but not clinical symptoms or laboratory measures, when compared with medication alone ( 89636 ).

Social anxiety disorder.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy may modestly improve symptoms of anxiety in patients with social anxiety disorder; however, relaxation therapy seems to be less effective than other modalities.

Some small clinical studies in patients with social anxiety disorder show that relaxation therapy improves symptoms of anxiety when compared with no treatment; however, cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) or task concentration training appear to be more effective than relaxation therapy ( 16253 , 16256 ). Another small clinical study in patients with social anxiety disorder shows that relaxation therapy does not improve anxiety symptoms when compared with conventional therapy ( 88759 ).

Stress.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in otherwise healthy patients with stress.

A small clinical study in patients in the 3rd trimester of pregnancy shows that practicing relaxation therapy for 10-15 minutes twice daily for one month along with usual care reduces stress, anxiety, and depression when compared with usual care alone ( 107421 ).

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE).

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in patients with SLE.

A small clinical study in patients with SLE shows that 12 weeks of relaxation therapy is less effective than graded exercise therapy for improving fatigue ( 89606 ).

Tinnitus.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in patients with tinnitus.

One small clinical study in patients with intrusive tinnitus shows that attending five 40-minute relaxation therapy sessions over 15 weeks and practicing relaxation therapy at home twice daily for the same duration does not improve tinnitus-associated distress and interference with activities when compared to baseline ( 96283 ).

Tourette syndrome.

It is unclear if practicing relaxation therapy is beneficial in children and adolescents with Tourette syndrome.

A small clinical study in children and adolescents with Tourette syndrome shows that individual, one-hour weekly training sessions of relaxation therapy for 6 weeks does not improve tic severity or behavior measures when compared with quiet time and awareness training ( 89644 ).

Urticaria.

Relaxation therapy has only been evaluated in combination with other interventions; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A small clinical study in patients with chronic urticaria shows that hypnosis with relaxation therapy might improve symptoms of pruritus, but not urticaria, when compared to baseline ( 89638 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Also, it is unclear if these findings are due to relaxation therapy, hypnotherapy, or the combination.

Low back pain . Small clinical studies suggest that practicing relaxation therapy may modestly improve symptoms in patients with low back pain, although it does not appear to be as effective as massage.

Small clinical studies in patients with chronic low back pain show that relaxation therapy might improve low back pain when compared to baseline or a wait-list control in the short-term; however, the effect of relaxation therapy on long-term pain control in patients with chronic low back pain is unknown ( 89630 , 103687 ). Another small clinical study in patients with chronic low back pain shows that massage therapy 30 minutes twice weekly for 5 weeks is more effective than relaxation therapy for improving low back pain, depression, anxiety, and sleep ( 29533 ).

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . Small clinical studies suggest that relaxation therapy may modestly improve some symptoms in adults with TMD, although it may be most beneficial when used in combination with cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).

A British Medical Journal clinical practice guideline strongly recommends cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), with or without relaxation therapy or biofeedback, for managing chronic pain associated with TMD among adult patients ( 115549 ). This guideline was informed by a network meta-analysis evaluating nearly 60 different interventions in adults with chronic pain associated with TMD. The analysis found, with moderate certainty, that CBT augmented with biofeedback or relaxation therapy is among the most effective of the conservative interventions for pain relief. When relaxation therapy is evaluated alone, very low-certainty evidence suggests that it may decrease pain when compared with sham procedures. However, its potential efficacy is rated in the bottom third of interventions, with jaw mobilization and trigger point therapy having the most evidence of efficacy behind augmented CBT ( 115550 ).

More evidence is needed to rate relaxation therapy for these uses.

There are various types of relaxation techniques. Generally, patients in relaxation therapy are taught a handful of skills and methods to achieve relaxation and instructed to practice them on a regular basis. Relaxation can be achieved using methods including imagery, breathing exercises, focused muscle tensing and relaxing, cue-controlled relaxation, and others. Relaxation therapy is often practiced in combination with other interventions including guided imagery and biofeedback ( 16252 , 96283 ). Two commonly studied techniques include Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR) and the Benson Relaxation Technique. With PMR, the patient tenses particular muscle groups, often while inhaling and holding the breath, and then relaxes the muscles while exhaling. Patients are often instructed to observe how the muscles feel during the process. With BRT, the patient relaxes the whole body while focusing on breathing and the silent repetition of a single word ( 115559 , 115561 , 115567 , 115570 ).

None known.

None known.

Relaxation therapy is a behavioral therapy used to relieve psychological distress and fatigue. It is thought that psychological distress results in increased sympathetic nervous system tone, which could have a potentially adverse effect on disease processes. Relaxation therapy is thought to decrease sympathetic tone, which slows the heart rate, decreases blood pressure. This is thought to decrease anxiety, improve mood, and impart a sense of control ( 16250 , 16251 , 16252 , 115563 ). Relaxation therapy can be conducted with a therapist or trainer or as a self-practice.
