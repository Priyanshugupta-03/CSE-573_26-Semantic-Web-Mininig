## Overview

There is no single accepted definition for "functional foods." Rather, multiple agencies have developed their own definitions for this group of foods and food products. The Food and Nutrition Board of the National Academy of Sciences defines functional foods as "any modified food or food ingredient that may provide a health benefit beyond that of the traditional nutrients it contains" ( 102931 ). The International Life Sciences Institute defines them as "foods that, by virtue of the presence of physiologically active components, provide a health benefit beyond basic nutrition." And the American Dietetic Association (ADA) defines them as foods that are "whole, fortified, enriched, or enhanced." However, the ADA also adds the caveat that these foods must be consumed as "part of a varied diet on a regular basis, at effective levels" for consumers to experience any health benefits ( 102930 ). These definitions are relatively vague and open to interpretation. This has sometimes led to disagreement amongst scientists and healthcare professionals regarding which foods or food products should be labeled as functional foods ( 102931 ). However, there is agreement that functional foods can be derived from animals or plants. Additionally, their supposed beneficial qualities can occur naturally, or through fortification or genetic modification ( 102930 ). Functional foods are increasingly popular in the US. In fact, functional foods are reported as one of the fastest growing segments of the food economy in the US. Additionally, Japan, Canada, and the United Kingdom have devoted major health initiatives to research the safety and effectiveness of functional foods ( 102930 , 102931 ).

Functional foods are intended to be consumed in moderation. Consuming too much of any one food can lead to undesired health consequences, such as nutrient levels that are either too high or too low. Additionally, functional foods are intended to be incorporated into a varied and well-rounded diet ( 102920 , 102921 ). Foods that are commonly classified as functional foods cover a wide spectrum of dietary options. Some foods are said to be inherent functional foods, such as green tea, which has antioxidants, and salmon, which contains omega-3 fatty acids. Some functional foods are created through processing or fortification. For example, foods may be fortified to include more fiber or calcium. Additionally, some functional foods are in the form of nutrition bars and contain herbs, supplements, vitamins, minerals, and or/protein. In addition to nutrition bars, fermented foods are also commonly included in the functional food category. A common example is yogurt, which often contains live bacterial cultures known as probiotics ( 102920 , 102921 ).

Functional foods are foods or food products which are believed to provide additional health benefits to those consuming them. These health benefits are most often purported to result from increased intake of a specific nutrient or set of nutrients via a food or food product that contains high concentrations of those nutrients ( 102920 ). Some foods that have been classified as functional foods have been evaluated in clinical research, whereas others have not. See specific monographs for more information.
