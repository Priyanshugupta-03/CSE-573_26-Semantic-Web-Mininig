## Overview

Job's tears is a grain-bearing perennial plant grown in China, Japan, Korea, Taiwan, and other Asian countries. It is considered a nutritious health food in many Asian countries, and has a history of use in traditional Chinese medicine ( 15362 , 15363 , 15369 ). The seeds of Job's tears are tear-shaped and come in several colors including yellow, brown, white, and purple. The seeds are sometimes used as ornamental beads.

## People Use This For

Orally, Job's tears is used for allergic rhinitis, aphthous ulcers, hyperlipidemia, cancer, warts, toxoplasmosis, osteoarthritis, neuralgia, obesity and weight loss, and respiratory tract infections.

Possibly Safe when consumed orally and appropriately as a food. Job's tears has been safely eaten in amounts up to 60 grams daily for 4 weeks ( 15368 ). There is not enough information available to know if Job's tears extracts from the root or seed are safe when taken as a supplement.

PREGNANCY: Possibly Unsafe when used orally. In animal models, Job's tears seed extract induces embryo toxicity and increases uterine contractions during pregnancy ( 15373 ); avoid using during pregnancy.

LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Orally, Job's tears is generally well tolerated when consumed as a food ( 15368 ). There is no good scientific evidence on the safety and adverse effects of Job's tears taken orally in medicinal amounts.

#### Dermatologic

Oral use of Job's tears has been associated with Baboon syndrome -- a condition characterized by a red and itchy rash most often located on the buttocks. In one case report, a 53-year-old woman taking a specific Job's tears-containing supplement (Ibane, JW Pharm) from South Korea experienced severe red and itchy skin around her trunk and groin area. While the supplement also contains riboflavin, pyridoxine, ascorbic acid, nicotinamide, biotin, and L-cysteine, Job's tears was thought to be the cause of this patient's reaction. After two weeks of not taking the supplement the patient's skin returned to normal ( 95438 ).

Hyperlipidemia.

Preliminary evidence shows that consuming Job's tears 60 grams daily in place of rice for 4 weeks can significantly lower total and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol in patients with hyperlipidemia ( 15368 ). More evidence is needed to rate Job's tears for this use.

#### Adult
Oral:

Research is limited; typical dosing is unavailable.

#### ANTIDIABETES DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Preliminary evidence shows that constituents of Job's tears might have hypoglycemic effects ( 15363 ). Theoretically, concomitant use with drugs that decrease blood glucose levels might increase the risk of hypoglycemia. Some antidiabetes drugs include glimepiride (Amaryl), glyburide (DiaBeta, Glynase PresTab, Micronase), insulin, pioglitazone (Actos), rosiglitazone (Avandia), chlorpropamide (Diabinese), glipizide (Glucotrol), tolbutamide (Orinase), and others.

#### CHLORZOXAZONE (Parafon Forte, Paraflex)
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Animal research suggests that Job's tears might enhance absorption of chlorzoxazone in the small intestine. Single dose and short-term oral administration of Job's tears bran ethanolic extract along with oral administration of a five-drug cocktail containing chlorzoxazone increases chlorzoxazone peak plasma concentration and area under the plasma concentration-time curve (AUC) without altering major cytochrome P450 activities in the liver ( 106531 ). This effect has not been reported in humans.

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOGLYCEMIC POTENTIAL

Laboratory research suggests that constituents of Job's tears might have hypoglycemic effects ( 15363 ). Theoretically, concomitant use with other herbs and supplements that decrease blood glucose levels might increase the risk of hypoglycemia. Some of these products include bitter melon, cowhage, ginger, glucomannan, goat's rue, fenugreek, kudzu, willow bark, and others.

#### PERIOPERATIVE

Job's tears might affect blood glucose levels ( 15363 ). Theoretically, Job's tears might interfere with blood glucose control during and after surgical procedures. Tell patients to discontinue Job's tears at least 2 weeks before elective surgical procedures.

None known.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the presentation or treatment of overdose with Job's tears.

#### General

The applicable parts of Job's tears are the root and the seeds. The root contains benzoxazinoid compounds ( 15361 ).The seed consists of 8.5% water, 17.6% protein, 52% starch, and 2.3% ash ( 15362 ). The seed also contains about 3% to 7% oil, which consists of about 1.6% phytosterols such as sitosterol, sitosterone, campersterol, and camperstanol ( 15362 , 15364 , 15372 ). The seed oils include palmitic, stearic, oleic, and linoleic fatty acids ( 15371 ). The seeds also contain 3 to 5 times the amount of B and E vitamins found in rice ( 111468 ).

#### Anti-obesity effects

In overweight or obese young adults, replacing refined grains with powdered Job's tears seeds, 60 grams daily at any meal for 6 weeks, decreases the percentage body fat, and total cholesterol and triglyceride levels when compared with baseline, but does not change body weight. Job's tears seed also lowers the inflammatory markers, tumor necrosis factor alpha and interleukins 6 and 10, and the marker of oxidative stress, malondialdehyde ( 111468 ).

#### Antidiabetic effects

Job's tears seeds have hypoglycemic activity in animal models, which is thought to be due to the coixan A, B, and C constituents 15363). In a mouse model of nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), extracts of Job's tears seeds improve hyperglycemia, glucose tolerance, and insulin resistance ( 106512 ). Preliminary research in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that taking a porridge prepared from dried Job's tears seeds mixed with yogurt once daily for 12 weeks reduces plasma glycated albumin levels when compared with baseline ( 111465 ).

#### Antihypertensive effects

Research in hypertensive animals shows that adding 12% or 24% dehulled Job's tears seeds to the diet for 12 weeks limits further increases in blood pressure, when compared with a regular diet. In overweight or obese humans, eating 60 grams of dehulled Job's tears seeds daily for 6 weeks decreases systolic blood pressure, especially in those with high pressures at baseline. Possible mechanisms of an antihypertensive effect include reduction in levels of endothelin-1, a mediator of vasoconstriction and salt and water retention, and increased magnesium levels, stimulating prostacyclin and nitric oxide production to cause vasodilation ( 111220 ).

#### Antimicrobial effects

Powdered seed extract of Job's tears is thought to have activity against cutaneous warts. It's theorized that it stimulates cytotoxic activity against viral diseases ( 15362 ). Job's tears seed extract seems to have biostatic in vitro activity against the parasite Toxoplasma gondii, which causes toxoplasmosis. Components of Job's tears seed appear to activate macrophages against Toxoplasma gondii ( 15362 ). Some evidence shows that taking Job's tears seed in tablet form can increase cytotoxic T-lymphocyte and natural killer cell activity in animal models ( 15363 ).

#### Antineoplastic effects

Some extracts of Job's tears seed have anticancer activity. In animal and vitro studies, a methanolic extract of Job's tears seed inhibited lung cancer cell growth through cell cycle arrest and apoptosis; however, a water extract did not have this activity ( 15365 ). A methanolic extract of Job's tears seed also inhibited cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) expression, but not COX-1 expression, in lung cancer cells in animal and in vitro studies ( 15367 ). In an animal model of colon cancer, hulled Job's tears seed extract also inhibited COX-2 expression, but did not prevent the formation of colon tumors ( 15369 ). A lipid extracted from Job's tears seed also inhibited pancreatic cancer cell growth in vitro through cell cycle arrest and apoptosis ( 15371 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

Several constituents of Job's tears have antioxidant activity, including syringic acid, ferulic acid, syringaresinol, 4-ketopinoresinol, and the lignan mayuenolide ( 15368 ).

#### Gastrointestinal effects

Animal research shows that ingesting hulled Job's tears seed seems to alter the culture counts of enteric micro-organisms. Animals ingesting Job's tears appear to have higher cultures of lactic acid-producing bacteria and fewer enteric bacteria ( 15363 ). Human research in healthy adult males shows that consuming cooked Job's tears seeds, 106 grams daily for 7 days, increases intestinal Faecalibacterium abundance and alters the ratio of the different types of peripheral lymphocytes (natural killer, memory, helper and regulatory T-cells) ( 111467 ).

#### Hepatic effects

Research using fatty liver cell cultures or animal models of  nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), shows that dehulled Job's tears seeds, and extracts from them, reduce NAFLD progression by attenuating hepatic fat deposition, improving liver function, insulin resistance and steatosis, and reducing inflammation. Hepatic triglyceride and cholesterol levels, adipose leptin levels, and plasma fasting glucose levels are lowered. Downregulation of fatty acid synthesis and upregulation of fatty acid beta-oxidation occurs, along with decreased levels of C-reactive protein, tumor necrosis factor-alpha and interleukins 1-beta and 6. Increased plasma and adipose adiponectin levels contribute to the improved insulin sensitivity and reduced lipid accumulation in the liver ( 106512 , 111469 ).

#### Immunologic activity

There is interest in Job's tears for allergies due to suspected anti-inflammatory activity. Job's tears root contains benzoxazinoid compounds, some of which might inhibit the release of histamine from mast cells in vitro ( 15361 ). Additionally, in animal models, Job's tears hulled seed extract decreases antigen-stimulated production of IgE antibodies. A methanolic extract appears to have greater activity than a water extract ( 15366 ).

#### Lipid effects

In a mouse model of nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), extracts of Job's tears seeds attenuate increases in total and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, and triglycerides ( 15368 , 106512 ). Preliminary research in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that taking a porridge prepared from dried Job's tears seeds mixed with yogurt once daily for 12 weeks reduces total and LDL cholesterol levels, and increases high density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels when compared with baseline ( 111465 ). Other research in healthy individuals shows that consuming 40-55 grams Job's tears seed powder as the sole carbohydrate source for breakfast, daily for 12 weeks, reduces total and HDL cholesterol, and triglycerides levels when compared with baseline ( 111466 ). Constituents of Job's tears that may contribute to lipid lowering effects include polyphenols, phytosterols, and fiber, which includes 32% slowly digestible or resistant starch ( 111466 ).

#### Metabolic effects

There is interest in Job's tears for weight loss. In animal models of obesity, a Job's tears seed extract reduces food intake, body weight, cholesterol levels, adipose tissue mass, and leptin in animals fed a high fat diet ( 15370 ). Job's tears oil also appears to reduce LDL cholesterol, insulin, and leptin in animal models ( 15372 ).
