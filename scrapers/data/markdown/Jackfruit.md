## Overview

The jackfruit tree is native to southwestern India but is now widely cultivated in other countries, including Sri Lanka, Malaysia, Burma, Indonesia, East Africa, Jamaica, and Brazil ( 90490 ). The wood of the tree is used for furniture and musical instruments, and the fruit and seeds are consumed as food ( 90491 ). Jackfruit leaves, roots, bark, and latex have been used for medicinal purposes in Ayurveda ( 98374 , 98375 , 98396 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of jackfruit.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid amounts in excess of those found in foods.

#### General

There is currently a limited amount of information on the adverse effects of jackfruit.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Orally : Serious allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis, in sensitive individuals.

#### Immunologic

Orally, the fruit of jackfruit might cause serious allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis, in sensitive individuals ( 98378 , 112050 ). In one case report, a 34-year-old female developed anaphylaxis with symptoms of cough, dyspnea, facial swelling, and urticaria after eating 10 pieces of dried jackfruit. There is speculation that latex allergy might result in a cross-reactivity to jackfruit ( 98378 ). In another case report, a 21-year-old male developed anaphylaxis with symptoms of ocular pruritis, periorbital swelling, dyspnea, and urticaria 15 minutes after eating a small piece of dried jackfruit. The patient had a history of latex allergy and tested positive for jackfruit and latex allergies upon skin prick testing ( 112050 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Diabetes.

Small clinical studies suggest that oral jackfruit flour or leaf extract might improve glycemic control in patients with diabetes.

A small clinical study in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that replacing rice or wheat flour in the diet with green jackfruit flour 30 grams daily for 12 weeks reduces glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by 0.27%, fasting blood glucose by around 13 mg/dL, and postprandial blood glucose by around 28 mg/dL when compared with placebo flour ( 112155 ). Another small clinical study in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that taking jackfruit leaf extract 10 mL/kg one hour prior to an oral glucose tolerance test attenuates the percentage increase in blood glucose at 1.5 and 2 hours after eating when compared with a control ( 26885 ). The jackfruit extract was prepared by boiling 200 grams of jackfruit leaves in one liter of water and then reducing the volume of the extract to 200 mL.

Wound healing.

Although there has been interest in using topical jackfruit for wound healing, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of jackfruit for this purpose.

More evidence is needed to rate jackfruit for these uses.

Oral:

Research is limited; typical dosing is unavailable.

#### ANTICOAGULANT/ANTIPLATELET DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Theoretically, jackfruit latex might increase the risk of bleeding when taken with anticoagulant or antiplatelet drugs.

Jackfruit latex, which is the sap from jackfruit bark, contains chemicals that might increase the risk of bleeding. In vitro research shows that jackfruit latex contains a glycoprotein constituent that prolongs the activated partial thrombin time (aPTT) and reduces the activity of blood coagulation factors XIa and alpha-XIIa ( 98376 ). Jackfruit latex also contains a protease constituent that has been shown to have fibrinolytic activity in vitro ( 98377 ). Other parts of jackfruit, including the fruit and leaves, have not been shown to contain chemicals with fibrinolytic and anticoagulant activity.

---

#### ANTIDIABETES DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Theoretically, jackfruit might increase the risk of hypoglycemia when taken with antidiabetes drugs.

Small clinical studies show that jackfruit flour and leaf extract can reduce fasting and postprandial blood glucose ( 26885 , 112155 ). Monitor blood glucose levels closely. Dose adjustments might be necessary.

---

#### CNS DEPRESSANTS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Theoretically, jackfruit seeds might cause additive sedation when taken with CNS depressants.

Animal research shows that jackfruit seeds can cause sedation ( 26886 ).

#### ANTICOAGULANT/ANTIPLATELET HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS

Theoretically, jackfruit latex might have anticoagulant effects.

Taking jackfruit latex, which is the sap from jackfruit bark, with other products that increase the risk of bleeding might have additive effects ( 98376 , 98377 ). This potential interaction applies only to jackfruit latex and not other jackfruit plant parts. See products with anticoagulant activity here . See products with antiplatelet activity here .

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOGLYCEMIC POTENTIAL

Theoretically, jackfruit might have hypoglycemic effects.

Taking jackfruit with other products with hypoglycemic potential might increase the risk of hypoglycemia. Small clinical studies show that jackfruit flour and leaf extract can reduce fasting and postprandial blood glucose ( 26885 , 112155 ). See other products with hypoglycemic potential here .

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH SEDATIVE PROPERTIES

Theoretically, jackfruit seeds might have sedative effects.

Taking jackfruit seeds with other products with sedative properties might cause additive sedation. Animal research shows that jackfruit seeds can cause sedation ( 26886 ). See other products with sedative properties here .

#### CROSS-ALLERGENICITY

Jackfruit might cause an allergic reaction in individuals sensitive to birch pollen or latex ( 26887 , 98378 , 112050 ). Until more is known, avoid or use with caution in individuals with allergies to birch pollen or latex.

#### PERIOPERATIVE

Jackfruit seeds might have sedative effects ( 26886 ), and jackfruit latex might have anticoagulant effects. Theoretically, jackfruit seeds might cause additive CNS depression and jackfruit latex might cause excessive bleeding if used perioperatively. Tell patients to discontinue jackfruit seeds and latex at least 2 weeks before elective surgical procedure.

None known.

#### General

The applicable parts are the fruit, seeds, leaves, latex, bark, and roots of jackfruit. The seeds and fruit have been used for food, while the leaves and roots have been used orally for medicinal purposes. Jackfruit latex and bark are usually used topically for medicinal purposes ( 98374 , 98375 , 98396 ). Jackfruit seeds have been shown to contain about 5% protein and 11% dietary fiber ( 98375 ). Jacalin, a lectin isolated from jackfruit seeds, is considered the major protein of the seeds ( 26880 , 26882 , 26888 , 26889 , 26890 , 26891 ). Jackfruit seeds have also been shown to contain potassium, calcium, manganese and magnesium ( 26892 , 98376 ). The flesh of jackfruit is a source of carbohydrates, vitamin A, and also fiber and protein ( 98375 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

There is interest in using jackfruit for inflammatory disorders. Preliminary research shows that certain phenolic constituents in jackfruit such norartocarpetin, oxyresveratrol, and especially artocarpesin have in vitro anti-inflammatory effects, such as reducing protein expression of cyclooxygenase 2 (COX-2) ( 98374 ).

#### Antibacterial activity

Traditionally, jackfruit bark is made into poultices to be used topically, while jackfruit leaves are heated and placed on wounds to prevent infection and promote healing. In vitro evidence suggests that extracts of jackfruit bark, wood, leaves, fruit, and seeds have antibacterial effects ( 26877 , 98396 ).

#### Antidiabetic effects

In traditional Ayurvedic medicine, the hot water extract of mature jackfruit leaves is used to reduce blood sugar in diabetes ( 98375 ). Preliminary clinical research shows that taking jackfruit leaf extract orally can attenuate postprandial percentage increases in blood glucose in patients with diabetes ( 26885 ). Additional preliminary clinical research shows that jackfruit meal has a low glycemic index. It contains compounds such as lectin which can bind glucose slowing its absorption or digestion resulting in a lower glycemic response ( 98375 ).

#### Antifungal activity

In vitro research shows that jackin, a chitin-binding lectin from jackfruit, inhibits the growth of Fusarium moniliforme and Saccharomyces cerevisiae ( 26382 ). However, other in vitro research suggests that extracts of jackfruit bark, wood, leaves, fruit, and seeds do not have antifungal activity ( 26877 ).

#### Antiviral activity

In vitro research shows that a jackfruit lectin has antiviral activity against herpes simplex virus type 2, varicella-zoster virus, and cytomegalovirus ( 26879 ).

#### Gastrointestinal effects

Animal research suggests that polysaccharides extracted from jackfruit pulp have protective effects on intestinal barrier function, possibly by inhibiting inflammatory genes, increasing the expression of occludin, a protein involved in the regulation of the intestinal barrier, and activating signaling pathways associated with short-chain fatty acid production ( 112154 ).

#### Hematological effects

There is interest in using jackfruit latex to reduce the formation of clots in disorders such as strokes, pulmonary embolus, and deep vein thrombosis. In vitro studies suggest that jackfruit milky latex contains a glycoprotein constituent that might prolong the activated partial thrombin time (aPTT) by inhibiting serine protease and reducing the activity of blood coagulation factors XIa and alpha-XIIa ( 98376 ). Jackfruit latex also contains a protease constituent that has been shown to have fibrinolytic activity in vitro ( 98377 ). Other in vitro research suggests that jackin, a lectin isolated from jackfruit seeds, induces hemagglutination in human and rabbit erythrocytes ( 26382 ).

#### Immunological activity

In vitro research suggests that various lectins extracted from jackfruit, particularly the seeds, exhibit immunostimulative effects, mainly with respect to lymphoblastic transformation and mitogenic stimulation, often by stimulating CD4+ T lymphocytes and natural killer cells ( 26879 , 26880 , 26881 , 26882 , 26883 , 26884 ).
