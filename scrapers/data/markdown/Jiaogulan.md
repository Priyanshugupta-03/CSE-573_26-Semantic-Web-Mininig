# Jiaogulan
Scientific Name Gynostemma pentaphyllum (synonyms: Gynostemma pedatum, Vitis pentaphylla) Family Cucurbitaceae Other Common Names Adaptogène, Adaptogen, Amachazuru, Dungkulcha, Fairy Herb, Ginseng du Sud, Gynostemma, Herbe de l'Immortalité, Immortality Herb, Jiao Chu Lan, Jiao Gu Lan, Miracle Grass, Penta Tea, Plante de l'Immortalité, Southern Ginseng, Thé de l'Immortalité, Xian Cao, Xianxao
|

---

Caution

Jiaogulan is sometimes called Miracle Plant, which should not be confused with Aloe (Miracle Grass), Gymnema (Miracle Plant), or Miracle Fruit . Jiaogulan is also sometimes called Southern Ginseng, which should not be confused with other plants that are referred to as ginseng, including American Ginseng , Blue Cohosh , Canaigre , Codonopsis , Eleuthero , Panax Ginseng , and Panax Notoginseng .

## Overview

Jiaogulan is a plant that grows throughout many parts of Asia including Vietnam, Japan, Malaysia, and China ( 94053 , 94054 , 94055 ).

Possibly Safe when used orally and appropriately, short-term. Tea containing jiaogulan 3 grams has been taken twice daily with apparent safety for up to 3 months ( 94054 , 95519 , 95520 ). Jiaogulan extract has been used with apparent safety at a dose of up to 450 mg daily for up to 4 months ( 7069 , 7070 , 57056 , 94058 , 100961 , 106651 ). An in vitro study suggests that low, medium, and high doses of jiaogulan polysaccharides are safe in a model of the human intestinal epithelial barrier ( 110700 ).

PREGNANCY: Possibly Unsafe when used orally. Ginsenoside Rb1, which is identical to the jiaogulan constituent gypenoside 3, has teratogenic effects in animal models; avoid using ( 10447 ).

LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

|

#### General

Orally, jiaogulan seems to be well tolerated when used for up to 4 months.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Orally : Diarrhea and nausea.

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, jiaogulan may cause diarrhea and nausea ( 6 , 106651 ). Dry mouth has also been reported with oral jiaogulan use ( 106651 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Orally, jiaogulan may cause dizziness and insomnia ( 106651 ).

Possibly Effective
Diabetes.

Oral jiaogulan extract powder may improve glycemic control in patients newly diagnosed with type 2 diabetes when used alone or with diabetes medications.

A meta-analysis of two small clinical trials in adults with newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes shows that taking jiaogulan whole plant extract powder, 1.5 grams dissolved in 60 mL of room temperature water, twice daily before meals for 8-12 weeks reduces glycated hemoglobin by 1%, fasting blood glucose by 29 mg/dL, and post-prandial blood glucose by 80 mg/dL when compared with green tea ( 111503 ). In one of the trials, both the jiaogulan and the placebo groups were also started on a sulfonylurea 4 weeks prior to the study and remained on it throughout the trial ( 95519 ). In the other trial no participants were taking diabetes medications ( 95520 ). Additionally, a very small cross-over trial that was not included in the aforementioned meta-analysis shows this same jiaogulan protocol reduces fasting blood glucose and improves insulin sensitivity at 4 weeks when compared with green tea in adults newly diagnosed with type 2 diabetes ( 94054 ). The washout period was 2 weeks, and switching to placebo reversed the improvements in glycemic control. All three clinical trials above were conducted in Vietnam by the same research group with the same jiaogulan extract product and protocol ( 94054 , 95519 , 95520 ). The validity of these findings may be limited by the use of a potentially non-inert green tea placebo, as green tea itself has been studied for its effects on preventing and treating diabetes.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Aging.

Although there has been interest in using oral jiaogulan for aging, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of jiaogulan for this purpose.

Athletic performance.

It is unclear if oral jiaogulan is beneficial for improving athletic performance.

One very small, cross-over clinical trial in generally healthy, untrained, young adult males in Australia shows that taking 450 mg of jioagulan dried leaf extract daily for 4 weeks reduces the time to complete a 20 kilometer time trial by approximately 4% (112 seconds), but does not reduce time to exhaustion, when compared with placebo. ( 115476 ).

Cancer.

Although there has been interest in using oral jiaogulan for preventing or treating cancer, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of jiaogulan for this purpose.

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

Although there has been interest in using oral jiaogulan for CVD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of jiaogulan for this purpose.

Hypercholesterolemia.

Small clinical studies suggest that oral jiaogulan extract may lower cholesterol levels in patients with hypercholesterolemia.

Two small clinical studies in patients with hypercholesterolemia show that taking jiaogulan extract can decrease total cholesterol and increase the high-density lipoprotein (HDL)/total cholesterol ratio ( 7069 , 7070 ). The dose of jiaogulan extract used in one study was 10 mg three times daily ( 7069 ).

Hypertension.

Although there has been interest in using oral jiaogulan for hypertension, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of jiaogulan for this purpose.

Memory.

Although there has been interest in using oral jiaogulan for memory, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of jiaogulan for this purpose.

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

It is unclear if oral jiaogulan extract is beneficial in patients with NAFLD.

One small clinical study in patients with NAFLD shows that taking jiaogulan extract three times daily for 4 months in conjunction with controlled dieting does not improve markers of liver function, body mass index, cholesterol levels, kidney function, or glucose levels when compared with placebo ( 57056 ).

Obesity.

Small clinical studies suggest that oral jiaogulan extract may slightly reduce body weight in patients who are overweight or obese.

Two small clinical studies in adult patients who are overweight or obese show that taking jiaogulan extract 225 mg twice daily for 12-16 weeks reduces body weight by 1.3 kg and body mass index (BMI) by around 0.4 kg/m2 when compared with placebo ( 94058 , 106651 ).

Respiratory tract infections.

Although there has been interest in using oral jiaogulan for various respiratory tract infections, including bronchitis, rhinosinusitis, and the common cold, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of jiaogulan for these conditions.

Stress.

It is unclear if oral jiaogulan extract is beneficial in patients with chronic stress.

One small clinical study in healthy adults with self-perceived chronic stress and anxiety at baseline, but without a confirmed diagnosis of anxiety, depression, or other mental health disorders, shows that taking jiaogulan extract 200 mg twice daily for 8 weeks while being exposed to repetitive stressful tasks decreases anxiety scores by almost 17% when compared with placebo. While no subjective assessments of stress were included, salivary cortisol levels and other stress markers did not improve with jiaogulan when compared with placebo ( 100961 ).

More evidence is needed to rate jiaogulan for these uses.

#### Adult
Oral:

Jiaogulan extract is most often used in doses of 200-225 mg twice daily for 8-16 weeks. See Effectiveness section for condition-specific information.

#### Standardization & Formulation

Most jiaogulan products are standardized by the amount of gypenosides or saponins they contain. However, jiaogulan contains a wide variation of gypenosides which makes standardizing according to specific gypenosides difficult ( 6 , 94053 ). In one study, an extract of jiaogulan called actiponin (TG Biotech), contained 1.1% w/w of a saponin known as damulin A ( 94058 ). In another study, an extract of jiaogulan was standardized to 1.4-2.1 mg/gram of a flavonol glycoside known as ombuoside ( 100961 ).

#### AUTOIMMUNE DISEASES

Theoretically, jiaogulan might exacerbate autoimmune diseases by stimulating disease activity. Advise patients with autoimmune diseases such as multiple sclerosis (MS), systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), rheumatoid arthritis (RA), or others to avoid or use jiaogulan with caution. Clinical and animal studies suggest that jiaogulan can stimulate the immune system ( 6 , 94056 ).

#### BLEEDING DISORDERS

Theoretically, jiaogulan might increase the risk of bleeding due to its antiplatelet effects. Until more is known, use with caution in patients with bleeding disorders. In vitro research suggests that jiaogulan has antiplatelet effects ( 7071 ).

#### PERIOPERATIVE

Jiaogulan has antiplatelet effects ( 7071 ), which might cause excessive bleeding if used perioperatively. Tell patients to discontinue jiaogulan at least 2 weeks before elective surgical procedures.

None known.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the presentation or treatment of overdose with jiaogulan.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the pharmacokinetics of jiaogulan.

#### General

The applicable parts of jiaogulan are the aerial parts ( 94053 ). The leaves of jiaogulan contain a large number of triterpene saponins known as gypenosides ( 57052 , 57053 , 57054 , 57055 , 57057 , 94053 , 94056 , 106650 ). There are over 189 different saponins in jiaogulan ( 94056 ). Many of the gypenosides are identical to the ginsenosides found in Panax ginseng. The amount of saponins in the leaves of jiaogulan is similar to the amount found in ginseng roots ( 6 ). Other components of jiaogulan include flavonoids, sterols, polysaccharides, polyphenols, phenolic acids, carotenoids, malonic acid, lutein, palmitic acid, linolenic acids, amino acids, and trace elements such as copper, iron, zinc, and others ( 94053 , 94056 , 106650 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

In an in vitro study, gypenoside XLIX extracted from jiaogulan inhibited nuclear factor-kappaB activation via a peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-alpha-dependent ( 57055 ). Animal research shows that jiaogulan extract reduces inflammatory cytokines and improves recovery of motor function after lipopolysaccharide-induced inflammation ( 106650 ). Additionally, in an animal model of  non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, high dose jiaogulan extract reduces inflammatory cytokines via the inhibition of inflammatory pathways ( 115477 ).

#### Anticancer effects

In vitro, gypenosides extracted from jiaogulan decreased cancer cell viability, arrested the cell cycle, and induced apoptosis in human colon cancer cells, and induced apoptosis in human hepatoma cells ( 57051 , 57052 , 57057 , 94056 ). Also in vitro, various saponins found in the aerial parts of jiaogulan have exhibited cytotoxic activities against human leukemia cells, prostate, stomach, and hepatocellular cancer cell lines. Antiproliferative effects have also been shown in vitro against human lung cancer, glioblastoma, and breast cancer cells ( 57060 , 94056 ). In mice, gypenosides have reduced the size of oral solid tumors and suppressed advanced sarcoma tumor growth, as well as increased survival and enhanced immune responses. Gypenosides have enhanced the effects of 5-fluorouracil in colorectal cancer, and may inhibit tumorigenesis ( 94056 ). In exploratory clinical studies, jiaogulan has been associated with reduced cancer relapse and metastasis rates, as well as reduced mortality over 5 years when compared to control. In breast cancer, jiaogulan may increase natural killer cell activity. It may also improve immune function in patients that recently received chemotherapy ( 94056 ).

#### Antidiabetic effects

Preliminary laboratory research suggests that jiaogulan extract might lower blood glucose levels by increasing insulin release ( 94054 , 94055 ). However, evidence from clinical research shows that jiaogulan does not increase insulin levels in humans ( 94054 ). Instead, jiaogulan is thought to reduce blood glucose levels by increasing insulin sensitivity through inhibition of protein tyrosine phosphatase 1B activity ( 94054 , 95519 ) and by decreasing glucose absorption through inhibition of alpha-glucosidase activity and down-regulation of glucose transporter 2 expression ( 100962 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

Preliminary evidence suggests the gypenosides in jiaogulan have antilipid peroxidation activity and protect vascular endothelial cells and their membranes from oxidative damage ( 9349 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

There is some evidence that the gypenoside constituents have several cardiovascular effects and may prevent the development of atherosclerosis and slow the aging process ( 9349 ). In animal research, jiaogulan seems to lower blood pressure, heart rate, blood vessel resistance, increase coronary blood flow, and inhibit platelet aggregation ( 7071 ).

#### Cholesterol-lowering effects

In vitro, an extract of jiaogulan activated liver X receptors, which play an important role in cholesterol homeostasis by serving as regulatory sensors of cholesterol levels in tissues ( 57053 ). There is also preliminary evidence a combination of jiaogulan with Nelumbo nucifera (Lotus flower or seed) and Crataegus cuneata (Hawthorn) might have both a cholesterol- and triglyceride-lowering effect. It is unclear whether these effects are due to an interaction among these agents or if one of the agents is primarily responsible ( 9348 ).

#### Coagulation effects

An in vitro study of human blood samples found that a water extract of jiaogulan significantly inhibited the platelet aggregation induced by adenosine 5'-diphosphate and compound agonists, accelerated disaggregation, and inhibited experimental thrombosis ( 7071 ). However, in another in vitro study, another extract from jiaogulan Makino contained a platelet aggregation factor ( 57059 ). Some research suggests that hot water extracts of jiaogulan contain a factor that induces platelet aggregation, but the effect occurs only with washed platelets and is not observed in platelet-rich plasma. This suggests the factor is unlikely to cause thrombosis in vivo ( 9350 ).

#### Gastrointestinal effects

In mice with diet-induced non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, jiaogulan extract altered the gut microbiota by reducing some opportunist pathogens at high doses and increasing some potential probiotics at low doses ( 115477 ).

#### Hepatic effects

In mice with diet-induced non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, jiaogulan extract reduces levels of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) and improves markers of oxidative stress, including malondialdehyde (MDA) and superoxide dismutase (SOD) ( 115477 ).

#### Neurologic effects

In animal research, jiaogulan may protect against cerebral ischemic damage ( 7071 ).

#### Weight loss effects

AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) is an enzyme that regulates energy balance. Its activation leads to increased fat oxidation and glucose uptake. In addition, activation of AMPK inhibits both fat and cholesterol synthesis. In vitro and in vivo research shows that two specific saponins found in jiaogulan, damulin A and B, increase phosphorylation of AMPK, which then stimulates beta-oxidation ( 94058 ). A study in mice given low, medium, and high dose oral jiaogulan polysaccharides suggests that jiaogulan may prevent weight gain and obesity. Researchers also found increased short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) and greater gut microbiota quantity and diversity ( 110700 ).
