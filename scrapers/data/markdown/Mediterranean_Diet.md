# Mediterranean Diet
Other Common Names Diet Mediterranean, Dieta mediterránea, Green-Mediterranean Diet, MeDi, Mediterranean Eating Pattern, Mediterranean-Style, Mediterranean-Style Diet, Régime méditerranéen
|

---

## Overview

The Mediterranean diet is a plant-based diet that emphasizes the intake of foods such as olive oil, fish, and moderate amounts of red wine. The diet first piqued the public's interest over 60 years ago, when data from the Seven Countries Study found that the diet may have benefit for preventing cardiovascular disease ( 97094 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. The Mediterranean diet has been safely used in many clinical studies ( 97097 , 97104 , 97107 , 97114 , 97115 , 113519 , 113521 , 113524 ). The diet focuses on consumption of fruits, vegetables, olive oil, fish and other foods, which are generally considered safe.

PREGNANCY: Possibly Safe when the Mediterranean diet is used appropriately during pregnancy ( 101073 , 101076 , 106878 , 106879 , 109895 , 113519 ). However, the red wine component of the Mediterranean diet should be avoided in pregnancy.

LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the Mediterranean diet during lactation; however, the Mediterranean diet is a balanced diet that would not be expected to be harmful as long as nutritional needs are met.

Possibly Effective
Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

The Mediterranean diet seems to have modest benefit in the primary and secondary prevention of CVD.

Observational research suggests a lower incidence of CVD in people who adhere to the Mediterranean diet. Specifically, a meta-analysis of 12 observational studies in females and 8 observational studies in males suggests that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 24% and 22% lower incidence of CVD in females and males, respectively, when compared with low adherence ( 113522 ). Similarly, a meta-analysis of 13 observational studies suggests that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 27% lower incidence of CVD when compared with low adherence ( 97107 ). Conversely, a meta-analysis of 8 observational studies in adults with diabetes suggests that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet is not associated with a lower incidence of CVD when compared with low adherence ( 103560 ). Observational research also suggests that the Mediterranean diet is associated with a lower risk of cardiovascular outcomes. A meta-analysis of 8 observational studies and one clinical study suggests that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 27% lower risk of cardiovascular mortality. Additionally, a meta-analysis of 14 observational studies and 2 clinical studies suggests that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 25% lower risk of cardiovascular events ( 115459 ), including a 28%, 33%, and 24% reduction in the risk of coronary heart disease (CHD), myocardial infarction (MI), and stroke, respectively ( 97107 ). Risk reduction has also been observed in certain sub-populations. A meta-analysis of observational studies in adults with type 1 or type 2 diabetes suggests that the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with 20% to 27% reductions in the incidence of CHD, stroke, and MI; it is also associated with 12% to 21% reductions in mortality from CVD, CHD, and stroke. The number of studies included in each comparison ranged from 2-16 ( 103560 ). An analysis of pooled data from two observational studies in adults with a history of smoking suggest a 30% reduction in risk for abdominal aortic aneurysm among those with the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet when compared with the lowest adherence. This finding varied by sex, with a 23% lower risk observed among males and 62% lower risk observed among females ( 106870 ). However, results from clinical research suggest that the Mediterranean diet has only modest benefit, if any, for primary prevention of CVD. A nonrandomized study involving nearly 7500 patients with high cardiovascular risk shows that following a Mediterranean diet with supplemental extra virgin olive oil or tree nuts reduces the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), a composite outcome including MI, stroke, or CVD mortality, by 30% when compared with following a low-fat diet ( 97097 ). However, when clinical endpoints were analyzed separately, the Mediterranean diet reduced only the risk of stroke by 40%; there was no significant reduction in the risk of the other individual endpoints ( 97097 , 97117 , 100687 ). Further, another analysis of this study has found that following a Mediterranean diet reduces the risk of initiating or escalating antihypertensive therapy among untreated or treated patients, respectively, and attenuates the risk of MACE in those receiving antihypertensive therapy when compared with a low-fat diet ( 106871 ). This study, known as the PREDIMED study ( 97097 ), has been criticized for various reasons. First, the control diet used was not notably different from the Mediterranean diet apart from increased consumption of olive oil or nuts in the Mediterranean diet group. This makes it unclear whether the beneficial effects are attributable to the Mediterranean diet, the addition of olive oil or nuts to the diet, or the combination ( 99509 ). More importantly, the original study was retracted ( 113526 ) because of serious protocol violations affecting approximately 21% of participants. These protocol violations included nonrandomized enrollment and incorrect randomization. Based on these findings, the study was republished as a nonrandomized study ( 97097 ); however, some scientists still question the controversial nature of this publication and its resulting secondary publications ( 101078 ). Still, a smaller clinical study including 180 hypertensive patients with high cardiovascular risk shows that following the Mediterranean diet for 2 years reduces the risk of stroke by 67% when compared with a low-fat diet ( 95762 ). With regard to secondary prevention, most studies show that the Mediterranean diet has modest benefit. One clinical study in 605 patients with a history of MI within the last 6 months shows that following a Mediterranean diet for 46 months reduces the risk of CVD mortality and non-fatal MI by 72%, CVD mortality by 65%, and total mortality by 56% when compared with usual care ( 95764 , 100687 ). Another large clinical trial in patients with established CHD, including acute MI, hospitalization for unstable angina, or chronic high-risk ischemic heart disease, shows that following a Mediterranean diet for up to 7 years reduces the risk of major cardiovascular events by about 25%, as well as the progression of atherosclerosis, when compared with following a low fat-diet. However, a sub-analysis shows that this benefit only occurs in males. Also, the definition of a low-fat diet in this study was a diet comprised of up to 30% fat with at least 55% carbohydrates ( 109886 , 109890 ). Also, another small study in 101 patients 6 weeks post-MI shows that following a Mediterranean diet does not significantly reduce the risk of cardiac events, including total and CVD mortality, MI, heart failure, unstable angina, or stroke, when compared with following a low-fat diet ( 90873 , 100687 ).

Cognitive function.

The Mediterranean diet seems to improve cognition in healthy older adults. However, the impact of the Mediterranean diet on specific cognitive domains appears to differ between observational and clinical research.

Observational research in men has found that following the Mediterranean diet over a period of approximately 22 years is associated with a reduced risk of developing a self-reported decline in cognitive function. The men with the best adherence to the Mediterranean diet over this period had approximately 25% and 36% lower odds of moderate or poor cognitive function, respectively, when compared with men with minimal adherence ( 101077 ). Furthermore, observational research in healthy older adults has found that following the Mediterranean diet is associated with improved episodic memory and global cognition, but not working or semantic memory ( 97112 ). Results from two clinical studies also in healthy older adults show that following the Mediterranean diet improves delayed recall, working memory, and global cognition, but not episodic memory, immediate recall, attention, processing speed, or verbal fluency ( 97112 ). Reasons for the inconsistent results are unclear, but may related to differences in study design, follow-up periods, and reporting of cognitive tests and domains.

Dementia.

Strict adherence to the Mediterranean diet seems to be associated with reductions in the risk of developing dementia and improvements in disease progression.

Meta-analyses of observational studies in older adults suggest that higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with an 11% to 31% reduced risk of dementia and 27% reduced risk of Alzheimer dementia, specifically, when compared with the lowest adherence ( 97095 , 115455 ). Other observational research in healthy adults has found that strict adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 27% lower risk of mild cognitive impairment, a 36% lower risk of Alzheimer disease, and up to a 72% lower risk of dementia when compared with the lowest adherence ( 97121 , 106865 , 106866 ). However, modest adherence to the Mediterranean diet does not appear to be beneficial ( 97121 ). Based on these and other results, some experts recommend the Mediterranean diet for dementia prevention, noting that strict adherence to the diet appears to be a key factor for achieving any benefit ( 95765 ). Observational research in patients already diagnosed with mild cognitive impairment has found that strict adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 48% reduced risk of progression from mild cognitive impairment to Alzheimer disease when compared with the lowest adherence ( 97121 ).

Diabetes.

The Mediterranean diet seems to have modest benefit in patients with diabetes and also seems to be associated with reductions in the risk of developing gestational or type 2 diabetes.

Observational research suggests that adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with up to a 30% lower risk of developing type 2 diabetes ( 97099 , 97110 , 97119 , 97100 , 106874 ). Additionally, up to 7 years of follow-up data from adults with cardiovascular risk factors originally assigned to either a low-fat diet or Mediterranean diet in the PREDIMED study suggests a 52% lower risk of developing type 2 diabetes with the highest versus the lowest cumulative yearly average adherence to the Mediterranean diet ( 113517 ). Both clinical and observational research also suggest that following the Mediterranean diet may reduce the risk of developing gestational diabetes. A meta-analysis of 6 clinical trials in adults shows that following the Mediterranean diet during pregnancy lowers the risk of gestational diabetes by 26% when compared with control diets ( 113519 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of 5 observational studies suggests that higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 30% lower risk of gestational diabetes when compared with the lowest adherence ( 113519 ). Another observational study suggests that high adherence to a Mediterranean-style diet around the time of conception is associated with a 37% lower odds of gestational diabetes when compared with the low adherence ( 109895 ). The effect of the Mediterranean diet in patients already diagnosed with type 2 diabetes is less clear. Although the 2022 Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes from the American Diabetes Association recommend an eating plan emphasizing elements of a Mediterranean-style eating pattern rich in monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats to improve glucose metabolism and reduce cardiovascular risk in patients with diabetes ( 106900 ), clinical research suggests that the Mediterranean diet may have only modest benefit in patients with diabetes. Meta-analyses of clinical research have found that adherence to the Mediterranean diet modestly reduces glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by 0.3% to 0.5% but does not affect fasting insulin or fasting blood glucose in patients with diabetes. Additionally, following the Mediterranean diet does not appear to significantly reduce weight in patients with diabetes ( 97096 , 97108 , 97100 ). However, meta-analyses show that combining the Mediterranean diet with a structured meal plan and exercise leads to clinically significant reduction in weight of up to 8 kg after 1 year of intervention ( 97100 , 97102 ). Other clinical research has investigated the potential renoprotective effects of the Mediterranean diet. A large study in adults with coronary heart disease (CHD), some of whom had type 2 diabetes and mild kidney impairment at baseline, suggests that excellent adherence to the Mediterranean diet for 5 years may attenuate reductions in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), particularly in those with mild kidney impairment, type 2 diabetes, and/or obesity, when compared with a low-fat diet rich in complex carbohydrates ( 107983 , 115463 ). The validity of these findings is limited due to lack of a standard control diet.

Stroke.

The Mediterranean diet reduces the risk of stroke in patients at high risk for cardiovascular disease.

Observational research has found that following the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 29% reduced risk of stroke ( 97116 ). In addition, a meta-analysis of observational research has found that the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 20% reduced incidence of stroke and a 13% reduced risk of stroke mortality when compared with the lowest adherence ( 103560 ). Also, clinical research in patients at high risk for cardiovascular disease shows that following the Mediterranean diet reduces the risk of stroke by 40% to 67% when compared with following a low-fat diet ( 97097 , 97107 , 97111 , 100687 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Hypertension.

The Mediterranean diet does not seem to produce clinically meaningful reductions in blood pressure in patients with hypertension, although the effect of the Mediterranean diet when combined with salt restriction warrants additional investigation.

Meta-analyses of clinical research, usually in patients with hypertension, show that the Mediterranean diet might slightly reduce blood pressure, but the reduction is not clinically significant. For example, reductions in systolic blood pressure of less than 1 mmHg and up to 3 mmHg, or diastolic blood pressure of 2 mmHg have been reported with the Mediterranean diet when compared with control diets ( 97114 , 100687 , 106872 , 109246 ). These findings led the American Heart Association to release a scientific statement suggesting that some limited clinical research shows that the Mediterranean diet reduces systolic and diastolic blood pressure by a small amount ( 108372 ).The magnitude of blood pressure reduction was reduced when both hypertensive and normotensive individuals were analyzed together ( 97115 ). Alternatively, a large clinical trial in patients with higher normal blood pressures or grade 1 hypertension shows that following the Mediterranean diet with salt restriction for 3 months lowers systolic blood pressure by about 3 mmHg when compared with the Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet with salt restriction, by 7.5 mmHg when compared with salt restriction alone, and by 15 mmHg when compared with no intervention. Additionally, the Mediterranean diet lowers in-office diastolic blood pressure, the odds of hypertension, and ambulatory blood pressure when compared with salt restriction alone and with no intervention, but not when compared with the DASH diet ( 112718 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Academic performance.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for improving academic performance in children and adolescents.

A meta-analysis of 16 observational studies in children and adolescents suggests that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet has a weak association with greater academic performance ( 115460 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the heterogeneity of assessment methods used across studies and the possible introduction of teacher bias.

Asthma.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of asthma in children.

Observational research has found that following the Mediterranean diet is associated with modestly reduced odds of wheezing and developing asthma in children 6-12 years of age ( 97103 ).

Breast cancer.

The Mediterranean diet may be associated with reductions in the risk of mortality in patients with breast cancer.

Observational research in patients with breast cancer suggests that the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet prior to diagnosis is associated with a 28% reduction in the risk of all-cause mortality when compared with the lowest adherence. Among those 55 years or older, high adherence to the Mediterranean diet prior to diagnosis is associated with 45% and 35% reductions in the risk of all-cause and breast cancer mortality, respectively, when compared with the lowest adherence ( 106861 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis combining this study with 1-2 other observational studies in patients with breast cancer suggests that higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 22% reduction in all-cause mortality and a 33% reduction in non-breast cancer mortality, but may not be associated with a lower risk of breast cancer mortality when compared with lower adherence ( 113520 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of information on post-diagnosis dietary habits and the low to very low certainty of evidence.

Cancer.

Strict adherence to the Mediterranean diet may be associated with a reduced risk of certain cancers, but not a reduced risk of mortality or cancer recurrence.

Observational research has found that strict adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 13% reduction in the risk of cancer mortality. It is also associated with a reduced risk of colorectal, prostate, breast, gastric, liver, pancreatic, and lung cancers, but not esophageal, ovarian, and bladder cancers. The Mediterranean diet is also not associated with a reduced risk of mortality or recurrence in cancer survivors ( 97105 , 97118 , 97120 , 97123 ). The relationship between the Mediterranean diet and endometrial cancer is unclear, as results from observational research are conflicting ( 97101 , 97120 ).

Chronic kidney disease (CKD).

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for preventing kidney disease.

Observational research has found that the highest adherence to a Mediterranean-style diet is associated with a 13% to 42% reduced risk of CKD, and a possible decreased risk of mortality in adults with CKD, when compared with the lowest adherence ( 109900 , 110151 , 110152 ). Other observational research in older adults has found that the highest adherence to a Mediterranean diet pattern is associated with a 48% reduced risk of kidney function decline when compared with the lowest adherence ( 109894 ). In addition, a large study in adults with CHD suggests that excellent adherence to the Mediterranean diet for 5 years may attenuate reductions in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) in those with mild kidney impairment, but not severe kidney impairment, at baseline when compared with a low-fat diet rich in complex carbohydrates ( 107983 , 115463 ). The validity of this finding is limited due to lack of a standard control diet.

Colorectal cancer.

The Mediterranean diet may be associated with reductions in the incidence of rectal cancer, but the available research is conflicting.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 10% reduced incidence of colorectal cancer when compared with the lowest adherence. Sub-group analysis suggests that this is related to the incidence of rectal cancer and not cancer of the proximal or distal colon. In addition, there was no association with colorectal cancer-associated mortality ( 103565 ). However, any benefit appears to be small and many individual studies have not shown benefit ( 103561 , 103565 ).

Coronary heart disease (CHD).

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for patients with CHD.

A large clinical trial in patients with established CHD, including acute myocardial infarction, hospitalization for unstable angina, or chronic high-risk ischemic heart disease, shows that following a Mediterranean diet for up to 7 years reduces the risk of subsequent major cardiovascular events by about 25%, as well as the progression of atherosclerosis, when compared with following a low fat-diet. However, a sub-analysis shows that this benefit only occurs in males. Also, the definition of a low-fat diet in this study was a diet comprised of up to 30% fat with at least 55% carbohydrates ( 109886 , 109890 ). Observational research, including one meta-analysis, evaluating patients with and without cardiovascular disease (CVD) has found that, overall, the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with reductions in CHD incidence of up to 28% and CHD mortality of 17% when compared with the lowest adherence ( 97107 , 103560 , 106870 ). A meta-analysis of 4 observational studies also suggests a lower incidence of CHD among those with the highest adherence to a Mediterranean diet when compared with those with the lowest adherence ( 113522 ). A large observational study in patients with and without existing CHD has found that adherence to the Mediterranean diet is not associated with risk of sudden cardiac death when a fully adjusted model accounting for many socioeconomic and lifestyle factors was used. However, in a subgroup analysis of patients without CHD at baseline, the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet was associated with a 51% and 41% reduction in sudden cardiac death in the minimally adjusted and fully adjusted models, respectively, when compared with the lowest adherence. No association was observed between diet and sudden cardiac death in patients with a history of CHD at baseline ( 106869 ). Other research has investigated the potential renoprotective effects of the Mediterranean diet. A large study in adults with CHD, some of whom also had type 2 diabetes and mild kidney impairment at baseline, suggests that excellent adherence to the Mediterranean diet for 5 years may attenuate reductions in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) when compared with a low-fat diet rich in complex carbohydrates. This benefit was most pronounced in those with mild renal impairment, type 2 diabetes, and/or obesity at baseline ( 107983 , 115463 ). The validity of this finding is limited due to lack of a standard control diet.

Depression.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for the treatment or prevention of depression.

Observational research in healthy adults has found that adhering to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a lower risk of depression and depressive symptoms ( 97113 , 97116 ). Observational research in healthy older adults found that consuming moderate amounts of wine as part of the Mediterranean diet is associated with a lower risk of depression when compared to those abstaining from wine ( 96696 ). Preliminary clinical research in adults with depression has also been conducted. One study in young males with depression given usual care shows that following a Mediterranean diet for 12 weeks modestly improves symptoms of depression and quality of life when compared with social support. Symptoms were reduced to low or minimal levels in 36% of patients following the Mediterranean diet, compared with 0% of patients in the social support group ( 109892 ). Another study shows that a Mediterranean-style dietary intervention, by way of food hampers and cooking workshops for 3 months, in addition to fish oil capsules providing docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) 900 mg daily and eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) 200 mg daily, improves symptoms of depression by approximately 68% when compared with a social group meeting every 2 weeks. However, it is not clear if this benefit is due to the Mediterranean diet, the use of fish oil supplements, the provided food, the cooking classes, or any combination of these factors ( 101075 ).

Dyslipidemia.

The Mediterranean diet may be associated with reductions in the incidence of hypercholesterolemia, as well as improvement in some lipid levels.

Observational research has found that levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol are lower in individuals with at least moderate adherence to a Mediterranean diet when compared with lower adherence. A sub-analysis found that the odds of having hypercholesterolemia were 46% lower in males with at least moderate adherence to the Mediterranean diet when compared with lower adherence. In males, there was no association between Mediterranean diet adherence and odds of hypertriglyceridemia or with levels of high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol. In females, there was no association with any outcome ( 103566 ). This study took place in Greece, where adherence to the Mediterranean diet would be expected to be higher than most other places around the world, including the US.

Eclampsia.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of eclampsia.

Observational research has found that the highest adherence to a Mediterranean-style diet around the time of conception is associated with a 28% lower odds of eclampsia or pre-eclampsia when compared with the lowest adherence ( 109895 ).

Erectile dysfunction (ED).

The Mediterranean diet seems to be associated with a reduced risk of developing ED.

Observational research in male health professionals has found that the highest adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 7% to 22% reduction in the risk of developing ED when compared with the lowest adherence. The greatest effects are seen in those less than 60 years of age ( 106863 ).

Familial hypercholesterolemia.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial in patients with familial hypercholesterolemia.

A cross-sectional study in patients with heterozygous familial hypercholesterolemia has found that strong adherence to a Mediterranean diet is inversely associated with apolipoprotein B (apoB) levels, but not LDL levels ( 106868 ).

Frailty.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of frailty in older adults.

A meta-analysis of 12 prospective cohort studies, including over 89,000 older adults, suggests that higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 34% lower risk of frailty when compared with lower adherence. Likewise, meta-analysis of 7 cross-sectional studies also suggests a 56% lower odds of frailty with higher versus lower adherence to the Mediterranean diet ( 113523 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies; particularly the cross-sectional studies.

Gastric cancer.

The Mediterranean diet may be associated with reductions in the risk of gastric cancer.

A meta-analysis of 11 observational studies, with over 1 million study participants, suggests that higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 32% lower risk of gastric cancer when compared with lower adherence, although the risk reduction may only be among male participants. The decrease in gastric risk may exist for both gastric cardia and non-cardia adenocarcinoma ( 113518 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies. Subsequently, an observational study conducted in Greece among patients with gastric cancer suggests that higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet over the lifespan is associated with longer overall survival and less advanced tumor histopathological characteristics when compared with those with lower adherence ( 113140 ). However, the interpretation of these results may be limited by potential recall bias as diet questionnaires were only administered after the cancer diagnosis.

Gingivitis.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for gingivitis.

A small clinical study in adults with generalized gingivitis shows that sufficient adherence to a Mediterranean diet for 6 weeks improves gum bleeding, but not gingival index or plaque build-up, when compared with placebo ( 108070 ). The validity of these findings is limited due to short duration of treatment.

Impaired glucose tolerance (prediabetes).

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial in patients with prediabetes.

A clinical study in patients with prediabetes shows that following a Mediterranean diet does not improve glycemic control when compared with a personalized, algorithm-guided dietary strategy targeting postprandial glucose responses ( 106873 ). Also, a small clinical trial in children with prediabetes shows that following the Mediterranean diet does not affect glycated hemoglobin levels, waist, arm, or hip circumferences, body fat levels, or body mass index when compared to a control group receiving recommendations for healthy eating ( 109897 ). However, the study may not have been adequately powered to detect a difference between groups.

Infertility.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for infertility.

Meta-analyses of 3-5 observational studies in adult females or couples with either subfertility or infertility suggests there might be no association between adherence to the Mediterranean diet and successful implantation, clinical pregnancy, or live birth. Additionally, 1 moderate-sized clinical study in adult females shows that adopting some elements of a Mediterranean diet, such as additional olive oil and vitamin D, improves early embryo development but doesn't improve rates of clinical pregnancy and live birth ( 113519 ).

Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD).

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for the treatment or prevention of MASLD.

A meta-analysis of moderate-quality clinical research shows that following a Mediterranean diet for up to 2 years has some beneficial effects in adults with MASLD, including modest reductions in alanine transaminase (ALT) and improvements in fatty liver index and liver stiffness values, but with no changes in aspartate transaminase (AST), hepatic steatosis, or body weight. In most cases, the control groups in these studies received standard care only or followed a low-fat diet ( 109898 ). A similar meta-analysis of clinical studies in adults with MASLD shows that following the Mediterranean diet, often with energy restriction, for less than a year may slightly improve liver stiffness, but not liver transaminase levels, when compared with control diets ( 113521 ). Another meta-analysis of clinical research shows that most hepatic and metabolic outcomes, including liver fat and stiffness, serum lipids, and anthropometric indices, were similar in groups following an ad-libitum Mediterranean diet or a low-fat diet for 12 weeks ( 115457 ). Additionally, in one study, some liver transaminase levels were reduced only in the low-fat diet group ( 109893 ). The reason for the discordant findings among studies is not clear but could be due in part to variations in study durations, how diet adherence was evaluated, the macronutrient distribution, and the types of foods consumed in the Mediterranean diets studied in different countries ( 113521 ). The Mediterranean diet has also been investigated in children. A meta-analysis of 5 clinical studies in children and adolescents with MASLD shows that following a Mediterranean diet for 3-29 months modestly reduces liver transaminase levels when compared with control diets ( 115458 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the quasi-experimental nature of 3 of the 5 included studies. One of these clinical studies in adolescents with obesity shows that following either a Mediterranean diet or a low-fat diet for 12 weeks improves hepatic steatosis severity, liver transaminase levels, glycemic control, and anthropometric measurements to a similar extent when compared to baseline, with no improvement in lipid parameters ( 107969 ). There is also interest in whether the Mediterranean diet can reduce the risk of developing MASLD. A large observational study in healthy adults shows that the risk of liver steatosis was reduced by 87% and 45%, respectively, in people with high or moderate adherence to a Mediterranean diet when compared with the lowest adherence. Results remained significant in multi-adjusted models ( 106877 ).

Metabolic syndrome.

The Mediterranean diet seems to be associated with a reduced risk for metabolic syndrome, as well as improvements in certain outcomes for people with metabolic syndrome.

Observational research has found that strict adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 19% reduced risk of developing metabolic syndrome when compared with low adherence ( 97106 ). The Mediterranean diet has also been evaluated in those with existing metabolic syndrome. A large meta-analysis of mostly good- to high- quality observational research in patients with metabolic syndrome has found that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with improvements in waist circumference and levels of triglycerides and high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, but not fasting blood glucose or systolic blood pressure, when compared with low adherence ( 106875 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies. Furthermore, clinical research in patients with obesity, most of whom also have metabolic syndrome, shows that following a calorie-reduced Mediterranean diet for 12 months reduces body weight, body mass index (BMI), waist circumference, systolic blood pressure, and fasting blood glucose, and increases HDL cholesterol when compared with following a standard calorie-restricted low-fat diet. The Mediterranean diet does not affect low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c), or diastolic blood pressure when compared with the low-fat diet ( 101072 ).

Obesity.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of obesity or improving weight loss.

Clinical research in adults with obesity shows that following a calorie-reduced Mediterranean diet for 12 months reduces body weight by 3.8 kg and waist circumference by 2.6 cm when compared with following a standard calorie-reduced low-fat diet ( 101072 ). In adults with abdominal obesity or dyslipidemia, clinical research shows that following a Mediterranean diet for 18 months reduces body weight and waist circumference by an additional 2.3% and 1%, respectively, when compared with providing basic health guidance. Following a Mediterranean diet, drinking green tea, and taking Wolffia globosa, which provides plant-based polyphenols, reduced body weight and waist circumference by an additional 1.2% and 1%, respectively. All individuals in this study were also given physical activity recommendations ( 109888 ). However, clinical research in healthy adults who are overweight shows that the Mediterranean diet is no more effective than a lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet for weight or fat loss when calorie restriction is implemented over a 3-month period ( 101074 ). The Mediterranean diet has also been investigated in children. In adolescents in Europe, observational research has found that adherence to the Mediterranean diet does not seem to be associated with adiposity or risk of being overweight ( 103563 ). A large clinical study in adults with overweight or obesity shows that following a Mediterranean diet providing an energy deficit of approximately 500 kcals daily takes 3 months to achieve a 5% weight loss, compared with only one month in patients following a very low-calorie ketogenic diet. However, there were no differences between groups in absolute weight loss or changes in body mass index (BMI). Waist circumference and fat mass were modestly reduced, and fat-free mass modestly increased, in the adults consuming the Mediterranean diet when compared to the very low-calorie ketogenic diet. A sub-analysis shows that these benefits only occurred in females and individuals under the age of 50 years ( 109889 ). The American Heart Association released a scientific statement suggesting that some clinical research shows that following the Mediterranean diet can modestly reduce body weight and BMI. This statement is based on a meta-analysis from 2011 ( 108372 ).

Osteoarthritis.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for the prevention of osteoarthritis or the improvement of symptoms.

Clinical research in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that following a Mediterranean diet for 12 weeks modestly reduces pain, weight, and waist circumference, and improves physical function, when compared with a regular diet. However, only pain was modestly reduced when compared to a low-fat diet ( 109896 ). Observational research has found that a higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a very slight reduction in the risk of worsening osteoarthritic pain over 4 years. It was also associated with a 9% reduced risk for developing symptomatic arthritis; however, there was no effect on the diagnosis of osteoarthritis based on radiographic analysis only ( 103562 ).

Overall mortality.

It is unclear if following a Mediterranean diet reduces the risk of all-cause mortality.

A meta-analysis of 28 observational studies suggests that for each 2-point increment in adherence to the Mediterranean diet, on a score of 0-9, there is a 10% reduction in the risk of all-cause mortality over the next 4-32 years. This association was strongest in studies conducted in the Mediterranean region, from which the diet originates ( 103564 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of 19 observational studies in older adults suggests that higher adherence to a Mediterranean diet is associated with a 23% reduction in the risk of all-cause mortality when compared with lower adherence ( 115459 ). The validity of these findings is limited by heterogeneity of the included studies.

Pancreatic cancer.

The Mediterranean diet may be associated with reductions in the risk of pancreatic cancer.

A meta-analysis of 8 large observational studies, with over 1 million study participants, suggests that higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with a 22% lower risk of pancreatic cancer when compared with lower adherence ( 113525 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies.

Parkinson disease.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for reducing constipation in adults with Parkinson disease.

A very small clinical study in adults with Parkinson disease and constipation shows that following the Mediterranean diet in addition to standard care for 8 weeks does not improve constipation symptom scores when compared with standard care alone ( 115462 ). However, the interpretation of these results may be limited by variable adherence to the study diet, the small sample size, and short duration of the study.

Pre-eclampsia.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of pre-eclampsia.

A meta-analysis of 5 large clinical trials shows that following the Mediterranean diet during pregnancy doesn't lower the risk of pre-eclampsia when compared with control diets ( 113519 ). However, observational research suggests that the highest adherence to a Mediterranean-style diet around the time of conception is associated with a 28% lower odds of eclampsia or pre-eclampsia when compared with the lowest adherence ( 109895 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of 3 observational studies suggests that higher adherence to a Mediterranean diet pre-pregnancy is associated with a 39% lower risk of pre-eclampsia when compared with lower adherence ( 113519 ).

Pregnancy-induced hypertension.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of pregnancy-induced hypertension.

A meta-analysis of 6 clinical trials shows that following the Mediterranean diet during pregnancy probably doesn't lower the risk of pregnancy-induced hypertension when compared with a control diet ( 113519 ).  Additionally, observational research suggests that the highest adherence to a Mediterranean-style diet around the time of conception is not associated with a lower odds of pregnancy-induced hypertension when compared with the lowest adherence ( 109895 ).  However, a meta-analysis of 3 other observational studies suggests that higher adherence to a Mediterranean diet pre-pregnancy is associated with a 35% lower risk of pregnancy-induced hypertension when compared with lower adherence ( 113519 ).

Preterm labor.

The Mediterranean diet may be beneficial for reducing the risk of preterm labor.

A meta-analysis of 7 clinical trials shows that following the Mediterranean diet during pregnancy lowers the risk of preterm birth by 55% when compared with control diets. The risk reduction appears to be greater when the Mediterranean diet is adopted during the first trimester rather than after the first trimester ( 113519 ). Observational research suggests that the highest adherence to a Mediterranean-style diet around the time of conception is not associated with a lower odds of preterm labor when compared with the lowest adherence ( 109895 ). However, a meta-analysis of 3 other observational studies suggests that higher adherence to a Mediterranean diet pre-pregnancy is associated with a 40% lower risk of preterm birth when compared with lower adherence ( 113519 ).

Prostate cancer.

The Mediterranean diet may be associated with reductions in the risk of overall mortality, but not prostate cancer-related mortality, in patients with prostate cancer.

Observational research in patients with prostate cancer has found that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet prior to diagnosis is associated with a 26% reduction in the risk of overall mortality when compared with low adherence. Additional benefit is observed in all-cause mortality among those with high adherence to the Mediterranean diet and high physical activity prior to diagnosis when compared with low adherence and low or moderate physical activity. There was no association with prostate cancer mortality ( 106862 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of information on post-diagnosis dietary and physical activity habits. The study also may have been underpowered to detect differences in prostate cancer mortality.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial in patients with RA.

Preliminary clinical research shows that following the Mediterranean diet for 6-12 weeks is beneficial for improving some RA symptoms, such as overall clinical assessment, physical function, swollen joints, and pain, when compared with control. However, the effect of the Mediterranean diet on other symptoms, including fatigue, tender joints, stiffness, and quality of life, seems to differ between studies. Control groups have included a regular diet or dietary information ( 97098 , 110153 , 110154 ). A small open label study conducted in Greece in adult females with mild to moderate RA in remission shows that following the Mediterranean diet and a physical activity program for 12 weeks improves disease activity scores when compared with a control diet and usual care ( 113524 ). The interpretation of these results is limited by the difficulty in blinding study participants to the type of diet they are consuming. It is also unclear if improvements were due to the Mediterranean diet, physical activity, or the combination.

Small for gestational age (SGA).

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for preventing SGA in infants.

A small, observational study has found that the birth of SGA newborns occurred in only 6% of pregnancies reporting high adherence to the Mediterranean diet, compared with 21% of pregnancies in the low adherence group ( 106878 ). However, other observational research has found that adherence to a Mediterranean-style diet around the time of conception is not associated with the odds of SGA birth ( 109895 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the retrospective nature of the studies and the potential confounding from lifestyle habits and SGA risk factors. A large clinical study in patients at high risk for developing SGA during pregnancy shows that SGA occurred in 14% of patients with high adherence to a Mediterranean diet with supplemental extra virgin olive oil or walnuts beginning halfway through pregnancy, compared with 22% of those receiving usual care ( 106879 ).

Stillbirth.

It is unclear if the Mediterranean diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of stillbirth.

Observational research suggests that the highest adherence to a Mediterranean-style diet around the time of conception is not associated with lower odds of a stillbirth when compared with the lowest adherence and that following a Mediterranean diet pre-pregnancy is not associated with a lower risk of still birth when compared to control diets ( 109895 , 113519 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the Mediterranean diet for these uses.

The Mediterranean diet is a special diet with the following characteristics: a high ratio of monounsaturated-to-saturated fat; high consumption of fruits, vegetables, legumes, and whole grains; moderate to modest intake of red wine; and moderate intake of dairy products. Additionally, the diet usually involves increased fish intake and low consumption of meat ( 97094 ). The high monounsaturated-to-saturated fat ratio usually includes a high consumption of olive oil and nuts. Finally, this diet is typically low in highly processed foods, refined grains, and sugars ( 97110 , 97117 ). Some observational research uses different methods to determine levels of adherence to the Mediterranean diet ( 103564 , 109894 , 109895 , 109900 , 113525 ). One method asks respondents to rate their consumption of a food item with a score ranging from 0, for less adherence, to 5, for better adherence. The highest score attainable is 55 and is based on number of servings per week or month of items such as grains, fruits, vegetables, legumes, fish, and olive oil. Lower scores are given for consumption of foods such as red meat, poultry, full-fat dairy products, and alcohol. A score of at least 23 indicates moderate adherence ( 103562 , 103566 ). The Mediterranean Diet Adherence Screener index (MEDAS) uses a scale of 0-14 to score the intake frequency of specific foods, for example olive oil, nuts, vegetables, legumes, and wine ( 109894 ). Another method, the Alternate Mediterranean Diet (aMed) score, uses a scale of 0-9, which is based on a score of one or zero for the presence or absence of an intake above the median for vegetables, fruits, nuts, whole grains, legumes, fish, and the monounsaturated to saturated fat ratio ( 109895 , 109900 ).

#### General

Mediterranean diet is a balanced and nutrition-rich diet. Its individual components may explain some of its clinical benefits. For in depth information about how individual components work see individual monographs for wine, fish, olive oil, legumes, etc.

#### Cardiovascular effects

The Mediterranean diet is rich in polyunsaturated fats such as omega-3 fatty acids from nuts, olive oil and fish. The increased content of polyunsaturated fats is thought to reduce cardiovascular risk ( 99509 ). Human research shows that following the Green-Mediterranean diet for 6 months, which includes the consumption of green tea and Wolffia globosa, induces changes in the gut microbiome, especially an increase in Prevotella and decrease in Bifidobacterium. These changes are associated with improvements in cardiometabolic biomarkers. The Mediterranean diet in the absence of these green products did not have the same effect on the gut microbiome ( 109891 ). Clinical research in the general population shows that following a Mediterranean diet enriched with olive oil may reduce triglyceride levels in adults ( 115453 ). Additionally, a traditional Mediterranean diet may reduce levels of triglycerides, total and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, and systolic blood pressure in children when compared with control ( 115453 , 115456 ). Observational research also suggests that olive oil, vegetables, fruit, and legumes appear to be the dietary factors most associated with reduced cardiovascular risk ( 97107 ).

#### Fertility effects

The effect of the Mediterranean diet on sperm quality has been evaluated. A clinical study in healthy young adults shows that following a Mediterranean diet improves sperm concentration and total sperm count when compared with a low-fat diet ( 106864 ). Additionally, observational research in males with potential fertility issues suggests that higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with better sperm parameters, including sperm count, motility, viability, and morphology, when compared with lower adherence ( 115461 ).

#### Glycemic effects

The Mediterranean diet is high in dietary fiber. The dietary fiber slows gastric emptying rate and carbohydrate absorption. This in turn reduces insulin release and postprandial blood glucose levels, which may benefit patients with diabetes ( 97110 ). It has also been suggested that the phenolic compounds and unsaturated fats in olive oil and tree nuts may play a role in improving insulin sensitivity ( 113517 ).

#### Neurological effects

Some experts purport that the Mediterranean diet helps with dementia because it might promote cerebrovascular health and increase clearance of amyloid beta from the brain. However, this has not been shown in clinical research. Furthermore, higher fish intake and lower meat intake has been associated with higher volume of gray matter and white matter in the brain. In theory, the increased total brain volume could also improve cognition ( 97112 ). Clinical research in adults with obesity suggests that following exercise guidance in conjunction with a calorie-restricted Mediterranean diet enriched with an additional daily intake of 800 mg polyphenols may provide neuroprotective effects against age-related neurodegeneration by reducing grey matter atrophy and preserving hippocampal volume ( 107982 ).
