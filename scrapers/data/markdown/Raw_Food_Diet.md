## Overview

Raw food diets are characterized by a high consumption of uncooked and unprocessed foods. Supporters of raw food diets claim that raw foods have a high nutritive content and are a healthier alternative to the modern diet ( 103154 ). Most people who follow these diets state that they are interested in improving health, preventing illness, and living in a natural and healthy way ( 103156 ). There are many different forms of the raw food diet, some of which follow vegetarian or vegan guidelines, and others that allow for raw meat and fish. Additionally, raw food can be incorporated into the diet to different degrees, often denoted by the percentage of a diet that consists of raw food. Strict adherence typically involves consuming 90% to 100% raw foods ( 103155 , 103156 , 103158 ). KEY HIGHLIGHTS

- Sometimes used for obesity, hypertension, and to improve overall health, but there is no strong evidence to support its use for any purpose.
- Possibly unsafe when the diet is followed with strict adherence, especially long-term, due to risk for malnutrition. There is insufficient reliable evidence available about the safety of the raw food diet when followed with partial or moderate adherence.

## History

The raw food diet seems to have developed out of the Natural Hygiene movement in the US and has since gained worldwide popularity ( 103155 ).

#### General

When followed with strict adherence, raw food diets may not provide adequate nutrition. Observational research in long-term adherents of raw food diets found that 15% of men and 25% of women had a body mass index (BMI) below the normal weight range. Those with strict adherence had an increased risk for a low BMI. Inadequate nutrition also increases the risk for amenorrhea, with as many as 30% of women following a raw food diet reporting partial or complete amenorrhea ( 103156 ). Following a raw food diet might also reduce high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels ( 103155 ) and increase the risk for dental erosion ( 103157 ). Some people following a raw food diet have reported an increased sensitivity to alcohol, cigarette smoke, chemical odors, and medication ( 103158 ).

#### Cardiovascular

Observational research in adults following a raw food diet in Germany found that diet adherents were more likely to have low low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and triglyceride levels. However, adherents were also more likely to have low high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels ( 103155 ).

#### Dental

In one clinical evaluation, adherents of the raw food diet were more likely to experience dental erosion. Only 2% of raw food diet adherents had no erosive defects, compared with 13% of a healthy control group. About 37% of raw food diet adherents had at least one moderate erosion and 61% had at least one severe erosion, compared with 55% and 32%, respectively, in the control group ( 103157 ). The investigators speculate that this increased risk for severe erosion is due to an increased consumption of acidic fruits.

#### Gastrointestinal

In one clinical study, patients following a 40% raw food diet reported nausea, vomiting, and malaise after consuming typical restaurant and banquet foods. Participants also reported gastrointestinal symptoms when consuming certain foods, such as citrus fruit, nuts, and tomatoes ( 103158 ).

#### Genitourinary

An observational study of long-term raw food diet adherents found that 30% of women had partial or complete amenorrhea. The rate of amenorrhea was higher in women with strict adherence to the diet than in those who followed a moderate raw food diet ( 103156 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

An observational study of long-term raw food diet adherents found that 15% of men and 25% of women had a body mass index (BMI) below the normal weight range. This occurred at a higher rate in those with strict adherence to the diet than in those who followed a moderate raw food diet ( 103156 ).

#### Other

In one clinical study, following a 40% raw food diet resulted in reports of increased sensitivity to alcohol, cigarette smoke, chemical odors, and medication ( 103158 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Hypertension.

In a small clinical study in adults with hypertension, following a 40% raw food diet reduced diastolic blood pressure by an average 18 mmHg when compared with baseline. The investigators did not report on changes in systolic blood pressure. Dieters also experienced an average 3.8 kg weight loss ( 103158 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Obesity.

Although the raw food diet is sometimes recommended for the management of obesity, it has only been evaluated in normal weight adults. In a small clinical study in adults with hypertension, following a 40% raw food diet reduced diastolic blood pressure and resulted in an average 3.8 kg weight loss when compared to baseline ( 103158 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group. An observational study of otherwise healthy, long-term raw food diet adherents found that, on average, men following the diet experienced a 10 kg weight loss and women experienced a 12 kg weight loss. However, 15% of men and 25% of women had a body mass index (BMI) below the normal weight range, suggesting that the diet may not provide adequate nutrition when followed long-term. People who followed a strict raw food diet were more likely to be underweight than those who followed a moderate raw food diet ( 103156 ).

Move evidence is needed to rate the raw food diet for these uses.

The raw food diet is characterized by a high consumption of uncooked and unprocessed foods, such as fruits, vegetables, nuts, sprouted grains, beans, and seeds. However, there are many variants of the raw food diet. Adherents can consume a mixed raw food diet, which involves the consumption of raw meat and fish. Others choose to follow an ovo-lacto-vegetarian or vegan form of the diet. In the most popular variant of the diet in Germany, cereals and dairy products are completely eliminated ( 103155 , 103156 ). Raw food adherents often eat food items separately instead of mixing them with other foods in the form of a meal ( 103154 ). Raw food diets can be followed with different levels of adherence. Strict adherents typically consume 90% to 100% of the diet as raw food. Moderate adherents consume 70% to 80% raw food. Partial adherents, on the other hand, may consume only 40% of the diet as raw food ( 103156 , 103158 ).

No known.

Advocates of the raw food diet believe that raw foods have a higher nutritive value, while cooked foods are toxic and more difficult to digest ( 103154 ). However, there is a lack of evidence supporting these claims. In fact, initial research suggests that raw food diet adherents may actually be at risk for malnutrition and low nutrient levels ( 103154 , 103155 ). Observational research in adults following a raw food diet in Germany found that diet adherents were more likely to have low low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol and triglyceride levels and increased beta-carotene levels. However, adherents were also more likely to have low high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, lycopene, and vitamin B12 levels ( 103154 , 103155 ). Additionally, raw food diet adherents were at an increased risk for malnutrition, with up to 15% to 25% reporting a body mass index (BMI) below the normal weight range ( 103156 ). Raw food diet proponents claim that animals suffer fewer degenerative diseases, such as Alzheimer disease and cancer, because they do not consume toxins in cooked foods. However, there is no scientific evidence to support this claim.
