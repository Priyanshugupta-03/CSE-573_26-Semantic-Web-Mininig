# Blessed Thistle
Scientific Name Cnicus benedictus (synonym: Carduus benedictus) Family Asteraceae/Compositae Other Common Names Carbenia Benedicta, Cardo Bendito, Cardo Santo, Carduus, Chardon Béni, Chardon béni, Chardon Bénit, Chardon Marbré, Cnici Benedicti Herba, Cnicus, Holy Thistle, Safran Sauvage, Spotted Thistle, St. Benedict Thistle

Caution

Blessed thistle should not be confused with Milk Thistle .

## Overview

Blessed thistle is a flowering plant with sharp prickles over the stem and leaves which is native to the Mediterranean region ( 93575 ). Blessed thistle has traditionally been used for its purported antimicrobial, antitumoral, appetite stimulating, cholagogue, diuretic, galactagogue, and wound healing effects.

Likely Safe when used orally in amounts commonly found in foods. Blessed thistle has Generally Recognized As Safe (GRAS) status in the US ( 4912 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of blessed thistle when used in medicinal amounts.

PREGNANCY: Likely Unsafe when used orally ( 4 , 12 ); avoid using.

LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

|

#### General

Orally, there is limited information available about the adverse effects of blessed thistle when used in medicinal amounts.

#### Dermatologic

Topically, blessed thistle can cause an allergic reaction, such as dermatitis, in individuals sensitive to the Asteraceae/Compositae family. Members of this family include ragweed, chrysanthemums, marigolds, daisies, and many other herbs ( 12 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, when blessed thistle is used in doses higher than 5 grams per cup of tea, it can cause stomach irritation and vomiting ( 12 ).

#### Immunologic

Blessed thistle can cause allergic reaction in individuals with sensitivity to the Asteraceae/Compositae family, which includes ragweed, chrysanthemums, marigolds, daisies, and many other herbs ( 12 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Dyspepsia.

Although there has been interest in using oral blessed thistle for dyspepsia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of blessed thistle for this purpose.

More evidence is needed to rate blessed thistle for this use.

#### Adult
Oral:

Traditionally, blessed thistle tea has been prepared by steeping 1.5-3 grams of the dried, flowering tops in boiling water and then straining. One cup of tea is taken three times daily. As a liquid extract (1:1 in 25% alcohol), blessed thistle has been used in doses of 1.5-3 mL taken three times daily.

#### General

The applicable parts of blessed thistle are the flowering tops, leaves, and upper stems. Constituents of blessed thistle include alkaloids, cnicin, benedictin, mucilage, polyacetylene, absinthin, triterpenoids, flavonoids, tannins, and phytosterines ( 36641 , 36656 , 36657 , 93575 ). Blessed thistle contains volatile essential oils, including P-cymene, fenchon, citral and cinnamaldehyde ( 36641 ), and is comprised of 8% tannins ( 4 ). Blessed thistle also contains lignans, including trachelogenin, arctigenin, and nortracheloside ( 36659 ).

#### Analgesic effects

In a rat model of nociceptive pain, a methanolic extract of blessed thistle leaf, as well as the major constituent of blessed thistle, cnicin, attenuates pain behaviors when compared with control ( 106721 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

In animal research, cnicin had mild anti-inflammatory effects ( 13466 ). The constituents, arctiin and tracheloside, are metabolized following oral ingestion, producing compounds that inhibit cyclic-AMP phosphodiesterase and histamine release from mast cells ( 11 , 36646 ).

#### Anti-microbial effects

Blessed thistle may have bacteriostatic properties due to its volatile oil content. The sesquiterpene lactone constituent, cnicin, may have antibacterial and antitumor activity ( 11 , 36639 ).  Its constituents cnicin and polyacetylene have shown antibacterial properties in vitro ( 36640 , 36641 , 36649 ). More specifically, in vitro studies have shown activity against Bacillus subtilis, Brucella species, Escherichia coli, Proteus species, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Staphylococcus aureus and Streptococcus faecalis ( 36640 , 36649 , 36641 ). However, blessed thistle constituents do not seem to have in vitro effects against Klebsiella, Pseudomonas, S. aureus, S. typhi, or yeast ( 36660 , 36643 , 36661 ). One lignan constituent, arctigenin, has been found to reduce in vitro human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) replication by inhibiting HIV type-1 integrase ( 36647 )

#### Anti-proliferative effects

The sesquiterpene lactone constituent, cnicin, may have antitumor activity ( 11 , 36639 ).  In vitro, cnicin and the lignan arctigenin have exhibited cytotoxic activity against leukemia, hepatoma, and sarcoma cells ( 36645 , 36648 , 36649 , 36650 , 36652 ).

#### Gastrointestinal effects

Traditionally, blessed thistle is used for loss of appetite and indigestion. Cnicin and other bitter constituents of blessed thistle stimulate secretion of saliva and stomach acid, which is thought to aid digestion and increase hunger ( 19 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS effects

There is interest in using blessed thistle extract to treat Alzheimer disease (AD) and Parkinson disease (PD) due to its in vitro ability to inhibit acetylcholinesterase and tyrosinase ( 93575 ). Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors stop the breakdown of acetylcholine. These agents are used in AD to compensate for decreased acetylcholine levels and to alleviate AD symptoms. Tyrosinase acts in the neuromelanin-biosynthetic pathway and can contribute to dopamine neurotoxicity and neurodegeneration in PD.
