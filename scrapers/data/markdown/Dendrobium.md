## Overview

Dendrobium is a large genus of orchids that are native to areas of China, Hong Kong, Taiwan, India, Thailand, Vietnam, and other temperate and tropical Asian regions. Many are epiphytes or lithophytes, which grow attached to other plants or rocks but take all their nutrients from the air or rainwater ( 114750 ). Some species of dendrobium, such as Dendrobium officinale and D. nobile, are considered critically endangered plants; they are now being cultivated to provide increased harvests and stress resistance ( 99899 , 114941 ). Dendrobium nobile and Dendrobium officinale are recognized in the Chinese Pharmacopoeia; however, other Dendrobium species are also of interest for medicinal use ( 99899 ). Dendrobium has been used in some supplements for athletic performance, with some experts touting dendrobium as a replacement for the stimulant dimethylamylamine (DMAA). However, there is concern that these products may actually be adulterated with synthetic stimulants. One specific dendrobium-containing supplement (Craze, Driven Sports) was found to contain phenethylamine, an amphetamine-like stimulant, which some experts say does not occur naturally in any significant amount in dendrobium plants ( 17966 , 17967 , 17968 ). The manufacturer was the subject of a class action lawsuit ( 17965 ) and received an official warning letter from the FDA ( 100517 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, dendrobium is used for athletic performance. Dendrobium is also used for dry mouth, cough, abdominal pain, gastritis, peptic ulcers, ulcerative colitis, Crohn disease, erectile dysfunction (ED), anorexia nervosa, tuberculosis, and nausea and vomiting.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of dendrobium.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Orally, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted. The only reports of adverse effects, including jitteriness, upset stomach, and insomnia, are related to the use of a combination product that was found to contain amphetamine-like ingredients ( 90320 , 100517 ). It is unclear whether any of these effects can be attributed to dendrobium.

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, upset stomach has been reported in patients taking a specific pre-workout supplement (Craze, Driven Sports) purportedly containing dendrobium extract plus creatine, betaine anhydrous, and other ingredients (doses unknown) daily for 6 weeks ( 90320 ). It's unclear whether this was due to dendrobium, other ingredients, or the combination. It should be noted that this product has been identified and formally documented by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as being adulterated with amphetamine-like ingredients ( 100517 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Orally, jitteriness and transient difficulty sleeping have been reported in patients taking a specific pre-workout supplement (Craze, Driven Sports) purportedly containing dendrobium extract plus creatine, betaine anhydrous, and other ingredients (doses unknown) daily for 6 weeks ( 90320 ). It's unclear whether these were due to dendrobium, other ingredients, or the combination. It should be noted that this product has been identified and formally documented by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as being adulterated with amphetamine-like ingredients ( 100517 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of dendrobium for any use.

#### ANTICONVULSANTS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

The dendrobium constituent dendrobine is reported to have convulsant effects ( 7162 ). Theoretically, combining dendrobium with anticonvulsant drugs might decrease drug effectiveness and increase the risk of seizure. Some anticonvulsant drugs include phenobarbital, primidone (Mysoline), valproic acid (Depakene), gabapentin (Neurontin), carbamazepine (Tegretol), phenytoin (Dilantin), and others.

#### SEIZURE THRESHOLD LOWERING DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

The dendrobium constituent dendrobine is reported to have convulsant effects ( 7162 ). Theoretically, combining dendrobium with pro-convulsant drugs might increase the risk of seizure. Some drugs that lower the seizure threshold include anesthetics (propofol, others), antiarrhythmics (mexiletine), antibiotics (amphotericin, penicillin, cephalosporins, imipenem), antidepressants (bupropion, others), antihistamines (cyproheptadine, others), immunosuppressants (cyclosporine), narcotics (fentanyl, others), stimulants (methylphenidate), theophylline, and others.

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOTENSIVE EFFECTS

The dendrobium constituent dendrobine is reported to have hypotensive effects ( 7162 ). Theoretically, combining dendrobium with other herbs or supplements with hypotensive effects might increase the risk of hypotension. Some of these herbs and supplements include andrographis, casein peptides, cat's claw, coenzyme Q-10, fish oil, L-arginine, lycium, stinging nettle, theanine, and others.

---

#### SEIZURE THRESHOLD LOWERING HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS

The dendrobium constituent dendrobine is reported to have convulsant effects ( 7162 ). Theoretically, combining dendrobium with other pro-convulsant supplements might increase the risk of seizure. Some seizure threshold lowering supplements include butanediol (BD), cedar leaf, Chinese club moss, EDTA, folic acid, gamma butyrolactone (GBL), gamma hydroxybutyrate (GHB), ginkgo, glutamine, huperzine A, hydrazine sulfate, hyssop oil, juniper, L-carnitine, melatonin, rosemary, sage, wormwood, and others.

#### EPILEPSY

The dendrobium constituent dendrobine is reported to have convulsant effects ( 7162 ). Theoretically, taking dendrobium might increase the risk of seizure. Until more is known, use cautiously or avoid in epileptic patients or patients prone to seizure.

None known.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the presentation or treatment of overdose with dendrobium.

#### Absorption

The polysaccharide constituents of dendrobium are not absorbed ( 114941 ).

#### General

The stems of dendrobium are most commonly used for medicinal purposes, although other parts of the plant have also been used. Two species of dendrobium are primarily used medicinally, Dendrobium nobile and Dendrobium officinale. Polysaccharides are the major constituents in dendrobium, and typically consist of the monosaccharides glucose and D-mannose, with lesser amounts of galactose, xylose, rhamnose, and arabinose ( 100586 , 114935 , 114941 , 114942 ). Other constituents include alkaloids (dendrobine, dendroxine, dendramine), glycosides (dendrosides, dendronobilosides, citrusin-C), phenylpropanoids (moupinamide), phenanthrene, zhepiresinol, bibenzyl compounds (dendrophenol, gigantol), flavonoids (naringenin, isoliquiritin), volatile oils, and amino acids ( 7162 , 90320 , 114749 , 114750 , 114937 , 114942 ).

#### Analgesic effects

The dendrobine constituent is thought to have analgesic effects ( 7162 ). However, these effects have not been documented in humans.

#### Anti-aging effects

In mice with signs of aging induced by D-galactose, alcoholic extracts of dendrobium stem reverse age-related weight gain and lipid accumulation and improve immune function. The extracts also protect against thinning of the dermis and development of necrotic neuronal cells in the hippocampus, which cause cognitive decline. Mechanisms include downregulating aging and senescence genes in the brain, increasing activity of the antioxidants superoxide dismutase, catalase, and glutathione, and reducing malondialdehyde levels ( 114934 ).

#### Antidiabetes effects

In a mouse model of type 2 diabetes, water extracts of dendrobium stems, and leaf polysaccharide extracts reduce food intake, glucose intolerance, insulin resistance, and weight gain. Dendrobium also partially reverses histopathological damage, including pancreatic islet cell loss, atrophy and necrosis, and hepatic steatosis and necrosis. It also improves beta-cell function, and hepatic glycogen levels ( 114936 , 114944 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

In vitro research shows that some but not all polysaccharides present in Dendrobium officinale appear to have free radical-scavenging effects ( 100588 ). In vitro and animal research also shows that the polysaccharides from dendrobium have gastroprotective effects through inhibition of oxidative stress-induced apoptosis of gastric mucosal cells ( 100587 ). In sleep-deprived, amnesic mice, an ethanolic extract of dendrobium repairs oxidative damage in the brain cortex and hippocampus ( 114750 ).

#### Cardioprotective effects

A water extract of Dendrobium officinale seems to exert some cardioprotective effects in a mouse model of myocardial ischemia, possibly through antioxidant effects and inhibition of cardiac cell apoptosis ( 99900 ).

#### Cognitive effects

In mice that are sleep-deprived, rendering them amnesic, an ethanolic extract of dendrobium reverses cognitive defects, and improves short-term, long-term, and spatial memory. It decreases serotonin and malondialdehyde activity, and increases norepinephrine, superoxide dismutase, and catalase activity in the brain, all of which are disrupted by sleep deprivation. It also repairs oxidative damage in the brain cortex and hippocampus ( 114750 ).

#### Convulsant effects

The dendrobine constituent is thought to have convulsant effects ( 7162 ). However, these effects have not been documented in humans.

#### Gastrointestinal effects

In vitro and in vivo research shows that dendrobium extracts have beneficial effects on the intestinal microbiome, restoring its diversity and abundance ( 114749 , 114938 ). Common findings are a decreased Firmicutes / Bacteroidetes ratio ( 114934 , 114935 , 114936 ), increased abundance of Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus, Bacteroides, Actinobacteria, Desulfobacteria, and Prevotella ( 114749 , 114933 , 114934 , 114935 , 114936 , 114940 , 114941 ), and decreased abundance of Firmicutes, Lachnoclostridium, Proteobacteria, Escherichia-Shigella, Ruminococcus, and Brevibacterium ( 114749 , 114934 , 114935 , 114938 , 114940 , 114941 ). The polysaccharides present in dendrobium are metabolized by gut bacteria to short-chain fatty acids, including butyric and propionic acids, providing carbon sources for some gut bacteria, and lowering the intestinal pH, which promotes growth of beneficial bacteria ( 114935 , 114936 , 114941 ). In a rat model of ulcerative colitis, giving a dendrobium extract orally for 4 weeks decreases diarrhea, rectal blood loss, weight loss, inflammatory cell infiltration in the colon, and expression of the inflammatory cytokines interleukin (IL)-1-beta, IL-6, and tumor necrosis factor ( 114942 ). Mechanisms include increased expression of the colon tight junction proteins occludin, claudin-1, and zona occludens-1 (ZO-1), leading to reduced intestinal permeability,  suppression  of the NLRP3 inflammasome, and beta-arrestin-1 activation ( 100586 , 114942 ).

#### Hepatic effects

In animal models of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), and non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, dendrobium extracts protect against hepatic fat deposition, inflammation, and steatosis. Delivery of lipopolysaccharides from the gut to the liver is reduced due to a reduction in intestinal permeability brought about by increased expression of the tight junction proteins occludin, claudin-1, and ZO-1. Levels of inflammatory markers such as interleukin (IL)-1-beta, IL-6, and tumor necrosis factor-alpha are reduced. Quantities of lipid droplets, triglycerides, fatty acids, and cholesterol in the liver are also reduced, and increases in the liver enzymes, aspartate aminotransferase, alanine aminotransferase, and gamma-glutamyl transferase are reversed. The extracts also decrease fatty acid synthase, stearoyl-coenzyme A desaturase-1, and fatty acid transport protein-2 levels, and increase the fatty acid beta-oxidation-related proteins, carnitine palmitoyltransferase-1-alpha and acyl-coenzyme A oxidase-1 ( 114939 , 114940 ).

#### Hyperglycemic effects

The dendrobine constituent is thought to have hyperglycemic effects ( 7162 ). However, these effects have not been documented in humans.

#### Hypotensive effects

The dendrobine constituent is thought to have hypotensive effects ( 7162 ). However, these effects have not been documented in humans.

#### Immunomodulating effects

In vitro research shows that dendrobium extracts have immunomodulating activity, inhibiting activation of the NF-kappa-B and MAPK signaling pathways, thereby inhibiting expression of tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), interleukin (IL)-6, granulocyte-macrophage colony stimulating factor, monocyte chemoattractant protein-1 (MCP-1), and thymus and activation-regulated chemokine (TARC) in HaCaT cells ( 100588 , 114937 ). In animal research, dendrobium stem extracts reduce levels of the inflammatory mediators, C-reactive protein and TNF-alpha, and increase levels of complement component-4 (C4) ( 114941 ).  In mice with cyclophosphamide-induced immune deficiency, polysaccharides extracted from dendrobium leaf reverse atrophy of the thymus and spleen, restore the colon epithelial barrier, and reverse liver necrosis and inflammatory cell infiltration. The extract restores serum immunoglobulin A levels, inhibits expression of the proinflammatory cytokines IL-1-beta, IL-6, transforming growth factor-beta-1, and promotes expression of the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 ( 114933 ). In a mouse model of allergic rhinitis, giving an extract of dendrobium reduces nasal scratching and sneezing, and reverses nose and lung inflammation and infiltration of inflammatory cells. Levels of the proinflammatory cytokines IL-4, IL-6, and IL-17 are decreased, while levels of regulatory T-cells, and the anti-inflammatory cytokines IL-2, IL-10, and interferon gamma are increased. Differentiation of the regulatory T-cells is protected due to inhibition of the P13K/AKT/mTOR pathway by the extract ( 114749 ). Research in animals with atopic dermatitis shows that an ethanol extract of dendrobium applied to the skin reduces scratching, skin thickness, and infiltration of mast cells and eosinophils, while decreasing levels of immunoglobulin E, IL-4, and IL-6 ( 114937 ).

#### Stimulant effects

Some dendrobium-containing products are used as a pro-workout supplement for boosting performance. This is due to stimulant effects of some purported constituents of dendrobium. Some products contain the stimulant phenylethylamine, which appears to have amphetamine-like effects ( 17966 , 17967 , 17968 ). However, experts do not believe that phenylethylamine is a naturally occurring constituent of dendrobium. It's thought that some products may be spiked with this stimulant ( 17966 ).

#### Uric acid lowering effects

In a rat model of hyperuricemia, continuous administration of a macroporous resin extract of dendrobium leaf for 9-12 weeks decreases serum uric acid,  increases fecal uric acid, and increases the fractional excretion of uric acid. Mechanisms for the hypouricemic effects include reduced xanthine oxidase activity in the serum, liver, and intestine, decreased adenosine deaminase activity in the liver and intestine, and regulation of the intestinal uric acid transporters GLUT9 and ABCG2 ( 105849 , 114943 ).

#### Weight-loss effects

In animal research, extracts of dendrobium stems inhibit weight gain in mice fed a high-fat diet ( 114938 , 114940 ).
