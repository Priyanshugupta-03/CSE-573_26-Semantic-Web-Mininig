## Overview

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) is an ancient system of medicine that encompasses a range of practices including acupuncture, acupressure, meditation, martial arts, herbal medicine, Feng Shui, and massage. Some additional therapies include diet, nutrition and lifestyle counseling, and mind-body practices, such as tai chi and qi gong. These modalities and treatment techniques share a theoretical framework defined by the yin and yang relationship ( 102105 ). TCM is widely practiced in Asian countries, including China, Taiwan, and Singapore ( 102105 ). Most Chinese hospitals have pharmacies that provide both modern conventional medications and TCM herbal preparations ( 7126 ). TCM has also experienced a surge in popularity in Western countries, where it is sometimes used for promoting wellness, preventing illness, and treating chronic conditions ( 15310 , 102105 ). Many practitioners of TCM are not legally recognized or licensed in North America. Some people who practice TCM have a Doctor of Oriental Medicine degree (OMD or DOM); however, this reflects training, not licensure. Standards for this degree are not consistently defined ( 15310 ).The prevalent use of herbs and combinations of herbal ingredients as part of TCM has led to concerns related to product quality ( 15310 ). There have been reported issues with misidentification of herbal species, which can lead to substitution of potentially toxic plants ( 12783 , 12784 , 12785 , 12786 , 12787 ). In several instances, TCM herbal formulations have also been found to be contaminated with high levels of heavy metals including lead, arsenic, and mercury. Many other products have been found to contain prescription pharmaceuticals ( 8010 ).

Traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) began over 2000 years ago. At that time there was no scientific concept of disease or pharmacology in terms that can be related to our modern understanding of medicine. Therefore, the principles of TCM were formed based on the philosophy of Taoism rather than science ( 15314 ). In TCM, people are diagnosed through evaluation of the tongue and the radial pulse, and by a series of signs and symptoms that cannot be directly correlated to Western medicine ( 15309 ). To understand TCM, it is important to know the meaning of the terminology used in its practice.

#### Acupuncture

Acupuncture involves inserting needles into over 350 key points along meridians in the body. Stimulating these points through a needle or pressure (e.g., acupressure) is thought to stimulate the body to correct the flow and balance of qi ( 15309 ).

#### Diagnosis

Pulse diagnosis is an important diagnostic tool used in TCM, often in conjunction with tongue diagnosis. TCM practitioners take radial pulses on each side and in three different positions. The pulses are thought to provide information about the internal organs ( 15309 ). The appearance of the tongue is also used to assess potential illness. Cues such as tongue moistness, coloring, or coating suggest certain conditions ( 15309 ).

#### Energy

In TCM, health concerns are mainly understood as disturbances in the flow of energy through the body. Qi, pronounced "chee," refers to the total energy of the body ( 7126 , 15309 ). There are 12 meridians forming a continuous pathway throughout the body. Qi circulates through the body on these meridians. Qi gong is an exercise, practiced either through physical movements or meditation, that is used to stimulate qi and maintain energy flow and balance. Tai chi is a specific form of qi gong that was originally developed in China as a martial art for self-defense ( 15872 , 96397 ). It involves controlled breathing and slow and rhythmic circular body movements, which is thought to facilitate the flow of qi ( 96395 , 96397 ).

#### Herbs and herbal combinations

As with all TCM therapies, herbals are used to correct energy flow and balance. Herbs are described as having four qualities: Action. Herbs are often described based on their primary action such as a "tonic" or an astringent ( 7126 ). Affinity. Herbs can be described based on which organ system they have an affinity for. In TCM, most herbs are taken as a fixed formula or a fixed combination of several herbs. Each individual herb is thought to address a particular imbalance in an ill person. Specific formulations are prescribed based on TCM-diagnosed imbalances. In each herbal formula there is usually a chief or main ingredient along with several other supportive ingredients ( 7126 ). Nature. The nature of an herb usually describes whether it has "cooling" or "heating" effects. An herb's nature might also be described as energizing or relaxing ( 7126 ). Taste. Herbs can have different effects based on tastes such as sweet, sour, bitter, spicy, salty, or bland ( 7126 ).

#### Moxibustion

In moxibustion, an herb is burned above the skin or on acupuncture points for therapeutic effect. This can be performed by burning a cone, stick, or loose herb a short distance away from the skin. The burning herb may also be placed on the head of an acupuncture needle to increase the temperature gradient of the needle ( 29750 , 29751 ).

#### Yin and yang

In China, yin and yang are believed to be the two forces that control the universe. Virtually all medical problems and illnesses are considered to be due to imbalances in one of these forces. Yin is the feminine side of nature and includes tranquility, darkness, cold, wetness, and depth. Yang is masculine and represents light, heat, activity, dryness, and height. Yin and yang are not the same as good and bad. Instead they are considered complementary forces. TCM intends to correct imbalances of these forces to cure disease ( 7126 ).
