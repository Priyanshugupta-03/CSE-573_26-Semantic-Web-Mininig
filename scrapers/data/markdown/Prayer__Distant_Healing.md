Caution

Do not confuse prayer or distant healing with Guided Imagery , Mindfulness , Mantra Meditation , Qi Gong , Reiki Therapy , or Spiritual Healing .

## Overview

Prayer is one of the oldest and most widespread interventions used with the intention of combatting illness and promoting good health ( 99381 ). Prayer is a "reverent petition," or the act of asking for something while aiming to connect with God or another object of worship. Prayer on behalf of the ill or dying, considered a form of complementary medicine, has played a prominent role throughout history and is a part of many religious traditions ( 28090 ). Distant healing is the projection of healing intention across distance for the well-being of another. Distant healing encompasses a broader range of practices than prayer, and may or may not employ recruitment of God, religious principles, or spiritual forces to aid in the process ( 28090 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Prayer and distant healing have been practiced safely in clinical research, without reports of adverse effects ( 16204 , 28060 , 28061 , 28063 , 28065 , 28066 , 28067 , 28069 , 28070 , 28072 )( 28073 , 28078 , 28083 , 28084 , 28085 , 28085 , 28087 , 28088 , 28088 , 28089 )( 88729 , 99374 , 99375 , 99376 , 99377 , 99378 , 99379 , 99381 , 99382 , 99383 )( 99384 ).

However, prayer or distant healing should not replace treatment for an illness when standard treatments exist, as this may result in disease complications or even death ( 28093 ).

PREGNACY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no reason to expect safety concerns when used appropriately. Prayer or distant healing should not replace treatment for an illness when standard treatments exist, as this may result in disease complications or even death ( 28093 ).

#### General

Prayer and distant healing are well tolerated. No adverse effects have been reported in clinical research.

Possibly Ineffective
Overall mortality.

Intercessory prayer does not appear to affect overall mortality in acutely ill patients.

A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials shows that daily intercessory prayer for up to 15 months does not reduce the risk of death in adult or pediatric patients with various health conditions, such as leukemia or heart disease, when compared with control ( 28077 , 99381 ). Additionally, population research in elderly adults has found that regularly participating in a religious activity, such as prayer, is not associated with a reduction in mortality rate after 6 years of follow up when compared with those not participating in religious activities ( 28080 ).

Postoperative recovery.

Intercessory prayer does not appear to improve postoperative recovery or reduce postoperative complications in adults undergoing cardiac surgery.

Preliminary clinical research in adults undergoing CABG surgery shows that daily, directive, intercessory prayer by Christian prayer groups for 14 days does not reduce postoperative complications, such as cardiac or infectious complications, when compared with no intervention. In addition, patients who were informed that they were receiving prayer developed 14% more complications when compared with patients who received prayer but were blinded to prayer status ( 16204 ). Other preliminary clinical research in adults shows that listening to a pre-recorded, directive prayer throughout cardiac surgery does not reduce the duration of intubation, intensive care, or hospitalization when compared with listening to a specific relaxation tape, Hemi-Sync, or a control tape ( 28083 ).

Warts.

Intercessory prayer and distant healing do not appear to affect healing rates in patients with warts.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with long-term warts on the hands shows that receiving intercessory and ritual prayer by a Muslim prayer leader once does not affect wart healing rates after 6 weeks when compared with blinded or non-blinded control ( 99378 ). Preliminary clinical research in patients with various skin warts also shows that regular distant healing by a professional healer for 6 weeks does not affect healing rates or wart size when compared with control ( 28089 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Alcohol use disorder.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in patients with alcohol use disorder.

Preliminary clinical research in adults initiating alcohol dependence treatment shows that daily intercessory prayer by volunteers, combined with standard treatment, for 6 months does not decrease alcohol consumption when compared with standard treatment alone ( 28061 ).

Anxiety.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in otherwise healthy patients with anxiety.

Preliminary clinical research in undergraduate students shows that daily nondirective, intercessory prayer by a single volunteer for 3-6 weeks reduces anxiety scores when compared with no intervention. However, the validity of this study is limited by inclusion of subjects with and without anxiety at baseline ( 28063 ).

Arrhythmia.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for arrhythmia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Cancer.

It is unclear if participating in prayer, or being the recipient of prayer, is beneficial in patients with cancer.

Preliminary observational research in Christian adults with advanced cancer has found that higher individual prayer activity is associated with longer overall survival when compared to those with lower individual prayer activity. Subgroup analyses suggest that this association is more prominent in patients still receiving antineoplastic therapy than those exclusively receiving palliative care ( 99380 ). Other preliminary clinical research in adults with cancer shows that nondirective intercessory prayer by a Christian prayer group for 6 months modestly improves spiritual well-being scores, but does not improve quality of life scores, when compared with no intervention ( 99379 ).

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in patients with CVD.

While some preliminary clinical research inconsistently showed that intercessory prayer was beneficial for patients admitted to the hospital with CVD, a larger, more recent, clinical trial failed to confirm these results. Two small clinical trials show that daily, directive, intercessory prayer by Christian or mixed religion prayer groups throughout a CVD-related hospitalization reduced either the length of hospitalization or new cardiac complications when compared with control ( 28067 , 28084 ). However, a large clinical trial in adults hospitalized with CVD-related illnesses shows that daily, directive, intercessory prayer for 28 days does not reduce length of hospitalization or new cardiac complications when compared with control ( 28085 ). Other preliminary clinical research in adults recently discharged from a CVD-related hospitalization shows that weekly intercessory prayer for 26 weeks does not prevent cardiac arrest, CVD-related emergency department visits, CVD-related hospital readmissions, or death when compared with control ( 28066 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

It is unclear if distant healing is beneficial in patients with CFS.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with CFS shows that distant healing by professional healers for 6 months does not improve mental health or general health status when compared with control. Subgroup analyses show that results were not different between subjects that did and did not know their treatment allocation ( 99384 ).

Critical illness (trauma).

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for critical illness, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Depression.

It is unclear if prayer or distant healing is beneficial in patients with depression.

Preliminary clinical research in adults admitted to a hospital for depression shows that daily distant healing by trained healers for 6 weeks does not reduce depression scores when compared with control ( 28070 ). Other preliminary clinical research in adults with depression shows that weekly nondirective, intercessory prayer with a Christian minister, first for 90 minutes and then for 60 minutes each session, for 6 weeks reduces depression and anxiety scores by 71% and 80%, respectively, when compared with no intervention ( 99374 ). The improvements in depression and anxiety scores persisted for at least one year ( 99375 ).

Diabetes.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for diabetes, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

HIV/AIDS.

It is unclear if prayer or distant healing is beneficial in patients with HIV/AIDS.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with advanced AIDS shows that distant healing by professional healers of varied faiths for 6 months reduces AIDS severity scores, new AIDS-related illnesses, and hospitalizations by 70%, 83%, and 75%, respectively, when compared with control. However, disease-related markers, such as CD4+, were not different between groups ( 28060 ). In contrast, more recent clinical research in adults with advanced AIDS shows that distant healing by professional healers for 6 months does not improve AIDS severity scores, new AIDS-related illnesses, hospitalizations, or disease-related markers, such as CD4+, when compared with untrained distant healers or control ( 88729 ).

Hypertension.

It is unclear if distant healing is beneficial in patients with hypertension.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with hypertension shows that weekly distant healing by professional healers for 15 weeks does not decrease systolic or diastolic blood pressure when compared with control ( 28072 ). In contrast, other preliminary clinical research shows that regular distant healing for up to 6 months decreases systolic blood pressure, but not diastolic blood pressure, by 42% more than control ( 28073 ).

Infertility.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for infertility, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Kidney failure.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in patients with kidney failure.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with kidney failure shows that directive intercessory prayer by a Catholic prayer group at least 5 times per week for 6 weeks does not affect blood pressure, hematocrit, weight gain, hospitalizations, or depression scores when compared with no intervention ( 28078 ).

Leukemia.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in children with leukemia.

Preliminary, low-quality, clinical research in children with leukemia shows that daily intercessory prayer by a Christian prayer group for 15 months does not improve overall survival when compared with no intervention. However, the study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 28065 ).

Low birth weight.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial for preventing low birth weight.

Preliminary clinical research in pregnant females shows that receiving daily, directive, intercessory prayer by volunteers for 9 days does not reduce the risk of low birth weight when compared with control ( 99376 ).

Meningitis.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for meningitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Menopausal symptoms.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for menopausal symptoms, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in patients with migraine headache.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with migraine headache shows that participating in a weekly 45-minute, nondirective, self-prayer program for 8 weeks, in addition to taking propranolol 40 mg twice daily, slightly reduces migraine pain scores when compared with taking propranolol alone ( 99382 ).

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if distant healing is beneficial in patients with chronic pain.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with chronic pain shows that daily to weekly distant healing by professional healers for up to 2 months does not improve pain scores, pain functioning scores, or quality of life scores when compared with simulated healing (sham intervention) or no intervention ( 28069 , 99383 ).

Preterm labor.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial for preventing preterm labor.

Preliminary clinical research in pregnant females shows that receiving daily directive, intercessory prayer by volunteers for 9 days does not reduce the risk of preterm labor when compared with control ( 99376 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if prayer or distant healing is beneficial in patients with RA.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with RA shows that nondirective intercessory prayer for 3 days by Christian ministers does not improve RA pain or severity scores or reduce the number of swollen or tender joints when compared with no intervention ( 28087 ). Similarly, preliminary clinical research in adults with RA shows that regular distant healing by professional healers for 6 weeks does not  improve pain or quality of life scores when compared with control. Subgroup analyses in patients aware of their treatment allocation showed similar results; however, the study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 99377 ).

Sepsis.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in patients with sepsis.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with sepsis shows directive intercessory prayer, once, 4-10 years AFTER the hospitalization occurred, is associated with 12.5% shorter length of stay, but no difference in mortality, when compared with control. The validity of these results is limited by the assumption that prayer affects events that already occurred ( 28074 ).

Sickle cell disease.

It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in patients with sickle cell disease.

Population research in adults with sickle cell disease has found that regular church attendance, but not regular prayer, is associated with lower pain scores when compared with those who do not regularly attend church or pray ( 28088 ).

Stress.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for stress, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Stroke.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for stroke, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Substance use disorder.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for substance use disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Wound healing.

Although there has been interest in using prayer or distant healing for wound healing, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of prayer or distant healing for this purpose.

Cardiac bypass graft (CABG) surgery . It is unclear if prayer is beneficial in patients undergoing CABG surgery.

Preliminary clinical research in adults undergoing CABG surgery shows that daily, directive, intercessory prayer by Christian prayer groups for 14 days does not reduce mortality or postoperative complications, such as cardiac or infectious complications, when compared with no intervention. In addition, patients who were informed that they were receiving prayer developed 14% more complications when compared with patients who received prayer, but were blinded to prayer status ( 16204 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the effectiveness of prayer and/or distant healing for these uses.

Prayer can be defined as "reverent petition," the act of asking for something while aiming to connect with God or another object of worship. Prayer on behalf of the ill or dying has played a prominent role throughout history and across cultures. Prayer is a part of many religious traditions and an aspect of spiritual, or faith, healing. It has also been studied as a form of complementary medicine ( 28090 ). Prayer is a highly prevalent form of social support and a coping mechanism for patients dealing with serious illness or undergoing medical procedures. Many patients welcome offers from their health care practitioners to pray with them for their well-being ( 28090 ). Different types of prayer may be used for different purposes ( 16200 ). "Intercessory prayer", involves a person or group asking a god to help another person in need. Intercessory prayer is organized, regular, and practiced by those who hold a belief that they are praying to God or a higher power ( 99381 ). "Conversational prayer" is an informal prayer or conversation with God or a higher power that involves seeking guidance, asking for health, and expressing thanks. "Meditative prayer" uses meditation on spiritual ideas in an attempt to reach a better understanding of God or a higher power. "Ritual prayer" is the recitation of standard, well-known prayers ( 16200 ). Distant healing may be defined as the projection of healing intention across distance for the well-being of another. Distant healing may or may not employ recruitment of God, religious principles, or spiritual forces to aid in the process. Distant healing is a broader concept than prayer. It includes prayer but also includes secular approaches to exerting remote healing influence. These include tasks such as attempting to mentally project influence across distance for another's well-being, without asking the intercession of a supreme being separate from oneself.

The mechanism for the function of distant prayer is unknown and the proposed mechanism might vary based on different religious beliefs ( 99381 ). The practice of direct or self-administered prayer can function as a coping behavior in diseases, for example in mental illnesses such as depression and anxiety ( 99374 ). Prayer might induce a relaxation response, with similar psychophysiological benefits as those attained in meditation and relaxation exercises. Prayer and spiritual faith may also contribute to a positive or expectant state of mind, supporting relaxation, reducing anxiety, and countering the effects of stress in the body. These mental and physiological factors may also operate similarly to or trigger the same mechanisms that account for the observed benefits of the placebo effect ( 99374 , 99377 ). The proposed mechanism for distant healing has been explained in relation to quantum mechanical theory. Theoretically, the human mind might intentionally form a quantum system with another via the use of intention, ritual, or prayer to create a system of mental connectedness. In this case, healing would occur during the connection of consciousness between individuals ( 28098 ). Prayer might have therapeutic effects through a variety of possible mechanisms. Meditative and other types of prayer might result in relaxation including a slowed heart and breathing rate and reduced blood pressure. Prayer may also improve positive emotions, resulting in improved feelings of well-being. Prayer might also be associated with a large placebo effect ( 16200 , 16203 ). Spiritual healing, especially intercessory prayer, has been the subject of clinical trials; however, these trials have produced inconsistent findings and most have had significant methodological flaws. Some experts argue that attempting to objectify and quantify supernatural spiritual activity in a clinical trial is a failed concept because of substantial inter-individual variability and the inability to control for placebo effects ( 16203 ).
