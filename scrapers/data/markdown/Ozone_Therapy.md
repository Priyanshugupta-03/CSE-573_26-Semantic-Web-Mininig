## Overview

Ozone is a naturally occurring unstable gas that consists of three oxygen atoms. This form of oxygen is a potent oxidizing agent ( 108270 ). Ozone is used medicinally for its antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory effects ( 108269 ).

## People Use This For

Ozone therapy is used for herniated disc, dental caries, dental hypersensitivity, dental plaque, diabetes, diabetic foot ulcers, pressure ulcers, hearing loss, HIV/AIDS, gingivitis, intermittent claudication, coronary heart disease (CHD), Meniere disease, Parkinson disease, tinnitus, osteomyelitis, empyema, abscesses, necrotizing fasciitis, age-related macular degeneration, back pain, cancer, rheumatoid arthritis (RA), temporomandibular disorders (TMD), postoperative pain, carpal tunnel syndrome, complex regional pain syndrome, fibromyalgia, halitosis, hepatitis, lichen planus, osteoarthritis, postherpetic neuralgia, periodontitis, Raynaud syndrome, wound healing, and other conditions. Ozone is also used to disinfect water.

Possibly Safe when injected into a herniated disc by a healthcare professional. Ozone has been injected into a herniated disc without evidence of adverse effects in multiple preliminary clinical trials ( 98150 , 98154 , 108270 , 108275 , 108279 ). However, ozone injected into the spine has been linked with rare cases of heart attack, pulmonary embolism, and death ( 102986 , 102989 ) and injection into a herniated disc has been linked with rare cases of Anton syndrome ( 98153 , 102994 ).

Possibly Unsafe when used via inhalation. Inhaled ozone therapy has been linked to cases of Anton syndrome, involving blindness secondary to ischemic stroke or encephalopathy ( 98145 ). Short-term exposure to inhaled ozone can impair breathing and deaden the sense of smell. Long-term exposure can cause lung damage ( 108278 ).

Likely Unsafe when used intravenously. Intravenous ozone infusion can cause pulmonary embolism, which can result in death ( 16183 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of ozone therapy when used by other routes of administration.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Ozone therapy, applied locally or rectally, has not been linked to serious adverse effects ( 16181 , 16182 , 106465 ); however, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes with these routes of administration has not been conducted. Inhaled ozone therapy and ozone therapy injected into a herniated disc have been linked to cases of Anton syndrome, involving blindness secondary to ischemic stroke or secondary to encephalopathy, and also cases of heart attack, pulmonary embolism, and death ( 98145 , 98153 , 102986 , 102989 , 102994 ). Intravenously, ozone infusion has been linked to pulmonary embolism and death ( 16183 ).

#### Cardiovascular

Inhaled ozone therapy and ozone therapy injected into a herniated disc have been associated with cases of Anton syndrome. Anton syndrome involves blindness secondary to ischemic stroke. The patient is typically unaware of the blindness due to cerebral damage ( 98145 , 98153 ). Intravenously, ozone infusion has been linked to pulmonary embolism and death ( 16183 ). Ozone injected into the spine has been linked with cases of heart attack, pulmonary embolism, and death. In a case report, an 80-year-old female developed pulmonary embolism resulting in death after receiving two ozone injections into the spine near a herniated disc ( 102986 ). In another case-report, a 66-year-old female developed a spinal cord infarction and ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) after receiving an ozone injection for disc herniation ( 102989 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Inhaled and injected ozone has been associated with blindness and encephalopathy. In a case report, a 36-year-old female presented with Anton syndrome, or ischemic stroke resulting in sudden bilateral blindness, nausea, vomiting and loss of strength after using inhalation ozone therapy for 1 week intended to help with hearing loss and tinnitus from Meniere disease ( 98145 ). Reversal of blindness was not reported. In another case report, a 54-year-old male developed Anton syndrome and blindness after an ozone injection for disc herniation. The blindness was followed by severe frontal headache, vomiting, and nausea ( 98153 ). In a case report, a healthy 66-year-old male presented to the emergency room with encephalopathy, fever, severe vision loss in both eyes, memory impairment, nausea, and vomiting after receiving an intramuscular paravertebral cervical injection of 20 mL containing ozone and lidocaine 2% ( 102994 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Back pain.

Research has evaluated ozone therapy injections for back pain caused by a herniated disc. Most observational research has found that injecting ozone into a herniated disc is associated with reduced back pain ( 88747 , 98150 , 108270 ). Preliminary clinical research and an analysis of preliminary clinical research also show that injecting 5-20 mL of ozone 20-30 mcg/mL into the herniated disc or injecting it into paravertebral muscles seems to improve back pain when compared with baseline or active control ( 98150 , 98154 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that injecting ozone into the herniated disc is as effective as microdiscectomy for reducing back pain ( 108275 ). Additionally, one small clinical study suggests that ozone therapy is most effective in males, patients with isolated radicular pain, patients with calcified disc herniation, and/or patients with symptoms lasting less than one year ( 108270 ).

Carpal tunnel syndrome.

A small clinical study in patients with mild or moderate carpal tunnel syndrome shows that receiving a single local intraarticular ozone injection in addition to wearing a resting volar wrist splint for 8 weeks results in greater pain reduction (64%) when compared with only wearing the wrist splint (45%) ( 102983 ).

Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19).

Research on the use of ozone therapy, including nebulized ozone and ozone autohemotherapy, for the treatment of COVID-19 has yielded mixed findings. It is also unclear if ozone autohemotherapy is beneficial for long-term symptoms occurring after recovery from COVID-19.

A small clinical study in adults with severe COVID-19 pneumonia shows that receiving ozone 8 mg via autohemotherapy twice daily for 5 days is associated with a time to clinical improvement of 7 days, compared with 28 days in those receiving standard care alone ( 106460 ). Another small, nonrandomized clinical study in adults hospitalized with mild to severe COVID-19 pneumonia shows that receiving ozone 3 mg via autohemotherapy once daily for 7 days along with standard care is associated with a mortality rate of 5%, compared with a mortality rate of 28% in those receiving standard care alone ( 106462 ). Conversely, a clinical study in adults with mild to moderate COVID-19 shows that receiving a combination of ozone 6 mg via twice daily 150-mL rectal insufflations and ozone 0.125 mg once daily via autohemotherapy along with standard care does not improve oxygenation, inflammatory markers, or disease severity when compared with standard care alone ( 106461 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the single blinded or unblinded nature of the studies. The use of nebulized ozone has also been investigated in patients with COVID-19 pneumonia receiving standard care. A small preliminary clinical trial shows that exposure to nebulized ozone gas with distilled water and olive oil for 5 days reduces length of stay in the hospital by about 2 days when compared with a control group not treated with ozone. After 5 days, 93% of patients no longer tested positive for COVID-19, compared with 20% of those given standard care alone. The ozone treatment protocol consisted of three, 10-minute sessions daily, each session separated by 5 minutes, providing 0.2 ppm ozone gas as cold vapor ( 108274 ). The use of ozone autohemotherapy has also been investigated in patients with post-acute symptoms of COVID-19. Preliminary clinical research in patients with fatigue following a positive COVID-19 infection in the past 6 months shows that receiving ozone 40-50 mg via autohemotherapy 1-3 times weekly for 2-3 weeks modestly improves feelings of fatigue over baseline in at least 67% of patients, with recovery in approximately 40% ( 108277 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a control group.

Dental caries.

A small meta-analysis shows that ozone therapy has inconsistent antimicrobial effects when compared with chlorhexidine and sealants for the treatment of dental caries ( 106465 ). The studies in this analysis were limited by risk of bias and imprecision of data.

Diabetes.

Preliminary clinical research shows that ozone therapy administered locally or through rectal insufflations of ozone gas improves blood glucose and markers of oxidative stress ( 16181 ). A clinical study in adults with type 2 diabetes shows that receiving a topical oral ozone gas regimen along with conventional periodontal treatment every 3 months does not improve glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels at 12 months when compared with conventional periodontal treatment alone ( 106463 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the unblinded nature of the study.

Diabetic foot ulcers.

Topical non-invasive ozone therapy has been evaluated for use in the treatment of diabetic foot ulcers with mixed findings. Invasive ozone therapy combined with topical ozone therapy has shown some benefit.

Most preliminary clinical research shows that adding topical non-invasive ozone therapy to standard therapy does not further improve ulcer healing, reduce ulcer size, or reduce amputations when compared to standard therapy alone ( 98149 , 106464 ). However, a more recent observational trial suggests that topical non-invasive ozone therapy is associated with a modest reduction in ulcer surface area and diameter after 21 days when compared with standard therapy alone. There were also modest reductions in the length of hospital stay, the number of revision surgeries required, and mortality ( 108273 ). Also, preliminary clinical research shows that non-invasive topical ozone therapy seems to reduce bacterial count ( 106464 , 108273 ). A combination of invasive and topical ozone therapy has shown benefit in some studies. One small clinical trial shows that ozone therapy via rectal insufflation with 10 mg of ozone and local ozone treatment, administered by covering the ulcer with a plastic bag, sealing and vacuuming the bag, and then filling the bag with 60 mg/L ozone for one hour, and finally applying ozonized sunflower oil to the lesion, seems to improve lesion healing and decrease the duration of hospitalization. However, it does not decrease the number of ulcer lesions when compared with antibiotic therapy ( 88741 ). Another preliminary clinical study in patients with diabetic foot ulcers shows that receiving ozone therapy in addition to standard therapy speeds up complete healing of diabetic foot ulcers when compared with standard therapy alone. Ozone therapy involved application of ozonated olive oil gel (Ozolive) to the wounds every 12 hours, subcutaneous local injection of ozone and a mixture of ozone given rectally or intravenously in addition to taking oral vitamin C. Treatment spanned 20 sessions ( 102992 ). This study was limited by incomplete outcome reporting. For example, amputation rates were not compared between treatment groups.

Fibromyalgia.

Observational research in patients with fibromyalgia has found that receiving ozone therapy mostly via autohemotherapy and some by ozone rectal insufflations is associated with improved symptoms in 70% of patients when compared with baseline ( 102997 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Gingivitis.

A small clinical study in adults with plaque-induced gingivitis shows that combining gaseous ozone therapy with periodontal treatment is less effective for reducing gingival inflammation than periodontal treatment alone. Effects were similar between smokers and non-smokers ( 106466 ). Similarly, a meta-analysis shows that adjuvant use of ozone therapy with nonsurgical periodontal treatment is no more effective for improving clinical attachment loss, periodontal parameters, or bacterial counts when compared with nonsurgical periodontal treatment alone or in combination with chlorhexidine ( 106467 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies.

Hearing loss.

Preliminary clinical research shows that ozone therapy via autohemotherapy where 100 mL of a patient's blood is mixed with a 1:1 gaseous mixture of oxygen and ozone and reinjected via intravenous (IV) infusion twice weekly for 5 weeks might improve hearing loss compared to placebo in patients with sudden sensorineural hearing loss ( 88743 ). This study is limited by incomplete reporting of results and flawed methodology.

Herniated disc.

Observational research in patients with persistent radicular pain refractory to conservative management and/or epidural steroid injection found that injecting ozone into the herniated disc is associated with a reduction in herniated disc surface area of about 20% in 75% of cases ( 108270 ). Also, a small clinical study shows that injecting ozone into the herniated disc is as effective as microdiscectomy for reducing radicular leg pain and disability and improving overall health and mobility ( 108275 ).

HIV/AIDS.

Preliminary clinical research shows that giving ozone via autohemotherapy three times weekly for 8 weeks does not improve immune response in patients with HIV ( 88745 ).

Intermittent claudication.

Preliminary clinical research shows that receiving intravenous ozone therapy 60 mcg/mL  in 500 mL of normal saline along with 30-minute aerosol oxygen-ozone baths to the lower legs daily for 10 days seems to improve walking distance compared to balneotherapy in patients with intermittent claudication ( 88738 ).

Meniere disease.

Preliminary clinical research shows that inhaled ozone 8 mcg/mL therapy at 60 mL/minute for 10 minutes plus tympanic membrane pressure massage for 10 minutes daily for 10 days does not improve hearing loss compared to baseline in patients with Meniere's disease ( 88749 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Meta-analyses of small, low-quality clinical studies in patients with knee osteoarthritis show that intra-articular ozone therapy improves pain for up to 6 months when compared with placebo. However, ozone therapy seems to be less effective than hyaluronic acid or platelet-rich plasma ( 102982 , 102987 , 102995 ). The validity of these analyses is limited by a high risk of bias and short duration of the included studies.

Periodontitis.

Topical ozone therapy has been evaluated for use in the treatment of periodontitis with mixed findings. Two small clinical studies in patients with chronic periodontitis show that applying ozone as ozonated olive oil to gum pockets is as effective as chlorhexidine 0.2% or 1% for improving most periodontal measures. In one study, ozonated olive oil was applied once immediately after scaling and root planing and then once more one week thereafter. In the other, ozonated olive oil gel (Bioemmei Srl) was applied two weeks after professional oral hygiene ( 102988 , 108276 ). However, in one of these studies the improvements in clinical attachment and gingivitis indices were similar to chlorhexidine 1% at 1 month, but not at 3 months ( 108276 ). Also, a small clinical study in patients with severe generalized periodontitis shows that applying gaseous ozone into periodontal pockets twice weekly for 2 weeks does not improve periodontal parameters when compared with baseline ( 102996 ). This study was limited due to a lack of comparator group.

Irrigation with aqueous ozone solution has also been investigated. A small clinical study in patients with chronic periodontitis shows that irrigating periodontal pockets with ozonized water for 2.5 minutes reduces dental plaque, but not gingival score, gum attachment, or probing pocket depth, when compared with using chlorhexidine gluconate 0.2% at a 3-month follow-up ( 102993 ).

Postherpetic neuralgia.

A preliminary clinical study in patients with postherpetic neuralgia shows that receiving ozone 40 mL via autohemotherapy three times weekly for 2 weeks addition to pharmacotherapy seems to improve pain at 1 month and 3 months when compared with pharmacotherapy alone ( 102990 ).

Postoperative pain.

A meta-analysis of small clinical trials in patients undergoing surgery for an impacted mandibular third molar shows that adjuvant ozone application modestly reduces pain and analgesic use for up to 7 days after surgery, but does not affect swelling or jaw movement restriction, when compared with no ozone treatment ( 108271 ). In one of the studies included in the analysis, a specific ozone product (Ozone gel, Aqua Ozone) was applied to the tooth socket for 2 minutes twice daily for 3 days in addition to using analgesics ( 98151 ). Other modes of application evaluated in the meta-analysis include the use of an extraoral ozone probe and ozonated water irrigation ( 108271 ).

Ozone therapy has also been examined in patients undergoing a root canal. Preliminary clinical research shows that aqueous ozone treatment with passive ultrasonic or sonic activation modestly reduces postoperative pain when compared with no ozone treatment or ozone treatment without activation or with manual dynamic activation ( 108269 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Preliminary clinical research in patients with RA shows that adding ozone therapy as 25 mg/L to 40 mg/L via rectal insufflation for 5 days weekly for 4 weeks to methotrexate therapy seems to improve disease activity measures and pain when compared with methotrexate alone ( 98148 ).

Scleroderma.

A small preliminary clinical trial in patients with digital ulcers related to systemic scleroderma shows that local ozone treatment modestly improves digital ulcer severity and hand function when compared with standard treatment alone. The hand was exposed to oxygen-ozone in a bag for 30 minutes daily for one week, followed by twice weekly for the next 3 weeks ( 108272 ). Another small clinical study shows that exposing the digits to oxygen-ozone for 30 minutes daily for 20 days results in the healing of digital ulcers in 96% of participants, compared with 44% of those given standard treatment with calcium channel blockers alone. Digits were exposed to a total volume of 20-50 mL of ozone at 52 mcg/mL in a bag using an ozone generator device (Humazon Promedic) ( 108280 ). Exposure to ozone also reduced ulcer pain and the number and duration of Raynaud attacks ( 108272 , 108280 ).

Tinnitus.

Preliminary clinical research shows that applying ozone autohemotherapy, where 100 mL of a patient's blood is mixed with a 1:1 gaseous mixture of oxygen and ozone, does not improve tinnitus compared to baseline and compared to a betahistidine control in patients with idiopathic tinnitus for at least 6 months ( 98152 ).

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . Limited research suggests that applying ozone topically may reduce pain in patients with TMD, but it does not appear to improve jaw mobility. Preliminary clinical research in patients with masticatory myofascial pain due to TMJ dysfunction shows that applying topical ozone therapy at 60% intensity to the site of greatest pain for 10 minutes three times per week for 2 weeks modestly reduces pain but does not improve jaw movement when compared with sham ozone therapy ( 98146 ). Ozone therapy has also been compared to active controls such as medication and occlusal splints. A small clinical study shows that applying topical ozone therapy at 30% intensity for 10 minutes three times per week for one week improves pain to a greater degree when compared with taking ketoprofen 150 mg and thiocolchicoside 8 mg twice daily for 7 days; however, mouth opening is not improved ( 98147 ). Another small clinical study in patients with TMD shows that receiving 60% ozone exposure to pain points for 10 minutes three times weekly for two weeks reduces pain to a similar degree as wearing an occlusal splint every night for four weeks ( 102985 ). This study was limited due to small sample size and differences in treatment duration.

More evidence is needed to rate ozone therapy for these uses.

Ozone therapy is administered via various routes. It is applied locally to teeth or tissues, through rectal insufflations, nasally, orally, intravaginally, and cutaneously. It is also applied ex vivo to blood, which is then reinfused (autohemotherapy) ( 16181 , 16182 , 16183 , 88749 , 108272 , 108273 , 108280 ). Ozone therapy has also been given as an intradiscal injection ( 88747 , 98150 , 98154 , 108270 , 108275 ). Some practitioners also infuse ozone intravenously; however, this can result in oxygen embolism and death ( 16183 ). For application, ozone gas is mixed with oxygen gas. In most cases, the mixture contains no more than 5% ozone, and 95% or more consists of oxygen gas ( 16183 ). However, a 1:1 mixture has been used in some clinical research ( 88743 , 98152 ).

Ozone is a naturally occurring unstable gas that consists of three oxygen atoms. This form of oxygen is a potent oxidizing agent ( 108270 ). Some researchers think that low ozone doses can significantly influence biochemical pathways to result in a therapeutic benefit. Ozone is thought to modulate antioxidant enzymes and nitric oxide pathways. By modulating antioxidant enzymes, low dose ozone might have a role in preventing oxidative damage, which plays a role in many conditions, including diabetes ( 16181 ). When applied to plasma, ozone generates specific amounts of hydrogen peroxide and lipid oxidation products. These molecules then diffuse into blood cells and affect various biochemical pathways. Repeated small doses of ozone therapy result in upregulation of antioxidant enzymes, including superoxide dismutase (SOD) and others ( 16182 , 16183 , 16184 ). Ozone therapy may also have anti-inflammatory effects. In human and laboratory research, ozone therapy seems to modulate levels of inflammatory cytokines and prostaglandins ( 108275 ). In patients with COVID-19, nebulized ozone has been shown to reduce levels of the inflammatory C-reactive protein (CRP) ( 108274 ). Research in animal models of diabetes suggests that ozone reduces oxidative damage, preserves beta-cell function, and improves glycemic control ( 16181 ). Ozone therapy is also of interest for wound healing, where it might improve microcirculation by improving the cell membranes in the capillary vessels ( 108272 ). Also, tissue biopsies of digital ulcers in patients with scleroderma suggest that local ozone therapy results in increased expression of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), which plays a role in wound healing, and decreased levels of endothelin-1 type A receptor (ETAR) autoantibodies, which can contribute to inflammation ( 108280 ).
