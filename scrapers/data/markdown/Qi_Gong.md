# Qi Gong
Other Common Names Ba Duan Jin, Baduanjin Qi Gong, Biyun Method, Biyun Qi Gong, Chan Mi Gong, Chi Gung, Chi Kung, Chi'I Kung, Dantian, Daoyin Qi Gong, Dongeui Qi Gong, Eight-Section Brocades Qi Gong, Energy Healing, Energy Health, Energy Medicine, Energy Therapy, Energy Work, EQT, External Qi Gong Therapy, Five Animals Qi Gong, Guolin, Hua Gong, Médecine Énergétique, Medical Qi Gong, QG, QI, Qi Gong Therapy, Qi Gong Yangsheng, Qigong, Qigong Healer, Qigong Therapy, Qigongology, Santé Énergétique, Thérapie Énergétique, Wuqinxi, Yi Jia Gong

Caution

Qi gong should not be confused with Kampo Medicine , Reiki Therapy , Tai Chi , or Traditional Chinese Medicine .

## Overview

Qi gong is a type of martial art-like exercise that is thought to integrate the mind and body and regulate the Qi ( 96796 , 96797 ). Qi gong originated in China and includes meditation, breathing, and focus ( 96789 , 96795 , 96797 ). There are several varieties of Qi gong. Some involve self-directed exercise with slow movements, whereas another form is an external type of bodywork that is performed by a trained practitioner or therapist ( 96789 , 96796 , 96797 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Qi gong has been safely practiced for up to 1 year in clinical research without reports of adverse outcomes ( 15856 , 15857 , 15858 , 15864 , 15865 , 15866 , 15867 , 15868 , 15869 , 15870 )( 15871 , 15872 , 15874 , 15875 , 88849 , 88853 , 88859 , 88866 , 88867 , 88874 )( 88875 , 88876 , 96793 , 96796 , 103912 , 107428 , 107430 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available. Pregnant patients should be advised to consult with their personal healthcare provider before initiating Qi gong or any other exercise program.

#### General

Practicing internal and external Qi gong is generally well tolerated.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Dizziness. Also, muscle soreness, muscle tension, and pain have been reported at similar or lower rates to other types of exercise.

#### Musculoskeletal

Practicing Qi gong has been associated with muscle soreness, muscle tension, and pain. In one clinical study, muscle soreness was reported in around 16% of individuals practicing Qi gong, while 30% of individuals following an exercise program reported this adverse effect. Muscle tension and pain (back pain or non-specific pain) were reported in 11% and 12% of Qi gong practitioners, respectively ( 96796 ).  Practicing Qi gong has been rarely associated with shoulder pain and plantar fasciitis ( 96793 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Practicing Qi gong has been associated with dizziness. In one clinical study, dizziness was reported in around 19% of individuals practicing Qi gong, while only 5% of individuals following an exercise program reported this adverse effect ( 96796 ).

Possibly Effective
Cancer-related fatigue.

Practicing Qi gong may reduce cancer-related fatigue.

Meta-analyses of clinical research in patients with cancer shows that practicing Qi gong, including Baduanjin Qi gong, improves cancer-related fatigue when compared with no treatment, usual care, or other types of support ( 96787 , 96788 , 106508 ).

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

Meta-analyses of small clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong modestly improves lung function, walking distance, quality of life, and dyspnea in patients with COPD.

A meta-analysis of 10 small, low-quality clinical studies in patients with COPD show that practicing various forms of Qi gong for 6 months to 1 year along with conventional therapy improves walking distance, lung function, and quality of life when compared with conventional therapy alone with or without control interventions like breathing techniques or walking ( 103912 ). Another meta-analysis of 16 small, low-quality clinical studies in patients with COPD shows that practicing Liuzijue Qi gong 5-21 times weekly for 3-12 months improves dyspnea, lung function, walking distance, and quality of life when compared with breathing exercises or conventional therapy alone ( 107430 ). A more recent clinical study in a small group of patients aged 40-75 years with stable COPD shows that practicing Qi gong for 30 minutes twice weekly for 12 weeks leads to similar improvements in endurance capacity when compared with aerobic exercise using a cycle ergometer ( 106510 ).

Hypertension.

A meta-analysis of several small clinical studies suggests that practicing Qi gong may reduce blood pressure in patients with hypertension.

Most clinical research in patients with hypertension shows that practicing Qi gong lowers systolic blood pressure (SBP) and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) and possibly reduces the risk of mortality ( 15858 , 88848 , 88849 , 96789 , 96794 , 96795 ); however, poor study quality limits the reliability of these findings. A meta-analysis of 14 small clinical studies in patients with hypertension shows that practicing Qi gong for 2-12 months reduces SBP by around 9 mmHg and DBP by around 5 mmHg when compared with no intervention. However, reductions in blood pressure with Qi gong were no different than those observed with aerobic exercise ( 107426 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Ankylosing spondylitis.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in patients with ankylosing spondylitis.

A small clinical study in patients with ankylosing spondylitis shows that training in Baduanjin Qi gong twice weekly for 4 weeks and then 3 times weekly for 8 weeks reduces fatigue and morning stiffness when compared with no intervention ( 103914 ).

Asthma.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in patients with asthma.

A small clinical study in patients with asthma shows that practicing Qi gong Yangsheng daily for 6 months improves peak flow variability and decreases hospitalizations, antibiotic use, and sick leave when compared to baseline ( 88853 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Atrial fibrillation.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in patients with atrial fibrillation.

A small clinical study in patients with chronic atrial fibrillation shows that practicing Qi gong exercises for 16 weeks improves exercise tolerance and walking distance when compared with a wait-list control ( 15871 ).

Autism spectrum disorder.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in children with autism spectrum disorder.

A small clinical study in children with autism spectrum disorder shows that administering a specific Qi gong sensory training program improves sensory impairment and adaptive behavior when compared to baseline ( 88854 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Back pain.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong may slightly improve low back pain, although it does not seem to improve back pain when compared with exercise programs targeting back pain.

A small clinical study in office workers with chronic low back pain shows that practicing a specific type of Qi gong (Guan Yin Zi Zai Gong) for 1 hour weekly for 6 weeks reduces pain and improves function when compared with a waitlist control ( 103908 ). However, another small clinical study in older adults with chronic low back pain shows that taking twelve 90-minute Qi gong (Nei Yang Gong) classes over 3 months does not improve back pain by a clinically meaningful amount, although it does seem to modestly improve function, when compared with no intervention ( 103911 ). Furthermore, a small clinical study in patients with chronic low back pain shows that practicing Qi gong for 12 weeks at home and as part of a once weekly supervised program might be less effective than following a specific exercise program designed to reduce back pain ( 96796 ).

Breast cancer.

Several small, low-quality clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong may improve quality of life and mood in patients with breast cancer.

A meta-analysis of 17 small, low-quality clinical studies in patients with breast cancer shows that practicing any form of Qi gong improves quality of life and symptoms of depression and anxiety, but does not reduce fatigue or sleep disturbances, when compared with control ( 106507 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity and risk of bias in the included studies and the imprecision of data.

Cancer.

Several small, low-quality clinical studies suggest that practicing Baduanjin Qi gong may improve quality of life and sleep quality in patients with cancer.

A meta-analysis of ten small, low-quality studies in patients with cancer shows that practicing Baduanjin Qi gong for 1-6 months improves quality of life and sleep quality when compared with control ( 106508 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity and risk of bias in the included studies. Conversely, a small clinical study in patients with various cancers shows that receiving training in medical Qi gong once or twice weekly and practicing at home for 8 weeks does not improve quality of life, gastrointestinal symptoms, or inflammation when compared with usual care alone ( 88855 ). However, this study may have been inadequately powered to detect differences between groups.

Chemotherapy-induced leukopenia.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong shortens the duration of leukopenia in patients receiving chemotherapy.

A small clinical study in patients with breast cancer receiving chemotherapy shows that practicing Chan-Chuang Qi gong for up to 1 hour daily for 21 days produces a more rapid normalization of white blood cell count when compared with no additional intervention ( 88869 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong may modestly reduce symptoms of fatigue, anxiety, and depression in patients with CFS.

Two small clinical studies in patients with self-reported CFS shows that training in Five Elements and Balance Qi gong or Baduanjin Qi gong for 30 minutes or more twice weekly for 5-9 weeks, followed by 12 weeks of self-practice, modestly reduces fatigue, anxiety, and depressive symptoms when compared with waitlist control ( 103797 ). However, these studies used online questionnaires to assess results and did not require a formal CFS diagnosis. Another small clinical study in patients with CFS shows that adding mindfulness meditation and medical Qi gong techniques to usual care for about 2 hours per week for 9 weeks improves physical function when compared with usual care alone ( 88817 ). It is unclear if these findings are due to Qi gong, mindfulness meditation, or the combination.

Cocaine dependence.

It is unclear if Qi gong is beneficial in patients with cocaine dependence.

A small clinical study in recently abstinent cocaine-dependent individuals shows that participating in external Qi gong therapy (EQT) does not reduce cocaine craving and associated symptoms when compared with sham-EQT ( 103910 ). However, this study may not have been adequately powered to detect smaller differences between treatment groups.

Cognitive impairment.

It is unclear if Qi gong is beneficial in patients with cognitive impairment.

A small clinical study in community-dwelling Chinese adults aged 60-80 years with mild cognitive impairment shows that practicing Qi gong at least twice weekly in 60-minute sessions for 12 months increases global cognitive function scores by about 2 points from baseline, compared with no change in patients enrolled in a stretching control. Memory, global cognitive ability, visuospatial/constructional ability, and language ability are also improved ( 106502 ). However, it is unclear if the improvement from baseline differed between groups.

Complex regional pain syndrome.

It is unclear if Qi gong is beneficial in patients with complex regional pain syndrome.

A small clinical study in patients with late-stage complex regional pain syndrome type I shows that receiving instruction from a Qi gong master and practicing Qi gong for 40 minutes 6 times over 3 weeks decreases pain and anxiety when compared with sham Qi gong instruction ( 88861 ).

Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19).

Qi gong has only been evaluated in combination with acupressure; its effect when practiced alone is unclear.

A small clinical study in patients 20-80 years of age hospitalized with COVID-19 shows that practicing Qi gong and receiving acupressure therapy twice daily until discharge along with standard care reduces dyspnea and shortens the duration of hospitalization and cough by around 2 and 3 days, respectively, when compared with standard care alone ( 107429 ). It is unclear if these findings are due to Qi gong, acupressure, or the combination.

Depression.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong may modestly improve symptoms in patients with major depressive disorder.

A meta-analysis of seven small clinical studies in patients with major depressive disorder shows that practicing Qi gong for 90-350 minutes weekly for 1-12 weeks reduces depressive symptoms when compared with controls such as waitlist, health education, and cognitive behavioral therapy ( 103904 ). This analysis was limited due to the high heterogeneity and small size of the included studies. Also, a small clinical study in elderly patients with depression shows that following a Qi gong exercise program for 16 weeks reduces symptoms of depression and increases feelings of well-being when compared with reading the newspaper ( 15874 ).

Diabetes.

Several small, low-quality clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong may modestly improve glycemic control in patients with type 2 diabetes.

Qi gong has been evaluated for its effects on glycemic control in patients with diabetes, with mixed findings ( 15869 , 88863 , 88864 , 96792 , 106504 ). A meta-analysis of 21 small clinical studies in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that practicing Qi gong for 6 weeks to 1 year reduces fasting blood glucose by around 18 mg/dL and glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by around 0.8% when compared with no exercise. When compared with aerobic exercise, Qi gong appears to lower HbA1c by around 0.6%, but does not significantly reduce fasting blood glucose ( 96792 ). Some small clinical studies also show that Qi gong improves triglyceride and total cholesterol levels, but not body weight, in patients with type 2 diabetes ( 15869 , 88863 , 88864 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the high heterogeneity and low quality of the available clinical research.

Fibromyalgia.

Several small clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong may be modestly beneficial in patients with fibromyalgia, although it does not appear to be superior to other active treatments.

While some conflicting evidence exists, most research on the use of Qi gong therapy in patients with fibromyalgia shows promising results ( 15872 , 88865 , 88866 , 88867 , 96793 ). A meta-analysis of seven small clinical studies in patients with fibromyalgia shows that practicing Qi gong for approximately 6-12 weeks leads to short-term improvements in pain, sleep quality, fatigue, and quality of life when compared with a wait-list control or usual care. However, Qi gong does not seem to be more effective than other active treatments ( 96793 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the small sample size, high heterogeneity, and low quality of the available studies.

Gastritis.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in patients with gastritis.

A small, uncontrolled clinical study in patients with chronic atrophic gastritis shows that practicing Daoyin-Tuna Qi gong exercises for 1 hour four times daily improves symptoms of gastritis when compared to baseline ( 88868 ). The validity of these findings is limited by a lack of control group.

Heart failure.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong may modestly improve quality of life and exercise capacity in patients with compensated heart failure.

A meta-analysis of eight small clinical studies in patients with compensated heart failure shows that practicing any form of Qi gong along with routine management improves quality of life by 9-10 points on the Minnesota Living with Heart Failure Questionnaire when compared with routine management with or without general exercise. Improvements in exercise capacity, including a 60-meter increase in 6-minute walking distance and a 1.24-mL/kg/minute increase in peak oxygen consumption, also were observed when compared with routine management alone ( 106505 ). These findings are limited by the heterogeneity and risk of bias of the included studies.

Lung cancer.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial for improving lung function in patients with lung cancer.

A small clinical study in patients with lung cancer experiencing fatigue, dyspnea, and anxiety shows that practicing Qi gong for at least 30 minutes 5 times a week for 6 weeks does not improve the composite symptom score, which includes fatigue, dyspnea, and anxiety, or individual symptoms of fatigue and anxiety, when compared with control. However, there were improvements in quality of life and the individual symptoms of dyspnea and cough when compared with control ( 106509 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the high dropout rate.

Menopausal symptoms.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in patients with menopausal symptoms.

A small, uncontrolled clinical study in perimenopausal patients aged 45 years and older shows that Ping Shuai Qi gong exercise, practiced with an instructor for 30 minutes each day for 12 weeks, improves menopausal symptoms and sleep quality when compared to baseline ( 18218 ). The lack of a control group limits the validity of these findings.

Muscular dystrophy.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in patients with muscular dystrophy.

A small clinical study in patients with muscular dystrophy shows that practicing internal Qi gong daily for 3 months improves perceived general health, but not balance, when compared with usual care ( 88870 ).

Neck pain.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial for neck pain.

A meta-analysis of two small clinical studies in patients with neck pain shows that practicing internal Qi gong for 6 months, but not 3 months, modestly improves neck pain when compared with a wait-list control ( 96797 ).

Opioid withdrawal.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in adults with heroin use disorder.

A small clinical study in patients with heroin use disorder shows that practicing Pan Gu Qi gong and receiving qi adjustments from a Qi gong master for 10 days decreases withdrawal symptoms, anxiety, and relapses when compared with no treatment or detoxification with lofexidine ( 88862 ). A meta-analysis of this and another small clinical study in patients with heroin use disorder also shows that practicing Qi gong reduces anxiety when compared with medication treatment alone ( 103907 ).

Osteoarthritis.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in patients with knee osteoarthritis.

A small clinical study in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that training in Baduanjin Qi gong for 40 minutes three times weekly for 12 weeks modestly improves pain, stiffness, and function, but not postural stability, when compared with no additional intervention ( 103915 ).

Osteoporosis.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in females with osteoporosis.

A small clinical study in middle-aged females shows that practicing Baduanjin Qi gong for 12 weeks limits declines in bone mineral density (BMD) when compared with no intervention ( 15866 ).

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial for relieving chronic pain.

A meta-analysis of ten small clinical studies in patients with chronic pain shows that practicing internal or external Qi gong for up to 6 months reduces pain when compared with no treatment or sham Qi gong, but not when compared with a wait-list control ( 96797 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the high heterogeneity and small sample size of the available clinical research.

Parkinson disease.

Several small clinical studies suggest that practicing Qi gong may be modestly beneficial in adults with Parkinson disease; however, it may be no better than other forms of low-impact exercise.

A meta-analysis of seven small, low-quality clinical studies in patients with Parkinson disease shows that practicing Qi gong 2-5 times weekly for 7-48 weeks improves motor symptoms, walking ability, and balance when compared with no intervention ( 103799 ). However, Qi gong seems to be no better than exercise interventions such as walking and cycling ( 15875 , 103799 ). Similarly, a small clinical study in patients aged 40-85 years with Parkinson disease without cognitive or mobility impairment shows that practicing Qi gong for 60 minutes 4 times a week for 12 weeks improves reaction and balance, physical coordination, gait, and range of motion when compared to baseline. However, no differences in most of these improvements were observed when compared with standard care ( 106511 ).

Physical performance.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong can improve physical performance in older adults.

A small clinical study in elderly patients with coronary artery disease caused by atherosclerosis shows that practicing Qi gong exercises weekly for three months improves physical activity, balance, and coordination when compared with usual care alone ( 88857 ). Another small clinical study in middle-aged and older postmenopausal females shows that practicing Qi gong twice weekly for 12 weeks improves muscle strength and postural control when compared with no intervention ( 107428 ).

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing external Qi gong may not improve pain associated with PMS.

A meta-analysis of two small clinical studies in healthy patients with PMS shows that practicing external Qi gong under the guidance of a trained Qi gong practitioner for 6-8 weeks does not decrease pain when compared with sham Qi gong ( 96797 ).

Psychological well-being.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial for improving psychological well-being in otherwise healthy adolescents.

A meta-analysis of five small clinical studies in adolescents not diagnosed with major psychiatric disorders shows that practicing Qi gong for 2 months to 1 year reduces symptoms of anxiety and depression and lowers cortisol levels, but does not improve self-esteem or feelings of stress, when compared with wait-list controls or sham Qi gong therapy ( 107427 ).

Quality of life.

Several small clinical studies suggest that practicing internal Qi gong may modestly improve quality of life in older adults with chronic disease.

A meta-analysis of 13 small, low-quality clinical studies in older adults with chronic disease shows that practicing internal Qi gong improves quality of life, but does not seem to improve depressive symptoms or feelings of self-efficacy, when compared with controls such as waitlist, no intervention, health education, and walking ( 103800 ). Internal Qi gong, such as Dantian, Baduanjin, and Yijinjing, was practiced in 30- to 90-minute sessions for a minimum of 8 weeks and up to 6 months ( 88874 , 88875 , 103800 ).

Sleep apnea.

Qi gong has only been evaluated in combination with tai chi; its effect when practiced alone is unclear.

A small clinical study in patients with obstructive sleep apnea shows that training in Qi gong and tai chi for 1 hour with a physiotherapist 3 times weekly, and also exercising at home for 2 days weekly, for 12 weeks reduces the incidence of apnea and hypopnea and improves sleep quality when compared with exercising 5 times weekly ( 103916 ). It is unclear if the benefits are due to Qi gong, tai chi, or the combination.

Stress.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial for reducing stress in otherwise healthy adults.

A small clinical study in hospital employees shows that practicing Qi gong for 6 weeks improves perceived stress when compared with no treatment ( 88876 ). Another small clinical study in students 13-18 years of age shows that practicing Qi gong for 6 weeks improves both state and trait anxiety scores from baseline by 14% and 7%, respectively, particularly in males. However, anxiety scores and salivary cortisol levels were not improved when compared with control ( 106503 ).

Stroke.

It is unclear if practicing Qi gong is beneficial in patients recovering from a stroke.

A small clinical study in patients undergoing post-stroke rehabilitation shows that practicing Chan-Chuang Qi gong for 10 days in addition to standard care improves quality of life when compared with standard care alone ( 103798 ).

More evidence is needed to rate Qi gong for these uses.

Many varieties of Qi gong exist; the two main categories are internal and external Qi gong. Internal Qi gong involves self-guided or directed exercise with slow movements. Patients typically attend training with a Qi gong master and then perform Qi gong at home thereafter. External Qi gong involves administration of specialized forms of massage by a trained Qi gong practitioner. Qi gong also incorporates breathing, focus, and visualization of the movement of qi through the body( 15856 , 15858 , 15864 , 15865 ). Specialized forms of Qi gong uniquely targeted to the treatment of specific conditions have also been developed. Each specialized form incorporates the main principles of Qi gong, including breathing, intentional movement, relaxation, stance, visualization, and awareness. Qi gong sessions may last for anywhere from 20-90 minutes and can be performed as often as daily or as little as once per week. The duration of treatment is dependent on the indication for treatment.

#### General

Qi gong is a broadly defined traditional Chinese therapy that incorporates low-to-moderate intensity exercise and posture, movements, meditation, and breathing patterns ( 15856 , 15858 , 15864 , 15865 ). Qi gong attempts to regulate mind, body, and breath ( 15873 ). Qi means "vital energy" or "life force" and gong means "work" or "discipline." Therefore, Qi gong can be translated to mean "energy work." In traditional Chinese medicine, it is believed that disease is caused by a blockage or unbalanced energy flow in the body. Like many therapies in traditional Chinese medicine, Qi gong is thought to help unblock or balance energy flow and help the body heal. Several Qi gong exercises have been developed to specifically open the 12 energy channels or "meridians" in the body that are recognized in traditional Chinese medicine. Opening these channels allows qi to flow throughout the body ( 15865 ).

#### Anti-hypertensive effects

Qi gong is thought reduce blood pressure in patients with hypertension by reducing sympathetic nervous system activity and decreasing catecholamine levels ( 15858 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

Qi gong is sometimes used for arthritis. In human research, some studies have shown that Qi gong has anti-inflammatory effects, reducing levels of C-reactive protein ( 96788 ).

#### Anti-stress effects

In clinical research, female computer operators who practice Qi gong have a lower heart rate and body temperature, as well as reduced levels of noradrenaline in the urine, compared to those who don't practice Qi gong. This suggests that Qi gong exercise reduces sympathetic nervous system tone and might reduce feelings of stress ( 15870 ). Also, some research suggests that Qi gong reduces levels of cortisol ( 96788 ).

#### Cardioprotective effects

Practicing Qi gong might improve glycemic control and insulin sensitivity, reduce cholesterol and blood pressure, reduce stress, and increase fitness. These changes could possibly contribute to a reduction in cardiovascular disease risk ( 96789 ).

#### Exercise effects

There is interest in using Qi gong to improve physical activity in older adults. In these patients, Qi gong exercises produce a moderate level of exertion, and increase heart rate and feelings of exertion comparable to walking exercises ( 15865 ). A combination of Tai chi plus Qi gong exercise also appears to improve balance in these patients ( 15868 ).

#### Immune effects

There is interest in Qi gong for improving immune function and immune response in older adults. Preliminary clinical research shows that a combination of Tai chi and Qi gong in older adults results in an increased antibody response to influenza vaccination ( 15857 ).

#### Neurological effects

In preliminary clinical research, practicing Qi gong for an average of 12.4 months improves P3 wave latency on electroencephalogram (EEG) in patients with Parkinson disease. This might correlate to possible benefit in cognitive function ( 88872 ).
