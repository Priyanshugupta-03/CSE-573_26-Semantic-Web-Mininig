# Transcutaneous Electrical Acustimulation
Other Common Names Acuestimulación eléctrica transcutánea, Acupoint Electrical Stimulation, Acupoint Stimulation, Acupuncture Point Stimulation, Acupuncture-like Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation, Acustimulation électrique transcutanée, Acustimulation, Acustimulation Wristbands, AcuTENS, Percutaneous Acupoint Electrical Stimulation, TEAS, Transcutaneous Acupoint Electrical Stimulation, Transcutaneous Electrical Acupoint Stimulation
|

---

Caution

Transcutaneous Electrical Acustimulation should not be confused with Traditional Chinese Medicine or similar systems such as Kampo Medicine . Also, it should not be confused with similar treatments, such as Acupressure , Acupuncture , Moxibustion , or Trigger Point Therapy .

## Overview

Transcutaneous electrical acustimulation involves applying low intensity electrical current to specific acupuncture points without puncturing the skin ( 98967 , 104742 ). This differs from transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS), which does not utilize acupoints. It also differs from electroacupuncture, in which an electrical current is passed through embedded acupuncture needles ( 107057 , 107075 ).

Likely Safe when transcutaneous electrical acustimulation is used appropriately, short-term. A specific acustimulation device (ReliefBand, Woodside Biomedical) has been used with apparent safety in clinical research with no reports of adverse effects. This device is commercially available and approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for certain types of nausea, retching, and vomiting ( 98961 , 98964 ). Acustimulation via other methods has also been used with apparent safety in clinical research for up to 12 weeks ( 98963 , 98965 , 104735 , 104741 , 104742 , 104746 , 107034 , 107048 , 107049 , 107052 )( 107053 , 107054 , 107057 , 107061 , 107062 , 107063 , 107068 , 107072 , 107073 , 107078 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no reason to expect safety concerns when used appropriately.

#### General

Transcutaneous electrical acustimulation is well tolerated.

#### Dermatologic

Itchiness, skin redness, and swelling have been reported rarely in clinical research ( 104735 , 104741 , 104742 , 107054 , 107056 , 107060 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Insomnia and temporary hand paralysis have been reported in one person each in a clinical trial ( 104742 ).

Possibly Effective
Postoperative ileus.

Acustimulation seems to be effective for postoperative ileus.

A meta-analysis and individual clinical research shows that acustimulation modestly improves postoperative gastrointestinal function when compared with sham acustimulation or routine treatment alone ( 104743 , 107062 , 107065 , 107066 , 107077 ). Improvements include the time to first flatus or first defecation, the number of spontaneous bowel movements, symptoms such as appetite, belching, and abdominal distension, and the overall incidence of postoperative ileus or gastrointestinal dysfunction ( 104743 , 107062 , 107065 , 107066 , 107077 ). Acustimulation has been given during or after the operation until postoperative day 3 for cesarian section ( 104743 ), gastric surgery ( 107065 , 107077 ), and colorectal surgery ( 107062 , 107065 ).

Postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV).

Acustimulation seems to be effective for preventing PONV; however, it is unclear whether acustimulation is effective for treating PONV or whether it is more effective than antiemetics alone for preventing PONV.

Most clinical research from meta-analyses and individual trials shows that acustimulation reduces the risk of PONV by up to 46% for at least 2 hours, and in some cases for up to 48 hours, after surgery. In addition, a meta-analysis of four studies shows that acustimulation at the P6 acupoint reduces the need for antiemetic rescue ( 98962 , 98964 , 98970 , 98971 , 98973 , 104735 , 104736 , 104738 , 104739 , 104746 )( 107056 , 107058 , 107065 , 107068 , 107071 , 107077 ). Many studies used acustimulation of the P6 point starting before anesthesia and continuing for up to 72 hours after surgery using a specific acustimulation band (ReliefBand) ( 98962 , 98964 , 98970 , 98971 ) or HANS electrodes ( 104736 ). The ReliefBand acustimulation device has shown benefit for PONV after gynecological laparoscopy ( 98962 ), laparoscopic cholecystectomy ( 98964 ), and plastic surgery ( 98970 , 98971 ). The device seems to be most effective for preventing PONV when used peri-operatively and postoperatively as opposed to only preoperative use ( 98970 ). Preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing radical mastectomy shows that acustimulation at acupoints P6 and CV17 30 minutes prior to anesthesia reduces the incidence of PONV more than acustimulation at the P6 point only ( 107068 ). Some research has assessed acustimulation when used as an adjunctive treatment to the conventional antiemetic ondansetron, or in place of ondansetron. One small clinical study in patients receiving PONV prophylaxis with droperidol shows that wearing an acustimulation band (ReliefBand) seems to be comparable for reducing nausea and vomiting at 24 and 72 hours after surgery when compared with intravenous (IV) ondansetron 4 mg given once with sham acustimulation. Furthermore, giving ondansetron with the acustimulation band was shown to be superior for reducing PONV when compared with ondansetron alone ( 98971 ). Notably, IV ondansetron has about a 4-hour duration of action, so a single dose of ondansetron and wearing an acustimulation band at 24 and 72 hours might not be a fair active control comparison. Other clinical research in children ages 4-12 years undergoing tonsillectomy shows that acustimulation of 20 Hz for 5 minutes before the induction of anesthesia and 5 minutes after surgery is as effective as ondansetron 0.15 mg/kg and more effective than no treatment for reducing the incidence of postoperative retching and vomiting on the day of surgery and the day after discharge. There were no differences between groups the day after surgery or immediately after surgery ( 107056 ). Some research has assessed acustimulation when used as an adjunctive treatment to the conventional antiemetic dexamethasone. In patients undergoing laparoscopic surgery, acustimulation in combination with dexamethasone is more effective than dexamethasone alone, and as effective as tropisetron plus dexamethasone for reducing the incidence of PONV in the 24 hours following surgery. There was no significant difference in the need for rescue antiemetics between groups ( 107058 ). Acustimulation has also been tried for treatment of PONV. A small clinical study in patients that developed PONV after outpatient laparoscopic surgery shows that using a combination of intravenous ondansetron 4 mg and wearing an acustimulation band (ReliefBand) reduces the need for rescue antiemetics when compared with ondansetron alone or acustimulation bands alone ( 98960 ). However, some research suggests that acustimulation may not be beneficial when used alone in patients undergoing emetogenic procedures. In a small clinical study in patients undergoing a gastroscopy procedure, wearing the acustimulation band (ReliefBand) starting 15 minutes prior to the procedure and throughout the procedure did not improve nausea or vomiting when compared to sham acustimulation or no treatment ( 98969 ). However, when acustimulation is added to treatment with dexamethasone and tropisetron, PONV associated with laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy is reduced by an additional 46% over the next 48 hours when compared with dexamethasone and tropisetron alone. The severity of symptoms and need for antiemetic medication was also reduced ( 107060 ).

Postoperative pain.

Acustimulation seems to be effective for reducing postoperative pain in most patients.

Clinical research shows that acustimulation modestly reduces postoperative pain and/or the need for postoperative analgesics when compared with sham acustimulation or routine treatment alone ( 104738 , 104739 , 104740 , 104741 , 104743 , 104746 , 107064 , 107065 , 107071 , 107074 )( 107077 ). Types of surgery have included caesarian section ( 104743 ), inguinal hernia repair ( 104741 ), thoracoscopic surgery ( 104738 , 104739 ), spinal cord surgery ( 104740 ), total hip arthroplasty ( 104746 ), radical mastectomy ( 107064 ), ureteroscopic lithotripsy ( 107074 ), gastrectomy ( 107077 ), and others. In some cases, acustimulation is initiated prior to, and possibly during, surgery, as well as intermittently after surgery ( 104738 , 104739 , 104746 , 107064 ). In others it has only been used postoperatively for up to 3 days ( 104740 , 104741 , 104743 , 107074 , 107077 ). However, not all research agrees. When used in combination with the antiemetics dexamethasone and tropisetron in female patients undergoing laparoscopic sleeve gastrectomy, acustimulation does not reduce postoperative pain over 48 hours or the need for opioid medication ( 107060 ).

Postoperative cognitive dysfunction . Acustimulation seems to be effective for preventing postoperative cognitive dysfunction.

A meta-analysis of generally good quality clinical research shows that acustimulation alone or in combination with other therapies reduces the incidence of postoperative cognitive dysfunction in the first week after surgery by approximately 60% when compared with control groups given sham intervention, no intervention, or other treatments. Although there were also improvements seven days after surgery, these benefits were not observed on day 5 ( 107071 ). Additional preliminary clinical research in elderly patients undergoing laparoscopic cancer surgery shows that acustimulation from 30 minutes before anesthesia induction to the end of surgery modestly reduces the cumulative duration of cognitive decline on postoperative days two and three when compared with no acustimulation. However, there was no effect on the incidence of cognitive decline ( 107070 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

Acustimulation does not seem to be beneficial for CINV.

One large clinical trial including primarily breast cancer patients receiving highly emetic chemotherapy such as cisplatin or doxorubicin for the first time shows that wearing a specific acustimulation device (ReliefBand) does not improve CINV when compared with not wearing the device or when compared with acupressure bands (Seaband) ( 98967 ). Another relatively large study in patients undergoing transcatheter arterial chemoembolization with cisplatin for liver cancer shows that although acustimulation twice daily for 6 days improves feelings of anorexia, it does not reduce the incidence or severity of CINV when compared with sham acustimulation ( 107054 ). A small clinical study in females with breast cancer about to receive their second treatment shows that wearing a specific acustimulation band (ReliefBand) does not improve CINV when compared with an acustimulation band placed in the incorrect location or with no band ( 98966 ). Also, a small clinical trial in patients with liver cancer receiving chemotherapy shows that acustimulation given via electrodes attached to acupoints on the foot is no different for CINV than sham acustimulation ( 98968 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Alcohol use disorder.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for alcohol use disorder.

In patients hospitalized for alcohol withdrawal, clinical research shows that acustimulation with a HANS stimulator twice daily, in combination with diazepam titrated down from 30 mg daily to 5 mg daily over 2 weeks, moderately reduces withdrawal symptoms and alcohol cravings and improves sleep quality when compared with sham acustimulation and diazepam. These improvements start around day 3 and continue throughout the 14-day treatment period ( 104742 ).

Chemotherapy-induced constipation.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for chemotherapy-induced constipation.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with lung cancer on various cisplatin chemotherapy regimens shows that acustimulation given six times weekly for 4 weeks modestly improves constipation when compared with no acustimulation and continued use of fiber, stimulant laxatives, or enemas ( 107059 ).

Chemotherapy-related fatigue.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for chemotherapy-related fatigue.

Clinical research in patients with lung cancer shows that acustimulation given eight times over 28 days improves feelings of fatigue related to the first treatment cycle with gemcitabine and platinum-based drugs when compared with no treatment or sham acustimulation ( 107051 ). It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for fatigue related to other types of chemotherapy.

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

Although there is interest in using acustimulation for CFS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acustimulation for this condition.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for COPD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with stable and chronic COPD shows that acustimulation every other day for 4 weeks modestly improves some measures of lung function and reduces the negative impact of COPD when compared with placebo acustimulation. There were no differences between groups for severity of dyspnea or 6-minute walking distance ( 107057 ).

Constipation.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for constipation.

Preliminary clinical research in patients within 24 hours of a first ischemic stroke shows that one hour of acustimulation twice daily for 2 weeks reduces the incidence of stroke-induced constipation to 43%, compared with 68% of those given sham acustimulation. Spontaneous bowel movement frequency was increased by 1.5 per week and both stool consistency and quality of life were improved ( 107075 ).

Cyclic vomiting syndrome (CVS).

Although there is interest in using acustimulation for CVS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acustimulation for this condition.

Dyspepsia.

Although there is interest in using acustimulation for dyspepsia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acustimulation for this condition.

Fatigue.

Although there is interest in using acustimulation for fatigue, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acustimulation for this purpose.

Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD).

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for GERD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with refractory GERD shows that acustimulation twice daily for 4 weeks in combination with esomeprazole 20 mg twice daily modestly improves overall GERD symptoms when compared with esomeprazole alone or esomeprazole plus sham acustimulation. However, there was no difference in the number of acid reflux episodes or in overall symptoms when compared with esomeprazole plus domperidone 10 mg three times daily ( 107076 ). In additional preliminary clinical research in patients with GERD with ineffective esophageal motility, acustimulation one hour twice daily over 4 weeks modestly decreases symptoms such as belching, heartburn, regurgitation, pain, and nausea, and the need for rescue proton pump inhibitors, when compared with sham acustimulation ( 107067 ).

Hypertension.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for hypertension.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with hypertension shows that four weekly sessions of acustimulation for 12 weeks reduces systolic, but not diastolic, blood pressure by an additional 7 mmHg when compared with antihypertensive agents and education related to lifestyle modifications. Benefits seem to last for an additional 12 weeks after acustimulation discontinuation ( 107073 ).

Infertility.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for improving pregnancy rates in patients undergoing in vitro fertilization (IVF).

A large clinical trial in patients undergoing IVF due to tubal or male-cause infertility shows that acustimulation at 20-50 mA for 30 minutes once daily for 10-13 days increases endometrial thickness on the day of chorionic gonadotropin administration and the number of retrieved oocytes when compared with mock acustimulation.  However, there was no effect on the number of high-quality embryos or the rate of clinical pregnancy ( 107063 ). Also, preliminary clinical research shows that acustimulation starting 30 minutes before oocyte retrieval until completion does not affect pregnancy outcomes when compared to mock acustimulation. However, this study was not designed to investigate reproduction as a primary outcome ( 107034 ). In contrast, another large study in patients undergoing IVF due to bilateral tubal blockage shows that 30 minutes of acustimulation at 2/100 Hz at 24 hours prior to oocyte retrieval and again two hours before embryo transfer improves clinical pregnancy rates and live birth rates, but does not affect the number of mature oocytes, normally fertilized zygotes, or good quality embryos, when compared with no acustimulation. There was no effect of acustimulation administered at 2 Hz or 100 Hz ( 107052 ).

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for IBS.

A small longitudinal study in patients with diarrhea-predominant IBS who discontinued conventional treatment 5 days before starting the study shows that acustimulation improves symptoms such as sensation of gas, pressure, and need to defecate, but not rectal tone, when compared with sham acustimulation ( 98972 ).

Kidney failure.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for hemodialysis-associated fatigue or uremic pruritus.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with kidney failure receiving hemodialysis shows that ten sessions of acustimulation treatment over 5 weeks modestly improves feelings of fatigue when compared with sham acustimulation ( 107050 ). Also, preliminary clinical research in patients with uremic pruritus who are receiving hemodialysis shows that acustimulation three times weekly for 4 weeks reduces the use of antihistamine medications and also reduces the severity of discomfort related to pruritus by 58%, compared with a reduction of only 27% in patients given routine treatment only. However, acustimulation was no more effective than acupressure ( 107078 ).

Labor pain.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for reducing labor pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acustimulation near the onset of labor decreases pain, but to a lesser extent than patient-controlled epidural analgesia. In addition, acustimulation was associated with a shorter duration of first and second stages of labor when compared with use of the epidural ( 107069 ).

Motion sickness.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for motion sickness.

One small clinical study in healthy patients with motion sickness shows that wearing a specific acustimulation device (ReliefBand) on the wrist during a 15-minute driving simulation reduces discomfort when compared with placebo. However, acustimulation was no more effective than auricular acupressure beads ( 98961 ).

Neck pain.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for neck pain.

A small clinical study in patients with chronic neck pain shows that receiving acustimulation with a wristband for 30 minutes twice weekly for 4 weeks, in addition to standardized neck exercises, moderately reduces pain in about 70% of patients, compared to about 13% of patients receiving sham acustimulation ( 98959 ).

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD).

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for OCD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with OCD shows that acustimulation as add-on therapy to cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), with or without clomipramine, for 12 weeks modestly improves obsessive and compulsive symptoms starting at 2 weeks and continuing through 12 weeks when compared with sham acustimulation ( 98963 ).

Opioid withdrawal.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for opioid withdrawal. Details : Preliminary clinical research in patients with heroin use disorder shows that 2-3 acustimulation sessions daily for ten days reduces withdrawal symptoms such as musculoskeletal pain, craving, anxiety, agitation, sleepiness, and gastrointestinal symptoms by approximately 60% when compared with placebo acustimulation, with improvements occurring as soon as day two. Acustimulation also reduced rescue buprenorphine requirements by 90% when compared with placebo acustimulation ( 107053 ).

Pain (acute).

Small clinical studies suggest that acustimulation may be beneficial for reducing various forms of acute pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acustimulation starting 30 minutes before oocyte retrieval until completion reduces immediate pain by approximately 24% and pain one hour later by 32% when compared to mock acustimulation ( 107034 ). Other preliminary clinical research in adults undergoing 180 seconds of cold immersion shows that 15 minutes of acustimulation is similarly effective to morphine 0.05 mg/kg for reducing pain and more effective than no treatment. The combination of morphine plus acustimulation was more effective than either option alone ( 107055 ).

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for chronic pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients undergoing radical mastectomy shows that acustimulation at acupoints PC6 and CV17 30 minutes prior to anesthesia reduces the occurrence of chronic pain 6 months after surgery to 22%, compared with about 35% in those given sham acustimulation. The use of remifentanil during surgery was also reduced. However, in patients still reporting pain at 6 months, the level of pain was not reduced. Acustimulation at only the P6 point was not effective for reducing the incidence of chronic pain ( 107068 ).

Pancreatitis.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for acute pancreatitis. Details : Preliminary clinical research in patients with mild to moderate acute pancreatitis shows that adding 30 minutes of acustimulation twice daily during the first two days of hospitalization with routine treatment modestly decreases abdominal pain and improves overall symptom severity when compared with sham acustimulation. There was also a large effect on bloating, and time to first defecation was reduced by approximately 14 hours. There was no difference between groups in nausea or vomiting or length of hospital stay ( 107072 ).

Postoperative sleep disturbance.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for improving postoperative sleep.

Clinical research shows that acustimulation for 30 minutes daily for 4 days, starting the night before thoracoscopic surgery, modestly improves sleep quality and decreases symptoms of insomnia over the 3 days after surgery when compared with sham acustimulation ( 104738 ).

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for PTSD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with PTSD shows that acustimulation once weekly for 12 weeks plus cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), with or without sertraline, increases the likelihood of having an improvement in overall symptom reduction by at least 30%, as well as the likelihood of remission by moderate to large amounts, when compared with sham acustimulation plus CBT with or without sertraline ( 107048 ).

Pregnancy-induced nausea and vomiting.

Although there is interest in using acustimulation for pregnancy-induced nausea and vomiting, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of acustimulation for this purpose.

Pre-procedural anxiety.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for reducing preoperative anxiety.

Clinical research in patients awaiting anesthetic for thoracoscopic pulmonary resection for lung cancer shows that administering acustimulation for 30 minutes modestly increases sedation when compared with sham acustimulation ( 104739 ).

Pruritus.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for uremic pruritus.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with uremic pruritus who are receiving hemodialysis shows that acustimulation three times weekly for 4 weeks reduces antihistamine use, as well as the severity of discomfort related to pruritus by 58%, compared with 27% in patients given routine treatment alone. However, acustimulation was no more effective than acupressure ( 107078 ).

Spasticity.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for muscle spasticity associated with a brain injury.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with muscle spasticity related to a brain injury occurring at least 3 months prior shows that receiving acustimulation at 100 Hz five times weekly for 4 weeks modestly reduces muscle spasticity at the wrist for up to one month after treatment when compared with acustimulation at 2 Hz or 0 Hz. However, muscle spasticity in other parts of the body was not improved ( 107061 ).

Stroke.

It is unclear if acustimulation is beneficial for improving muscle function after a stroke.

Preliminary clinical research shows that acustimulation five times weekly for three weeks starting within 10 days of a stroke modestly improves some measures of ankle muscle spasticity and strength when compared with placebo acustimulation or standard rehabilitation alone. However, benefits were not consistent and there was no difference in the ability to walk 7-8 minutes unassisted or the overall ability to walk within 3 weeks ( 107079 ).

More evidence is needed to rate acustimulation for these uses.

Acustimulation can be given using electric units with electrodes such as a transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) unit or a HANS dual-channel unit. The electrodes are attached to specific acupoints on the body to which a small electric current is administered; this is called transcutaneous electrical acupoint stimulation (TEAS) ( 98972 , 98973 ). The ReliefBand (Woodside Biomedical) is a US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved non-invasive, portable acustimulation device. This watch-like device is battery-powered and can administer a current at 31Hz up to 35mA, in five strengths ( 98961 , 98964 ). Various other devices have been used in clinical research, including 120Z TES stimulator (ITO Co Ltd), Nihon Medex XFT-320 (Shenzhen XFT Electronics Ltd), SNM-FDC01 (Ningbo Maida Medical Device, Inc), Huatuo Electronic Acupuncture Treatment Instrument SDZ-II (Suzhou Medical Supplies Factory Co., Ltd), SDP-330 (Suzhou Medical Appliances Co, Ltd.), SNM-FP03 or SNM-FDC01 (Ningbo Maida Medical Device Inc.), 6805-D model (Shantou City Medical Equipment Co., Ltd.), and G6805-II (Shanghai Medical Equipment Technology Co. Ltd.) ( 107048 , 107051 , 107055 , 107057 , 107059 , 107061 , 107063 , 107065 , 107067 , 107068 , 107072 , 107073 , 107074 , 107075 , 107077 , 107078 , 107079 ). Various frequencies, intensities, and waveforms are used ( 104738 ). Common currents have included 2-100 Hz in constant or pulsed mode ( 107034 , 107048 , 107052 , 107053 , 107054 , 107057 , 107058 , 107059 , 107061 , 107062 , 107064 , 107065 , 107067 , 107068 , 107070 , 107072 , 107075 , 107076 , 107077 , 107078 , 107079 ). Common acupoints used for acustimulation are the P6 Neiguan point on the wrist, which is used for nausea and vomiting, and the ST36 Zusanli point below the knee, which is used for gastrointestinal discomfort, stress, and fatigue ( 98972 ). These and other acupoints, such as BL21, BL23, BL27, BL60, CV17, LI4, LI11, SP6, SP8, SP10, ST37, LR3, LV3, GB20, GB34, GV20, PC8, SJ5, and REN13, have been stimulated alone or in combination in clinical research ( 107034 , 107048 , 107050 , 107051 , 107052 , 107053 , 107054 , 107055 , 107056 , 107057 , 107058 , 107059 , 107061 , 107062 , 107063 , 107064 , 107065 , 107067 , 107068 , 107069 , 107070 , 107071 , 107072 , 107074 , 107075 , 107076 , 107077 , 107078 , 107079 ). Sessions normally last for 30-60 minutes ( 107051 , 107053 , 107054 , 107057 , 107059 , 107061 , 107064 , 107079 ), and occur 2-7 days a week for 2-12 weeks ( 107050 , 107051 , 107053 , 107057 , 107059 , 107061 , 107073 , 107078 , 107079 ), or as multiple sessions daily ( 107053 , 107076 ). Occasionally, acustimulation is used prior to, during, and daily for three days after surgery ( 107034 , 107058 , 107062 , 107064 , 107065 , 107070 , 107074 , 107077 ).

None known.

None known.

Similar to acupuncture, acustimulation involves stimulating specific acupoints on the body along pathways called "meridians." According to traditional Chinese medicine, stimulating acupoints corresponds to stimulating or balancing specific organs, emotions, or sensory feelings ( 15991 ). In traditional Chinese medicine, it is thought that disease is caused by an imbalanced or blocked flow of energy or "qi." Therefore, stimulating acupoints is thought to stimulate energy flow, unblock energy, and rebalance energy, which results in healing ( 15996 ). Some experts also theorize that stimulating acupoints might result in the release of endorphins and/or opioids, which naturally reduce pain ( 15991 ). Other theories suggest that acustimulation selectively activates opioid receptors in the cerebrum, resulting in modulation of various neurological systems ( 104742 ). In human research, acustimulation has been shown to decrease cortisol ( 104741 ). It has also been shown to reduce levels of substance P, a neuropeptide involved in pain, and increase levels of beta-endorphin ( 107034 , 107065 ). In various patients, acustimulation has beneficial effects on levels of inflammatory and other cytokines ( 107062 , 107064 , 107070 , 107072 , 107077 ). Acustimulation may improve the lower esophageal sphincter pressure and esophageal contractions, as well as gastric accommodation and slow waves, in patients with GERD or pancreatitis ( 107067 , 107072 ). In addition, benefits on constipation may be related to effects on autonomic function ( 107075 ). Acustimulation appears to increase follicular levels of neuropeptide Y, which may play a role in reproduction ( 107052 ).
