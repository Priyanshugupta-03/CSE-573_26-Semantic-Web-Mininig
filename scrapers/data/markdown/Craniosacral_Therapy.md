## Overview

Dr. John Upledger, an osteopathic doctor, coined the term craniosacral therapy, which refers to a form of therapeutic manipulation involving tissue, fluid, membranes, and energy ( 98274 , 98275 ). It is a technique used by massage therapists, chiropractors, naturopathy practitioners, osteopathic doctors, and others. Craniosacral therapy is not licensed or regulated in North America and does not have educational and practice standards.

## People Use This For

Craniosacral therapy is a form of massage therapy used for a variety of conditions including asthma, headache, migraine, pain, colic, fibromyalgia, stress and fatigue, temporomandibular disorder (TMD), back pain, chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), spinal cord injury, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), depression, herniated disks, vertigo, neck pain, autism, learning disabilities, fibromyalgia, cerebral palsy, and other conditions.

Possibly Safe when used appropriately by trained practitioners. Craniosacral therapy has been used in clinical trials for up to 20 weeks with apparent safety ( 98274 , 98275 , 98276 , 98277 ). However, craniosacral therapy should be avoided by patients with recent trauma or fracture, especially of the brain and spinal cord.

CHILDREN: Craniosacral therapy should be avoided in children younger than 2 years as the skull may not be completely formed yet.

PREGNANCY: Possibly Safe when used appropriately by trained practitioners. Craniosacral therapy has been used with apparent safety in clinical trials involving pregnant women for up to 8 weeks ( 98276 ).

LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of craniosacral therapy during lactation. However, there is no reason to expect adverse effects in healthy lactating women when used appropriately.

#### General

Craniosacral therapy seems to be well tolerated. No adverse events have been reported in clinical research evaluating craniosacral therapy ( 98274 , 98275 , 98276 , 98277 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Back pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that receiving ten 50-minute sessions of craniosacral therapy seems to be as effective for reducing chronic low back pain as receiving ten 30-minute sessions of standard massage. However, neither group experienced an improvement in disability ( 98274 ).

Colic.

A small clinical study in infants with colic shows that receiving up to three 30-40 minute craniosacral therapy sessions over 14 days reduces crying and improves sleep by about 3 hours in one day when compared with no intervention ( 104008 ).

Fibromyalgia.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with fibromyalgia shows that receiving craniosacral therapy for 1 hour twice weekly for 20 weeks seems to reduce the tender points and pain severity when compared with a sham magnet-therapy ( 98275 ).

Neck pain.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with chronic neck pain shows that receiving craniosacral therapy for 45 minutes once weekly for 8 weeks improved pain in 74% of patients, compared to 41% of patients receiving sham therapy. The improvement in neck pain lasted for up to 3 months ( 98277 ).

Pain (chronic).

A meta-analysis of 2-4 small clinical trials shows that receiving craniosacral therapy modestly improves chronic pain when compared with usual treatment, sham therapy, or active treatments like trigger point therapy and massage ( 104009 ). The validity of this finding is limited because the analysis pooled results related to heterogenous forms of chronic pain such as back pain, epicondylitis, headache, fibromyalgia, and pelvic girdle pain.

Pregnancy-related pain.

Preliminary clinical research in pregnant women with pelvic girdle pain shows that administering 45 minutes of craniosacral therapy once weekly for 2 weeks and then biweekly for 6 weeks in conjunction with standard therapy modestly reduces pain and improves function when compared with standard therapy alone ( 98276 ).

Vertigo.

A small, non-randomized clinical study in patients with chronic peripheral vestibular vertigo shows that receiving craniosacral therapy once weekly for 6 weeks moderately improves balance and dizziness when compared with taking dimenhydrinate twice daily ( 104007 ). The validity of this finding is limited because patients self-selected their desired treatment and dimenhydrinate was given on a schedule instead of as needed, which could have worsened sedation and dizziness in the control group.

More evidence is needed to rate craniosacral therapy for these uses.

Craniosacral therapy practitioners first lightly touch their patients to evaluate the cranial rhythm impulse of the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Practitioners then use techniques called "subtle manipulations" over the skull and other areas with the aim of restoring balance by removing restrictions to CSF movement. These techniques involve specific hand placement and light touch or palpations. Treatment sessions usually last between 30 and 60 minutes ( 15927 , 98274 , 98277 ).

#### NEUROLOGICAL TRAUMA

Case reports suggest that craniosacral therapy might worsen symptoms of traumatic brain injury. Some patients report worsening head pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, palpitations, psychiatric disturbances, and others. This worsening of symptoms might be due to delay of proper evidence-based care ( 15928 ).

Craniosacral therapy is a form of massage therapy focusing on the skull and spine. Stress or lesions affecting the connective tissue between the skull and the spine are thought to cause chronic pain and many other conditions. Practitioners of craniosacral therapy believe that the bones of the skull can be gently manipulated in a way that treats medical conditions caused by stress or lesions in that area. Practitioners believe that they can touch the head and become attuned to the patient's rhythm, resulting in improved energy flow, removal of energy blockages, or improved flow of cerebrospinal fluid ( 15927 , 98274 ). However, the pulsation of the craniosacral system felt by practitioners may be pulsation of the cardiovascular system. Another theory is that the gentle hands-on bodywork of craniosacral therapy provides sensory stimulation which activates pain inhibitory pathways centrally or at the spinal level. This is intended to provide pain relief in patients with chronic pain ( 98276 ).
