# Anti-Inflammatory Diet
Other Common Names Anti-inflammation diet, Dieta antiinflamatoria, Dietary inflammatory index, DII, Low Inflammatory Diet, Régime anti-inflammatoire
|

---

## Overview

An anti-inflammatory diet usually involves eliminating foods that induce inflammation while also consuming foods and supplements with anti-inflammatory properties ( 98357 , 98906 ). There is no one specific anti-inflammatory diet. An evidence-based, validated scale called the dietary inflammatory index (DII) has been developed to evaluate the inflammatory potential of specific dietary patterns. Anti-inflammatory diets most often refer to diets that are rich in foods scored low on the DII, while pro-inflammatory diets consist of foods rated high on this scale ( 98357 , 98906 , 98934 ).

Possibly Safe when used appropriately. Anti-inflammatory diets have been evaluated in clinical trials without reports of serious adverse effects ( 98357 , 98906 , 98983 , 104690 ). Additionally, there is no reason to expect safety issues as long as nutritional needs are being met.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of anti-inflammatory diets during pregnancy or lactation, as this has not been specifically evaluated in clinical trials. Observational research has found that consuming an anti-inflammatory dietary pattern is associated with a reduced risk of any obstetric complication, such as gestational diabetes, miscarriage, low birthweight, and breastfeeding failure, when compared with consuming a more pro-inflammatory dietary pattern ( 110162 , 110171 ).

#### General

Anti-inflammatory diets seem to be well-tolerated as long as nutritional needs are met.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Orally : Diarrhea, gas, heartburn, and nausea.

#### Gastrointestinal

Gastrointestinal symptoms such as upset stomach with diarrhea, gas, heartburn, and nausea have been reported ( 104690 ).

Possibly Effective
Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

Consuming a diet with a higher inflammatory score seems to increase CVD incidence and mortality.

Multiple observational studies have found that consuming an anti-inflammatory diet is associated with a reduced rate of cardiovascular events and cardiovascular mortality when compared with consuming a diet with a higher inflammatory score. Also recent meta-analyses of observational research have found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 41% higher risk of CVD incidence and an approximate 30% higher risk of mortality when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 98957 , 98974 , 98982 , 98984 , 98987 , 104696 , 110163 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Anxiety.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the treatment or prevention of anxiety.

A meta-analysis of observational research in patients with mental health symptoms or disorders has found that the highest dietary inflammatory index (DII) score is associated with a 27% increased risk of anxiety symptoms and an 85% increased risk of distress when compared with the lowest DII score ( 108457 ). This meta-analysis did not define lowest and highest DII scores, limiting the validity of these findings.

Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of ADHD.

Observational research from Greece and Spain has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet during pregnancy is associated with 18% reduced odds of a male child, but not a female child, having ADHD symptoms at 4 years of age when compared to an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104707 ). Observational research from more northern European countries, including England, France, The Netherlands, and Poland, has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet during pregnancy is associated with increased odds of the child having ADHD symptoms at 7-10 years of age when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet. A one-unit increase in the pro-inflammatory diet score during pregnancy was found to be associated with a 7% increased risk of having borderline to clinical ADHD symptoms. When adjusted for the childs diet, this association did not persist ( 104698 ). It is unclear whether these discrepancies are related to differences in diets specific to geographical location or differences in micronutrient intake during pregnancy. In addition, neither of these analyses examined the association of a pro-inflammatory diet with an official ADHD diagnosis.

Bipolar disorder.

Although there has been interest in using the anti-inflammatory diet for bipolar disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the anti-inflammatory diet for this condition.

Bladder cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of bladder cancer.

Some observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a greater incidence of bladder cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 98989 ). In contrast, other observational research shows that a pro-inflammatory diet is not associated with a higher overall incidence of bladder cancer ( 104700 ).

Breast cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of breast cancer or breast cancer-related mortality.

Although some individual studies disagree ( 98991 , 98992 ), a meta-analysis shows that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet has been associated with a 33% greater incidence of breast cancer when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104708 ). Similarly, another meta-analysis of observational research shows that the highest dietary inflammatory index (DII) score (indicating a more pro-inflammatory diet) is associated with a 16% greater risk of breast cancer when compared with the lowest DII score. Sub-analyses suggest this relationship is stronger in postmenopausal adults, individuals with a body mass index of 30 kg/m 2 or greater, and studies conducted in Europe, Asia, or developing countries. Additionally, this relationship was only observed in cohort studies with follow-up durations greater than 16.5 years and in case-control studies lasting less than 3.5 years ( 108455 ). Highest and lowest DII scores were not reported, limiting the validity of these findings. Some research has also evaluated whether an anti-inflammatory diet can affect breast cancer mortality. Prior to diagnosis, it is unclear whether a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a higher risk of death from breast cancer, as the available evidence is mixed ( 98991 , 98992 ). However, observational research has found that consuming the most pro-inflammatory diet for at least 15 years after diagnosis is associated with more than a 2-fold increased risk of overall mortality when compared with consuming the most anti-inflammatory diet. There was no association in patients with less than 15 years follow-up ( 104693 ).

Cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of overall cancer risk. See other Effectiveness entries for information on specific cancer types.

Observational research has found that consuming the most pro-inflammatory diet increases the odds of having any type of cancer by 32% to 58%, as well as the odds of cancer mortality by about 14%, when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104692 , 104708 , 110163 ). Sub-analyses have found that the odds of digestive tract cancers, hormone-dependent cancers, respiratory tract cancers, and urothelial cancers are increased by 14% to 64% with a pro-inflammatory diet ( 104692 ). However, it is unclear if following an anti-inflammatory diet actually reduces the risk of cancer.

Child development.

It is unclear if following an anti-inflammatory diet during pregnancy is beneficial for the mental development of the child.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet during pregnancy is associated with increased odds of the child having symptoms of anxiety, depression, and aggression at 7-10 years of age when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet. A one-unit increase in the pro-inflammatory diet score during pregnancy was found to be associated with a 7% increased risk of the child having borderline to clinical symptoms ( 104698 ).

Chronic kidney disease (CKD).

It is unclear if the anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of CKD.

Observational research in patients with CKD has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a more than two-fold increased odds of being in a higher stage of CKD when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet. However, there was no association between diet and kidney function markers ( 101191 ).

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

It is unclear if the anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of COPD.

Observational research in adults aged 20-49 years has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 66% increased risk of developing COPD when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet, with a linear relationship between the inflammatory potential of the diet and the development of COPD. There is also an inverse correlation between the inflammatory potential of the diet and some measures of lung function ( 110168 ).

Colorectal adenoma.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of colorectal adenomas.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with 28% to 41% higher odds of colorectal adenomas in males when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. This association was much weaker in females ( 98981 ).

Colorectal cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of colorectal cancer.

Meta-analyses of observational research have found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 40% to 43% greater incidence of colorectal cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 98988 , 104708 ). After diagnosis, however, observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet for up to 6 months is not associated with a reduced incidence of colorectal cancer recurrence or mortality ( 104694 ).

Coronary heart disease (CHD).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of CHD.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 15% to 24% greater incidence of CHD when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. This association was most evident in females ( 110166 ).

Depression.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of depression.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 22% to 45% increased odds of having symptoms of depression or being diagnosed with depression when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. Effects were stronger in females ( 101192 , 104703 , 110170 ). Other observational research in premenopausal females has found that the odds of experiencing depression is 3.2- to 6.3-fold higher in those with the top 75% DII scores when compared with the lowest 25%. In postmenopausal females, only the most pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 2.1-fold increased odds of experiencing depression when compared with the most anti-inflammatory diet ( 110157 ). However, the lack of correlation with C-reactive protein levels in this study suggests that the association between the DII score and depression may not be mediated by inflammation. Another meta-analysis of observational research in patients with mental health symptoms or disorders has found that the highest dietary inflammatory index (DII) score (indicating a more pro-inflammatory diet) is associated with a 28% increased risk of depressive symptoms when compared with the lowest DII score, with each 1-unit increase in DII score associated with a 6% increased risk of depressive symptoms ( 108457 ). This meta-analysis did not define lowest and highest DII scores, limiting the validity of these findings.

Diabetes.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of diabetes.

A meta-analysis of 48 observational studies has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is not associated with risk of type 2 diabetes. However, when the analysis is limited to eight high-quality studies, consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 58% increased risk of type 2 diabetes when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet ( 110159 ). Also, other observational research has found that consuming an anti-inflammatory diet is associated with a reduced risk of diabetes when compared with consuming a pro-inflammatory diet. This association is mainly mediated by the effect of the diet on body mass index ( 101197 ). A small clinical study in patients with diabetes or pre-diabetes shows that following an anti-inflammatory diet program for 6 weeks is about as effective as a diet based on the American Diabetes Association (ADA) recommendations for the management of pre-diabetes and diabetes. Any benefits of either diet were likely associated with weight loss ( 101190 ).

Endometrial cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of endometrial cancer.

Some observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 46% greater incidence of endometrial cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 98990 ). In contrast, other observational research has not found an association between a pro-inflammatory diet and the incidence of endometrial cancer or survival in the general population. However, there was a 60% increased risk of endometrial cancer in individuals with a body mass index (BMI) of at least 35 kg/m2 who consumed a pro-inflammatory diet when compared with those who consumed an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104699 ).

Esophageal cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of esophageal cancer.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 2.7-fold increased incidence of esophageal cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104708 ).

Familial Mediterranean fever.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for familial Mediterranean fever.

Observational research in patients aged 7-24 years has found that consuming an anti-inflammatory diet, in addition to taking vitamin D 4000 IU, turmeric 10 mg, and flaxseed 2 small spoons daily for 6 months, may improve the duration, frequency, and severity of familial Mediterranean fever attacks, and is associated with a 2-point improvement in mini-mental state examination (MMSE) scores, when compared with baseline ( 108459 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Additionally, it is unclear if this effect is due to consuming an anti-inflammatory diet, the supplements, or the combination.

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the treatment of fibromyalgia.

Observational research in females with fibromyalgia has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with increased pressure pain sensitivity at specific sites when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. However, there was no association between the diet and self-reported global pain, disease severity, fatigue, sleep, or feelings of anxiety ( 101196 ). Also, a small clinical trial in females with fibromyalgia shows that consuming an anti-inflammatory diet for 3 months modestly improves overall severity, pain, function, gastrointestinal symptoms, sleep quality, fatigue, and quality of life when compared with general healthy eating recommendations. However, there was no change in inflammatory biomarkers. This diet was also low in fermentable oligo, di, and monosaccharides and polyols (FODMAPs) for the first month ( 110172 ).

Gastric cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of gastric cancer.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 95% increased risk of gastric cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. When analyzed by sex, these findings were limited to males ( 101193 ). It is unclear if following an anti-inflammatory diet reduces the risk of gastric cancer.

Gingivitis.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the treatment of gingivitis.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with gingivitis shows that following an anti-inflammatory diet for 4 weeks reduces gingival inflammation by a small amount but does not reduce gingival bleeding or plaque, when compared with not following an anti-inflammatory diet ( 101198 ). The anti-inflammatory diet used in this study was not scored using the dietary inflammatory index (DII) and had no effect on inflammatory biomarkers.

Heart failure.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of heart failure.

Observational research has found that consumption of a diet with the highest anti-inflammatory potential is associated with a 14% to 19% reduced risk of heart failure in current or ex-smokers when compared with those consuming a diet with the lowest anti-inflammatory potential ( 104702 ). This study used the anti-inflammatory diet index (AIDI) to score the inflammatory potential of the diets instead of the more common dietary inflammatory index (DII). Similarly, observational research from the National Survey of the National Center for Health Statistics has found that consuming an anti-inflammatory diet (DII score: 1 to 4.6) is associated with an 11% increased risk of heart failure when compared with those consuming an anti-inflammatory diet (DII scores: -3.9 to 0.6) ( 108454 ). This study included patients with and without an existing diagnosis of heart failure, limiting the validity of these findings.

Hypertension.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of hypertension.

In females aged 40-65 years, observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a slight increased risk of hypertension when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104715 ). Conversely, other observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 2.9-fold increased odds of hypertension in males, but not females, when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet ( 110164 ). In adolescents in the US, observational research has found that consuming the most pro-inflammatory diet is associated with an increased risk of high blood pressure when compared to the least inflammatory diet ( 110165 ).

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the treatment of IBD.

A retrospective review of 27 adults suggests that implementing the IBD-Anti-Inflammatory Diet (IBD-AID) with at least 60% compliance is associated with a subjective reduction in IBD symptoms in 89% of patients ( 98983 ). However, observational research has found that the inflammatory potential of the diet is not associated with symptom activity in individuals with IBD ( 101189 ).

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of IBS.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 36% increased odds of having IBS when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. When examining individual groups, the association was found only in females and in those with a body mass index (BMI) of at least 25 kg/m2. There was no association between diet and severity of IBS symptoms ( 101188 ).

Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of kidney stones or stone recurrence.

Observational research from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet (dietary inflammatory index [DII]: 2.9 to 5.5) is associated with a 38% increased likelihood of kidney stone development and a 61% increased risk of stone recurrence when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet (DII scores: -5 to 0.1) ( 108458 ). Information on dietary intake was from a single 24-hour dietary recall, which may not accurately reflect long-term intake, therefore limiting the validity of these findings. Other observational research has found that consuming the most pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 72% higher odds of kidney stone occurrence over the lifespan when compared with those consuming the most anti-inflammatory diet ( 110156 ).

Laryngeal cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of laryngeal cancer.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 3.3-fold increased odds of having laryngeal cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. The strongest effects were seen in individuals less than 60 years of age or with a body mass index of at least 25 kg/m2 ( 104709 ).

Liver cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of liver cancer.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 2.4-fold increased odds of hepatocellular carcinoma when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. Sub-analyses found associations only in males and in hepatitis B- and C-negative patients ( 104710 ). It is unclear if following an anti-inflammatory diet actually reduces the risk of liver cancer.

Low birth weight.

It is unclear if consuming an anti-inflammatory diet during pregnancy is beneficial for preventing low birth weight in the infant.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with 8% increased odds of having an infant weighing less than 2500 grams when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. There is no association between diet and having an infant weighing less than 1500 grams or of having a small for gestational age (SGA) infant ( 101187 ).

Lung cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of lung cancer.

A meta-analysis of two observational studies has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is not associated with an increased risk of lung cancer when compared with an anti-inflammatory diet. However, the evidence from the two individual studies is mixed ( 104708 ).

Metabolic syndrome.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of metabolic syndrome.

Some individual observational studies and a meta-analysis of observational research has found that consuming the most pro-inflammatory diet is associated with having metabolic syndrome and its individual components when compared to the least pro-inflammatory diet ( 104701 , 110161 ). However, other observational studies and an earlier meta-analysis have found that there is no association between the inflammatory potential of the diet and the occurrence of metabolic syndrome in adults or adolescents ( 98982 , 104695 , 110165 ). Further observational research in patients who already have metabolic syndrome has found that the diet does not seem to affect the risk for having a cardiovascular event ( 98974 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the treatment of MS.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with relapsing-remitting MS shows that consuming a modified anti-inflammatory diet for 12 weeks reduces fatigue and improves quality of life by a small amount when compared with healthy diet recommendations. The modified anti-inflammatory diet recommendations included abundant amounts of spices, fruits, and vegetables, as well as whole grain, plant protein, and healthy fat recommendations ( 104705 ). In contrast, observational research has found that the inflammatory potential of a diet is not associated with the clinical condition, including strength and disability, of individuals with multiple sclerosis ( 101711 ).

Myocardial infarction (MI).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of an MI.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a higher incidence of MI in males, but not in females, when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 98934 ).

Nasopharyngeal cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of nasopharyngeal cancer.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 60% higher incidence of nasopharyngeal cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104711 ).

Neuropathic pain.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the treatment of neuropathic pain.

A small clinical study in patients with spinal cord injury (SCI) shows that following a specific anti-inflammatory diet program for 12 weeks modestly reduces neuropathic sensory pain when compared with following a control diet ( 98906 ). However, the validity of these findings is limited by the unexplained difference in baseline sensory pain between groups. Also, the anti-inflammatory diet program used in this study involved avoiding foods commonly associated with intolerance and consuming multiple supplements, such as omega-3 fatty acids, antioxidants, chlorella, zinc, selenium, turmeric, and vegetable protein.

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of NAFLD.

Observational research of adults in Iran has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet (dietary inflammatory index [DII]: 0) is associated with higher fatty liver index scores and increased liver enzyme levels when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet (DII score: -4) ( 108460 ). The validity of these findings is limited due to unclear reporting.

Non-Hodgkin lymphoma.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of non-Hodgkin lymphoma.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 61% increased risk of non-Hodgkin lymphoma when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. Sub-analyses found that this relationship was seen only in males and in individuals with diffuse large B-cell lymphoma ( 104712 ).

Obesity.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of obesity or for weight loss.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with obesity shows that consuming an energy-restricted anti-inflammatory diet for 24 weeks results in weight and visceral fat loss, as well as improvements in cardiometabolic parameters, when compared to baseline. However, it was no more effective than a standard energy-restricted diet. The anti-inflammatory energy-restricted diet was based on whole-grain products, legumes, fruit and vegetables, nuts and seeds, fish, herbs and spices, etc. ( 104697 ). Another clinical trial in overweight patients with osteoarthritis shows that following an anti-inflammatory low-calorie diet for 2 months results in an additional 4.1 kg weight loss when compared to a low-calorie diet without a focus on anti-inflammatory foods. Both diets were based on an energy deficit of 500 kcals. The anti-inflammatory diet was comprised of about 66% of energy intake from fruits and vegetables, along with whole grains and protein derived from plants, fish, and low-fat dairy sources ( 110174 ). Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a higher risk for overweight or obesity in both adults and children when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 98986 , 101195 ). In contrast, observational research has not found an association between the inflammatory nature of the diet and risk of central adiposity. However, consuming the most pro-inflammatory diet was associated with a 1.8 cm increase in waist circumference when compared with the least pro-inflammatory diet ( 104706 ).

Oral cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of oral cancer.

Observational research has found that consuming the most pro-inflammatory diet is associated with over a 2-fold increased risk of oral cancer when compared with consuming the most anti-inflammatory diet ( 104713 , 110167 ). One study found that an age of at least 60 years, being female, and smoking or alcohol use all increased the risk associated with the pro-inflammatory diet ( 104713 ).

Osteoarthritis.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the treatment of osteoarthritis.

A small clinical trial in overweight patients with mild to moderate symptoms of knee osteoarthritis shows that following an anti-inflammatory low-calorie diet for 2 months results in modest improvements in weight, pain, physical function, and quality of life when compared to a low-calorie diet without a focus on anti-inflammatory foods. Both diets were based on an energy deficit of 500 kcals. The anti-inflammatory diet was comprised of about 66% of energy intake from fruits and vegetables, along with whole grains and protein derived from plants, fish, and low-fat dairy sources ( 110174 ).

Osteoporosis.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of osteoporosis or changes in bone mineral density (BMD).

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a higher incidence of osteoporosis and fractures when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. However, individual studies are inconsistent ( 104704 ). Additional observational research in the US has found that consuming a more pro-inflammatory diet is associated with up to a 6-fold increased risk of osteoporosis in females, but not males, when compared with consuming the most anti-inflammatory diet. There was no association with osteopenia ( 110155 ). Conversely, a small cross-sectional study in postmenopausal females has found that a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with an increasing incidence of osteopenia, but a decreased incidence of osteoporosis ( 110160 ). This study is limited by its small size. Some research has evaluated the association between an anti-inflammatory diet and BMD. A meta-analysis of observational research has found that the BMD of the lumbar spine and hip are reduced by 1% and 0.5%, respectively, with each one-unit increase in the dietary inflammatory index (DII) score. However, the DII is not associated with changes in total hip BMD ( 104704 ). Other observational research in the US has found that consuming a more pro-inflammatory diet is associated with reduced BMD in the hip and femoral neck in females, and in the lumbar spine in males ( 110155 ). However, a small cross-sectional study in postmenopausal females has found that increased consumption of a pro-inflammatory diet is not associated with BMD ( 110160 ).

Ovarian cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of ovarian cancer.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a higher incidence of ovarian cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104708 ). Another meta-analysis of observational research has found that the highest dietary inflammatory index (DII) score (indicating a more pro-inflammatory diet) is associated with a 42% increased risk of ovarian cancer when compared with the lowest DII score, with every one-point increase in the DII score associated with a 10% increased risk of ovarian cancer. Sub-analyses suggest that this relationship is observed only in postmenopausal adults ( 108456 ). This meta-analysis included studies that employed DII scores as well as studies using empirical dietary inflammatory pattern scores; ranges of these scores were not reported, limiting the validity of these findings.

Overall mortality.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for reducing overall mortality.

Three large observational studies and a meta-analysis of observational research have found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with an increase in risk of all-cause mortality when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. In the meta-analysis, the risk increased by 23% ( 101199 , 110163 ). Other observational research has found that consuming an anti-inflammatory diet is associated with a reduced risk of all-cause mortality. Furthermore, every one-point increase in the dietary inflammatory index score is associated with an 8% increased risk of all-cause mortality ( 98957 ).

Pancreatic cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of pancreatic cancer.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 2.5-fold increased odds of having pancreatic cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104714 ). A meta-analysis of observational research has also found that consuming the most pro-inflammatory diet (dietary inflammatory index [DII]: 1.3) is associated with a 45% increased risk of developing pancreatic cancer when compared with consuming the least pro-inflammatory diet (DII: 5.6). A dose response meta-analysis of 4 studies has found that the risk of pancreatic cancer is increased by 8% with each one-unit increase in DII score ( 108453 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the high heterogeneity of included studies and moderate risk for bias.

Pregnancy-induced hypertension.

It is unclear if consuming an anti-inflammatory diet during pregnancy is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of pregnancy-induced hypertension.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 27% increased odds of developing pregnancy-induced hypertension when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 101187 ).

Preterm labor.

It is unclear if consuming an anti-inflammatory diet during pregnancy is beneficial for the prevention of preterm labor.

Observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 29% increased odds of preterm labor prior to 34 weeks, but not prior to 37 weeks, when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 101187 ).

Prostate cancer.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of prostate cancer.

Meta-analyses of observational research have found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 45% to 73% increased risk of prostate cancer when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 101194 , 104708 ).

Renal cell carcinoma.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of renal cell carcinoma.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a higher incidence of renal cell carcinoma when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 104708 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the treatment of RA.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with RA shows that consuming a diet rich in foods considered to have anti-inflammatory potential for 10 weeks does not improve the erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) or symptoms of swollen or tender joints when compared with a control diet ( 104690 ). A secondary analysis from this study shows that adherence of at least 80% to this specific anti-inflammatory diet improves ESR when compared with consuming a control diet ( 108461 ). However, a separate secondary analysis from this study shows that following this specific diet does not improve pain, fatigue, morning stiffness, disability, or quality of life when compared with a control diet ( 108463 ). The diet used in this study is rich in foods such as fruits, vegetables, legumes, omega-3 fatty acids, and fermented dairy products, but has not specifically been shown to reduce levels of inflammatory mediators in the body. A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with RA shows that consuming diets considered to be anti-inflammatory, including vegetarian diets and the Mediterranean diet, modestly reduces pain and swollen joint count, but not tender joint count or measures of inflammation, when compared with regular diets ( 109887 ). One small observational study in females with RA has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with increased disease activity and C-reactive protein levels when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet ( 110169 ).

Sarcopenia.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for the prevention of sarcopenia.

Observational research in older adults in the US has found that consuming a pro-inflammatory diet is associated with a 68% increased likelihood of having low muscle mass and low peak force when compared with consuming an anti-inflammatory diet. Also, each one-unit increase in the dietary inflammatory index was associated with a 12% increase in combined low muscle mass and strength ( 110173 ).

Schizophrenia.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for reducing the risk of schizophrenia.

Observational research in patients with mental health symptoms or disorders has found that the highest dietary inflammatory index (DII) score is associated with a 4-fold increased risk of schizophrenia when compared with the lowest DII score ( 108457 ). Actual DII scores were not reported, limiting the validity of these findings.

Ulcerative colitis.

It is unclear if an anti-inflammatory diet is beneficial for preventing relapse in patients with ulcerative colitis.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with ulcerative colitis of mild to moderate severity shows that a 7-week treatment with fecal microbiota transplantation while also consuming an anti-inflammatory diet induces a clinical response in 66% of patients, compared with 36% of those given optimized standard treatment. In patients with a clinical response, following an anti-inflammatory maintenance diet in conjunction with standard medications for an additional 40 weeks results in a 25% rate of remission, compared with 0% in those not following the anti-inflammatory diet ( 110175 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the anti-inflammatory diet for these uses.

There is no specific anti-inflammatory diet. The anti-inflammatory dietary style involves eliminating food intolerances and foods that induce inflammation while also consuming foods with anti-inflammatory properties. In some cases, supplements with anti-inflammatory properties, including omega-3 fatty acids, coenzyme Q10, green tea extract, and turmeric, are sometimes encouraged ( 98357 , 98906 , 101200 ). Dietary components considered anti-inflammatory according to the dietary inflammatory index (DII) include fiber, alcohol, mono- and poly-unsaturated fatty acids, omega-3 and -6 fatty acids, most essential vitamins and minerals, anthocyanidins, flavonols, flavan3ols, flavanones, flavones, isoflavones, and eugenol. Specific foods such as garlic, ginger, onions, thyme, oregano, saffron, turmeric, rosemary, caffeine, and tea are also considered anti-inflammatory ( 98988 ). Another scale, the anti-inflammatory diet index (AIDI), has also been used. Anti-inflammatory foods in the AIDI includes foods rich in antioxidants, dietary fiber, and mono- and poly-unsaturated fatty acids ( 104702 ). The IBD-Anti-Inflammatory Diet (IBD-AID) consists of lean meats, poultry, fish, eggs, specific carbohydrate sources, nut and legume flours, limited aged cheeses, honey, fresh cultured yogurt, kefir, miso and other cultured products. It also emphasizes the use of probiotics and the consumption of softened foods ( 98983 ). According to the empirical dietary inflammatory index or pattern (EDIP), anti-inflammatory dietary components include tea, coffee, dark yellow vegetables, leafy green vegetables, snacks such as potato chips, corn chips, and crackers, fruit juice, and pizza ( 100189 , 101712 ).

Proponents of the anti-inflammatory diet believe that diseases are the result of a pro-inflammatory state which occurs from consumption of too much pro-inflammatory food. Consuming more anti-inflammatory food is thought to have overall beneficial therapeutic and preventative effects ( 98357 , 98906 ). Some clinical research has shown that consuming a diet rich in anti-inflammatory nutrients is associated with improvements in levels of inflammatory markers, including cytokines, eicosanoids, and C-reactive protein ( 101200 ). An evidence-based, validated scale called the dietary inflammatory index (DII) has been developed to evaluate the inflammatory potential of specific dietary patterns. The DII includes 45 food parameters that were shown to impact inflammatory markers such as C-reactive protein (CRP), tumor necrosis factor (TNF), interleukin (IL)-6, IL-4, IL-10, and others. Based on the strength of the association and the quality of the research, these food parameters were given an inflammatory effect score. When an entire diet is evaluated, these scores are combined and summarized into the DII score. The possible DII scores range from maximally anti-inflammatory at -8.87 to maximally pro-inflammatory at +7.98 ( 98934 , 98357 ). Of the 45 food parameters incorporated into the DII, 36 are anti-inflammatory and 9 are pro-inflammatory. Typically, foods with low DII scores are nutrient-dense, flavorful, colorful, and calorie-sparse. Foods with a high DII score are typically white or colorless, nutrient-sparse, calorie-dense, and flavorless, although they may still have a strong taste (sweet or salty). Research suggests that a lower DII score predicts decreases in markers of inflammation ( 98988 ). Another scale, the anti-inflammatory diet index (AIDI), has also been used. On this scale, each 1-point increment has been associated with a 0.06 mg/L reduction in CRP ( 104702 ).
