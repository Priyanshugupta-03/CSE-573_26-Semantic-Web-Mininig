## Overview

Figwort is a flowering plant that grows in temperate regions of the northern hemisphere. Common figwort (Scrophularia nodosa) is found in western Asia and throughout Europe ( 100599 ). Other species of figwort are found in the United States and Canada ( 100598 ).  Figwort is sometimes used as a substitute for devil's claw due to its similar chemical composition ( 4 ).

## History

In 1963, p-methoxycinnamic acid was isolated from figwort ( 99895 ). In 1966, the chemical composition of figwort was first studied ( 99895 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, figwort is used for congestive heart failure (CHF) and hypertension. Topically, figwort is used for atopic dermatitis (eczema), pruritus, psoriasis, acne, hemorrhoids, burns, wound healing, and polymorphous light eruption (PMLE).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of figwort.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

No adverse effects have been reported; however, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of figwort.

#### Adult
Oral:

General : Traditionally as a tea, 2-8 grams of dried above ground parts steeped in 150 mL of boiling water for 5-10 minutes has been used. As a liquid extract, prepared 1:1 in 25% alcohol, 2-8 mL has been used. As a tincture, prepared 1:10 in 45% alcohol, 2-4 mL has been used ( 4 ).

#### DIURETIC DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Theoretically, overuse of figwort might compound diuretic-induced potassium loss ( 18 ). There is some concern that people taking figwort along with potassium depleting diuretics might have an increased risk for hypokalemia. Initiation of potassium supplementation or an increase in potassium supplement dose may be necessary for some patients. Some diuretics that can deplete potassium include chlorothiazide (Diuril), chlorthalidone (Thalitone), furosemide (Lasix), hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ, Hydrodiuril, Microzide), and others.

#### LITHIUM
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence PROBABLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Figwort is thought to have diuretic properties ( 18 ). Theoretically, due to these potential diuretic effects, figwort might reduce excretion and increase levels of lithium. The dose of lithium might need to be decreased.

None known.

#### DIABETES

Theoretically, figwort may alter blood glucose control ( 4 ); monitor closely.

#### VENTRICULAR TACHYCARDIA

Contraindicated ( 4 , 12 ).

None known.

No cytotoxic effects were reported with various extracts of figwort in a brine shrimp lethality assay ( 99895 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the pharmacokinetics of figwort.

#### General

The applicable parts of figwort are the above ground parts and root. Figwort constituents include saponins, tannins, and iridoid glycosides ( 99895 , 99896 ).

#### Analgesic effects

Animal research shows that some figwort extracts have analgesic effects ( 99895 ). The mechanism of these effects is unknown.

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

Harpagide, a constituent of figwort, may have anti-inflammatory activity ( 4 ).

#### Antibacterial effects

In vitro research shows that some figwort extracts have activity against Bacillus subtilis, Salmonella typhi, and Staphylococcus aureus but lack activity against Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Shigella flexneri ( 99895 ). The mechanism of these effects is unknown.

#### Antifungal effects

In vitro research shows that some figwort extracts have activity against Aspergillus flavus, Candida glabrata, Fusarium solani, Microsporum canis, and Trichophyton longifusus but lack activity against Candida albicans ( 99895 ). The mechanism of these effects is unknown.

#### Antioxidant effects

In vitro research shows that some figwort extracts have antioxidant effects through scavenging of free radicals ( 99895 ).

#### Cardiac effects

Harpagide, a constituent of figwort, may have cardioactive activity ( 4 ).

#### Diuretic effects

Figwort is reported to have diuretic effects ( 18 ).

#### Laxative effects

Animal research suggests that the figwort constituents aucubin and catalpol exert laxative effects ( 4 ).

#### Wound healing effects

In vitro research shows that iridoid glycoside constituents extracted from figwort can stimulate the growth of human dermal fibroblasts, although the mechanism of this effect is unknown ( 99896 ).
