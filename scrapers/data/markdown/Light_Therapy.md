## Overview

Light therapy, also known as phototherapy, involves exposing the skin to specific wavelengths of light in order to help with a variety of conditions. Light therapy may be performed using lasers, light-emitting diodes (LEDs), fluorescent lamps, dichroic lamps, or very bright full-spectrum light ( 98299 ). It may also be combined with pretreatment with a light-sensitizing drug, such as psoralen ( 28975 ). Note : This review focuses primarily on nontargeted light therapies. Photochemotherapy, photodynamic therapy (PDT), photopheresis, ocular light therapy, and targeted light therapy, such as laser and intense pulsed light (IPL), will not be extensively discussed.

Likely Safe when light therapy is used with proper administration techniques under the guidance of a clinician, short-term. Light therapy has been used in numerous clinical trials without severe side effects ( 28912 , 28918 , 28980 , 49182 , 98313 , 106586 , 109260 , 109268 , 109271 , 109272 ).

Possibly Safe when ultraviolet A (UVA) or ultraviolet B (UVB) therapy is used long-term. Some experts have raised concerns of an increased risk for skin cancer ( 28975 ). However, one review of the literature suggests that UVB therapy does not increase the risk for skin cancer when used for up to 5 years ( 28973 ). Other research using a cancer registry database also found no association between whole-body UVA1 phototherapy and basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, melanoma, non-Hodgkin's lymphoma, or prostate cancer during follow-up of up to 20 years ( 104117 ).

Possibly Unsafe when psoralens with UVA (PUVA) therapy is used long-term due to an increased risk for skin cancer. A meta-analysis and review of the available literature suggest that long-term treatment with PUVA increases the risk for squamous cell carcinoma. It is unclear if PUVA increases the risk for basal cell carcinoma or other forms of skin cancer ( 28976 , 28977 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when light therapy is used in recommended doses under the guidance of a clinician, short-term. Low-level red-light therapy has been used on the retina without adverse effects ( 109261 ). Also, light therapy has been used in infants with hyperbilirubinemia in clinical studies without short-term adverse effects ( 28903 , 28904 ). While observational research suggests that exposure to light therapy as a neonate, most often for hyperbilirubinemia, is associated with greater odds of cancer, most of these studies did not adjust for confounding variables or determine whether the intensity or duration of light therapy alters risk ( 109266 , 114530 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Unsafe when light therapy is used for longer than 24 hours in children less than 3 years old. A very large birth cohort study conducted in Japan suggests that infants exposed to more than 24 hours of light therapy have greater odds of developing food allergies at 2 years old, allergic disorders by 3 years old, and food allergies by 3 years old ( 114537 ). Another study in the same cohort of infants suggests that a dose-response relationship exists between light therapy exposures longer than 24 hours and an increased risk of developmental delays by 3 years of age in the domains of communication, gross motor, problem solving, and personal-social skills ( 114536 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Safe when bright light therapy is used short-term. Other types of light therapies, especially PUVA, have not been extensively evaluated in pregnancy or lactation. One preliminary study suggests that PUVA use does not result in teratogenic effects ( 28986 ). Until more information is available, avoid use in those who are pregnant and lactating.

#### General

Phototherapy seems to be well tolerated when used appropriately.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Mild burning, nausea, phototoxicity, pruritus, and redness.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Hypomania or mania, skin cancers such as squamous cell carcinoma.

#### Dermatologic

UV phototherapy may cause xerosis, pruritus, hyperpigmentation, photoaging, blisters, mild burning, redness, erosion, erythema, moderate heat sensation, skin dryness, thinning of skin (atrophy), sweating, flushing, and phototoxicity ( 28901 , 28911 , 28916 , 28918 , 28936 , 28938 , 49182 , 87850 , 106586 , 109272 )( 109274 , 114531 ). Long-term exposure to ultraviolet radiation may cause actinic damage and premature skin aging ( 28911 , 28912 , 28938 , 49182 ). Folliculitis has occurred in patients undergoing psoralens with UVA therapy (PUVA) ( 109274 ). There is some concern that long-term use of PUVA may increase the risk of skin cancers such as squamous cell carcinoma ( 28975 , 28976 , 28977 , 28978 ). However, most research has found that long-term UVA and UVB phototherapy, without psoralens, is not associated with an increased risk of skin cancer and that UVB and PUVA phototherapy might not increase the risk of skin cancer in non-Caucasian patients ( 28973 , 28974 , 28975 , 106826 , 106827 , 109274 , 109275 ). Also, in patients with vitiligo, a meta-analysis of observational research has found that UVB and/or PUVA light therapy are not associated with skin cancer risk, regardless of ethnicity or number of treatment sessions. However, only five studies were included in the analysis ( 109270 ). Additionally, other research using a cancer registry database found no association between whole-body UVA1 phototherapy and risk of basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, or melanoma during follow-up of up to 20 years ( 104117 ).

#### Endocrine

Weight loss, dehydration, and changes in body temperature have been reported in adults using light therapy ( 28903 , 106586 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Nausea is a commonly reported side effect of light therapy ( 28938 , 28979 , 106586 ). Changes in appetite, dry mouth, diarrhea, constipation, and abdominal pain have also been reported ( 106586 ).

#### Genitourinary

Erection problems have been reported in adults using light therapy ( 106586 ).

#### Immunologic

Exposure to light therapy for more than 24 hours as an infant is linked to higher odds of developing food allergies and other allergic disorders later in childhood ( 114537 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

Muscle pain, weakness, and fatigue have been reported in adults using light therapy ( 106586 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Headache, decreased sex drive, tremor, and trouble falling asleep have been reported in patients receiving light therapy ( 28931 , 28936 , 28979 , 106586 , 109264 , 109271 ).  Exposure to light therapy for more than 24 hours as an infant is linked to higher odds of development delays in the domains of communication, gross motor, problem solving, and personal-social skills by 3 years of age ( 114536 ).

#### Ocular/Otic

Light therapy may cause eye strain, visual disturbances, or ocular damage ( 28980 , 109271 ). Eye irritation and dry eyes have also been reported ( 28936 ).

#### Oncologic

There is some concern that long-term use of psoralens and UVA (PUVA) may increase the risk of skin cancers such as squamous cell carcinoma ( 28975 , 28976 , 28977 , 28978 ). However, most research has found that long-term UVA and UVB phototherapy, without psoralens, is not associated with an increased risk of skin cancer and that UVB and PUVA phototherapy might not increase the risk of skin cancer in non-Caucasian patients ( 28973 , 28974 , 28975 , 106826 , 106827 , 109274 , 109275 ). Also, in patients with vitiligo, a meta-analysis of observational research has found that UVB and/or PUVA light therapy are not associated with skin cancer risk, regardless of ethnicity or number of treatment sessions. However, only five studies were included in the analysis ( 109270 ). Additionally, other research using a cancer registry database found no association between whole-body UVA1 phototherapy and risk of basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, melanoma, non-Hodgkin's lymphoma, or prostate cancer during follow-up of up to 20 years ( 104117 ). Some observational research suggests that exposure to light therapy as a neonate, most often for hyperbilirubinemia, is associated with greater odds of cancer. However, most of these studies did not adjust for confounding variables or determine whether the intensity or duration of light therapy alters risk ( 109266 , 114530 ).

#### Psychiatric

Light therapy may cause agitation, and rarely hypomania or mania ( 28931 , 28936 ). Jumpiness, jitteriness, nervousness, irritability, anxiety, delusions, and aggressive behavior has been reported in patients with dementia ( 28979 , 106586 ).

Effective
Neonatal jaundice.

Light therapy reduces bilirubin levels in the treatment of neonatal jaundice.

Various guidelines recommend the use of phototherapy blue spectrum 460-390 nm with at least 30 mcW/cm2 light energy per nanometer for hyperbilirubinemia in infants ( 28906 , 28907 , 28908 , 28909 , 30521 ). One meta-analysis shows that both conventional phototherapy and a specific fiber-optic phototherapy (BiliBlanket) are effective for decreasing serum bilirubin levels when compared with no treatment in neonates with hyperbilirubinemia. The analysis also shows that conventional phototherapy is more effective than fiber-optic phototherapy and that combined therapies are more effective than either therapy alone ( 28904 ). Another meta-analysis of 6 studies shows that light-emitting diode (LED) phototherapy may be equally effective to conventional phototherapy for decreasing bilirubin levels in neonates with unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia ( 28903 ). A small meta-analysis of 4 studies shows that intermittent phototherapy is equally as effective as continuous phototherapy but is associated with fewer adverse effects ( 106824 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies. Some neonates can experience rebound hyperbilirubinemia after receiving phototherapy for neonatal jaundice. Observational research has found that neonates with low birth weight, sepsis, and hemolysis are at higher risk for rebound hyperbilirubinemia. Additionally, any neonate receiving intensive or a short duration of phototherapy is also at an increased risk for rebound hyperbilirubinemia ( 101358 ).

Psoriasis.

Light therapy, including ultraviolet B (UVB) or psoralens with ultraviolet A (PUVA), is effective for the treatment of psoriasis.

Various guidelines recommend the use of phototherapy, including UVB or PUVA light therapy, as a treatment option for psoriasis ( 28910 , 28911 , 101359 ). Using phototherapy, especially UVB and PUVA, is effective for decreasing severity of psoriasis ( 28916 , 98297 ). Other clinical research shows that the combined therapy of UVB or PUVA with topical calcipotriene for 12 weeks is more effective than phototherapy alone ( 83780 ). Additionally, some preliminary clinical research in patients with human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) and mild to moderate psoriasis shows that UV therapy is effective when compared with baseline ( 28913 ). Furthermore, some clinical research shows that PUVA therapy is effective for treating a specific type of psoriasis called palmoplantar pustulosis when compared with control or baseline ( 28914 ).

Seasonal affective disorder (SAD).

Light therapy is effective in the treatment of SAD.

Meta-analyses of clinical research show that light therapy at a brightness of 3,000 or 10,000 lux is beneficial for treatment of SAD, and higher intensity light therapy appears to be more beneficial than dimmer therapy for non-atypical symptoms of SAD ( 28920 , 28921 ). A meta-analysis of two studies shows that light therapy seems to be similarly effective to fluoxetine 20 mg daily for treating SAD ( 106586 ). Another meta-analysis shows that the blue and green wavelengths of light are effective for reducing symptoms of depression, while red light does not have this effect ( 28923 ). The patients in these studies were typically exposed to light therapy for 0.5-3 hours each day, and the light was placed 1-1.5 feet away from the patient's face. Because of the predictable nature of SAD, there is interest in the use of preventive light therapy. However, a systematic review of light therapy for the prevention of SAD identified only one small, low-quality study evaluating the use of bright light therapy and infrared light therapy for 26 weeks, which yielded inconclusive findings ( 106585 ).

Likely Effective
Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

Ultraviolet A (UVA) and ultraviolet B (UVB) light therapy improves symptoms of eczema.

Clinical research shows that UVA and UVB light therapy reduces itch, inflammation, and other symptoms of eczema when compared with placebo ( 28901 , 49182 ).

Mycosis fungoides.

Light therapy is effective for the treatment of mycosis fungoides.

Phototherapy, such as ultraviolet B (UVB) or psoralens with ultraviolet A (PUVA) light therapy, has been shown to be effective for the early-stage mycosis fungoides variant of cutaneous T cell lymphoma ( 28940 , 28941 , 28942 , 28943 , 28944 , 28945 , 28946 , 28947 , 28948 , 28949 )( 28950 , 28951 , 28952 ).

Possibly Effective
Bipolar disorder.

Light therapy seems to improve symptoms of bipolar depression.

Most research shows that light therapy is beneficial for bipolar disorder, although there is some conflicting data. Several meta-analyses of clinical studies in patients with bipolar disorder and bipolar depression more specifically show that light therapy with or without conventional medication reduces disease severity when compared with control or baseline ( 98309 , 104114 ). Most studies used white light, but a few used green and red light ( 98309 ). In a small subgroup analysis, bright light therapy at an intensity of at least 5,000 lux was most effective ( 104114 ). Individual clinical studies also confirm benefit in bipolar depression. One clinical study in people with bipolar depression shows that 7,000-lux bright white light for 15-60 minutes daily for 6 weeks improves remission rate and reduces depression scores when compared with a dim red control light ( 98308 ). Another clinical study shows that bright light therapy for 1 hour every morning for 2 weeks in addition to conventional medications results in a 78% response rate, compared with only a 43% response rate with conventional medications alone ( 98313 ). However, another meta-analysis of studies in people with bipolar disorder, type I or type II, found no difference between bright light therapy and placebo for rates of remission from depressive episodes, depressive symptom scores, or rates of manic switching ( 104113 ). This analysis reported high heterogeneity between studies for light intensity used, duration of treatment, concurrent therapies, and time of day for light therapy.

Circadian rhythm sleep disorders.

Light therapy seems to be beneficial for sleep-wake cycle disturbances, including circadian rhythm sleep disorder.

Light therapy, usually at intensities of about 3,000-10,000 lux, has been used to help with sleep-wake cycle disturbances ( 28936 , 98310 ). A meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with circadian rhythm sleep disorder shows that light therapy seems to improve circadian rhythm, wake-up time, alertness, and other symptoms ( 98310 ). The validity of these findings is limited by small study size and the lack of a comparator group in most of the included studies.

Depression.

Bright light therapy seems to improve symptoms in patients with non-seasonal depression when used alone or in combination with other treatments.

Meta-analyses show that bright light therapy modestly reduces depressive symptoms and improves non-seasonal depression when compared with control, although it does not seem to improve pregnancy-related major depression ( 28920 , 28931 , 98296 ). However, a small clinical trial in patients with untreated pregnancy-related or postpartum major depression shows that bright light therapy results in remission in 42% of patients, compared with 0% of patients given placebo. Depression scores were reduced by at least 50% in 75% of patients using bright light therapy, compared with only 18% of those given placebo. Treatment was at an intensity of 10,000 lux for 45 minutes daily upon awakening for 3 weeks ( 109264 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of clinical studies in older adults with dementia shows that light therapy improves depression scores when compared with a control. Subgroup analysis suggests that light therapy interventions of less than 1000 lux for 8 to 12 weeks and non-stable types of light therapy such as ceiling and natural light provide the greatest reductions in depressive symptoms ( 114540 ). Light therapy has also been studied in conjunction with other therapies. Two meta-analyses of a total of 17 clinical studies show that adding bright light therapy to antidepressants for up to 8 weeks is more effective than antidepressants alone for major depressive episodes, including non-seasonal depression ( 98306 , 104116 ). Bright light therapy also seems to improve symptoms of depression when used in conjunction with other alternative treatment methods, such as sleep deprivation ( 28931 ). However, bright light therapy might not work as well as exercise therapy for non-seasonal depression ( 28933 ).

Herpes labialis (cold sores).

Narrow waveband light therapy reduces the time to healing of cold sores.

Some clinical research shows that using 1072 nm narrow waveband light therapy from a specific over-the-counter device (ViruLiteTM CS, Virulite LLC) for up to 5 minutes reduces the healing time of cold sores by up to 4 days when compared with placebo or topical acyclovir ( 28953 , 28954 ).

Insomnia.

Light therapy seems to improve sleep in patients with insomnia.

A meta-analysis of clinical studies shows that light therapy seems to improve the total sleep time, sleep onset, sleep quality, fatigue, and other symptoms in patients with insomnia ( 98310 ). The validity of these findings is limited by small study size and the lack of a comparator group in most of the included studies.

Pruritus.

Ultraviolet B (UVB) light therapy reduces uremic pruritus in patients with kidney failure.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with kidney failure receiving hemodialysis shows that UVB light therapy reduces uremic pruritus when compared with control ( 28935 ).

Scleroderma.

Light therapy, including broadband ultraviolet A (UVA/UVA1) light therapy, improves symptoms in patients with localized scleroderma.

Clinical research, including a meta-analysis, in patients with localized scleroderma shows that using phototherapy such as broadband UVA/UVA1 improves symptoms of scleroderma ( 28917 , 28918 , 101363 ). However phototherapy is less effective for scleroderma than treatment with methotrexate ( 101363 ).

Vitiligo.

Light therapy seems to improve re-pigmentation in patients with vitiligo, when used alone or in combination with other treatments.

In patients with vitiligo, use of light therapy, such as ultraviolet B (UVB), narrowband UVB, broadband UVB, ultraviolet A (UVA), or psoralens with UVA (PUVA), has been shown to modestly improve re-pigmentation ( 84075 , 87850 , 98298 , 106825 ). A modest therapeutic response or better occurs in 62% of patients using narrowband UVB and 62% of patients using PUVA after 3-6 months ( 84075 , 87850 , 98298 ). A small clinical study shows that targeted PUVA as 3 sessions weekly for 12 weeks improves re-pigmentation in 75% of lesions in the first month, compared with only 25% of lesions treated with targeted broadband UVB light therapy; however, success between treatments was equal at the end of 3 months ( 106825 ). Light therapy in combination with other treatments such as lactic acid has been shown to be more effective for vitiligo than using individual treatments alone ( 84075 , 87850 , 98298 ). However, a small and preliminary clinical trial in patients achieving 75% repigmentation after treatment with light therapy or laser therapy shows that narrowband UVB therapy once a month for 12 months does not reduce relapse when compared with a control group receiving no maintenance ( 109263 ). However, this study may not have been adequately powered to detect differences between groups.

Choroidal neovascular membranes (CNVM) . Light therapy seems to improve visual acuity in patients with CNVM caused by age-related macular degeneration, punctate inner choroidopathy, or ocular histoplasmosis syndrome.

Some clinical research in patients with CNVM caused by age-related macular degeneration, punctate inner choroidopathy, or ocular histoplasmosis syndrome shows that photodynamic therapy (PDT) with verteporfin may improve visual acuity when compared with control or baseline ( 28959 , 28960 , 28961 , 28962 ). While adding verteporfin PDT therapy to anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) therapy does not further improve visual acuity compared when with anti-VEGF therapy alone, adding light therapy does seem to reduce the number of anti-VEGF therapy injections required, which might reduce the risk for harm with anti-VEGF therapy ( 101360 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Acantholytic disorders.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial for the treatment of Grover disease.

A very small observational study in patients with transient acantholytic dermatosis (Grover disease) shows that non-ionizing blue light phototherapy for 18 minutes three times weekly for 5 consecutive weeks decreases lesion count and itch severity but does not change quality of life when compared to baseline ( 114539 ). The validity of these results is limited by the observational nature of the study and a lack of control group.

Acne.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial for the treatment of acne; the available clinical research has yielded mixed findings.

Clinical research in patients with mild to severe acne shows that non-targeted light therapies, such as blue light (405-470 nm) and/or red light (635-670 nm), twice daily to once or twice weekly for 4-8 weeks, reduces the number of acne lesions when compared with baseline ( 28100 , 106829 ). However, a meta-analysis shows that blue light therapy significantly improved investigator-assessed acne lesions in only 3 of the 14 included studies ( 104112 ). Similarly, a meta-analysis in patients with moderate to severe acne shows that red light therapy is similarly effective for reducing acne severity when compared with conventional therapy control ( 106828 ). There were no significant differences in the number of inflammatory and non-inflammatory lesions between blue and red light and comparator groups overall or at any time point ( 104112 , 106828 ). The studies included in these meta-analyses were small, short-term, and some had a high risk of bias, limiting the validity of these findings. Light therapy might have a synergistic effect when used in combination with conventional acne treatments such as metronidazole. A moderate-sized clinical study in patients with moderate to severe acne shows that administering metronidazole by needle-free jet injection followed by blue light therapy (Harmony XL, Alma Lasers Ltd.) for 15 minutes twice weekly for 6 weeks reduces acne lesion count and improves measures of skin barrier function but does not improve objective measures of acne severity or pigmentation when compared with metronidazole or blue light therapy alone ( 114547 ).

Allergic rhinitis (hay fever).

It is unclear if endonasal light therapy is beneficial in patients with allergic rhinitis.

A meta-analysis of small preliminary clinical studies shows that endonasal light therapy might improve nasal symptoms of allergic rhinitis such as itching, nasal obstruction, runny nose, and sneezing when compared with baseline ( 98301 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group in most of the included studies.

Alopecia areata.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with alopecia areata.

A meta-analysis of low quality clinical studies in Chinese patients with alopecia areata shows that light therapy combined with topical minoxidil 5% reduces alopecia severity, improves treatment response rate, and may reduce recurrence rate when compared with topical minoxidil alone. Types of light therapy investigated include fractional carbon dioxide lasers, 308-nm excimer laser/light, narrow band-UVB, and He-Ne laser ( 114548 ).

Alzheimer disease.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with Alzheimer disease.

A small clinical trial in patients with Alzheimer disease given conventional treatment shows that light therapy for 30 minutes each morning at up to 14000 lux for 4 weeks modestly improves delirium and behavior when compared with a dim light control ( 109267 ). Another very small clinical study in Chinese patients with Alzheimer disease shows that near-infrared light therapy twice for 6 minutes separated by 30 minute breaks 5 days per week for 12 weeks in addition to donepezil 5 mg daily modestly improves cognitive impairment and activities of daily living but does not reduce the severity of cognitive symptoms as measured by Alzheimer's Disease Assessment Scale-Cognitive Subscale (ADAS-Cog) or objective measures of cognitive deterioration when compared with donepezil alone ( 114533 ). The validity of these results is limited by a lack of blinding.

Androgenic alopecia.

Low-level light therapy seems to improve hair density and thickness in patients with androgenic alopecia.

A preliminary clinical study in people with androgenic alopecia shows that using low-level light therapy at 655 nm via a helmet-type device for 25 minutes every other day for 16 weeks improves hair density and thickness to a greater degree than with a sham device. The light therapy group had a hair density increase of about 42 hairs/cm2 and a hair thickness increase of 7.5 micrometers when compared with baseline, whereas the sham device group had an increase in density of 0.7 hairs/cm2 and a decrease in thickness of 15 micrometers when compared with baseline ( 104115 ).

Anorexia nervosa.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in people with anorexia nervosa.

A very small clinical study in people with anorexia nervosa shows that bright light therapy at an intensity of 10,000 lux for 30 minutes improves feelings of well-being and psychological symptoms when compared with baseline ( 28956 ). The validity of these findings is limited by small study size and the lack of a comparator group.

Bulimia nervosa.

Bright light therapy might improve well-being and eating problems in patients with bulimia nervosa.

Some preliminary clinical research in people with bulimia shows that bright light therapy, such as 10,000 lux given for 30 minutes daily for 2 weeks, improves well-being and problems with eating  especially seasonal bulimia, when compared with baseline or a 50 lux dim red light control ( 28957 , 28958 ).

Cancer-related fatigue.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial for cancer-related fatigue.

The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for cancer-related fatigue list home-based bright white light therapy (1250 to 10,000 lux), for 30-40 minutes soon after awakening, as an appropriate nonpharmacologic treatment option for patients who have completed cancer treatment and for those actively undergoing cancer treatment ( 112093 ).

Cognitive impairment.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with cognitive impairments.

A meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with cognitive impairments shows that light therapy moderately improves behavioral disturbances and depression and modestly improves sleep when compared with control ( 98300 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneous patient populations, which included those with mild cognitive impairment, memory impairment, Alzheimer disease, or dementia.

Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19).

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial for COVID-19.

A small clinical trial in patients hospitalized for COVID-19 shows that narrowband ultraviolet B (NB-UVB) light therapy, at escalating daily doses for 8 days, does not reduce 28-day mortality or improve clinical laboratory values when compared with placebo therapy ( 109268 ). However, this study was designed as a pilot trial to be conducted prior to a larger study and was likely underpowered to detect differences between groups.

Delirium.

It is unclear if dynamic light therapy can reduce delirium in patients in the intensive care unit.

Preliminary clinical research shows that dynamic light application given as a single dose does not reduce the incidence and duration of delirium in patients in the intensive care unit when compared with placebo ( 98307 ). It is unknown if multiple applications of dynamic light would be effective.

Dementia.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with dementia.

Most early meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research, as well as individual clinical trials, in patients with dementia show that light therapy does not seem to be beneficial for cognition, sleep, function, depression, or some neuropsychiatric symptoms ( 28928 , 28929 , 98302 , 106830 ). However, there is some evidence that light therapy could improve certain outcomes in patients with dementia. A recent meta-analysis in older adults with various forms of dementia shows that light therapy improves cognitive function but not behavioral symptoms, psychological symptoms, or sleep disturbances when compared with control ( 114538 ). Furthermore, another meta-analysis of clinical studies in older adults with dementia shows that light therapy improves depression scores when compared with control. Subgroup analysis suggests that light therapy interventions of less than 1000 lux for 8 to 12 weeks and non-stable types of light therapy such as ceiling and natural light provide the greatest reductions in depressive symptoms ( 114540 ). Additionally, a more recent meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in patients with dementia shows, with low certainty, that light therapy has a small to moderate effect on nighttime awakening and sleep quality, but not sleep duration or efficiency, when compared with a variety of active and passive controls. Most studies used full-spectrum bright light ( 109273 ). Also, some clinical research shows that bright light treatment for 24 weeks improves some caregiver-determined measures of sleep quality when compared with placebo light therapy. The brightest light was used between 10 am and 3 pm each day ( 109260 ). Discrepancies in findings related to sleep may be related to the type of light therapy used, the length of treatment, and/or the endpoints measured.

Diabetic foot ulcers.

Light therapy might improve the healing of diabetic foot ulcers.

A meta-analysis of 4 small clinical studies shows that light therapy increases the proportion of completely healed diabetic foot ulcers by 57% when compared with no treatment or placebo ( 98312 ). Additionally, a small clinical study in patients with diabetic foot ulcers shows that polarized light therapy (Bioptron, MedALL, Switzerland) for 12 minutes three times weekly for 2 months, along with standard care, reduces the area, volume, and severity of ulcers when compared with standard care alone ( 114542 ).

Dry eye.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial for dry eye.

A small clinical trial in patients with dry eye shows that use of low-level light therapy with near-infrared light-emitting diodes at the periorbital area twice weekly for 3 weeks improves some, but not all, measures of dry eye symptoms when compared with a placebo light therapy ( 109269 ). Another small clinical study conducted in India in patients with meibomian gland dysfunction and evaporative dry eye shows that administration of intense pulsed light therapy with low-level light therapy three times 15 days apart reduces the severity of dry eye symptoms and improves tear stability but does not improve the quantity of tears produced when compared with a control group which underwent sham treatment ( 114534 ).

Dysmenorrhea.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that low-level light therapy might improve symptoms of dysmenorrhea.

Preliminary clinical research shows that receiving low-level light therapy with a specific device called the Color DNA-WSF seems to improve symptoms of dysmenorrhea when compared with a sham device ( 98304 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that low-level light therapy with near-infrared light-emitting diodes at two acupoints 20 minutes daily five days each week for three menstrual cycles is less effective for pain reduction than ethinyl estradiol 30 mcg and desogestrel 150 mcg daily. However, there were no differences between groups in terms of the proportion of individuals with clinically relevant pain reduction ( 109272 ).

Exercise-induced muscle soreness.

Light therapy does not seem to improve exercise-induced muscle soreness.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical studies in people with exercise-induced muscle soreness shows that light therapy does not improve range of movement, swelling, pain, or muscle damage when compared with placebo ( 98305 ).

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if bright light therapy improves symptoms of fibromyalgia.

A small clinical study in adults with fibromyalgia shows that 1-hour of bright light treatment using a wearable device (Re-Timer, Re-Time Pty Ltd.) every morning for 4 weeks does not improve pain, physical function, depressive symptoms, or sleep disturbances when compared with a dim light control ( 114532 ).

Graft-versus-host disease (GVHD).

Although there has been interested in using light therapy for GVHD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of light therapy for this purpose.

Herpes zoster (shingles).

It is unclear if broadband ultraviolet B (UVB) light therapy improves healing of herpes zoster rash.

Preliminary clinical research in older patients with severe pain from herpes zoster rash shows that adding broadband UVB light therapy to treatment with acyclovir, starting at 20 mJ/cm2 and increasing to up to 100 mJ/cm2 three times weekly for up to 15 sessions, improves healing of herpes zoster rash and decreases postherpetic neuralgia when compared with acyclovir alone ( 28955 ).

Human papillomavirus (HPV).

It is unclear if red light therapy is beneficial in patients with subclinical or latent HPV infection when used in combination with high-dose interferon.

A small clinical study in females with subclinical or latent HPV infection shows that receiving topical application of high-dose interferon-2b and red light therapy on the cervix and vagina once daily for 4 weeks does not improve HPV cure rates when compared with high-dose interferon alone. However, the study might not have been adequately powered to detect a difference between groups. The combination group had a 92% cure rate of HPV-associated vaginitis and cervicitis, compared with 54% in patients receiving high-dose interferon alone ( 101365 ).

Lichen planus.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with lichen planus.

A small clinical study shows that psoralen with ultraviolet A (PUVA) therapy, used every 2 to 3 days for a total of 12 treatments, can help to heal oral lichen planus when compared with no treatment ( 28964 ). However, other types of light therapy might not be effective. A meta-analysis of two preliminary clinical studies shows that photodynamic therapy is no better than control for improving the size of lichen planus lesions ( 101362 ). Furthermore, a network meta-analysis of clinical trials in patients with oral lichen planus shows that, of the herbal agents studied, photodynamic therapy has the greatest impact on pain and clinical scores, as measured by the Thongprasom scale. However, photodynamic therapy does not seem to improve clinical symptoms or increase rates of complete clinical resolution in these patients ( 111640 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

Although there has been interest in using light therapy for MS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of light therapy for this purpose.

Myopia.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with myopia.

Meta-analyses of clinical studies in Chinese children with myopia show that repeated low-level right light therapy for 6 to 12 months may slow the progression of myopia when compared with a control. Control groups included patients wearing single vision lenses, undergoing orthokeratology, or receiving treatment with atropine eye drops or a sham light device ( 114543 , 114545 ). A large preliminary clinical trial included in both meta-analyses discussed above in children with myopia shows that use of repeated low-level red-light therapy at the retina reduces the progression of myopia by up to 76% when compared with a control group not using light therapy. Treatment was at 1600 lux for 3 minutes twice daily, 5 days each week for 12 months ( 109261 ).

Onychomycosis.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial for resolution of onychomycosis.

A small clinical study in patients with onychomycosis shows that applying eight sessions of photodynamic therapy followed by red laser therapy or applying intense pulsed light seems to result in a 70% to 80% cure rate and a reduction in symptom severity when compared with baseline ( 101357 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Pain (chronic).

Although there has been interest in using light therapy for chronic pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of light therapy for this purpose.

Parkinson disease.

It is unclear if bright light therapy is beneficial in patients with Parkinson disease; the available research is conflicting.

There is mixed evidence of benefit from clinical research investigating the effects of light therapy in patients with Parkinson disease ( 98311 , 106831 , 109262 , 109265 , 109271 ). One small meta-analysis shows that receiving light therapy, as bright light or blue-green light to the eyes once or twice daily for 30-60 minutes, modestly improves motor function and depression severity, but not daytime sleepiness, fatigue, or anxiety, when compared to control groups receiving placebo light therapy at less than 300 lux ( 109271 ). However, other small meta-analyses of studies show that bright light therapy does not improve motor function, depression, or insomnia when compared with control ( 106831 , 109265 ). Although light therapy was shown to be beneficial for sleep disturbance, only two studies investigated this endpoint ( 109265 , 109271 ). These discrepancies may be related to the small and preliminary clinical trial design of the included studies or the slight differences in inclusion or exclusion criteria for the meta-analyses.

Pemphigus.

Although there has been interest in using light therapy for pemphigus, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of light therapy for this purpose.

Peripheral neuropathy.

Although there has been interest in using light therapy for peripheral neuropathy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of light therapy for this purpose.

Pityriasis lichenoides chronica (PLC).

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with PLC.

A very small observational study in children and adults with PLC suggests that narrowband UVB light therapy three times per week then reduced based on clinical response, over an average of 51 sessions, is associated with complete response in 67% of patients ( 114531 ). The validity of these results is limited by the retrospective, observational study design, small size, and lack of a comparator group.

Postpartum depression.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with postpartum depression.

A small clinical trial in patients with untreated pregnancy-related or postpartum major depression shows that bright light therapy results in remission in 42% of patients, compared with 0% of patients receiving placebo. Depression scores were reduced by at least 50% in 75% of patients using bright light therapy, compared with only 18% of those given placebo. Treatment was at an intensity of 10,000 lux for 45 minutes daily upon awakening for 3 weeks ( 109264 ).

Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD).

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with PMDD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with PMDD shows that light therapy does not reduce depressive symptoms when compared with placebo ( 83002 ).

Pressure ulcers.

Ultraviolet (UV) light therapy might improve the healing of pressure ulcers.

A meta-analysis of two small preliminary clinical trials shows that UV light therapy reduces the time to complete healing of pressure ulcers by about 2 weeks when compared to a control therapy ( 98299 ).

Sleep deprivation.

Bright light therapy has only been evaluated in combination with caffeine; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A small clinical trial in sleep deprived volunteers shows that receiving bright light therapy at an intensity of 10,000 lux in combination with caffeine reduces lane drifting, sleepiness, and vigilance, but not fatigue, during simulated driving when compared with placebo ( 91049 ).

Traumatic brain injury (TBI).

Light therapy does not seem beneficial in patients with TBI.

A recent meta-analysis of clinical studies in adults with a history of TBI shows that blue wavelength light therapy, mostly delivered through lightboxes, for around 35 days reduces sleep disturbances and depressive symptoms but does not improve sleepiness and fatigue when compared with long-wavelength or no light therapy. Subgroup analysis suggests that  blue wavelength light therapy may reduce sleepiness in those with mild TBI, but not in those with more severe TBI ( 114541 ). However, a small meta-analysis in patients with TBI shows that blue wavelength light therapy does not improve sleepiness when compared with amber light or no light therapy ( 106833 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies. Similarly, a small clinical study in patients with recent TBI undergoing acute rehabilitation shows that bright white light therapy does not improve sleep quality or duration when compared with red light control ( 106832 ).

Tuberculosis.

Although there has been interest in using light therapy for tuberculosis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of light therapy for this purpose.

Urticaria.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial in patients with chronic urticaria.

Using light therapy in the form of ultraviolet A (UVA) or psoralens with UVA (PUVA) decreases symptoms of chronic urticaria when compared to baseline ( 28938 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Warts.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial for the resolution of warts.

Some clinical research shows that photodynamic therapy is not effective for treating warts when compared with placebo ( 28965 ). Other clinical research shows that photodynamic therapy with pulsed dye laser therapy (595 nm, 20 J/cm) may be effective for warts; however, either treatment alone is not effective ( 28966 , 28967 ).

Wound healing.

It is unclear if light therapy is beneficial for wound healing.

Some clinical research shows that light therapy may help improve tissue repair and therefore improve wound healing when compared with baseline ( 28927 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

More evidence is needed to rate light therapy for these uses.

Numerous phototherapy devices have been developed using different light sources, such as blue and special blue fluorescent tubes, halogen, fiber-optic, and light-emitting diode (LED) sources. Light may be delivered directly or through concentrators and filters. Devices may deliver phototherapy to the entire body or to a specific area (spot treatment). Light boxes, equipped with high-intensity florescent lights, are typically used for mood and sleep disorders. Treatments may be administered in dermatologists' offices, phototherapy centers, or hospitals. Home phototherapy systems are also available. Some examples of phototherapy include : Dead Sea climatotherapy (DSC): A natural balneophototherapy which uses highly concentrated salt solutions (25%) ( 28969 ). Fiber-optic phototherapy: Optical fibers and a light source, for example a blanket with optical fibers that deliver light, has been used to wrap infants ( 28904 ). Intense pulsed light (IPL) therapy: A high-intensity polychromatic light ranging from visible to infrared (515-1,200 nm) and delivered over a short duration of time ( 28970 ). Ocular light therapy (OLT): Light and color shined into the eyes to stimulate cortical and hypothalamic regions of the brain ( 28971 ). Photodynamic therapy (PDT): Giving photosensitizing agents along with a light source and oxygen, which results in a photochemical reaction. Aminolevulinic acid and porfimer are approved drugs for photodynamic therapy for cancer ( 28972 ). Psoralens with ultraviolet A (PUVA) phototherapy: PUVA phototherapy combines the use of ultraviolet A (UVA) light with a photosensitizing agent called a psoralen. Prior to UVA radiation, the psoralen is taken by mouth or applied as a solution, liquid, or gel directly onto the skin. Additionally, bath PUVA phototherapy is the use of long-wave UV radiation plus a psoralen that is administered as a diluted bath solution. This can be used as an alternative to oral PUVA ( 28938 ). Ultraviolet A (UVA) therapy: UVA therapy involves exposing skin to UVA light (wavelengths: 320-400 nm). Broadband UVA irradiation (315-400 nm) contains both UVA1 (340-400 nm) and UVA2 (315-340 nm). Ultraviolet B (UVB) therapy: UVB therapy involves exposing skin to UVB light (wavelengths: 280-320 nm). Broad-spectrum UVB therapy uses a full spectrum of UVB radiation. Narrowband UVB phototherapy (NB-UVB, wavelength: 311 nm) uses a smaller spectrum of light.

#### SKIN CONDITIONS

When used for the treatment of various skin conditions, phototherapy can cause a skin rash called polymorphic light eruption (PLE). The prevalence of PLE differs with different skin conditions. Observational research has found that phototherapy-induced PLE occurs at a rate of about 0.7% to 16.7%, depending on the condition ( 101361 ). Use light therapy with caution in patients with skin conditions.

#### Antibacterial effects

Blue light in wavelengths of 407-420 nm has a bactericidal effect against Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes), the organism associated with acne vulgaris. The proposed mechanism for this effect is excitation of bacterial porphyrins, leading to release of singlet oxygen and reactive free radicals that exert bactericidal effects ( 104112 ).

#### Dermatologic effects

Animal and human research shows that light therapy seems to stimulate tissue repair and increase cell activity, which might improve healing ( 28927 ). Blue light therapy, often in conjunction with red light, may improve acne by eradicating Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes) ( 28984 ).

#### Hepatic effects

Phototherapy has been found to convert bilirubin to water-soluble photoisomers that bypass conjugation in the liver and are excreted in the bile and urine ( 28903 ). This is thought to be responsible for its effect in neonatal jaundice.

#### Hormonal effects

Exposure to light seems to have an effect on menstrual cycles, melatonin secretion, menstrual cycle symptoms, and disordered ovarian function. Females with psychological or endocrine disorders seem to be more susceptible to the effects of light-dark exposure ( 28985 ).

#### Mood disorder effects

Bright light therapy has been studied for various mood disorders, particularly seasonal affective disorder (SAD). Changes in the light-dark cycle and disruption in the timing of melatonin, as well as deficiencies in serotonin, may contribute to the pathophysiology of SAD ( 28982 , 28983 ). Possible mechanisms for antidepressant activity of bright light therapy include adjustment of sleep-wake rhythms, inhibition of melatonin secretion, and increases in serotonin and norepinephrine levels ( 104114 ).
