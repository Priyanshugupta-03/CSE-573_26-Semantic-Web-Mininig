# Focusing
Other Common Names Attentional Focus, Attentional Focus And Symptom Management Intervention (AFSMI), Body-State Focusing, Enfoque, External Focusing, Focusing Process, Focusing Therapy, Focusing-Oriented Therapy, Interactive Focusing Therapy, Internal Focusing

## Overview

Focusing is both a type of psychotherapy and a self-help skill used to aid a patient in coping with a particular situation or issue ( 21077 ). Focusing is also a tool used to improve physical function and performance ( 100413 , 100414 ).

## People Use This For

Focusing has been used for aging, cancer, child development, depression, immunomodulation, learning disabilities, muscle strength, pain, Parkinson disease, psychological disorders, psychosis, quality of life, schizophrenia, stress, social anxiety disorder, sprains, stroke rehabilitation, obesity, and trauma.

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Focusing has been used with apparent safety in several clinical studies ( 21077 , 100404 , 100405 , 100406 , 100407 , 100409 , 100410 , 100411 , 100412 , 100415 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient available evidence; however, there is no reason to expect safety issues if performed correctly.

#### General

Focusing is well tolerated when used appropriately, short term. No adverse effects have been reported in clinical research ( 21077 , 100404 , 100405 , 100406 , 100407 , 100409 , 100410 , 100411 , 100412 , 100415 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Athletic performance.

Some research has evaluated training utilizing either an internal or external focusing strategy. A small clinical study in male athletes shows that participating in an 8-week lower extremity training program with external focus improves hip abduction strength and hop distance, and decreases hip adduction and internal rotation, when compared with the same training program with internal focus, or with a control group not participating in the training ( 105845 ). For sprint performance, a meta-analysis of 6 studies shows that an external focus is better than internal, although a subgroup analysis suggests the effects of an external focus may be greater for low-skill sprinters than high-skill ( 111479 ). A large meta-analysis shows that an external focus improves learning and performance of athletic motor skills, when compared with an internal focus. In a subset of studies using electromyography, external focus was associated with more efficient neuromuscular processing ( 111476 ).

Cancer.

A small clinical study in cancer patients shows that practicing focusing for 6 months decreases depression and improves attitude, but does not affect energy, activity level, or mortality ( 21077 ). Clinical research in patients who have undergone breast cancer surgery shows that two phone sessions of Attentional Focus and Symptom Management Intervention (AFSMI) improves mood and reduces confusion, but does not have an effect on physical functioning, when compared with usual care ( 21073 ).

Child development.

A small clinical study in children ages 9-11 years shows that receiving instruction in internal or external focus in addition to standard training about basketball free throws does not impact motor learning when compared with receiving standard training alone ( 100410 ).

Knee pain.

Some research has evaluated physical training utilizing internal focus, external focus, or no specific focus. A small clinical study in recreationally active adults with knee pain persisting for at least 8 weeks shows that participating in a 6-week hip-knee strength training program with either an external or internal focus decreases pain and improves patient-reported motor function when compared with the same strength training program with no specific focus ( 105844 ).

Learning disabilities.

A small clinical study in children with intellectual disabilities shows that throwing a bean bag with an external focus on the bean bag improves motor learning when compared with an internal focus on the movement of the hand ( 100404 ). It is not clear how either of these approaches compare with the use of no focusing techniques.

Muscle strength.

A small clinical study in healthy males shows that practicing internal focus on muscle contraction over 8 weeks produces greater muscle hypertrophy when compared with using an external focus on the outcome of the exercise ( 100412 ). However, a meta-analysis of 10 studies shows that acutely, an external focus during resistance training improves muscle strength more than an internal focus. In the long-term, muscular strength gains from resistance training do not differ between the external and internal focus states, although subgroup analysis suggests a benefit for external focus during lower body exercises ( 111477 , 111478 ).

Pain (acute).

Preliminary research in young healthy adults using the cold pressor test for acute pain (immersing the none-dominant hand in cold water at 8°C for up to 90 seconds), shows that an external focus (imagining a sunny outdoor scene) is associated with a lower average level of perceived pain than an internal focus (concentrating on the immersed hand), and a reduced rate of removal of the hand from the cold water before 90 seconds ( 111481 ).

Pain (chronic).

A small clinical study in patients with chronic pain shows that practicing focusing for 5 sessions may slightly decrease and prevent worsening of pain and also improve activity levels when compared to baseline ( 21074 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Parkinson disease.

A small clinical trial in patients with Parkinson disease shows that receiving internal or external focus instruction during a 4-week balance training program does not improve balance or gait when compared with no intervention ( 100408 ).  Preliminary research in patients with Parkinson disease without gait freezing episodes, shows that an external focus improves walking automaticity, reduces step length variability, and improves performance of a secondary task while walking (keeping two interlocking rings from touching). However, in patients with Parkinson disease with gait freezing episodes, an internal focus reduces step length variability and strides per minute, increases gait stability, improves locomotion control and performance of a secondary task, and reduces falling risk and freezing episodes ( 111480 ).

Quality of life.

Preliminary clinical research in breast cancer patients shows that two days of alternative therapies including focusing and expressive writing, art, and movement result in improved well-being, body image, and quality of life when compared to baseline ( 21076 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Additionally, it is not clear if these effects are due to focusing, the other interventions, or the combination.

Social anxiety disorder.

A small clinical study in patients with social anxiety shows that participating in attention training for 6 weeks is comparable to 6 weeks of cognitive therapy for reducing symptoms measured by the Social Phobia and Anxiety Inventory ( 100405 ). Other preliminary clinical research has compared the effects of internal and external focus on social anxiety. One preliminary clinical study in students with social anxiety and high blushing shows that practicing "self-focusing" increases anxiety when compared with "task-focusing". In students that were low-blushing, practicing "self-focusing" and "task-focusing" did not alter anxiety levels ( 100415 ).

Sprains.

A small clinical study in patients with impaired movement following an ankle sprain shows that external focus training for 3 days improves ankle stability to a greater degree when compared with internal focus training ( 100409 ). A follow-up study assessed whether focus training affects the process of transferring weight. Healthy volunteers with a sprained ankle participating in three external focus training sessions during 3 consecutive days had better stability when transferring weight when compared with internal focus training ( 100411 ). It is not clear how either of these approaches compare with the use of no focusing techniques.

Stroke.

Limited research shows that different focusing practices may benefit patients recovering from stroke. Some clinical research shows that focusing on movement patterns rather than the accuracy of the movement may improve joint range, coordination, and function in patients recovering from stroke when compared to baseline ( 21075 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

There is also interest in comparing the benefits of internal focus and external focus in patients recovering from stroke. A small clinical study in stroke patients with impaired upper limb function shows that practicing an external focus increases the speed of grasping a jar when compared to practicing an internal focus ( 100406 ). Another small clinical study with a similar design and study population shows that both types of attention focus generally improve motor performance on reach-point and reach-grasp-fit tasks. However, the benefits of external focus are enhanced when preceded by internal focus ( 105843 ). Other research has evaluated longer term effects of focus training. A small clinical study in stroke patients with moderate or severe arm impairment shows that practicing external focus for 4 weeks is no different for improving motor skills when compared with practicing internal focus ( 100407 ). It is not clear how either of these approaches compare with the use of no focusing techniques.

More evidence is needed to rate focusing for these uses.

One type of focusing practice involves a mental technique to deal with difficult emotions. Some sources describe this type of focusing in six steps that are usually learned and practiced with a qualified focusing practitioner or mental health practitioner. The first step is referred to as clearing a space. When people are overwhelmed by emotion, negative self-talk, or the crisis nature of their situation, clearing a space is thought to open up a perspective that is wider than the intense emotional experience. The second step involves paying attention to a particular event or feeling and then noticing what physically occurs. For the third step, a word, phrase, or image that captures the experience is found and named the "handle". For the fourth step, the experience is compared with the "handle" several times to see whether they resonate. The patient continues to pay attention to it with an attitude of friendly acceptance. The fifth step is asking about the whole problem. This step connects the "handle" to the larger problem or the question originally asked in the beginning of the practice. The sixth and final step is receiving, which entails staying open and welcoming towards what emerges. A judgmental or critical attitude is counterproductive to the process of focusing ( 21077 ). Another type of focusing involves an "internal" or "external" attention focus for improving certain motor learning activities. "Internal" focus involves the patients focusing on a part of themselves, such as a body part or personal thoughts, while performing an activity. "External" focus involves focusing on an external item or outcome that is not related to the person performing an activity ( 100404 , 100411 , 105843 , 105844 , 105845 ).

None known.

None known.

"Internal" or "external" attention focus is of interest for improving certain motor learning activities. In starting a sprint, an internal focus would be "concentrate on fully extending the knees", while an external focus would be "concentrate on pushing the blocks away" ( 111479 ). The "constrained action hypothesis" theorizes that an "internal" focus on the body results in conscious activity which disrupts the body's automatic mechanisms. An "external" focus on the effect of movement outside the body is thought to produce a more automatic, reflexive, and efficient movement, which improves learning and performance ( 100404 ). This theory has not yet been proven in practice; clinical research has yielded conflicting results ( 100413 , 100414 ). There is also interest in using different focusing methods to prevent injury in athletes, but it is not clear if external or internal focusing is superior. One study in adolescent rugby players shows that practicing external focusing while training and performing a cognitive task might attenuate the increase of landing forces when compared with practicing internal focusing. This suggests that external focusing might improve landing mechanics and reduce risk of injury in athletes, but further research is needed to confirm this finding ( 100414 ).
