## Overview

The blood type diet is a diet designed using foods that are thought to be compatible with a patient's blood type ( 15911 ).

## History

A diet based on blood type was first created by Peter D'Adamo, ND. The diet was popularized in his book called "Eat Right 4 Your Type" ( 15911 ).

## People Use This For

The blood type diet is used for the treatment and prevention of a variety of diseases and conditions, including inflammation, disturbed metabolism, and obesity.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of using the blood type diet.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoiding using.

#### General

No adverse effects have been reported. However, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Obesity.

The blood type diet postulates that individuals with blood type A will experience cardiometabolic improvements from a plant-based diet, while individuals with blood type O will not. However, a secondary analysis of a clinical trial in obese patients has found that following a low-fat vegan diet for 16 weeks is associated with comparable improvements in weight, lipid levels, and glycemic control in all patients, regardless of blood type ( 104095 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the blood type diet for this use.

The blood type diet is structured around a person's ABO type, rather than the Rhesus (Rh positive or negative) type ( 15911 , 104095 ). Blood group O is "the hunter" and the earliest human blood group, thought to be muscular and active. Blood group O is suggested to consume high amounts of meat and a low amount of carbohydrates. Fruits and vegetables should be consumed, while wheat germ, whole wheat and corn should be limited. Dairy products and most nuts should be avoided. Blood group A is called the "cultivator" and should consume a high carbohydrate, low fat diet, consisting of a lot of fruits and vegetables, and to avoid dairy and meat. Blood group B, the "nomad", should consume a balanced diet of fruits, vegetables, dairy, and meat, but should avoid chicken. This is the only blood type that is advised to consume dairy products. Finally, group AB is the "enigma," having a blood type diet in between types A and B. These individuals should consume a mostly vegetarian diet with occasional meat, fish, and dairy, but should avoid chicken. Different levels of exercise are also recommended based on blood type: that type O's should exercise vigorously, B's moderately, and A's gently, while AB's need calming exercises.

Practitioners of the blood type diet believe that blood type influences how the body handles food and food components. Blood type is said to be a part of a person's biological descendancy. Proponents also believe that some blood types are more prone to certain diseases or conditions. They suggest that consuming a special diet based on blood type can decrease the risk of these conditions. Similarly, several supplements are sold targeting different blood types. Blood type diet proponents pay particular attention to dietary lectins, which are proteins that bind to carbohydrates. They suggest that people with certain blood types could respond adversely to lectins, resulting in disturbed metabolism, inflammation, and weight gain ( 15911 , 15912 ). Additionally, blood type diet proponents look at urine "indican" levels to assess protein digestion. Poor protein digestion is thought to result from blood type and increased lectin consumption. Blood type A people are said to be "slow oxidizers," have thicker blood, and a sensitive immune system. They are able to consume more carbohydrates than others, but should avoid red meat and dairy because lectins in these foods could be problematic. Blood type A people theorized to have a higher risk of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and cancer ( 104095 ). Blood type B people are said to be "fast oxidizers," have a strong immune system and a strong digestive system. They are supposed to avoid lectins in corn and wheat products. Blood type AB people are "slow oxidizers," but unlike blood type A people, they can sometimes tolerate dairy problems, without adverse effects. Blood type O people are "fast oxidizers." They can tolerate red meat, but should consume less carbohydrates and should avoid wheat. Lectins from wheat in this group can cause high insulin levels and lead to metabolic syndrome ( 15911 , 15912 , 104095 ). Blood type O people theorized to be commonly afflicted by hypothyroidism, high stomach acid, and thin blood. There is no reliable scientific information available about using therapies or diets based on blood type for reducing or preventing medical conditions.
