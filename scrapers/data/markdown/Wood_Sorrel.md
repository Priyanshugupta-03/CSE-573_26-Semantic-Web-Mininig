Caution

Do not confuse wood sorrel with similarly named plants such as Sorrel or Yellow Dock , which is sometimes called sheep sorrel.

## Overview

Wood sorrel is a flowering plant. Traditionally, the whole plant is used to make medicine.

Possibly Unsafe when used orally. Wood sorrel contains oxalic acid, which can be toxic when consumed in large amounts ( 6 , 17 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Unsafe when used orally. Wood sorrel contains oxalic acid, which can be toxic when consumed in large amounts ( 17 ).

PREGNANCY: Likely Unsafe when used orally because it might stimulate menses ( 19 ); avoid using.

LACTATION: Possibly Unsafe when used orally ( 6 , 17 ); avoid using.

|

#### General

There is currently a limited amount of information on the adverse effects of wood sorrel. A thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Orally : Dermatitis, diarrhea, nausea, and polyuria.

#### Dermatologic

Orally, consuming excessive amounts of wood sorrel can cause dermatitis. Wood sorrel contains oxalic acid, which is thought to be responsible for this adverse effect ( 4 , 17 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Orally, consuming excessive amounts of wood sorrel can cause diarrhea, nausea, and other gastrointestinal symptoms. Wood sorrel contains oxalic acid, which is thought to be responsible for these adverse effects ( 6 , 17 ).

#### Renal

Orally, consuming excessive amounts of wood sorrel can cause polyuria ( 6 , 17 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Gingivitis.

Although there has been interest in using oral wood sorrel for gingivitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of wood sorrel for this purpose.

Scurvy.

Although there has been interest in using oral wood sorrel for scurvy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of wood sorrel for this purpose.

Wound healing.

Although there has been interest in using oral wood sorrel for wound healing, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of wood sorrel for this purpose.

More evidence is needed to rate wood sorrel for these uses.

#### Adult

Research is limited; typical dosing is unavailable. Wood sorrel administration should be separated from food. Wood sorrel contains oxalate, which can bind to multivalent metal ions in the gastrointestinal tract and decrease mineral absorption ( 7 , 12 ).

#### COAGULATION DISORDERS

Theoretically, taking wood sorrel might decrease coagulation time. Wood sorrel contains oxalic acid. Oxalate constituents can alter calcium concentrations and decrease coagulation time ( 4 , 12 ).

#### GATROINTESTINAL (GI) CONDITIONS

Theoretically, taking wood sorrel might worsen certain GI conditions, such as stomach or intestinal ulcers. Wood sorrel contains oxalic acid, which is a mucosal irritant ( 19 ).

#### KIDNEY DISEASE

Theoretically, taking wood sorrel might worsen kidney disease. Wood sorrel contains oxalic acid, which can form insoluble oxalates that can damage the kidneys. Use with caution or avoid in individuals with history of kidney stones or kidney disease ( 12 , 19 ).

None known.

#### Presentation

Wood sorrel contains oxalic acid. Oxalic acid poisoning affects the skin, eyes, respiratory system, and kidneys. Once absorbed, oxalic acid reacts with calcium in plasma, forming insoluble calcium oxalate crystals that may precipitate in the kidneys, blood vessels, heart, lungs, and liver. Oxalate can have a corrosive effect on the digestive tract; symptoms of oxalate irritation include swelling of the mouth, tongue, and throat, leading to difficulty in speaking and breathing ( 12 , 17 ).

#### Treatment

There is insufficient reliable information available about the treatment of overdose with wood sorrel.

There is insufficient reliable information about the pharmacokinetics of wood sorrel.

#### General

The applicable part of wood sorrel is the whole flowering plant. It contains 0.3-1.25% oxalic acid ( 18 ). Oxalate content is highest in the fresh leaves and roots ( 17 , 19 ).
