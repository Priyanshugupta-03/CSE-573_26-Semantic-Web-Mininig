## Overview

Trager therapy is a form of bodywork that involves rhythmic touch and movement exercises. The goal of the therapy is generate positive sensory feelings that integrate mind and body, resulting in relaxation, improved physical mobility, and improved mental clarity ( 15880 , 15881 ).

## History

Trager therapy was developed by Milton Trager, MD (1908-1997). Milton Trager initially developed his approach while training as a boxer in Chicago. It wasn't until many years later that he attended medical school in Mexico and graduated in 1955. In 1980, The Trager Institute was founded to train and certify practitioners in this area ( 15881 ).

## People Use This For

Trager therapy is used for shoulder pain, headache, stress, fatigue, chronic lung disease, and to reduce muscle rigidity in patients with Parkinson disease.

Possibly Safe when used appropriately. Trager therapy has been safely used in several short-term, preliminary clinical trials ( 15876 , 15877 , 15878 , 15879 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Trager therapy seems to be well tolerated when used appropriately ( 15876 , 15877 , 15878 , 15879 ). No adverse effects have been reported.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Headache.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that Trager therapy can significantly reduce headache frequency and medication usage in people with chronic headaches ( 15876 ).

Parkinson disease.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that Trager therapy can reduce evoked stretch responses in rigid limbs of Parkinson disease patients ( 15877 ). This suggests that Trager therapy might help reduce muscle rigidity in these patients.

Shoulder pain.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that receiving ten Trager therapy treatments over a 5-week period can reduce chronic shoulder pain in people who use a wheelchair due to spinal cord injury ( 15878 ).

More evidence is needed to rate Trager therapy for these uses.

A typical Trager therapy session may last from 60 to 90 minutes. Trager therapists begin  by discussing the patient's medical history, current physical complaints, and any emotional problems. A Trager therapy session has two parts: tablework and mentastics. Tablework : The first part of the Trager session is called tablework. The patient lies on a special padded table. The therapist rubs, rocks, shakes, and stretches various part of the body to loosen tight muscles and joints. Tablework should not feel uncomfortable or painful. After the muscles are loosened, the second session starts. Mentastics : During the second half of the session, the practitioner teaches simple sequences of movements, called mentastics, to the patient. Mentastics are not endurance or strength building exercises. The light exercises are intended to promote relaxation and show the patient possible ways to move without pain. Some examples of mentastic movements include shifting from foot to foot and making large dance-like movements.

The goal of Trager therapy is to reduce tension-related pain and movement-restriction by generating positive sensory feelings that integrate mind and body, resulting in relaxation, improved physical mobility, and improved mental clarity ( 15880 , 15881 ). Trager therapy is intended to show and teach the patient how to make their movements easy and painless. Trager therapy involves two aspects. The first aspect is referred to as "table work". The patient lies on a table and the Trager therapy practitioner applies rhythmic movements. Before touching or applying the movements to the patient, the practitioner enters into a meditative state which is referred to as "hook-up." Practitioners believe that this places them in touch with a vibratory energy or life energy or "qi." The second aspect is called "Mentastics." This involves individual movements or "self-care" movements. Patients receive training individually or in group sessions on how to conduct these movements ( 15880 ). Trager therapy is intended to remove accumulated tension and stress. There is no reliable scientific information regarding how Trager therapy might work for treating or preventing medical conditions.
