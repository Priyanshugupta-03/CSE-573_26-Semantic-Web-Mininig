## Overview

Pulsed electromagnetic field (PEMF) therapy involves the use of electromagnetic radiation as a non-invasive therapy. Intermittent low frequency electromagnetic fields are used with no direct electric contact on the skin ( 109125 , 109133 , 109145 , 109146 ). Devices used in therapy vary in size and location of use. Some PEMF devices have been cleared as medical devices by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for various self-limiting indications ( 109130 , 109139 , 109144 , 109146 ).

Possibly Safe when used appropriately. PEMF therapy has been used under the supervision of a healthcare professional without safety concerns in numerous clinical trials ( 109124 , 109126 , 109127 , 109130 , 109139 , 109140 , 109143 , 109147 , 109148 , 109149 )( 109150 , 109155 , 109156 , 109158 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable evidence about the safety of PEMF therapy in pregnancy and lactation. Avoid use.

#### General

PEMF treatment is generally well tolerated.

#### Cardiovascular

In one study, two patients given PEMF treatment had orthostatic hypertension which resolved upon treatment discontinuation ( 109144 ).

Possibly Effective
Back pain.

PEMF therapy seems to be beneficial for reducing chronic low back pain. It is unclear if PEMF is beneficial for acute back pain.

A meta-analysis of 14 clinical trials shows that PEMF therapy is beneficial for reducing non-specific low back pain, but not for improving physical function. Benefits were large when compared with sham or placebo treatments, and moderate when used in conjunction to or when compared with other treatments, such as physical therapy or analgesics. Benefit was limited to patients with chronic pain of at least 3 months; however, few studies evaluated patients with acute pain ( 109154 ). Most of the available studies are small and individual results are mixed ( 109122 , 109126 , 109134 , 109154 ). One small clinical trial shows that although PEMF therapy using a specific device (Bio-Electro-Magnetic-Energy-Regulation (BEMER) device) modestly reduces pain and disability when compared with baseline, benefits are generally not different from sham therapy with an inactive device. There were also no beneficial effects on feelings of depression or anxiety or on sleep quality ( 109122 ). A small clinical trial in patients with chronic back pain despite previous spinal surgery shows that use of a specific PEMF device (Provant Therapy System, Regenesis Biomedical) with a pulse width of 42 microseconds for 30 minutes twice daily for 60 days modestly reduces low back and radiating leg pain when compared with sham PEMF therapy. However, use of a lower dose of 38 microseconds was no more effective than the sham device. Also, there was no significant difference between active PEMF and sham PEMF in relation to time to pain relief or total patients experiencing pain relief ( 109146 ).

Fractures.

PEMF therapy seems to be beneficial for improving the healing of fractures.

A meta-analysis of 14 clinical, moderate-quality studies shows that using PEMF therapy increases fracture healing rate by 22% when compared to sham or no control treatment. A sub-analysis shows that the largest benefit occurs in patients with nonunion or delayed union of the fracture. Use of PEMF therapy also modestly reduces pain and improves healing time ( 109155 ). Treatments were conducted for 0.5-24 hours daily for up to 6 months ( 109124 , 109137 , 109155 ). However, results from individual studies are mixed. This may be related to the specific populations investigated, which has included patients with fractures of the tibia, fibula, femur, metatarsal, mandibular bone, spine, and others ( 109124 , 109135 , 109137 , 109148 , 109155 ). Various devices have been used in clinical research, including study-specific devices and branded devices, such as Biomet EBI Bone Healing System (ZimVie), Cervical-Stim (OrthoFix), and Ossatec Bone Growth Stimulator ( 109124 , 109137 , 109155 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Most research shows that PEMF therapy is beneficial for osteoarthritis, especially knee osteoarthritis.

Most meta-analyses examining the effect of PEMF therapy on pain and function in patients with osteoarthritis, typically knee osteoarthritis, show that PEMF therapy is beneficial when compared with sham PEMF therapy. Two meta-analyses of clinical research show that PEMF treatment has a small to large beneficial effect on pain, stiffness, and/or physical function ( 109150 , 109152 , 109153 ). Two meta-analyses also show that PEMF therapy has a moderate beneficial effect on pain and activity level when compared with sham PEMF therapy, but not when compared with alternative treatments ( 109151 , 109152 ). In contrast, one earlier meta-analysis in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that PEMF therapy modestly improves physical function when compared with sham PEMF therapy. However, there was no improvement in pain or stiffness as measured on the Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Osteoarthritis (WOMAC) Index ( 109127 ). The reasons for any discrepancies may be due to various factors. The type of osteoarthritis studied in the individual trials included in each analysis may play a role. Most research has been conducted in individuals with knee osteoarthritis and some meta-analyses have excluded other forms. Cervical, ankle, and hand osteoarthritis have been less commonly studied ( 109127 , 109150 , 109151 , 109152 , 109153 ). Other factors may include the length of the trial ( 109151 , 109153 ), the exposure duration of each PEMF therapy session ( 109150 ), or the frequency or intensity of PEMF treatment ( 109152 ). Longer term studies are also needed; the majority of available studies lasted for 6 weeks or less ( 109150 , 109152 ).

Postoperative pain.

Small clinical trials suggest that PEMF therapy modestly reduces pain after various types of surgery.

Several small or preliminary clinical trials have suggested that PEMF therapy modestly improves pain and function and reduces analgesic use in patients undergoing various surgeries, including spinal surgery for vertebral fractures, total knee arthroplasty, various types of mammaplasty, and others. In most cases, comparisons were made against sham PEMF devices; however, standard care has also been used as a comparator ( 109123 , 109130 , 109135 , 109140 , 109143 , 109160 ). Also, one study in patients undergoing breast augmentation shows that treatment to one breast is as beneficial as treatment to both breasts for reducing pain in the week following surgery ( 109130 ). Specific devices used in clinical studies include I-ONE therapy (IGEA) and SofPulse/SofPulse Duo (Ivivi Technologies). Treatment was started immediately or within 7 days of surgery and used for up to 5 hours in total or for 1-24 hours daily for 7-60 days ( 109123 , 109130 , 109140 , 109143 ). Pain reduction has occurred as early as one hour after treatment initiation; in patients receiving treatment for 60 days, pain reduction persisted for up to 3 years ( 109123 , 109130 , 109140 , 109143 ). However, research evaluating PEMF therapy for oral surgery has not shown benefit. Clinical research in patients undergoing molar extraction shows that consistent use of a wearable PEMF device (Recovery RX, Bio-Electronics Corporation) for seven days postoperatively does not reduce pain or analgesic use when compared with sham or no treatment ( 109147 ). Also, a small clinical trial in patients with full-arch immediate loading implant surgery shows that use of a specific PEMF device (RecoveryRx, BioElectronics Corp.) on one cheek for 48 hours does not reduce postoperative pain or swelling when compared with an inactive device on the other cheek ( 109136 ). However, this study may not have been adequately powered to detect differences between groups, and it is possible that wearing the PEMF device on one cheek may benefit both cheeks.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH).

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for reducing symptoms of BPH.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with BPH and/or lower urinary tract symptoms shows that PEMF with a specific device (Magcell Microcirc, Physiomed Elektromedizin) on the perineal area for 5 minutes twice daily for 1-4 months modestly reduces prostate volume and improves symptoms when compared with baseline ( 109149 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Cancer.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for cancer, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this condition.

Depression.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for depression.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with moderate to severe, treatment-refractory depression shows that transcranial PEMF therapy for 8 weeks modestly reduces symptoms of depression when compared with baseline. Up to 51% of patients obtained remission ( 109132 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Diabetic foot ulcers.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for diabetic foot ulcer healing, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this condition.

Diabetic neuropathy.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for diabetic neuropathy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this condition.

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for fibromyalgia.

One clinical trial shows that PEMF therapy for 12 weeks with a specific device (Bio-Electro-Magnetic-Energy Regulation [BEMER] Innomed International) twice daily for 12 weeks is no more effective than sham treatment for reducing pain and stiffness ( 109139 ). However, a smaller clinical trial shows that another specific PEMF device (MRS 2000+ Home, Eschestrasse 500, FL-9492 Eschen) twice daily for 3 weeks modestly improves pain and function for up to 12 weeks when compared with sham treatment ( 109144 ).

Food allergies.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for food allergies, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this condition.

Herniated disc.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for herniated disc.

Clinical research in patients with cervical disc herniation shows that PEMF treatment five days each week for 3 weeks does not improve neck pain, disability, depression, anxiety, or quality of life when compared with sham treatment. However, 9 weeks later, pain was modestly reduced in patients using PEMF therapy when compared with sham. All patients also used transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) and a hot pack ( 109128 ). This study may not have been adequately powered to detect differences between groups.

Hypertension.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for hypertension.

A small preliminary clinical trial in patients with hypertension shows that using a specific PEMF device (Biomobie) three times daily for 12 weeks modestly reduces systolic and diastolic blood pressure when compared with baseline. Although blood pressure did not change in individuals using sham PEMF therapy, it is unclear whether PEMF therapy is beneficial when compared directly with sham ( 109145 ).

Insomnia.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for insomnia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this use.

Interstitial cystitis.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for interstitial cystitis pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this use.

Knee pain.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for patellofemoral pain syndrome, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this use.

Metabolic syndrome.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for metabolic syndrome.

A small preliminary clinical trial in patients with metabolic syndrome shows that PEMF therapy with a specific device (Bioboosti, Biomobie) three times daily for 12 weeks does not reduce rest or peak systolic or diastolic blood pressure when compared with sham PEMF therapy ( 109133 ). This study may not have been adequately powered to detect differences between groups.

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for MS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this condition.

Neck pain.

It is unclear if PEMF is beneficial for neck pain.

Clinical research in patients with chronic non-specific neck pain shows that 15 sessions of PEMF therapy with a specific device (ASA model PMT Quattro PRO-Italy) over 3 weeks does not reduce pain or improve function more than sham PEMF therapy. Both groups also received a hot pack, transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS), and a home-based exercise program as part of physical therapy ( 109129 ).

Osteoporosis.

PEMF therapy may be beneficial for improving bone mineral density in patients treated with conventional medications for postmenopausal osteoporosis. It is unclear if PEMF therapy alone is beneficial for postmenopausal osteoporosis or if it is beneficial for other types of osteoporosis.

A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials investigating the effects of PEMF therapy in postmenopausal adults with osteoporosis shows that using PEMF therapy in combination with conventional medications modestly increases the bone mineral density (BMD) of the lumbar vertebra, femur, and Ward's triangle and modestly reduces pain when compared with conventional medications alone. When compared with conventional medications, lumbar vertebra BMD was not affected; however, femur BMD was increased. Despite these findings, limited research suggests that there is no difference in BMD when PEMF therapy is compared with sham PEMF therapy ( 109156 ).

Parkinson disease.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for Parkinson disease.

Clinical research in patients with Parkinson disease shows that transcranial PEMF therapy with a specific device (Re5 NTS Parkinson Treatment System, Re5) for 30 minutes daily for 8 weeks does not improve hand tremor, movement speed, or force during chair rises when compared with sham treatment. However, in patients with higher performance at baseline, force during chair rises modestly improved. Also, hand tremor was modestly improved in patients with unilateral, but not bilateral, postural tremor ( 109158 , 109159 ). One small clinical trial of a longer duration shows that using PEMF therapy 30 minutes daily for three periods of 8 weeks each, separated by one week, increases movement speed when compared to baseline and an untreated control group. Also, after treatment, movement speed was similar to that of a group of healthy age-matched individuals ( 109157 ).

Postoperative swelling.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for postoperative swelling.

A small preliminary clinical trial in older patients undergoing total knee arthroplasty shows that using a specific PEMF device (I-ONE therapy, IGEA) four hours daily for 60 days, starting within 7 days of surgery, modestly improves postoperative swelling, pain, and function when compared with no treatment ( 109123 ). However, a small clinical trial in patients with full-arch immediate loading implant surgery shows that use of a different PEMF device (RecoveryRx, BioElectronics Corp.) on one cheek for 48 hours does not reduce postoperative swelling or pain when compared with an inactive device on the other cheek ( 109136 ). However, this study may not have been adequately powered to detect differences between groups, and it is possible that wearing the PEMF device on one cheek may benefit both cheeks.

Pressure ulcers.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for pressure ulcers.

A small clinical trial in patients with pressure ulcers given standard care shows that 30 sessions of PEMF treatment with a specific device (Pulsatron, Madras Institute of Magnetobiology) over 6 weeks does not improve pressure ulcer healing when compared with sham treatment ( 109125 ). However, this study may have been underpowered to detect differences between groups.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for RA, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this condition.

Stress.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for reducing stress.

A small clinical trial in individuals with work-related stress who are receiving coaching shows that receiving light therapy plus PEMF treatment twice a week for 12 weeks is no more effective for reducing stress or fatigue, or for increasing return to work, than sham treatment or coaching alone ( 109142 ).

Tendinopathy.

It is unclear if PEMF therapy is beneficial for shoulder tendinopathy in patients also participating in other types of physical therapy.

A small clinical trial in patients with a supraspinatus tear shows that ten sessions over two weeks of treatment with a specific PEMF device (Roland HC, Pagani Elettronica) in combination with transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) and ultrasound does not improve pain or function when compared with the TENS, ultrasound, and sham PEMF therapy ( 109141 ). This study may have been inadequately powered to detect differences between groups.

Venous leg ulcers.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for venous leg ulcers, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this use.

Wound healing.

Although there is interest in using PEMF therapy for wound healing, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of PEMF therapy for this use.

More evidence is needed to rate PEMF therapy for these uses.

PEMF therapy involves the use of a low-intensity and low-frequency magnetic field ( 109125 , 109136 , 109141 , 109143 , 109159 ). Exposure to PEMF in the frequency of up to 300 Hz is most commonly used ( 109152 , 109153 , 109155 ). Some specific devices evaluated in clinical research have been cleared as class II medical devices by the US FDA. For example, Provant Therapy System (Regenesis Biomedical) and SofPulse Duo (Ivivi Technologies, Inc.) are cleared for adjunctive use in the palliative treatment of postoperative pain and edema in soft tissue; Bio-Electro-Magnetic-Energy-Regulation (BEMER) device is cleared for temporarily increasing local blood circulation in healthy leg muscles and for stimulating healthy muscles in order to improve and facilitate muscle performance; RecoveryRx (BioElectronics Corp.) has been cleared for use in the treatment of postoperative pain by a medical professional. Other devices  evaluated in clinical trials include Roland HC (Pagani Elettronica), Pulsatron (Madras Institute of Magnetobiology); I-ONE therapy (IGEA), Re5 NTS Parkinson Treatment System (Re5) MDcure (Aerotel Ltd.), and Magcell Microcirc (Physiomed Elektromedizin AG) ( 109122 , 109123 , 109125 , 109128 , 109129 , 109130 , 109133 , 109134 , 109136 , 109139 )( 109140 , 109141 , 109143 , 109146 , 109147 , 109149 , 109157 , 109158 , 109159 ). PEMF devices have been used at wound sites on or underneath dressings, as well as at specific locations on the body, such as the shoulders, lower back, cervical region, perineum, cranium, or within bras, ( 109123 , 109125 , 109128 , 109129 , 109130 , 109132 , 109136 , 109140 , 109141 , 109143 )( 109147 , 109149 , 109157 , 109158 , 109159 ) or have involved a bed, couch, mat, or other platform for the patient to lie on ( 109125 , 109135 , 109139 , 109142 , 109144 ). The length of each session in clinical trials has ranged from 5 minutes to 4 hours, with 1-3 sessions daily or 2-7 sessions per week, for up to 24 weeks. These sessions may be clinic- or home-based ( 109122 , 109123 , 109125 , 109126 , 109129 , 109133 , 109134 , 109141 , 109142 , 109143 )( 109144 , 109145 , 109149 , 109153 , 109154 , 109155 , 109157 , 109158 , 109159 ). Occasionally, PEMF devices are programmed to apply fields for 15-30 minutes every 2-12 hours, usually while a patient is in the hospital ( 109130 , 109140 , 109143 ).

None known.

None known.

The principle of PEMF therapy is based on the theory that the magnetic field increases biochemical reactions in the cell, resulting in enhanced circulation, cellular regeneration, healing, and improved well-being ( 109122 , 109139 ). General effects of PEMF therapy in laboratory research suggest that it may benefit tissues by altering the expression of various genes and promoting angiogenesis and vasodilation ( 109146 , 109147 , 109149 ). PEMF therapy is of interest for musculoskeletal health. Laboratory research suggests that PEMF therapy promotes osteoblast differentiation and maturation ( 109148 , 109155 ). Other research suggests that PEMF therapy can induce human articular chondrocyte proliferation and prevent cartilage degradation ( 109127 , 109150 ). PEMF therapy has anti-inflammatory effects, which may contribute to its beneficial effects on pain and swelling. In human research, PEMF therapy reduces levels of interleukin-1 beta in surgical wound exudates. However, levels of other inflammatory factors were not affected ( 109140 , 109143 ). In animal research, PEMF therapy reduces infiltration of inflammatory cells into the joint ( 109138 ). Other laboratory research suggests that PEMF therapy stimulates anti-inflammatory pathways in various cells ( 109123 , 109130 ). PEMF therapy may also have neuroprotective effects. In laboratory research, it regulates neutrophic factors such as bone-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), S100, and nerve growth factor. It may also enhance nerve cell proliferation and differentiation and stimulate neurogenesis ( 109158 ).
