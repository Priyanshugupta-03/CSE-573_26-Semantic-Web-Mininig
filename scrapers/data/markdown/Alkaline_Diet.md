# Alkaline Diet
Other Common Names Acid Ash Diet, Acid-Alkaline Diet, Alkaline Acid Diet, Alkaline Ash Diet, Dieta Alcalina, pH Diet, Régime alcalin
|

---

## Overview

The alkaline diet is based on the idea that a person should consume foods that are slightly alkaline. Advocates claim that consuming a diet high in acidic foods may disrupt the pH of the bloodstream and increase the likelihood of chronic or degenerative diseases. Scientific research has not evaluated any specific alkaline diet plan. However, formulas have been developed which are intended to evaluate the acidic potential of dietary patterns. An alkaline dietary pattern incorporates foods which score low in dietary acidic potential ( 108379 , 108380 , 108399 , 109030 ).

Possibly Safe when used appropriately. The safety of alkaline diets has not been specifically evaluated in clinical trials. However, there is no reason to expect safety issues as long as nutritional needs are being met.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using unless under medical advisement.

#### General

Although a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted, an alkaline diet seems to be well-tolerated as long as nutritional needs are met.

Age-related cognitive decline.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for attenuating age-related cognitive decline.

An observational study suggests that consuming a diet with a lower dietary acid load is associated with better memory test scores and overall cognitive function in elderly adults aged 70 years and older, but not aged 60-69 years, when compared with a higher dietary acid load ( 116117 ).

Aging.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet to prevent aging, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this purpose.

Anxiety.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for anxiety.

An observational study in adults suggests that consuming a diet with the highest dietary acid load is associated with 13% greater odds of having more severe anxiety when compared with a diet with the lowest dietary acid load. Approximately half of the adults in this study had some degree of anxiety ( 113960 ).

Athletic performance.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for athletic performance.

A small clinical trial shows that consuming an alkaline-promoting diet increases the time to exhaustion of an anaerobic performance exercise by 21% when compared with an acid-promoting diet. There was no effect on time to exhaustion during the graded aerobic portion. The diet was consumed for 4-9 days in order to achieve an alkalotic or acidic fasted morning urine pH ( 108382 ).

Atopic disease.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for atopic disease, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this purpose.

Bacterial vaginosis.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of bacterial vaginosis.

A case-control observational study suggests that dietary acid load is not associated with the odds of experiencing bacterial vaginosis ( 113968 ).

Bladder cancer.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of bladder cancer.

A single observational study in a meta-analysis has found that consuming a diet with the highest dietary acid load does not increase the odds of having bladder cancer when compared with a diet with the lowest dietary acid load ( 109480 ).

Breast cancer.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of breast cancer; the available research is conflicting.

One meta-analysis of a small number of observational studies has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load does not increase the odds of having breast cancer when compared with a diet with the lowest acid load ( 108380 ). However, a meta-analysis including a more recent observational study based in the US has found a 32% increased risk of breast cancer in individuals with the highest dietary acid load when compared with the lowest ( 108396 ).

Cancer.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of overall cancer risk, cancer prognosis, or cancer-related mortality.

Meta-analyses of observational research suggest that consuming a diet with the highest acid load increases the odds or risk of having any type of cancer by 41% to 77% ( 108380 , 108396 , 113971 ) and increases the risk of poor cancer prognosis by 53% when compared with a diet with the lowest acid load ( 113971 ). However, a single observational study suggests that dietary acid load is not associated with cancer-related mortality ( 108377 ).

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for CVD prevention.

Observational research from Korea has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load increases the absolute risk of developing CVD from 8.5% to 9.6%. Most research also shows that it is associated with a 23% increased odds of having at least a 10% risk of CVD over 10 years. The increased risk was greater in adults at least 55 years old ( 108392 ). However, other observational research from Iran has found no association between dietary acid load and CVD risk ( 108397 ). The association between dietary acid load and cardiovascular mortality has also been investigated. Some observational research has found a U-shaped association, with increases in cardiovascular mortality associated with both the highest and lowest dietary acid loads. However, any association is small, and clinical significance is unclear ( 109033 ). Conversely, other observational research has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load increases the odds of cardiovascular mortality by about 16% when compared with the lowest dietary acid load ( 108377 ).

Chronic kidney disease (CKD).

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for CKD progression or prevention.

A meta-analysis of observational research suggests that consuming a diet with the highest acid load is associated with a 31% increased risk of CKD when compared with the lowest acid load ( 108385 ). The association between dietary acid load and CKD progression has also been investigated. One study in patients with CKD suggests that consuming a diet with the highest acid load is associated with a 3-fold increased risk of developing kidney failure over the next 14.2 years. This association was strongest in patients with moderate or advanced CKD with albuminuria at baseline but was independent of baseline estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ( 108381 ). Additionally, a small retrospective observational study in patients with CKD on maintenance hemodialysis suggests that consuming a diet with the highest acid load is associated with a 3.88-fold higher rate of 10-year all-cause mortality when compared with a diet with the lowest acid load ( 116115 ). Other observational research in patients with both CKD and diabetes suggests that an incremental increase of 10 mEq/day in dietary acid load is associated with a 27% to 31% increased risk of renal replacement therapy. However, in patients with CKD without diabetes, there is no association between dietary acid load and renal replacement therapy ( 113959 ).

Colorectal cancer.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of colorectal cancer.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load increases the odds of having colorectal cancer by 76% when compared with a diet with the lowest dietary acid load ( 108396 ).

Conjunctivitis.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for conjunctivitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this condition.

Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for long-term survival following CABG surgery.

Observational research has found that consuming a diet with the highest dietary acid load increases the odds of having a lower ejection fraction rate when compared with lower dietary acid loads. However, any beneficial association with ten-year survival is small and dependent on the method of determining the dietary acid load ( 108376 ).

Dental caries.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for preventing dental caries, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this purpose.

Dental hypersensitivity.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for dental hypersensitivity, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this purpose.

Depression.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of depression.

An observational study in adults suggests that consuming a diet with the highest acid load is associated with 21% greater odds of more severe depression compared with a diet with the lowest acid load in adult females; but is not associated with depression in adult males. Approximately one third of the adults in this study had depression ( 113960 ).

Diabetes.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for prevention of type 2 or gestational diabetes.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that dietary acid load is associated with an increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes. Each 5-unit increment in acidic load is associated with a 1% to 4% increased risk, dependent on the method of determining the acidic load. When comparing the highest and lowest dietary acid loads, the risk of type 2 diabetes was about 22% higher with the highest loads. However, a sub-analysis suggests that any beneficial association may be limited to females ( 108395 ). More recent observational research in Iranian adults has found that the highest tertile of dietary acid load is associated with a 2.2- to 4.4-fold increased odds of type 2 diabetes when compared with the lowest. However, there was no association between dietary acid load and type 2 diabetes risk when acid load was estimated using indices including protein/potassium ratios ( 108393 ). In contrast, one meta-analysis of observational research in mainly healthy individuals has found that dietary acid load is not associated with glycemic parameters, including fasting blood glucose or insulin levels, glycated hemoglobin, or measures of insulin resistance ( 108384 ). The association between dietary acid load and gestational diabetes has also been investigated. Observational research has found that the highest dietary acid load during early pregnancy is associated with an approximate 2-fold increased odds of developing gestational diabetes in the second trimester when compared with the lowest acid load ( 109034 ).

Diarrhea.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for diarrhea, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this condition.

Dry skin.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for dry skin, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this condition.

Esophageal cancer.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of esophageal cancer.

An observational case-control study suggests that consuming a diet with the highest acid load is associated with a 2.2-fold higher odds of having squamous cell esophageal cancer compared with the lowest acid load ( 113970 ).

Fatigue.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for fatigue, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this purpose.

Fractures.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of fractures.

A meta-analysis of observational research suggests that dietary acid load is not associated with fracture risk. The association with femoral or spinal bone mineral density is unclear. Findings appear to be dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 108389 ). However, an observational study conducted in China in older adults suggests that consuming a diet with a higher acid load is associated with a nearly 2-2.5 greater odds of hip fracture when compared with the lowest acid load ( 113969 ).

Hyperlipidemia.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for hyperlipidemia.

Meta-analyses of observational research have found that dietary acid load is not associated with levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL) cholesterol, or triglycerides ( 108384 , 116119 ). However, most of the studies in the analyses evaluated healthy individuals. A more recent observational study in older Iranian males has found that the highest dietary acid load is associated with more than a 2-fold increased risk of hypertriglyceridemia when compared with the lowest acid load. However, this finding is dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 108394 ).

Hypertension.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the treatment or prevention of hypertension.

A meta-analysis of observational research suggests that consuming a diet with the lowest acid load is associated with a 1.5-1.7 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure and a 0.7-1.1 mmHg reduction in diastolic blood pressure when compared with the highest acid load ( 116119 ). However, these differences are unlikely to be clinically significant. Additionally, findings are limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies, including highly variable study size and quality, type of dietary assessment tools used, and characteristics of study participants. Observational research has also evaluated the association of dietary acid load with hypertension risk. One meta-analysis of observational research suggests that consuming a diet with the highest acid load is associated with a 14% or 35% increased risk of hypertension when compared with the lowest. Also, dietary acid load was associated with both systolic and diastolic blood pressure levels ( 108383 ). Another meta-analysis of observational research has found that increased dietary acid load is associated with an increased risk of hypertension; however, this finding is dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 108400 ). More recent observational research has found that the highest dietary acid load is associated with an 18% increased risk of having high blood pressure when compared with the lowest ( 108378 ).

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of IBS.

An observational study suggests that dietary acid load is not generally associated with the odds of developing IBS or with IBS severity; however, this finding is dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 113967 ).

Kidney failure.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for preventing the progression of chronic kidney disease (CKD) to kidney failure.

Observational research in patients with CKD has found that consuming a diet with the highest dietary acid load is associated with a three-fold increased risk of developing kidney failure over the next 14.2 years. This association was strongest in patients with baseline moderate or advanced CKD with albuminuria but was independent of baseline estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ( 108381 ). However, in Black adults with hypertensive nephrosclerosis, dietary acid load is not associated with developing kidney failure over the next 8 years ( 109035 ).

Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis).

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for kidney stones.

Observational research has found that the highest dietary acid load is associated with an increased risk of calcium oxalate kidney stones when compared with the lowest acid load. However, this finding is dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 108390 ).

Leg cramps.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for leg cramps, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this purpose.

Lung cancer.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of lung cancer.

A single observational study has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load increases the odds of having lung cancer by about 2.2-fold when compared with a diet with the lowest dietary acid load. However, this finding is dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 108380 , 108396 ). Preliminary clinical research in patients with advanced non-small cell lung cancer treated with epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR)-tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) shows that following an alkaline diet increases progression-free and overall survival duration by approximately 6 months when compared with published historical averages ( 109481 ).

Metabolic syndrome.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of metabolic syndrome.

A meta-analysis of observational studies suggests that consuming a diet with the highest dietary acid load is associated with 42% to 76% greater odds of developing metabolic syndrome when compared with the lowest. The findings from the meta-analysis are limited by significant heterogeneity, which may have resulted from differences in dietary assessment methods, diagnostic criteria, and patient characteristics ( 113961 ). Observational research also suggests that the highest dietary acid load is associated with prevalence of individual metabolic syndrome components, especially obesity, high blood pressure, and high blood glucose ( 108378 ). One observational study in older Iranian males has found that the highest dietary acid load is associated with a 17-fold increased risk of metabolic syndrome when compared with the lowest. However, this finding is dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 108394 ).

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of migraine headache.

Observational research has found that the highest dietary acid load is associated with a 4.1- to 7.2-fold increased odds of having migraines when compared with the lowest ( 108398 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of MS.

A small observational study suggests that consuming a diet with the highest acid load in the past year is associated with a 3.6 to 4.2 greater odds of developing MS when compared with the lowest. However, after adjusting for dietary sodium, saturated fat, and fiber intake there was no longer an association ( 113963 ).

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of NAFLD; the available research is conflicting.

One observational study has found that the highest dietary acid load is associated with a modest increased odds of having NAFLD when compared with the lowest. However, this finding is dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 109037 ). Conversely, an observational study which controlled for various confounders has found that dietary acid load is not associated with NAFLD risk ( 109036 ). Another observational study has found that there may be a U-shaped relationship between dietary acid load and risk of NAFLD; a neutral dietary acid load was associated with the lowest odds of having NAFLD. The highest dietary acid load was not associated with an increased odds of NAFLD; however, the lowest dietary acid load is associated with a modest increased odds when compared with a neutral dietary acid load ( 108386 ). Higher quality research is needed to understand the possible relationship between dietary acid load and risk of NAFLD.

Obesity.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention or treatment of obesity.

A meta-analysis has found a small but significant association between dietary acid load and increased prevalence of obesity ( 109038 ). Some more recent observational research in adults has found that the highest dietary acid load is associated with a 35% increased risk or 2.4-fold increased odds of obesity when compared with the lowest acid load. However, this finding seems to be dependent on the method used to determine the dietary acid load ( 108378 , 108387 ). The association between dietary acid load and body mass index (BMI) or waist circumference is unclear due to mixed findings from meta-analyses of observational research in mainly healthy individuals ( 108384 , 116119 ). The association between dietary acid load and obesity has also been investigated in children and adolescents. Observational research in children suggests that dietary acid load is not associated with most anthropometric indices, including BMI ( 108379 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Although there has been interest in using an alkaline diet for osteoarthritis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of an alkaline diet for this condition.

Osteoporosis.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of osteoporosis.

An observational study in postmenopausal adults suggests that consuming a diet with an acid load above the median is associated with a 65% to 72% increased risk of osteoporosis when compared with an acid load below the median. Additionally, higher dietary acid load is associated with lower bone mineral density at the lumbar spine when compared with lower dietary acid load ( 113965 ).

Overall mortality.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for reducing overall mortality.

A single observational study has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load increases the odds of overall mortality by 12% or 13% when compared with a diet with the lowest dietary acid load. Odds of mortality increased with increasing dietary acid load ( 108377 ). Other observational research has found a U-shaped association, with modest increases in overall mortality associated with both the highest and lowest dietary acid loads ( 109033 ).

Pancreatic cancer.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of pancreatic cancer.

A single observational study has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load increases the odds of having pancreatic cancer by 64% to 73% when compared with a diet with the lowest acid load ( 108396 , 109482 ).

Pre-eclampsia.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of pre-eclampsia.

Observational research has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load early in pregnancy does not increase the odds of developing pre-eclampsia when compared with a diet with the lowest dietary acid load ( 109032 ).

Pregnancy-induced hypertension.

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of pregnancy-induced hypertension.

Observational research has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load early in pregnancy does not increase the odds of developing pregnancy-induced hypertension when compared with a diet with the lowest dietary acid load ( 109032 ).

It is unclear if an alkaline diet is beneficial for the prevention of prostate cancer.

A single observational study has found that consuming a diet with the highest acid load increases the odds of having prostate cancer by 3.4- to 3.9-fold when compared with a diet with the lowest dietary acid load ( 109483 , 108396 ).

An observational case-control study suggests that consuming a diet with a higher acid load in the past year is associated with a 3 times greater odds of ulcerative colitis diagnosis when compared with the lowest ( 113962 ).

More evidence is needed to rate an alkaline diet for these uses.

There is no specific alkaline diet. Most typical weight loss diets are not expected to decrease dietary acid load ( 109031 ). The average dietary acid load seems to be slightly higher in the US when compared with Europe ( 108382 ). Advocates of an alkaline diet commonly suggest consuming 75% to 80% alkaline foods and 20% to 25% acidic foods. Individuals who choose to follow the alkaline diet are generally recommended to consume eight ounces of water every two hours. However, fluid is not to be consumed with meals due to concerns that it can reduce the contact between alkaline saliva and food ( 108397 , 108399 , 109031 ). Acidic foods include meat, eggs, dairy, white flour, sugar, caffeine, saturated fats, processed foods, carbonated beverages, peanuts, and white rice. Also, sweets, chocolates, alcohol, and tobacco are not recommended. Advocates of the diet recommend soaking mildly acidic foods, such as whole grains and legumes, before cooking them. Many of the foods eaten in this diet are raw fruits and vegetables ( 108397 , 108399 , 109031 ). Dietary acid load In scientific research, dietary acid loads are determined using established formulas. Individual dietary patterns are determined using a food frequency questionnaire, 24-hour diet recall, or food intake records. Dietary acid load is then determined from these daily intakes. The most common formulas used in observational research are the Potential Renal Acid Load (PRAL) and the Net Endogenous Acid Production (NEAP) ( 108376 , 108378 , 108380 , 108383 , 108384 , 108385 , 108390 , 108393 , 108396 , 108397 )( 108398 , 108399 , 109030 , 109031 , 109033 , 109035 , 113959 , 113960 , 113961 ). The PRAL formula takes into consideration ionic dissociation, sulfur metabolism, and intestinal absorption rates for protein, as well as the minerals potassium, phosphate, magnesium, and calcium. NEAP considers the dietary content of protein and potassium. The net endogenous acid excretion (NAE) score takes into consideration anthropometric information and PRAL. Formulas have been validated against urinary pH ( 108379 , 109032 ). Some research suggests that PRAL is the most accurate for estimating the net acid production in the body ( 108386 ). In some studies, urine is obtained for measurements of pH, protein, and/or potassium. Some research suggests that the 24-hour urine pH may be more useful than fasting morning urine pH for determining dietary acid load ( 108384 , 108385 , 108393 , 109035 ). Less common measures of dietary acid load include ratios such as total protein/potassium, animal protein/potassium, and vegetable protein/potassium ( 108380 , 108385 , 108386 , 108393 , 108398 , 109032 , 109034 ). Prospective research involving a prescribed alkaline diet is limited. In one study, participants were instructed to increase consumption of fruits to at least 4 servings daily and vegetables to 6-8 cups daily, as well as to eat frequently and to consume starchy vegetables, dried fruit, and plant sources of fat to maintain energy intake. They were also instructed to decrease consumption of meats, cheeses, and grains including wheat, corn, and oats. Foods such as legumes, yogurt, egg white, and quinoa were allowed. The diet was followed for about 7 days until a fasted morning urine pH of at least 7.0 was achieved ( 108382 ).

The alkaline diet is based on the idea that a person should consume foods that are slightly alkaline, because the body's normal pH is slightly alkaline. The normal pH range of the bloodstream is between 7.36 and 7.44. Advocates believe that the pH level of the body's internal fluids affects every living cell in the body and claim that consuming a diet high in acidic foods, such as animal protein, sugar, caffeine, and processed foods, may disrupt the pH of the bloodstream and increase the likelihood of developing chronic or degenerative diseases. The alkaline diet focuses on maintaining an alkaline environment in the body. In a normal state, the human body is slightly alkaline in all areas, with the exception of the digestive and urinary tracts. It is theorized that metabolic, enzymatic, immunologic, and repair mechanisms function best when an alkaline environment is maintained. During illness or times of stress, the body usually takes on more acidic properties. The theory suggests that the acid formation associated with stress and inflammation from an illness, or from high-fat and high-protein foods, can be adequately and effectively neutralized only when sufficient mineral-buffering reserves are present, which can be found in a diet that is predominantly alkaline. Scientific research on the alkaline diet is limited, although the available research suggests that such a diet does modestly influence the acid-base status in the body ( 108377 , 108399 ). Diets rich in meat and dairy products contain sulfur-containing amino acids, including cysteine and methionine, as well as high levels of phosphorus. These compounds are thought to result in increased endogenous acid production via the production of sulfur metabolites and other compounds. In contrast, fruit and vegetables are rich in potassium salts and organic anions, and result in the production of the more alkaline bicarbonate ( 108389 , 108395 , 108399 , 109031 ). Observational research has found an association between increased dietary acid load and adverse health outcomes. However, any mechanism of action is unclear. Some scientists suggest that a higher dietary acid load induces low-grade metabolic acidosis, or a pH close to 7.35. Variations in dietary composition seem to alter blood pH by about 0.03 pH units and urine pH by about 1.0 pH unit. Low-grade metabolic acidosis might increase the production of inflammatory mediators and cortisol and decrease insulin sensitivity ( 108380 , 108382 , 108398 , 108399 , 109031 ). One or more of these mechanisms might impact health ( 108383 , 108398 , 108399 ). Some scientists also suggest that dietary acid load plays a role in the regulation of uric acid levels and that hyperuricemia is associated with certain chronic disease. Some observational research supports the association between high dietary acid load and risk of hyperuricemia ( 109030 ).

#### Anti-cancer effects

Proponents of the alkaline diet believe that healthy cells must maintain a slightly alkaline pH level. Some cells change their makeup in order to survive in an acidic environment; however, these cells may become cancerous in the long-term. Some individuals believe that cancer cells are acidic while healthy cells are alkaline. Thus, if cancer is completely removed by surgery, the chances that a new tumor will reappear in the same area increase if a person continues to eat an acidic diet. Although altering pH homeostasis in and around tumor cells might impact cell viability, the effect of an alkaline diet on cancer prevention or treatment has not been adequately studied ( 108391 , 108396 ).

#### Fertility effects

A more alkaline diet is of interest for improving fertility. Observational research in adults suggests that consuming a diet with a higher acid load is associated with 26% greater odds of diminished ovarian reserve when compared with a lower dietary acid load ( 116116 ).

#### Musculoskeletal effects

Dietary acid load is of interest for skeletal health. Although some research in humans suggests that diets with a higher acid load result in increased urinary calcium excretion, there is no evidence that calcium balance is affected. Also, research related to bone density and fracture risk are mixed ( 108388 , 108389 ).

#### Renal effects

Some patients are at increased risk of metabolic acidosis. In patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD), metabolic acidosis is a complication that can lead to progression of disease. There is some evidence to suggest that dietary changes to reduce urinary acid excretion might slow disease progression. However, not all research agrees, and the alkaline diet itself has not been investigated ( 108381 , 108385 ). Increased urinary pH, which is influenced by dietary intake, might increase the risk of kidney stone formation, possibly by increasing calcium and oxalate excretion ( 108390 ).
