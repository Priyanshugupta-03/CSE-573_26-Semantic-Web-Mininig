# Negative Ion Generator
Other Common Names Air Ionizer, Chizhevsky's Chandelier, Generadores de Iones Negativos, Générateur d'ions négatifs, Ion Therapy, Ionized Air, Negative Air Ionization, Negative Ion Patch
|

---

Caution

Negative ion generators should not be confused with Ozone Therapy , which may involve the use of an ozone generator that can also release negative ions.

## Overview

Negative ion generators are electrical devices that create charged particles called negative ions, resulting in "ionized air." They are marketed to improve health by reversing negative ion depletion and/or filtering dust and smoke particles ( 108180 , 108191 ). Negative ion generators are not regulated by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of negative ion generators. Negative ion generators distribute small amounts of ozone into the air ( 108200 ). Ozone is toxic in higher quantities, but these levels are not likely to be achieved with the use of a typical negative ion generator ( 108198 , 108199 ).

#### General

No adverse effects have been reported; however, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted. There is some concern that negative ion generators may distribute small amounts of ozone into the air, although this is unlikely to reach toxic levels.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Allergic rhinitis (hay fever).

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for allergic rhinitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Anxiety.

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for anxiety, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Asthma.

It is unclear if negative ion generators are beneficial for asthma.

A very small preliminary clinical trial in children and adults with asthma shows that use of a portable ionizer overnight improves peak expiration in some, but not all, patients when compared with baseline ( 108182 ). Furthermore, preliminary clinical trials in adults and children with asthma included in a systematic review, negative ion generators did not affect pulmonary function, symptom severity, or bronchodilator use when compared with placebo generators ( 108180 ).

Atopic disease.

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for atopic disease, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Bipolar disorder.

It is unclear if negative ion generators are beneficial for bipolar disorder.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with bipolar disorder shows that about 55% of patients treated with low-density ionization therapy for up to 45 minutes daily for 8 weeks were responders or remitters. These findings were not significantly different from those patients treated with blue light therapy. Of two patients treated with high-density ionization therapy, one subject responded and one did not ( 108183 ).

Bronchitis.

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for bronchitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for COPD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Cognitive function.

It is unclear if negative ion generators are beneficial for improving cognitive function in children.

A small preliminary clinical trial in children shows that breathing negatively ionized air modestly improves some measures of cognitive function when compared with breathing in regular air-conditioned air as a control ( 108181 ).

Depression.

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for depression, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Exercise-induced muscle damage.

It is unclear if negative ion generators are beneficial for exercise-induced muscle damage.

Preliminary clinical research in badminton athletes shows that using a negative ion generator producing medium- or high-density negative ions following a high-intensity squat program may modestly reduce muscle damage and promote recovery when compared with using a placebo or low-density negative ion generator. Use of the negative ion generator reduced levels of tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), creatine kinase, and lactate dehydrogenase. The ion densities, provided as ions/cm 3 , used in this study were 300 (low), 3,000 (medium), and 30,000 (high) ( 108201 ).

Influenza.

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for influenza prevention, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Insomnia.

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for sleep improvement, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Rhinosinusitis.

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for rhinosinusitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

Seasonal affective disorder (SAD).

It is unclear if negative ion generators are beneficial for SAD.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with SAD shows that treatment with high-density ionization therapy for 30 minutes daily for 20 days results in at least a 50% reduction of symptom severity or frequency in 58% of patients, compared with only 15% of those using low-density ionization ( 108179 ). However, other preliminary clinical research in patients with SAD shows that use of high-density negative ionization therapy for 30 minutes daily for 12 days is no more effective than low-density negative ionization ( 108197 ).

Stress.

Although there is interest in using negative ion generators for stress, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of negative ion generators for this condition.

More evidence is needed to rate negative ion generators for these uses.

Negative ion densities utilized in clinical research have varied. Ion densities of 2-2.7 million ions/cm 3 or 4.5 hundred trillion ions/second, with exposure of about 30 minutes, are considered high. Controls typically provide lower densities of 10-40 thousand ions/cm 3 or 1.7 hundred billion ions/second ( 108179 , 108183 , 108197 ). When used for longer periods, such as overnight or over the entire 24-hour period, lower ion densities are typically provided ( 108180 , 108182 ). The location of the unit is dependent on the suggested ion density for the specific condition of interest.

None known.

None known.

Ions are particles that hold either a positive or a negative charge and are theoretically naturally present in the air. Negative ion generators are claimed to produce negative ions by adding an electron to various gasses in the air. This is thought to reverse negative ion depletion, and possibly reduce levels of positive ions, while also filtering pathogens and particles ( 108176 , 108180 , 108191 ). Negative ion generators are used mainly for preventing infectious respiratory conditions and for the treatment of respiratory symptoms and depression. In vitro, negative ions inhibit the growth of various types of airborne bacteria, including Staphylococcus aureus and Escherichia coli, and may reduce transmission of other bacteria and viruses ( 108176 , 108191 ). Furthermore, use of a commercial air ionizer has been shown to reduce numbers of the allergen dust mites, Dermatophagoides pteronyssinus and D. farina, on surfaces such as a mattress ( 108177 ). In vitro, negative air ions seem to attach to dust, mold, and other allergen particles, possibly reducing exposure ( 108191 ). Some researchers suggest that negative air ions have antidepressant effects and that this may be related to a reduction in the levels of serotonin. It has been suggested that superoxide anions play a role. However, other researchers have found no effect of negative air ions on levels of serotonin in animal models ( 108191 ).
