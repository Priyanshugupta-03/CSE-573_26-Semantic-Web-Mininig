# Fat Smash Diet
Other Common Names Dieta «Aplastar la Grasa» (Fat Smash Diet), Extreme Fat Smash Diet, Régime Fat Smash

## Overview

The Fat Smash diet is a four phase, 90-day diet and exercise program that was developed by Dr. Ian K. Smith and published in a book by the same name. Recipes and exercise regimens designed by Smith are provided and instructions for dealing with food temptations are also included. The goals of the diet are to promote weight loss and long-term weight maintenance while preventing chronic medical conditions. Dr. Smith also released another book entitled Extreme Fat Smash diet. This diet program is intended for individuals looking to lose 10 to 25 pounds in a short period of time and deviates from the long-term approach of the original Fat Smash diet ( 102304 ).

## History

The Fat Smash Diet book was published in 2006 by Dr. Ian K. Smith. His second book, Extreme Fat Smash Diet, was published in 2007 ( 102304 ).

## People Use This For

The Fat Smash diet is used for obesity and to improve overall health.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the Fat Smash diet. However, there is no reason to expect safety issues as long as proper nutrition is maintained.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable evidence about the safety of the Fat Smash diet; avoid use.

#### General

A thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted; however, there is no reason to expect safety concerns as long as nutritional needs are met.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of the Fat Smash diet.

The Fat Smash diet includes four phases, which usually take about 90 days to complete. Phase I : This phase, referred to as "detox", is intended to last 9 days. It involves a vegetarian diet that allows for unlimited amounts of mostly vegetables and fruits, as well as brown rice, oatmeal, legumes, any type of beans, eggs, low-fat yogurt, and any type of tofu. Herbal tea of any variety and water are the only permitted beverages. During this phase, individuals begin a cardiovascular exercise regimen for 30 minute sessions, five times a week ( 102304 ). Phase II : This phase, referred to as "foundation", is intended to last 3 weeks. At this time, more foods can be incorporated into the diet, including seafood, dairy, cold cereals, coffee, and lean meats. Natural and artificial sweeteners, healthy fats, condiments, and diet soda can also be reintroduced. Individuals are to consume 4-5 meals daily that have the same calorie value but may vary in portion sizes. All 30-minute exercise sessions are increased by five minutes. Weightlifting and other forms of anaerobic exercises are permitted ( 102304 ). Phase III : Referred to as "construction", this phase is intended to last 4 weeks. A variety of foods, including lean meats, fruit juice, whole grains, pastas, and occasional desserts, are permitted in this phase. In order to reduce fats, all cooking should be limited to boiling, steaming, baking, or grilling. All exercise sessions are increased to 45 minutes and twice daily exercise is encouraged several times a week in order to increase metabolism. For those interested in further accelerating weight loss, it is recommended to repeat Phase I for one day of every week ( 102304 ). Phase IV : Referred to as "the temple", this phase is intended to be used long-term for weight maintenance. In this phase, foods that were previously restricted are now permitted in moderation. All exercise routines are extended to a full hour. Strength training is encouraged at least three times a week to increase the body's metabolism and increase lean body mass. If an adherent regresses on the diet plan or gains weight, he or she is encouraged to repeat a 10-day period of Phase I ( 102304 ).

The Fat Smash diet attempts to help individuals adjust to new eating and exercise patterns by using a four-phase model, with the goal of long-term incorporation into daily life. In order to encourage weight maintenance, the diet is designed to allow for occasional deviations from its proposed eating and exercise plan ( 102304 ).
