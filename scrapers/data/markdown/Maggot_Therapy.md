## Overview

Maggots are living fly larvae that consume decaying or necrotic tissue. Typically, larvae of the green bottle fly (Lucilia sericata) are used for therapeutic purposes, but related species such as Lucilia cuprina have also been used ( 108488 , 108491 ). Maggot therapy is a type of biosurgery or biotherapy that uses live maggots for cleaning non-healing wounds. Non-healing wounds are classified as those that show little or no improvement after four weeks or that do not heal in eight weeks. These wounds have a high risk of infection ( 27958 , 27968 ).

The use of non-sterile maggots may lead to serious infections ( 27986 , 27995 , 108490 ). In 2003, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) decided to regulate medicinal maggots as a medical device and in January 2004, the FDA issued a directive allowing the use of medicinal maggots ( 27982 ). One specific brand of maggot therapy, Medical Maggots, is approved by the US FDA as a prescription-only medical device for debridement of chronic wounds, including non-healing traumatic wounds, non-healing post-surgical wounds, neuropathic foot ulcers, pressure ulcers, and venous stasis ulcers ( 27958 , 27968 ).

Possibly Safe when sterile maggots are used topically under the direction of a qualified healthcare professional ( 27985 , 27995 , 27996 , 29043 , 29053 , 29057 ).

Possibly Unsafe when non-sterile maggots are used topically. The use of non-sterile maggots can lead to septicemia when exposed to open wounds ( 27976 , 27986 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid use.

#### General

Topically, maggot therapy seems to be generally well tolerated when a sterile product is used under medical supervision.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Bleeding and pain.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

The use of non-sterile maggots may lead to septicemia.

#### Hematologic

Topically, maggot therapy may cause bleeding ( 27987 , 27988 , 27989 , 27990 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Topically, maggot therapy may increase pain at the wound site ( 27973 , 27985 , 27990 , 27991 , 27992 , 27993 , 27994 , 27995 ).

#### Psychiatric

Anxiety associated with thoughts of maggots escaping the wound may be seen with maggot therapy ( 27991 , 27996 ).

#### Other

Topically, the use of non-sterile maggots may lead to septicemia ( 27986 , 27995 , 108490 ). Also, maggot therapy may be associated with fever and flu-like symptoms ( 27990 ). A case report describes a 70-year-old male with a large, chronic left temporal wound from invasive cancer who developed bacteremia due to Wohlfahrtiimonas chitiniclastica after maggot therapy. The infection was treated successfully with levofloxacin ( 108490 ).

Possibly Effective
Diabetic foot ulcers.

Most clinical research suggests that maggot therapy can improve outcomes in patients with diabetic foot ulcers.

Preliminary clinical studies, case series, and two meta-analyses of lower quality research in patients with diabetic foot ulcers show that maggot therapy is up to 80% more likely to achieve full wound healing when compared with standard care ( 27994 , 29047 , 29048 , 97659 , 97660 , 108488 , 108492 ). In addition to wound healing, some preliminary clinical research shows that maggot therapy might prevent the need for foot amputation and decrease the need for antibiotics in some of these patients ( 29047 , 29048 , 97659 , 97660 ). However, conflicting evidence exists ( 29046 ). Higher quality research is still needed to determine the overall benefit of maggot therapy.

Venous leg ulcers.

Clinical research suggests that maggot therapy may improve debridement of venous leg ulcers when compared with hydrogel, although it may increase the risk of adverse effects and may not be beneficial when compared with conventional treatment.

Clinical research in patients with chronic venous leg ulcers shows that maggot therapy leads to more rapid wound debridement when compared with hydrogel ( 27983 , 27984 , 29042 , 97661 ). In a retrospective, uncontrolled analysis of 38 patients with 55 venous leg ulcers, applying maggots twice weekly resulted in complete debridement in about 2 weeks, and complete healing of 76% of the ulcers in about 4 weeks. Bacterial load was also reduced after the first treatment session ( 108489 ). However, maggot therapy does not appear to improve quality of life or bacterial load when compared with hydrogel, and may be associated with more pain and adverse reactions ( 27983 , 27984 ). Also, maggot therapy does not seem to improve wound healing when compared with conventional treatment ( 97661 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Behcet disease.

It is unclear if maggot therapy is beneficial for lower extremity ulcers due to Behçet disease.

Preliminary clinical research in people with treatment-resistant lower extremity cutaneous ulcers due to Behçet disease shows that maggot therapy applied every 3-4 days while on bed rest is associated with complete debridement of about 92% of ulcers within a mean of 15 days, and a 23% mean reduction in ulcer size ( 108491 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a control group and statistical analysis.

Burns.

Although there is interest in using maggot therapy for burns, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of maggot therapy for this condition.

Leishmania lesions.

Although there is interest in using maggot therapy for Leishmania lesions, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of maggot therapy for this condition.

Postoperative infection.

It is unclear if preoperative maggot therapy reduces the risk of infection after surgical wound closure.

Preliminary clinical research shows that preoperative maggot debridement of soft tissue wounds reduces the risk of wound infection following surgical closure when compared with patients not given maggot therapy ( 27993 ). Anecdotal evidence also suggests that maggot therapy may improve wound healing in patients who already developed an infection after surgery ( 29053 ).

Pressure ulcers.

It is unclear if maggot therapy is beneficial for pressure ulcers.

Some preliminary clinical research in patients with pressure ulcers shows that maggot therapy improves wound healing, decreases necrotic tissue, and increases granulation tissue when compared with conventional therapy ( 27996 , 29041 ).

More evidence is needed to rate maggot therapy for these uses.

Using sterile larvae is very important when utilizing maggot therapy. To achieve sterility, maggots may be exposed to full strength hydrogen peroxide or another chemical for approximately two hours and then may be immersed in mercuric chloride or a similar solution. The eggs may also be sterilized using many different solutions. Some practitioners may expose the applied maggots to a bright light in order to drive them deeper into the wound. Some believe that as few as six maggots might be sufficient for a fingertip injury, although 500-600 may be required for more extensive wounds. Studies have used 7-15 larvae per square centimeter of the wound ( 108488 , 108489 , 108491 , 108492 , 108493 ). Self-retaining metal or glass devices have been developed to hold wounds open during therapy, to allow drainage of the wound, and to allow maggots access to the wound ( 29037 ). The sterile maggots are placed on the wound when about 1 day old, and the wound is covered with gauze and a dressing. Wound dressings used in maggot therapy are typically made of crinoline or gauze and should permit oxygen to reach the maggots, facilitate drainage, and allow inspection of the wound. The dressing is usually removed 1-4 days later, when the maggots are fully grown and about 10 mm long. Once mature, maggots will no longer digest the dead, infected tissue. When the dressing is removed, the wound is rinsed with sterile saline to remove the maggots ( 108492 ). Maggot therapy may need to be repeated once or twice per week until the wound is completely cleaned. Sometimes, one application is sufficient. However, rarely, therapy may need to continue for several weeks depending upon the size of the wound and the amount of dead tissue. In some cases, maggots have been placed every 3 days for the first 4 weeks, then once in the next 4 weeks, and once in the last 2 months ( 108489 ). Large quantities of larval enzymes may cause significant destruction of the skin if they are allowed to run on to unprotected skin around the margin of a wound. In some studies, a cage-like dressing has been made of gauze and micropore to keep the maggots in place, while in others, zinc oxide ointment was applied to the surrounding skin ( 108488 , 108491 , 108492 ).  In one study, patients with diabetic ulcers were randomized to have either free-range larvae applied to their wound, or larvae contained in a porous bag. While patient acceptance was higher with the larval bag, the rates of debridement and wound healing were higher with the free-range larvae. The effects of bagged larvae were likely due to secretions, whereas the free-range larvae were also able to consume necrotic tissue ( 108493 ).

#### IVERMECTIN (Stromectol, others)
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Theoretically, ivermectin might interfere with maggot therapy.

In vitro research shows that ivermectin decreases survival of Lucilia sericata ( 29020 ). Due to its larvicidal properties, ivermectin may theoretically decrease the effectiveness of maggot therapy, especially if applied to the skin.

#### FENUGREEK

Theoretically, fenugreek might interfere with maggot therapy.

In vitro research shows that fenugreek oil has larvicidal activity against the third stage larvae of Lucilia sericata ( 41065 ). Theoretically, use of fenugreek along with maggot therapy may decrease the effectiveness of maggot therapy.

#### BLEEDING DISORDERS

Some clinical evidence suggests that maggot therapy may increase the risk of bleeding complications in patients with bleeding disorders ( 27987 , 27988 , 27989 ).

None known.

#### General

The proposed mechanisms of action of maggot therapy for wound debridement include continuous larval motion, secretion of proteolytic enzymes and antibacterial substances, effects on epidermal growth factor and interleukin-6 (IL-6), and ingestion and digestion of bacteria and necrotic tissue ( 29059 , 29060 , 29061 , 29062 , 29063 ). They can also stimulate granulation and accelerate epithelialization ( 108489 ). Maggots consume necrotic tissue, but not viable tissue ( 108492 ). Maggots appear to secrete the following enzymes: aspartyl proteinase, carboxypeptidases A and B, chymotrypsin I, chymotrypsin-like serine proteinase, leucine aminopeptidase, lucifensin, metalloproteinase, trypsin, and chymotrypsin-like serine proteinases ( 29064 , 29065 , 29066 , 29067 , 29068 ). Histidine, valinol, and 3-guanidinopropionic acid have also been identified in maggot excretions ( 29069 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

In vitro, maggot secretions inhibit the production of proinflammatory cytokines while enhancing production of the anti-inflammatory cytokine interleukin (IL)-10 ( 29079 , 29080 ).

#### Antimicrobial effects

In vitro, maggot therapy is effective against various bacteria and yeast species ( 29070 , 29074 , 29075 ). However, other in vitro research suggests that maggot therapy may not have activity against specific bacteria, such as Enterococcus and Escherichia coli ( 29077 ). The key antimicrobial substance isolated from maggot secretions is lucifensin ( 29065 , 29078 ). Preliminary clinical research in patients with diabetic foot ulcers suggests that adding maggot therapy to standard therapy with antibiotics, offloading, and debridement might help eradicate Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus when compared with standard therapy alone ( 101672 ).

#### Wound-healing effects

Protein degrading enzymes secreted from maggots help to break down the protein in fibrin clots, as well as fibronectin, laminin, and acid-solubilized collagen types I and III. The degradation of proteins helps to remove blood proteins and dead cells in a wound ( 29066 , 29068 ). In vitro, products secreted from maggots promote fibroblast movement and reduce adhesion to proteins in the wound, including fibronectin and collagen ( 29086 , 29087 , 29088 , 29089 ). Also, histidine, valinol, and 3-guanidinopropionic acid, isolated from maggot excretions and secretions, promote the proliferation of endothelial cells ( 29069 ). Some human research suggests that maggot therapy improves tissue oxygenation at wound sites ( 27985 ).
