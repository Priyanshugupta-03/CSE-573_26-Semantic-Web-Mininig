## Overview

Music therapy involves the therapeutic use of music by a credentialed music therapist who leads the patient through therapeutic activities with music, including listening, improvising, and performing ( 28892 , 106198 ). Other music interventions, such as music medicine, can involve listening to or making music, and can be offered by healthcare professionals or self-administered ( 106198 , 106218 ). Music interventions are used to influence physical, mental, emotional, cognitive, and social well-being, as well as improve quality of life.

Likely Safe when used appropriately in combination with proven treatments for diagnosed conditions. Music therapy has been used in clinical studies without reports of adverse events ( 28805 , 28812 , 28813 , 28815 , 28816 , 28817 , 28820 , 28821 , 28822 , 28824 , 28839 )( 109082 ).

CHILDREN: Possibly Safe when used appropriately. Music therapy has been used in clinical research without reports of adverse effects ( 98163 ). However, the music should not exceed noise levels of 40-45 decibels. According to the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP), high noise levels might adversely affect newborn growth and development. Some experts also recommend limiting music therapy interventions to no more than 30 minutes in infants due to the potentially harmful effects of overstimulation in this population ( 98169 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no reason to suspect safety concerns when used appropriately.

#### General

Music therapy is well tolerated. Side effects have not been reported in clinical research.

|
Possibly Effective
Anxiety.

Music therapy or music listening interventions seem to reduce feelings of anxiety in a variety of patient populations.

When compared with a waitlist control or usual care, most meta-analyses of clinical research and individual clinical trials show that music therapy or music intervention has a small to medium beneficial effect on anxiety in many different populations, including students and new mothers, patients with eating disorders, cancer, coronary heart disease, dementia, or depression, patients who are in labor, and patients who are mechanically ventilated ( 28823 , 28827 , 28829 , 28831 , 28841 , 98156 , 98157 , 98158 , 98160 , 98161 )( 106202 , 106203 , 106222 , 109084 ). However, a meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research does not support the benefits of music therapy beyond the post-intervention period ( 106202 ). A sub-analysis of research in cancer patients suggests that any benefits of music interventions are limited to music listening in medical environments, especially when used before chemotherapy and not during chemotherapy, as opposed to music interventions organized by therapists ( 109084 ). Music therapy has been shown to decrease physiologic symptoms of anxiety such as heart rate, respiratory rate, and blood pressure in adults ( 28818 , 28821 , 28822 , 106198 ) and in children ( 28811 , 28812 , 28891 , 98156 ). Listening to music seems to reduce anxiety to a similar degree as muscle relaxation ( 28826 , 28829 ).

Autism spectrum disorder.

Music therapy seems to improve social behavior, and possibly other symptoms, in children with autism.

A meta-analysis of mainly small and low- to moderate-quality clinical trials in autistic individuals shows that therapist-administered individual or group music therapy results in small to moderate overall improvements in clinical findings and quality of life and decreases in symptom severity when compared with standard treatment or placebo therapy, such as therapies not involving music or music listening without interacting with a therapist. There was no benefit of music therapy on social interaction, verbal or non-verbal communication, quality of family relationships, identity formation, or adaptive behavior. It is unclear whether any benefits are maintained once treatment has been discontinued ( 109082 ). This meta-analysis is an update of a previous analysis which found that administering music therapy for up to 7 months improves social interaction and verbal and gestural communication skills ( 98163 , 109082 ). Small individual clinical studies have also suggested modest benefit ( 28839 , 104369 ).

Depression.

Music therapy or music listening seems to be beneficial in adults with depression as well as those with depressive symptoms.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in elderly adults shows that adding music therapy to standard treatment reduces depressive symptoms when compared with standard treatment alone ( 98174 ). Another meta-analysis shows that music therapy and music medicine, which involves listening to music in the absence of a therapist, modestly reduce depressive symptoms in a variety of populations, including patients with depression, dementia, or physical illnesses, as well as in relatively healthy populations, when compared with individuals given standard care or not given music therapy ( 106213 ). Further clinical research in older or postmenopausal adults shows that music therapy 1-7 times weekly for 6 or 8 weeks, or music therapy with physical activity for 12 weeks, improves symptoms of depression when compared with no treatment ( 28842 , 106212 , 109073 ). Passively listening to music may also be beneficial. Some preliminary research in depressed females suggests that listening to music reduces depressive symptoms and improves mood when compared to baseline ( 28845 , 28846 ). However, not all research agrees. One meta-analysis of three clinical trials in patients with cancer shows that music intervention does not reduce feelings of depression when compared with standard care ( 109084 ).

Hypertension.

Music interventions seem to modestly reduce blood pressure in adults with hypertension.

A meta-analysis of the available clinical research shows that music interventions reduce systolic blood pressure by 10 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by 6 mmHg in adults with hypertension when compared with breathing exercises or no treatment ( 98167 ).

Insomnia.

Music listening seems to be beneficial for improving sleep quality in children and adults of all ages.

A meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with insomnia shows that listening to pre-recorded music for 25-60 minutes daily, for 3 days to 5 weeks, improves sleep quality when compared with control ( 98166 ). Another meta-analysis in elderly individuals with poor sleep quality shows that listening to music for at least 4 weeks, especially sedative music, improves sleep quality ( 106207 ). One clinical trial shows that a soft music intervention improves sleep quality, efficiency, and latency in elderly Taiwanese adults with difficulty sleeping ( 28888 ). Another clinical trial shows that 45 minutes of music at naptime and bedtime for 3 weeks improves sleep quality, duration, and efficiency in children ( 28889 ). Additionally, some preliminary research suggests that listening to New Age and classical music before bedtime may improve sleep quality in elderly patients with sleep disturbances ( 28890 ).

Pain (chronic).

Music interventions seem to be beneficial for reducing pain in some chronic conditions.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that adding a music intervention to standard therapy modestly reduces pain and anxiety in patients with chronic pain from multiple sclerosis, osteoarthritis, fibromyalgia, cancer, and other conditions when compared with control ( 98162 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies.

Parkinson disease.

Music therapy and music-based movement therapy seem to improve Parkinson disease symptoms.

A meta-analysis of the available clinical research shows that music-based movement therapy training for 6 weeks to 2 years improves balance and disease severity when compared with movement therapy training without music ( 98173 ). Another meta-analysis shows that music-based movement therapy, usually for up to 3 months, modestly improves motor function, balance, walking speed, and overall mental health when compared with usual care, daily activities, or sham exercise. However, there was no effect on gait cadence, stride length, or quality of life; also, the long-term effects of the intervention are not clear ( 106210 ). Additional preliminary clinical research shows that a weekly session of music therapy for up to 13 weeks improves motor symptoms and mood when compared with baseline or physical therapy ( 28869 , 28872 ). Some research has assessed the effects of music interventions on individual symptoms of this condition. One small clinical study shows that using a specific program called Music Therapy Voice Protocol (MTVP) does not improve speech problems when compared to baseline ( 28871 ). Another small clinical study shows that listening to stimulating music improves some precision motor coordination measures, such as aiming and tracking lines, but does not improve steadiness or frequency of tapping, when compared to baseline ( 28870 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Postoperative pain.

Music therapy seems to reduce postoperative pain in children and adults, although its effects in infants are unclear.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that music reduces postoperative pain, anxiety, and use of analgesics in adults when compared to control ( 98165 ). Music therapy or recorded music also seems to reduce postoperative pain, anxiety, and distress in children ( 98170 ). Other meta-analyses in patients undergoing orthopedic surgery shows that music therapy reduces postoperative pain when compared with control ( 104099 , 109072 ). Self-selected music seems to be the most impactful ( 104099 ). The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that there is weak evidence from low-quality research suggesting that music therapy may be beneficial for patients experiencing post-cancer surgery pain. This evidence is based on findings suggesting that music therapy reduces pain and/or analgesic use ( 109161 ). In patients undergoing a cesarian delivery, music listening seems to reduce use of postoperative opioids, but not postoperative pain ( 106208 ). Individual clinical trials also show that music therapy reduces postoperative pain and anxiety and improves coping in adults and children recovering from cardiac surgery or angiography, hernia surgery, gynecological laparoscopy, knee surgery, or abdominal surgery ( 28807 , 28813 , 28814 , 28847 , 28848 , 28851 , 28852 , 28853 , 28854 , 28857 , 28868 , 109078 ). However, music interventions might not help with ACUTE postoperative pain in adults. Some preliminary clinical research shows that playing music as the patients are waking up from anesthesia does not decrease acute postoperative pain when compared to control ( 28855 , 28856 ). Music therapy has not shown benefit in infants. A clinical study in infants undergoing surgery shows that receiving perioperative music therapy does not reduce pain or distress when compared with no intervention ( 104096 ).

Pre-procedural anxiety.

Music therapy and music listening seem to reduce preoperative anxiety.

A meta-analysis of clinical studies shows that listening to music moderately reduces preoperative anxiety when compared with standard care alone ( 98157 ). Individual studies also show that music therapy and music listening interventions decrease anxiety and the need for anxiolytic medications prior to surgery or medical procedures ( 28805 , 28812 , 28813 , 28815 , 28816 , 28817 , 28820 , 28821 , 28822 , 28824 , 28825 , 28828 , 104089 , 104093 ). However, some conflicting evidence exists. Some preliminary clinical research shows that there is no difference between music interventions and control in patients experiencing anxiety before surgery or a medical procedure ( 28806 , 28808 , 28819 , 28860 , 106208 ). The differences in outcomes might be due to a number of factors, including small sample sizes, the degree of anxiety prior to surgery ( 28806 ), the type of music used, whether the patient was able to make their own choice ( 106208 ), and confounding variables such as inconsistent effectiveness of individual participating musical therapists ( 28808 , 28819 ).

Quality of life.

Music therapy and music listening seem to improve quality of life in adults with or without underlying medical conditions.

A meta-analysis of clinical studies evaluating music interventions (e.g., music therapy, music listening, and singing) in various patient populations shows that these music interventions, used alone or added to standard treatments, improve physical and mental quality of life scores when compared with baseline. However, only the changes in mental quality of life were considered clinically relevant. A subgroup analysis in patients with various medical conditions shows that adding music intervention to standard treatment improves mental, but not physical, quality of life when compared with standard treatment alone. The type, duration, or frequency of music intervention did not seem to affect outcomes ( 107804 ). These findings are limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies and patient populations; further research is needed to determine the benefits of music therapy in specific patient groups. A separate meta-analysis of small clinical trials in terminally ill patients shows that adding single or multiple sessions of music therapy to standard palliative care modestly improves quality of life ratings and reduces pain, anxiety, and depression when compared with standard care alone ( 104092 ). Also, a meta-analysis of two clinical trials in patients with cancer shows that music intervention improves quality of life when compared with standard care alone ( 109084 ).

Schizophrenia.

Music therapy seems to improve symptoms in patients with schizophrenia.

Meta-analyses of clinical trials in mostly hospitalized and some ambulatory patients with schizophrenia show that group music therapy, and sometimes individualized music therapy, improve negative and positive symptoms, social functioning, and general mental state for up to 26 weeks when compared with control ( 98164 , 104094 ). Music therapy also seems to help patients with more complex or severe forms of schizophrenia involving psychosis. A clinical study in hospitalized patients with schizophrenia and psychosis shows that music therapy given for up to 12 weeks, in addition to standard care, improves general symptoms, but not positive symptoms, when compared with standard care alone ( 28875 ). Another small clinical trial in hospitalized patients with schizophrenia or schizoaffective psychosis shows that receiving 15 one-hour group music therapy sessions over 4 months improves negative symptoms, quality of life, and social activity when compared with a waitlist control ( 28876 ). Preliminary clinical research shows that 25 weekly sessions of either music therapy with a musical therapist or music listening with a care worker are equally effective for improving negative symptoms when compared with baseline ( 109071 ). The validity of this study is limited by the lack of a comparator group, as well as inadequate power to detect a difference between groups.

Stroke.

Music therapy, especially rhythmic auditory stimulation, seems to improve recovery when used within 10 months of stroke.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients after stroke shows that a music intervention, or rhythmic auditory stimulation, improves gait velocity, stride length, gait speed, and quality of life when compared with control. Music therapy also seems to improve communication in people with aphasia following stroke ( 98168 ). More recent preliminary clinical research in patients who experienced a stroke an average of 8.5 months previously supports these findings when music rhythm is used in conjunction with rehabilitation and walking training three times daily 6 days a week for 4 weeks when compared with rehabilitation and walking training alone. All patients were also given conventional treatments ( 106201 ). However, administering music therapy after more than 10 months have elapsed does not seem to further improve functional mobility. A clinical study in patients that had a stroke 10 months to 5 years ago shows that receiving rhythm and music-based therapy twice weekly for 12 weeks does not improve gait, walking speed, or motor function when compared with continuing regular care ( 104088 ). Other research has evaluated specific forms of music therapy. A meta-analysis of small clinical trials shows that use of melodic intonation therapy for up to 16 weeks post-stroke improves communication, repetition, and naming, but not comprehension, when compared with no music therapy or conventional treatment ( 109080 ).  The long-term effects of this therapy are unclear. Five-element music, a specific type of music therapy practiced as a part of traditional Chinese medicine, has been studied for post-stroke aphasia. A meta-analysis of 6 clinical studies shows that five-element music intervention seems to improve repetition, naming, and spontaneous conversation when compared with using Western music or no intervention ( 104371 ). Since all of the included studies in these meta-analyses used specific types of music, it's unclear if these results can be extrapolated to other types of music.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Age-related cognitive decline.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for age-related cognitive decline.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that music therapy does not seem to improve cognitive function in healthy older adults when compared with control ( 98172 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the variability of administered interventions and the inconsistent use of cognition as a primary outcome. In contrast, preliminary clinical research shows that music therapy with physical activity over a 12-week period modestly improves cognitive function when compared to a waitlist control group ( 106212 ). However, the role of physical therapy in this outcome is unclear.

Alcohol use disorder.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for alcohol use disorder.

A small clinical trial in males with alcohol use disorder given usual treatment shows that using group music therapy in combination with an emotion-regulation module for 5 days a week for 2 weeks does not reduce anxiety or alcohol cravings or improve sleep when compared with usual treatment alone ( 109070 ).

Ankylosing spondylitis.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for ankylosing spondylitis.

In hospitalized patients with ankylosing spondylitis, preliminary clinical research shows that listening to traditional Chinese music over an 8-week period improves overall physical and psychological function when compared with patients who were randomized to routine treatment with or without painting. There were also improvements in mood, cognitive function, and self-esteem ( 106199 ).

Anorexia nervosa.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for anorexia nervosa. Details : Preliminary clinical research shows that post-meal music therapy modestly reduces anxiety in patients with anorexia nervosa when compared to not receiving music therapy ( 106203 ). However, it is unclear if this will result in long-term benefits.

Asthma.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for improving symptoms of asthma in children.

A clinical trial shows that teaching children with asthma simple tunes on a recorder, slide whistle, or melodica to play at home, along with either a single individualized music therapy session or with weekly group sessions, modestly improves lung function and quality of life when compared with standard care alone. Additionally, children receiving group music therapy sessions had reduced hospitalizations when compared with standard care alone ( 104100 ).

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for eczema, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this condition.

Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for ADHD.

A small clinical study shows that improvisational and instructional music therapy sessions, given once weekly for 25-45 minutes for 10 weeks, do not improve impulsivity in children with ADHD when compared with a control group ( 28873 ). However, this study was not adequately powered to detect a difference between groups.

Bipolar disorder.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for bipolar disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this condition.

Bulimia nervosa.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for bulimia nervosa, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this condition.

Burns.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for burn pain in adults or children; the available research is conflicting.

Although some individual research disagrees ( 28858 ), a meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in adult burn patients shows that music therapy or a music medicine intervention modestly reduces pain and anxiety and improves relaxation when compared to not receiving a music intervention ( 109077 ). The effect of music therapy has also been examined in children. One small study in children with burns shows that therapy with a music therapist during dressing changes does not seem to reduce anxiety or pain when compared to participating in verbal interaction ( 28857 ). However, this study was not adequately powered to detect a difference. In contrast, a small crossover study found that music therapy sessions during dressing changes reduced pain reported by children with burns when compared to control ( 28859 ).

Cancer.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for well-being or function in patients with cancer.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients with various types of cancers shows that adding music therapy to standard care seems to improve quality of life, anxiety, and depression when compared with standard care alone ( 104098 ). These findings are limited due to the high heterogeneity of the included studies. In addition, a specific type of music therapy practiced as a part of traditional Chinese medicine, called five-element music therapy, has been studied for cancer. A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical studies shows that five-element music intervention over a period of up to 3 months seems to improve some measures of depression, as well as sleep quality, quality of life, and functional ability, when compared with usual care. However, there was no effect on anxiety symptoms ( 106204 ). Since all of the included studies used a specific type of music, it's unclear if these results can be extrapolated to other types of music.

Cancer-related fatigue.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for cancer-related fatigue.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with hematological cancer shows that music interventions modestly reduce the severity of fatigue when compared to controls receiving standard care without music intervention ( 109081 ).

Cancer-related pain.

Some research suggests that music therapy modestly reduces chronic pain associated with cancer. However, due to the heterogeneity and generally poor quality of available research, it is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for cancer-related pain.

The Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology guideline indicates that there is insufficient evidence for or against the use of music therapy for general pain, palliative or chronic pain, or procedural pain in adult patients with cancer, or for procedural pain in children with cancer. This is due to negative findings and/or methodological flaws associated with the limited available research ( 109161 ). Some of the available small studies suggest potential benefit for cancer-related pain. A meta-analysis of six heterogeneous preliminary clinical trials shows that receiving 1-3 passive listening music therapy sessions for 30-60 minutes modestly reduces cancer-related pain when compared with standard care or just resting ( 104372 ). A very small clinical study in patients with cancer shows that listening to patient-chosen relaxing music twice daily for 3 days reduces pain when compared to baseline, but seems to be no different than listening to a 60-cycle hum sound therapy ( 28861 ).

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial in patients with CVD.

Preliminary clinical research shows that 3 sessions of music therapy over 2 days modestly reduces stress markers and cardiac complications in patients hospitalized for possible myocardial infarction and cardiac procedures when compared with control. Music therapy and relaxation therapy were equally effective in reducing stress markers and complications ( 28878 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for CINV.

A meta-analysis of 2-4 studies shows that music therapy does not reduce the incidence or severity of chemotherapy-induced acute or delayed nausea or acute vomiting. However, music therapy might reduce the severity of delayed vomiting and, using other criteria, music therapy did reduce the incidence of more severe nausea or vomiting. Additionally, one trial included in this meta-analysis shows that music therapy seems to modestly reduce the incidence of anticipatory nausea and vomiting  ( 106219 ). Also, a small clinical trial in cancer patients undergoing a bone marrow transplant shows that providing a music intervention in addition to standard antiemetic therapy reduces nausea and vomiting after chemotherapy when compared with antiemetic therapy alone ( 28880 ). Larger, well-designed studies are needed to confirm these findings.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

It is unclear if music listening is beneficial for COPD.

In patients with COPD, preliminary clinical research shows that music therapy, defined as both passive music listening and a combination of listening and singing, may have some benefit when compared to not listening to music. Music therapy modestly improves dyspnea, anxiety, and both systolic and diastolic blood pressure. However, there was no effect on symptoms of depression or other measures of respiratory function ( 106214 ).

Cognitive function.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for improving cognitive function, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this purpose.

Cystic fibrosis.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for cystic fibrosis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this condition.

Dementia.

Music therapy seems to improve mood and quality of life in patients with dementia. However, it's not clear if it reduces aggression or improves cognition.

Meta-analyses of small, heterogeneous clinical studies in patients with dementia shows that music-based therapeutic interventions improve depressive symptoms, emotional wellbeing, and quality of life when compared with active and inactive controls ( 104097 , 104751 ). Theoretically, music therapy reduces depressive symptoms by decreasing apathy and increasing engagement in patients with dementia ( 28833 ). General music-based therapeutic interventions do not seem to improve aggression or agitation in patients with dementia when compared with control ( 104751 ). However, some small, low-quality clinical studies show that music therapy administered by a trained therapist or playing music preferred by the patient might temporarily reduce agitation, activity disturbance, or hitting behaviors in patients with dementia ( 28834 , 28835 , 28837 , 106226 ). Differences in music therapy interventions and severity of dementia and agitation might have contributed to these differing findings. Research on the use of music therapy for improving cognition in patients with dementia is conflicting. Two meta-analyses of generally small clinical trials shows that most types of music therapy do not seem to improve cognition in patients with dementia ( 104091 , 106226 ). However, the type of music therapy used may impact outcomes. One meta-analysis shows that although passive music therapy does not seem to improve cognition, actively partaking in music therapy might modestly improve cognition when compared with control ( 104091 ). Also, another meta-analysis shows that music therapy has modest short-term effects on cognitive function in patients with dementia ( 106206 ). However, one meta-analysis of mainly interactive music therapies found no benefit when compared with usual care or an active control ( 106226 ). Also, most research shows that music therapy might not improve cognition in the long-term ( 28832 , 28836 , 106206 ).

Diabetes.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for diabetes, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this condition.

Epilepsy.

It is unclear if music listening reduces the incidence of seizures in children or young adults with epilepsy.

A meta-analysis of two small studies in children or young adults with epilepsy shows that listening to music by Mozart over a period of 15 days reduces seizure frequency by approximately 33% when compared with baseline ( 106220 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Infant development.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for infant development.

Preliminary clinical research in hospitalized premature infants shows that singing a lullaby and using multimodal stimulation for 15-30 minutes up to twice weekly until discharge improves weight gain and decreases days to discharge when compared to control ( 28887 ).

Kidney failure.

It is unclear if music listening reduces pain associated with hemodialysis.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that listening to various types of music over a period of 4-24 weeks has a large effect on the reduction of pain associated with hemodialysis when compared with not listening to music ( 106205 ).

Labor pain.

It is unclear if listening to music reduces labor pain.

A meta-analysis of small, heterogeneous clinical trials in primiparous adults shows that listening to music during labor modestly reduces pain and anxiety when compared with no additional intervention ( 104090 ). A second meta-analysis shows that music therapy modestly reduces labor pain during the latent and active phases when compared with standard care or another intervention such as massage ( 106222 ). Studies included in the available meta-analyses are generally small and low quality. The effects of listening to music in combination with other treatments during pregnancy has also been investigated. Preliminary clinical research shows that four sessions of group pregnancy and lactation education between weeks 13-34 of pregnancy, in conjunction with listening to classical music during the session and for 15 minutes each evening, results in moderate-rated labor pain, compared with severe-rated pain in individuals not participating in these sessions ( 109069 ). It is unclear if the benefits are due to listening to music, the education and breathing exercises, the overall in-class education program, or the combination.

Lactation.

It is unclear if music listening is beneficial for improving breast milk production. Details : Preliminary clinical research shows that music therapy involving passive music listening alone or with active listening for 1-14 sessions modestly improves breast milk production when compared to a control group given only standard training ( 106200 ).

Methamphetamine use disorder.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for methamphetamine use disorder.

In patients with methamphetamine use disorder, preliminary clinical research shows that 13 weekly sessions of group music therapy has a modest effect on some, but not all, measures of motivation towards avoiding methamphetamine use when compared with usual treatment alone. Usual treatment involved detoxification, medical care, and therapy. Some benefits lasted at least an additional 3 months, but feelings of anxiety and depression were not improved ( 106223 ).

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for migraine headache.

In adolescents with frequent migraine or tension-type headaches, preliminary clinical research shows that six sessions of music therapy over 8 weeks is no more effective than a control therapy involving a rhythm pedagogic program for headache frequency reduction ( 106221 ).

Motion sickness.

It is unclear if music listening is beneficial for motion sickness.

Preliminary clinical research shows that listening to music perceived as pleasant reduces visually-induced motion sickness when compared to listening to no music or to music perceived as unpleasant ( 106224 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for MS.

A small, low-quality clinical study in patients with MS shows that music therapy might not improve quality of life and depression when compared with matched controls ( 28886 ). This study might have not been adequately powered to detect a difference between groups.

Muscle cramps.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for muscle cramps, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this use.

Muscle strength.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for muscle strength, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this use.

Nausea and vomiting.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for general symptoms of nausea and vomiting, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this use.

Neurodermatitis.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for neurodermatitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this use.

Osteoarthritis.

It is unclear if music listening is beneficial for patients with osteoarthritis.

A small clinical study in elderly patients with osteoarthritis shows that listening to 20 minutes of relaxation music daily for 14 days decreases pain when compared with sitting quietly for 20 minutes ( 28862 ).

Pain (acute).

It is unclear if music listening is beneficial for relieving acute pain; most research suggests no benefit for periprocedural pain.

A small clinical study in patients presenting to the emergency department with minor musculoskeletal trauma shows that adding music distraction to standard care involving ice, elevation, and immobilization does not decrease pain when compared with standard care alone ( 28866 ). Preliminary clinical research shows that listening to music through headphones does not seem to reduce pain associated with cervical biopsy when compared to headphones without music or usual care ( 109076 ). Also, listening to MusiCure music for 30 minutes while waiting for a colonoscopy, as well as during the procedure, does not reduce pain when compared with no music and usual care ( 109074 ). In addition, pain during oocyte retrieval was not reduced when music therapy was used along with conscious sedation when compared with conscious sedation alone ( 109079 ). However, a small clinical study evaluating music listening after surgery suggests possible benefit. In patients that had surgery for leg fractures at least 2 weeks prior, listening to self-selected music for 30-60 minutes daily for 3 days decreases pain when compared with no intervention ( 28864 ).

Perioperative anxiety.

It is unclear if music listening is beneficial for reducing anxiety during cesarian delivery.

Preliminary clinical research shows that listening to music during a cesarian delivery modestly reduces perioperative anxiety when compared to using white noise, no noise, or routine care only ( 106208 ).

Postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV).

It is unclear if music listening is beneficial for reducing PONV.

In patients who underwent laparoscopic cholecystectomy, preliminary clinical research shows that listening to chosen music six times over a 24-hour period modestly reduces the severity of nausea and the incidence of vomiting after each session when compared to not listening to music ( 106225 ).

Postoperative recovery.

It is unclear if music listening is beneficial for improving postoperative recovery.

Preliminary clinical research shows that listening to music might decrease postoperative confusion and delirium in elderly patients undergoing elective hip and knee surgery when compared with control ( 28879 ). In infants undergoing mechanical ventilation after heart surgery, preliminary clinical research shows that listening to music 60 minutes three times daily modestly reduces the need for sedatives, the incidence of delirium, and the duration of mechanical ventilation when compared with routine care alone. However, there was no effect on agitation, success rates of ventilation withdrawal, or length of stay in the ICU ( 106211 ).

Postoperative sleep disturbance.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for reducing postoperative sleep disturbance.

In elderly postoperative patients in the intensive care unit, preliminary clinical research shows that providing interactive music therapy incorporating relaxation techniques for 20 minutes the day after surgery, in addition to listening to relaxing music for 30 minutes before bed, modestly improves subjective sleep quality when compared with passive listening to preselected relaxing music for 30 minutes or routine medical care alone ( 106217 ).

Postpartum depression.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for reducing postpartum depression.

A meta-analysis of small clinical trials shows that music therapy might modestly reduce postpartum depression when compared with standard treatment ( 104370 ).

Pruritus.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for pruritus, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this use.

Psoriasis.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for psoriasis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this condition.

Psychological well-being.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for improving psychological well-being.

Some preliminary clinical research in grieving children with behavioral problems shows that music therapy improves symptoms of grief when compared with social work sessions ( 28882 ), but not when compared with control ( 28883 ).

Radiation-induced fatigue.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for reducing fatigue during radiation treatment.

A clinical study in adults with breast or gynecological cancers shows that receiving 30-40 minutes of music therapy twice weekly, prior to receiving radiotherapy, for a total of 8-12 sessions, reduces fatigue on the Functional Assessment of Cancer Therapy (FACT) scale when compared with receiving radiotherapy alone ( 104087 ).The validity of this study is limited by the large number participants lost to follow-up.

Spinal cord injury.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for spinal cord injury, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this use.

Stress.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for stress.

Meta-analyses of clinical research show that music therapy and/or other music interventions have a medium to large effect on stress reduction when compared with a waitlist control, usual care, or other interventions ( 106198 , 106218 ).

Substance use disorder.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for substance use disorder.

One meta-analysis shows with low to moderate certainty that music therapy conducted by a qualified music therapist, when used in combination with standard care, is modestly more effective than standard care alone for reducing substance craving and favoring motivation for treatment, but does not affect depression, anxiety, treatment retention, or motivation to stay sober. However, this conclusion has low to moderate certainty due to heterogeneity and small number of studies. When compared with an active intervention such as verbal therapy or recreational music, motivation for treatment/change was modestly improved after one month, but there was no clear effect on motivation to stay sober or clean, substance craving, depression, or substance use ( 109083 ). Some research has compared a single session of motivational-educational songwriting with recreational music therapy that involved a social music game. In adults with substance use disorders who were in a detoxification unit, both treatments improved measures of urges, coping, and withdrawal when compared with baseline. However, there was no significant difference between the two forms of intervention ( 109075 ). Substances used included alcohol, cocaine, heroin, and prescription drugs.

Tension headache.

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for tension headaches.

In adolescents with frequent migraine or tension-type headaches, preliminary clinical research shows that six sessions of music therapy over 8 weeks is no more effective than a control therapy involving a rhythm pedagogic program for headache frequency reduction ( 106221 ).

Tinnitus.

Although there has been interest in using music therapy for tinnitus, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of music therapy for this use.

Traumatic brain injury (TBI).

It is unclear if music therapy is beneficial for patients with a TBI.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research in patients with TBI found that use of music therapy modestly improves rehabilitation outcomes, including gait velocity, stride length, and cadence ( 106216 ). More recent preliminary clinical research shows that the self-reported behavior of patients exposed to 60-minute music therapy twice weekly for 3 months was modestly improved when compared with patients on a waitlist. These behavioral changes were maintained for at least an additional 3 months. However, mood and quality of life were not affected and there was no change in caregiver reported behavior ( 106209 ).

More evidence is needed to rate music therapy for these uses.

Music therapy can involve listening to, making, or taking part in activities involving music for therapeutic effects or receiving instruction from a music therapist on using music as an intervention for therapeutic effects. Some experts make a clear distinction between music therapy offered by a therapist, which is often live, and pre-recorded music listening, which can be offered by other professionals or used without support, and is sometimes known as music medicine ( 98164 , 106198 , 106200 , 106213 , 106218 , 109083 ). The music therapist uses music experiences and relationships to promote health in individuals. Music therapy with a therapist can be performed one-on-one or in a group setting ( 98164 , 106198 , 106218 , 109082 , 109083 ). There are many different types of music therapy. Combined music therapy involves the use of music therapy in conjunction with therapeutic procedures such as hypnosis or behavior therapy. Executive music therapy involves playing an instrument or singing, while executive iatromusic therapy involves listening to music played by a musician ( 28892 ). Traditional Chinese medicine uses five-element music therapy which involves only the five tones of Jue, Zhi, Gong, Shang, and Yu ( 106204 ). Sometimes music therapy involves rhythmic auditory stimulation or music-based movement therapy which involves walking or moving to the rhythm ( 106201 , 106210 ). Music therapists can design individualized applications of music using improvisation, receptive listening, song writing, lyric discussion, imagery, performance, and learning through music. Music qualities, including the melody, tempo, and pitch, are used as part of the overall therapy which may include discussion of emotions and experiences. The number and frequency of sessions varies depending on outcome or preferences. The American Music Therapy Association (AMTA) is an organization focused on improving the quality and access to music therapy. The Certification Board for Music Therapists (CBMT) is a national organization providing certification to music therapists. Music therapists can work in private practice and in a variety of health care settings, including psychiatric hospitals, pediatric hospitals, prisons, rehabilitative facilities, medical hospitals, outpatient clinics, day treatment centers, agencies serving developmentally disabled persons, community mental health centers, drug and alcohol programs, senior centers, nursing homes, hospice programs, correctional facilities, and halfway houses ( 28892 , 106198 , 106213 , 106218 , 109075 ).

None known.

None known.

Similar to using verbal language for therapy, music therapy is thought of as a medium for expression and communication. In some patients with serious mental disorders, verbal language alone might not be sufficient as a therapeutic medium for communication. Some research has shown that the earliest communication between parents and infants has many musical qualities. Other research suggests that music therapy can have relationship-building, motivating, and emotionally soothing properties that may help patients that do not receive benefit from verbal therapy ( 98164 ). Research has shown that music therapy can enhance mood, which can lead to reduced anxiety and improvements in various psychological conditions ( 28850 , 28849 , 98164 ). Music therapy might also reduce anxiety and pain because it helps a person to focus attention away from stressful events and toward something pleasant and soothing ( 28860 , 98159 , 106215 ). Some research shows that music interventions have a beneficial effect on physiological changes associated with stress, such as increased heart rate, blood pressure, and levels of stress hormones. It also seems to stimulate the release of oxytocin and endorphins ( 106198 , 106200 , 106214 , 106218 ). Music is also thought to modulate parts of the brain involved in emotion and motivation ( 106198 , 109083 ). Five-element music therapy as part of traditional Chinese medicine uses five music tones with the intention of maintaining qi and promoting the health of internal organs ( 106204 ).
