## Overview

Pulse diagnosis is a technique used in many traditional systems of medicine, including traditional Chinese medicine (TCM), Ayurveda, and Unani medicine. It is often used in conjunction with tongue diagnosis. Pulse readings are conducted in many bodily locations and in multiple combinations, and then correlated to various disease states. Pulse diagnosis is described as a skill that is very difficult to master ( 15913 , 100472 ).

## History

In addition to TCM and Ayurveda, use of pulse diagnosis has also been recorded in Unani (Greco-Persian), Mayan, ancient Greek, and ancient Egyptian medicine. For example, Don Elijio, one of the last Mayan doctor-priests and one of the most respected healers in Central America until his death in 1996, had practiced pulse diagnosis, which had similarities to the TCM pulse diagnosis ( 100472 ). A specific type of pulse diagnosis, the contemporary Chinese pulse diagnosis (CCPD), is based on the traditional method of the Ding medical lineage in Shanghai which dates back to approximately the 15th century. The fundamentals of pulse diagnosis can be traced to the Neijing Suwen. CCPD is considered to have a birthright entitlement and was passed on to John He Feng Shen by Ding Ji Wan, the last inheritor of the Ding-Fei pulse system ( 100473 ).

## People Use This For

Pulse diagnosis is a technique used for disease diagnosis. Pulse diagnosis is also used to monitor and guide treatments.

There is insufficient reliable information about the safety of pulse diagnosis. This is a non-invasive procedure and would not be expected to cause harm; however, this technique should not be relied upon to identify or diagnose medical conditions in place of modern diagnostic techniques.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

No adverse effects have been reported. However, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of pulse diagnosis for identifying or diagnosing medical conditions.

Pulse diagnosis is a technique used in traditional systems of medicine, including traditional Chinese medicine (TCM), Ayurveda, and Unani medicine. It can be used in conjunction with tongue diagnosis. Practitioners of these systems of medicine assess a person's pulse for strength, speed, and other qualities ( 15913 ). The pulse is most commonly taken from the wrist. Each wrist is palpated with one or three fingers at three positions known as inch, bar, and cubit. These positions are palpated with three pressures: superficial, middle, and deep. While palpating, practitioners assess the pulse's rate, strength, length, and width ( 100472 , 100474 ). Traditionally, one finger palpation has been used for vertical dynamic characteristics (Fu, Zhong, and Chen) and horizontal dynamic characteristics (Cun, Guan, and Chi) of the pulse signal. Three finger palpation has been used to assess the pulse's static characteristics ( 100474 ). In some cases, pulse diagnosis is used to guide treatment in real time. For example, in Toyohari Meridian Therapy, a Japanese system of acupuncture, wrist radial pulse diagnosis is used to guide placement of acupuncture needles and determine treatment completion ( 100477 ).

Pulse diagnosis is a technique used in traditional Asian systems of medicine including traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) and traditional Indian medicine (Ayurveda). Practitioners of these systems of medicine assess the pulse for strength, speed, and other qualities. They believe that certain pulse qualities correlate with energy imbalances or humoral imbalances in specific organs or areas of the body. Therapies are often guided based on these pulse qualities ( 15913 ). In theory, because people with conditions such as cardiovascular disease and diabetes can have reduced arterial elasticity and endothelial function, their blood flow is different than that of healthy patients. Therefore, these patients are thought to have different pulse shape, amplitude, and rhythm ( 100478 ). Limited clinical research has shown that it is possible for a technology to differentiate pulse qualities between healthy patients, those with fatty liver disease, and those with cirrhosis ( 100476 ). However, there is no reliable scientific evidence that traditional pulse diagnosis is an accurate indicator of any medical conditions or disease ( 15913 ). Additionally, pulse diagnosis results seem to have a poor reliability and seem to vary from one practitioner to another. One study assessing 18 Traditional Korean Medicine (TKM) clinicians found poor to moderate interobserver reliability in making a pulse diagnosis in stroke patients ( 100475 ).
