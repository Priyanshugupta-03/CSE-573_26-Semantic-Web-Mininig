# Feingold Diet
Other Common Names ADHD Diet, Dieta Feingold, Feingold, Food Elimination Diet, Hyperactivity Diet, Régime contre l'Hyperactivité, Régime de Feingold, Régime Feingold

## Overview

The Feingold diet is a type of elimination diet. It was originally designed based on the theory that certain food ingredients cause medical conditions and behavioral problems. The diet involves the elimination of artificial colors and flavors, as well as food additives such as salicylates. If improvement occurs after these foods are eliminated, then some restricted foods can be reintroduced in moderation ( 15942 , 15944 , 15945 , 15946 ).

## History

The Feingold diet was developed by Benjamin Feingold in 1973.

## People Use This For

The Feingold diet was originally used for attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) in children. It is also used for aggression, asthma, dyslexia, otitis media, nocturnal enuresis, improving cognitive function, nervousness, seizures, sleep disorders, stomach aches, tics, and many other conditions in children.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the Feingold diet. This diet has not been studied to determine long-term safety; however, there are no known safety concerns.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

No adverse effects have been reported. However, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

Some preliminary clinical research suggests that initiating the Feingold diet might reduce subjective evaluations of hyperactivity in children with ADHD ( 15941 ); however, poor study quality limits the reliability of these findings. Additionally, other preliminary research shows no symptoms improvement with the Feingold diet ( 15943 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the Feingold diet for this use.

The Feingold diet typically involves a two-stage plan. The first stage involves eliminating artificial colors and flavors, aspirin-containing products, and foods containing natural salicylates. After 4 to 6 weeks, if an improvement occurs, the patients move on to the second stage. During the second stage, foods are reintroduced one at time, to determine which ones are well tolerated. This stage lasts until it is determined which of the eliminated products can be reintroduced. Salicylate-containing foods that are eliminated may include cherries, cloves, tangerines, berries, coffee, plums, apricots, cucumbers, tea, apples, currants, tomatoes, peaches, oranges, almonds, pears, nectarines, grapes, raisins, grapefruit, green peppers, and prunes. Because many fruits and vegetables are eliminated, the Feingold diet may require temporary supplementation of certain nutrients such as vitamin C. The following additives and ingredients are typically avoided during the Feingold diet: antioxidants, bishydroxyanisoile (BHA), bishydroxytoluene (BHT), benzoates, carminic acid, cochineal, colorings, concentrates, corn syrup, nitrites, sulfur dioxide, and sulfites. The Feingold diet encourages sugar and junk food to be consumed in moderation. However, junk food with many synthetic additives is to be avoided. Certain over-the-counter and prescription drugs, as well as mouthwash, toothpaste, cough drops, and various non-food products may have to be avoided to adhere to the elimination of certain additives. A sample day on the Feingold diet may include: Breakfast : One cup whole grain cereal, ½ cup 2% milk, two slices whole grain toast, two teaspoons butter. Lunch : One cup vegetable soup with barley (without tomato), one jack cheese sandwich on whole wheat bread, one cup milk (2% fat), two oatmeal cookies. Dinner : Three oz. chicken breast, one baked apple, one dinner roll, two teaspoons butter, one cup low-fat milk, ½ cup pudding.

The Feingold diet was originally designed based on the theory that artificial colors and flavors and salicylates cause hyperactivity or attention deficit-hyperactive disorder (ADHD) in children. This theory has now been expanded to include a long list of other conditions linked to these food ingredients such as asthma, infections, seizures, and many others. The Feingold diet is an elimination diet. It seeks to remove foods containing artificial colors or flavors and salicylates. The first step is to eliminate all foods containing these ingredients. If improvement occurs after these foods are eliminated, then some restricted foods can be reintroduced in moderation. Some anecdotal or quasi-experimental evidence suggest that introducing these foods and additives induce symptoms of ADHD ( 15942 , 28997 , 28998 ); however, higher quality research shows that the foods and additives do not induce symptoms of ADHD ( 15944 , 15945 , 15946 ).
