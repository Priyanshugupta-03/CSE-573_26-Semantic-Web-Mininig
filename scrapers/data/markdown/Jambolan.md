## Overview

Jambolan is an evergreen tree growing up to 30 meters in height. It is found in India, Bangladesh, Malaysia, South America, Asia, China, and Australia ( 18 , 13595 , 99063 , 99064 ). Jambolan is used in folk medicine for treating diabetes, and has long been used in Ayurvedic medicine ( 99063 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, jambolan is used for diabetes, flatulence, and gastritis. Jambolan is also used orally for constipation, acute diarrhea, bronchitis, asthma, and pancreatitis. Topically, jambolan is used as a gargle for sore throat, and is applied to the skin for ulcers. Jambolan berries are eaten for food and are made into wines, jam, and juice.

Possibly Safe when used orally and appropriately, short-term. Jambolan tea prepared from 2 grams of jambolan leaves per liter of water has been consumed in place of water with apparent safety in clinical research ( 13092 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

No adverse effects have been reported; however, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Diabetes.

Clinical research shows that drinking jambolan tea 2 grams, prepared with jambolan leaves in one liter of water, does not improve fasting blood glucose in patients with type 2 diabetes when compared with placebo ( 13092 ).

Clinical research in patients with prediabetes and those with newly-diagnosed type 2 diabetes shows that taking a combination product (GlycaCare-II, Sami-Sabinsa Group Ltd.) providing jambolan dried fruit extract 20 mg orally twice daily for 120 days is associated with similar decreases in glycated hemoglobin levels, and fasting and postprandial glucose levels, to metformin orally 500 mg twice daily. The combination product also contains cassia cinnamon bark extract, bitter melon fruit extract, heartwood extract, gymnema leaf extract, and Salacia bark extract ( 112762 ). It is not clear whether the changes are due to jambolan, other ingredients, or the combination. The validity of the results is also reduced by compliance issues and the lack of a per-protocol analysis.

Venous leg ulcers.

Clinical research in patients with venous leg ulcers shows that applying hydrocolloid gel dressings containing an extract of jambolan flowers, seeds, and bark for about 6 months does not improve the overall healing rate or speed of healing when compared with the hydrocolloid dressing alone ( 112763 ).

More evidence is needed to rate jambolan for these uses.

#### ANTIDIABETES DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Animal research shows that jambolan seed and bark extracts can lower blood glucose levels ( 13599 , 13600 , 13601 , 104282 ). Theoretically, jambolan might have additive effects when used with antidiabetes drugs. This might increase the risk of hypoglycemia in some patients. Monitor blood glucose levels closely.

---

#### CYTOCHROME P450 2C9 (CYP2C9) SUBSTRATES
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

There is some in vitro evidence that jambolan can inhibit CYP2C9 ( 99067 ). Theoretically, concomitant use of jambolan with CYP2C9 substrates may increase levels of drugs metabolized by CYP2C9.

---

#### SITAGLIPTIN (Januvia)
Interaction Rating Minor Be watchful with this combination.
Severity MILD Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (In vitro or animal study)

Animal research shows that jambolan seed extract reduces the sitagliptin maximum plasma concentration and area under the curve by 39% and 22%, respectively. However, blood glucose levels were actually reduced to a greater extent in mice taking jambolan and sitagliptin in combination when compared with either product taken alone ( 104282 ). Theoretically, jambolan seed extract might alter the clearance of sitagliptin, although this may not alter the clinical effects of sitagliptin.

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOGLYCEMIC POTENTIAL

Jambolan seed and bark extracts might lower blood glucose levels ( 13599 , 13600 , 13601 ). Theoretically, jambolan might have additive effects when used with other herbs and supplements that also lower glucose levels. This might increase the risk of hypoglycemia in some patients. Some herbs and supplements with hypoglycemic effects include alpha-lipoic acid, bitter melon, carqueja, chromium, devil's claw, fenugreek, garlic, guar gum, horse chestnut, Panax ginseng, psyllium, Siberian ginseng, and others.

#### PERIOPERATIVE

Jambolan seed and bark extracts might affect blood glucose levels ( 13599 , 13600 , 13601 , 104282 ). Theoretically, jambolan might interfere with blood glucose control during and after surgical procedures. Tell patients to discontinue jambolan at least 2 weeks before elective surgical procedures.

None known.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the presentation or treatment of overdose with jambolan.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the pharmacokinetics of jambolan.

#### General

The applicable parts of jambolan are the seeds, leaves, fruit, roots, stems, and bark. Jambolan seeds contain flavonoids, glycosides, triterpenoids, saponins, and tannin constituents, including corilagin, ellagic acid, gallic acid, and galloyl glucose ( 18 , 13595 ). The seeds also contain epicatechin, isoquercetin, caffeic acid, ferulic acid, guaiacol, resorcinol dimethyl ether, lignaglucoside, veratrole, beta-sitosterol, and palmitic acid ( 99063 ). The seed oil contains oleic, myristic, palmitic, linoleic, sterculiac, malvalic, and vernolic acids ( 18 ). Jambolan fruit contains ellagic acid, ellagitannins, polyphenolics, several anthocyanins (malvidin, petunidin, delphinidin, cyanidin, and peonidin). and several triterpenoids (maslinic, ursolic, corosolic, and oleanolic acids) ( 99065 , 99066 ). The fruit is also rich in vitamins and minerals ( 99066 ). Jambolan bark contains tannins, sterols such as beta-sitosterol, triterpenes (betulinic acid, friedelin, eugenin), and flavonoids (myricetin, kaempferol, quercetin, and astragalin) ( 18 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

Preliminary research suggests jambolan bark extract might have anti-inflammatory activity, possibly by inhibiting prostaglandin synthesis. However, it doesn't seem to have ulcerogenic effects ( 13603 ). Some research suggests that tannins extracted from jambolan might have gastroprotective and anti-ulcerogenic effects ( 13606 ).

#### Anti-obesity effects

Research in rats fed a high-carbohydrate, high-fat diet shows that giving jambolan seed powder reduces weight gain, adipose tissue weights, plasma lipid levels, and liver function changes compared with control rats receiving the diet alone. Liver function tests are improved, and inflammatory cell infiltration, fatty droplet deposition, and fibrosis in the liver are prevented ( 99063 ).

#### Anti-oxidant effects

Preliminary research indicates that jambolan seed and fruit pulp extracts have antioxidant effects ( 13604 , 13605 , 99065 ). In obese rats, giving jambolan seed powder for 56 days is associated with increases in antioxidant enzyme activity, including superoxide dismutase, catalase, and glutathione ( 99063 ). Jambolan seed extracts also appear to reduce tissue damage in the brain in experimental models of diabetes, possibly through an antioxidant effect ( 13607 ). Jambolan seed extracts also seem to protect against the effects of exposure to gamma radiation ( 13608 ).

#### Anticancer effects

Jambolan fruit pulp and seed extracts have shown antiproliferative activity against human lung cancer cells in vitro ( 99065 ).

#### Antidiabetic effects

Some animal research indicates that jambolan leaves and fruit do not have antidiabetic activity ( 13092 , 13588 , 13596 , 13597 , 13598 ), while extracts of the seeds and bark do ( 13599 , 13600 , 13601 , 99063 ). However, diabetic mice that received a triterpenoid-enriched extract of jambolan fruit for 10 days had reductions in hyperglycemia, glucose intolerance, and hepatic gluconeogenesis. Jambolan seemed to work by protecting pancreatic islet morphology, preserving pancreatic beta-cell function and improving insulin secretion ( 99066 ). Other preliminary research in animals suggests that jambolan seed might have a lipid-lowering effect in diabetes-related hyperlipidemia ( 13602 ).

#### Antimicrobial effects

Preliminary research suggests jambolan seed extracts might have antibacterial and antifungal activity ( 13609 ). Other preliminary research suggests essential oil in jambolan leaves might also have antibacterial effects ( 13610 ). In vitro, extracts of jambolan bark and leaves have shown virucidal activity against the H5N1 avian influenza virus ( 99064 ).

#### Effects on hepatic enzymes

Inhibition of hepatic cytochrome P450 (CYP) 2C9 enzymes by a jambolan seed extract has been reported in vitro. There was also dose-dependent inhibition of diclofenac metabolism which occurs via CYP2C9 ( 99067 ). The in vitro tests also suggested that jambolan seed extract causes very weak inhibition of CYP3A4 and CYP2D6, which is unlikely to be clinically significant ( 99067 ).
