# Low Purine Diet
Other Common Names Dieta baja en purinas, Gout Diet, Régime faible en purine
|

---

Caution

The low purine diet should not be confused with the Low Oxalate Diet , which reduces intake of compounds called oxalates.

## Overview

Low purine diets involve reducing the intake of foods that are high in purines and increasing the intake of foods that reduce serum uric acid levels ( 10130 , 103126 ). The diet is based on the premise that high levels of uric acid in the blood and joints are due to intake of dietary purines. Purines, including nucleotides, nucleosides, and bases, are found in meat and fish, legumes, and certain vegetables and mushrooms ( 106344 ). In the body, they are broken down to uric acid ( 103130 ). The low purine diet is theorized to be beneficial in individuals with gout and Lesch-Nyhan Syndrome, a rare genetic disorder that results in an overproduction of uric acid ( 103130 ).

Likely Safe when followed appropriately. The low purine diet has been used with apparent safety in clinical studies ( 103127 , 103131 ). Additionally, there is no reason to expect safety issues.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the low purine diet in pregnancy and lactation. However, there is no reason to expect safety issues as long as nutritional needs are met.

#### General

The low purine diet seems to be generally well tolerated. No adverse effects have been reported in clinical studies.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Gout.

It is unclear if the low purine diet is beneficial for gout symptom management.

The low purine diet has been evaluated for use in the management of gout. However, the available studies are small and low-quality ( 103127 , 103131 ). Although general population research in adults of European ancestry without gout suggests that the diet has a negligible effect on serum urate levels ( 103126 ), population research in Chinese adults has found that intake of purine-rich meat and legumes, mainly soy, is associated with an increased risk of hyperuricemia. However, there was no association with the intake of purine-rich fungi or vegetables ( 106344 ). Whether the association is due to dietary purine or other compounds in animal flesh and soy is not clear.  Similarly, observational research has found that the number of alcoholic beverages consumed per week is not associated with serum uric acid levels nor the risk of developing gout ( 110739 ). Due to the lack of available information, guidelines on the use of the low purine diet for gout are conflicting. The 2017 American College of Physicians guideline states that gout-specific counseling about dietary changes, including low purine recommendations, is no more effective than general dietary counseling to promote weight loss and reduce alcohol intake ( 103129 ). However, the 2017 British Society for Rheumatology guideline recommends that all overweight patients with gout should be counseled on dietary and lifestyle modifications, including a well-balanced diet, reduced intake of alcoholic drinks and high-purine foods, and increased intake of skimmed milk, low-fat yogurt, soybeans, and vegetable sources of protein ( 103128 ). A small retrospective study in adults with gout who were undergoing bariatric surgery shows that following either a low purine diet or a normal purine diet resulted in a significant reduction in serum urate levels and gouty attacks after surgery. However, those in the low purine group were able to discontinue allopurinol and experienced no gouty attacks 12 months after surgery ( 103127 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the small size and retrospective nature of the study. A small clinical study in adults with hyperuricemia shows that following a low purine and low oxalate diet leads to similar reductions in uric acid levels as taking allopurinol 150-300 mg daily, either alone or in conjunction with a low purine diet ( 103131 ). It is unclear if this serum uric acid reduction would be clinically beneficial in patients with gout.

Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis).

Although there is interest in using the low purine diet for the prevention of kidney stones, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the low purine diet for this condition.

Overall mortality.

It is unclear if the low purine diet reduces the risk of overall mortality.

While there is interest in the low purine diet to reduce mortality, observational research in adults living in China has found that higher dietary purine consumption is associated with lower mortality. Analysis by source of purine (i.e. animal, plant) provided similar results. However, subgroup analysis found that higher purine intake is not associated with lower mortality in females ( 110740 ).  It is unclear if these results are due to increased dietary protein or other confounders. Furthermore, it is unclear if these results can be extrapolated to geographic locations other than China.

More evidence is needed to rate the low purine diet for these uses.

The low purine diet encourages a reduction in foods that are high in purines, such as meat, seafood, alcohol, sugary soft drinks, and others. It also encourages increased intake of foods that can reduce serum uric acid levels, such as eggs, peanuts, skim milk, cheese, brown bread, margarine, and non-citrus fruits. Most experts recommend consuming at least 50% of calories from carbohydrates, by eating six to ten servings of bread or starchy foods, and five fruits and vegetables each day ( 103130 , 103131 ).

None known.

None known.

Uric acid is the waste product left over from the metabolism of chemical compounds called purines. It is hypothesized that increased uric acid levels may be caused by increased ingestion of purines ( 103130 ). Gout is caused by deposition of urate crystals in the joints. Urate crystals form and accumulate in body areas where blood flow is too slow to remove them. The knuckles, elbows, knees, and toes are common places of accumulation. If found between the joints, the surrounding tissue becomes inflamed. Then a nerve ending may become irritated, and extreme pain may result. If the crystals remain in the kidneys, kidney damage may occur ( 103130 ). However, clinical research on the use of the low purine diet is conflicting and of poor quality. Additionally, observational research suggests that dietary purine intake may not have a significant effect on serum urate levels. A diet-wide association study of over 16,000 adults of European ancestry found that beer, liquor, wine, potato, poultry, soft drinks, and meat (beef, pork, and lamb) were associated with increased serum urate levels. Eggs, peanuts, cold cereal, skim milk, cheese, brown bread, margarine, and non-citrus fruits were associated with reduced serum urate levels. However, dietary consumption only explained less than 0.3% of variance in serum urate. In contrast, about 24% of variance in serum urate levels was explained by genomic variation ( 103126 ).
