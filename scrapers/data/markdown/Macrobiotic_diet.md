# Macrobiotic diet
Other Common Names Dieta macrobiótica, Macrobiotics, Macrobiotism, Ma-Pi 2 Diet, Régime macrobiotique, Zen Macrobiotics
|

---

## Overview

The macrobiotic diet is a dietary approach that stresses vegetarianism and consumption of whole, healthy foods ( 98873 ). Some versions of the macrobiotic diet include dairy, fish, and occasional meat consumption to reduce the risk of vitamin deficiency. The macrobiotic diet has been traditionally used for its anti-inflammatory properties.

Possibly Safe when used appropriately along with adequate supplementation or dietary modifications to ensure that nutritional needs are met. Strict adherence to the macrobiotic diet has been associated with increased risk of calcium, vitamin D, iron, vitamin B2, and vitamin B12 deficiencies. However, the risk of these deficiencies can be reduced by including foods such as dairy, fish, or occasional meat, or by taking appropriate nutritional supplementation, to ensure proper daily nutrition ( 27949 , 27950 , 27951 , 27952 , 27953 , 27954 , 98873 ).

Possibly Unsafe when used without adequate supplementation or dietary modifications to ensure that nutritional needs are met. Strict adherence to the macrobiotic diet has been associated with increased risk of calcium, vitamin D, iron, vitamin B2, and vitamin B12 deficiencies ( 27949 , 27950 , 27951 , 27952 , 27953 , 27954 , 98873 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACATATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid use.

CHILDREN: POSSIBLY UNSAFE ...when a strict macrobiotic diet is used in infants or children. Nutritional deficiencies have been reported in children following a strict macrobiotic diet ( 27951 , 27954 , 27955 , 27956 ). Some epidemiological research has also found that a strict macrobiotic diet is associated with lower intelligence and reduced height for age compared to an omnivorous (normal) diet in children. This might be due to nutrient deficiencies caused by this diet ( 27948 , 27952 ). There is insufficient available information available about the safety of a less strict macrobiotic diet in infants, children, or adolescents.

#### General

The macrobiotic diet is generally well tolerated when used with adequate supplementation or dietary modifications to ensure that nutritional needs are met.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Increased risk of rickets, osteoporosis, and deficiencies in calcium, iron, vitamin B2, vitamin B12, and vitamin D. In children, following a strict macrobiotic diet has been associated with reduced height for age and worsened cognitive function, likely due to the associated nutrient deficiencies.

#### Hematologic

The macrobiotic diet has been associated with deficiencies in calcium, iron, vitamin B2, vitamin B12, and vitamin D ( 27949 , 27951 , 27952 , 27954 , 27957 , 98873 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

The macrobiotic diet has been associated with increased risk of rickets and osteoporosis, likely due to calcium and vitamin D deficiency ( 27949 , 27954 , 27957 , 98873 ). Following a strict macrobiotic diet that does not include additional intake of fish and fat has also been associated with reduced height for age in children less than 8-years-old ( 27952 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Following a strict macrobiotic diet has been associated with worsened cognitive function in children. Epidemiological research found that consuming a strict macrobiotic diet is associated with lower psychological and intelligence test scores compared to an omnivorous (normal) diet in children. Increased occurrence of vitamin B12 deficiency in children following the macrobiotic diet might be the reason for the worsened cognitive function ( 27948 ). However, other epidemiologic evidence did not find that following a macrobiotic diet delays cognitive development in children ( 27947 ). The discrepancy in results might be due to the nutritional variation within the different macrobiotic diets.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Biliary disorders.

Although there has been interest in using the macrobiotic diet for gallstones, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the macrobiotic diet for this purpose.

Cancer.

Although there has been interest in using the macrobiotic diet for various cancers, including colon cancer, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the macrobiotic diet for these conditions.

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

Although there has been interest in using the macrobiotic diet for CVD, including coronary heart disease, hyperlipidemia, and hypertension, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the macrobiotic diet for these conditions.

Diabetes.

It is unclear if the macrobiotic diet is beneficial in patients with diabetes.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that following the calorie-restricted Ma-Pi 2 macrobiotic diet for 21 days modestly reduces fasting blood glucose when compared with baseline ( 98874 ). Furthermore, a small clinical study in patients with well-controlled diabetes shows that following the Ma-Pi 2 macrobiotic diet for 6 months slightly reduces glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels when compared with following a control diet, but the magnitude of reduction is not clinically meaningful ( 98876 ). A post-hoc analysis of the 21-day Ma-Pi 2 macrobiotic diet study in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that weight loss induced by the diet is associated with increased sclerostin levels and a trend towards increased bone turnover when compared with the control diet, suggesting that the Ma-Pi 2 diet might worsen bone health ( 95733 ). It is unclear if the Ma-Pi 2 macrobiotic diet is safe when used long-term.

Endometriosis.

Although there has been interest in using the macrobiotic diet for endometriosis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the macrobiotic diet for this purpose.

Hyperlipidemia.

It is unclear if the macrobiotic diet is beneficial in patients with hyperlipidemia.

In otherwise healthy males, epidemiological research has found that following a macrobiotic diet is associated with lower total cholesterol levels and an improved ratio of high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol to total cholesterol when compared with following vegetarian and non-vegetarian diets ( 27945 ). It is unclear if a macrobiotic diet can lower cholesterol levels in patients with hyperlipidemia.

Menopausal symptoms.

Although there is interest in using the macrobiotic diet for menopausal symptoms, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the macrobiotic diet for this purpose.

Obesity.

Although there has been interest in using the macrobiotic diet for obesity, including metabolic disorders, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the macrobiotic diet for these conditions.

More evidence is needed to rate the macrobiotic diet for these uses.

Generally, the macrobiotic diet stresses that 40% to 60% of daily calories should be consumed from vegetables and whole grains, with animal products consumed only in small amounts on a monthly basis. In more restrictive macrobiotic diets, animal products are not consumed at all. The macrobiotic diet recommends avoidance of foods from the nightshade family, such as potatoes and tomatoes, and avoidance of or limited meat and dairy consumption ( 98873 ). Compared to the typical American diet as seen from the 2009-2010 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES), the macrobiotic diet includes a lower amount of energy from fat and higher total dietary fiber. The macrobiotic diet also meets or exceeds the recommended daily allowance (RDAs) for most nutrients, except calcium, vitamin D, and vitamin B12 ( 98873 ). The Ma-Pi 2 diet is a specific type of vegan macrobiotic diet designed by Mario Pianesi. This diet focuses on consumption of whole grains, vegetables, and legumes. It also involves consumption of fermented products and seaweeds, and the main liquid in this diet is roasted green tea, or Beicha tea. This diet is approximately 12% protein, 18% fat, and 70% carbohydrate and it usually involves a level of calorie restriction ( 98874 ).

The macrobiotic diet gained popularity among cancer patients due to case reports in which individuals attributed their recovery from cancers with poor prognoses to the diet. Some experts attribute the anticancer effects of the macrobiotic diet to its anti-inflammatory activity. The macrobiotic diet appears to have a lower dietary inflammatory index (DII) compared to the typical American diet, as described by the 2009-2010 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) ( 98873 ). However, there is no reliable evidence that the macrobiotic diet helps prevent or cure cancer. There has been interesting in using the macrobiotic diet for diabetes. Some experts believe that gut microbiome dysbioses contributes to insulin resistance in patients with diabetes. Preliminary clinical research in overweight patients with diabetes suggests that following the Ma-Pi 2 macrobiotic diet for 21 days helps modulate gut microbiome dysbioses. At the end of the study, there was an increase in the levels of Faecalibacterium, Roseburia, Lachnospira, Bacteroides and Akkermansia bacteria found in the stool. These bacteria produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) such as acetic, propionic, and butyric acids, which are thought to improve metabolic regulation in diabetes. There was also a reduced amount of  pro-inflammatory bacteria such as Collinsella and Streptococcus found in the stool of the overweight patients. Some experts interpreted this to mean that the Ma-Pi 2 macrobiotic diet has anti-inflammatory effects that may benefit patients with diabetes ( 98872 ).
