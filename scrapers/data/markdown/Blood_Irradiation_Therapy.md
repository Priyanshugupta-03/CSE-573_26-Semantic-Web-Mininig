## Overview

Blood irradiation therapy is a clinical procedure during which a patient's blood is exposed to laser or ultraviolet light. The treatment may be administered in a vein via a catheter, through the blood vessels of the nose, or through the skin. The therapy is believed to have originated in Asia, and the majority of research has been conducted in Russia ( 27851 ).

## History

A report on the development of blood irradiation therapy was published in 1948 ( 27497 ). In 1981, the use of intravenous laser blood irradiation therapy was reported by Meshalkin, a researcher in Russia ( 27498 , 27499 ). In 1986, German researcher Karandashov and colleagues were the first to apply blue light for blood irradiation in patients with hypertension and angina pectoris ( 27498 , 27500 ). Blood irradiation therapy was government certified in 2005 in Germany, resulting in its use throughout Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Italy, and Australia over the next few years ( 27851 ).

## People Use This For

Intravenously, blood irradiation therapy is used for atopic disease, angina, asthma, biliary disorders, burns, wound healing, bursitis, cancer, cellulitis, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), coronary heart disease (CHD), diabetes, diabetic foot ulcers, encephalopathy, athletic performance, hyperlipidemia, hemorrhagic shock, infections, kidney disorders, osteomyelitis, pain, pancreatitis, peptic ulcers, peripheral arterial disease (PAD), periodontitis, peritonitis, pneumonia, postoperative side effects, miscarriage, radiation sickness, rheumatoid arthritis (RA), schizophrenia, sepsis, thrombophlebitis, trauma, tuberculosis, and typhoid fever. Intranasally, blood irradiation therapy is used as an anticoagulant. Transcutaneously, blood irradiation therapy is used for arrhythmia, coronary heart disease (CHD), diabetes, hypertension, pancreatitis, postoperative side effects, and vascular diseases.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of blood irradiation therapy.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

There is currently a limited amount of information on the adverse effects of blood irradiation therapy. Case reports suggest that it may cause hypotension, urticaria, hypoglycemia, nasal bleeding, rigor, and bronchospasm ( 27858 ).

#### Cardiovascular

Case reports suggest that blood irradiation therapy may cause hypotension ( 27858 ).

#### Dermatologic

One case report suggests that blood irradiation therapy may cause urticaria ( 27858 ).

#### Endocrine

One case report suggests that blood irradiation therapy may cause hypoglycemia ( 27858 ). Preliminary clinical research shows that intravascular blood irradiation therapy reduces blood glucose levels in some people ( 27857 ).

#### Hematologic

Case reports suggest that blood irradiation therapy may cause nasal bleeding ( 27858 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

Case reports suggest that blood irradiation therapy may cause rigor ( 27858 ). Preliminary clinical research suggests that intravascular blood irradiation therapy may exacerbate the inflammatory process in some patients with rheumatoid arthritis ( 27867 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

One case report suggests that blood irradiation therapy may cause bronchospasm ( 27858 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Angina.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with angina shows that blood irradiation therapy might reduce ventricular premature beats and enhance the antianginal and anti-ischemic effects of conventional medications, when compared with conventional medications alone ( 27854 , 27855 ).

Asthma.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with mild, moderate, or severe asthma shows that intravascular blood irradiation therapy with the He-Ne laser, 8-10 sessions lasting 30 minutes each, along with conventional medications, might improve asthma symptoms and reduce the need for corticosteroids, when compared with baseline ( 27852 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a control group.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

Preliminary clinical research shows that intravascular blood irradiation therapy might reduce fever, cough, erythrocyte sedimentation rates, microbial growth in sputum, and lung rale sounds, and improve forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV1), when compared with a control in patients with COPD ( 27866 ).

Diabetes.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with type 2 diabetes shows that intravascular blood irradiation therapy might decrease blood glucose levels when compared with baseline ( 27857 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a control group.

Peptic ulcers.

Preliminary clinical research shows that intravenous blood irradiation therapy combined with Actovegin, a calf blood extract that is illegal in the U.S. and Canada, might reduce pain and inflammation and improve the composition of gastric mucosa in patients with gastroduodenal ulcers ( 27868 ). Additional clinical research shows that intravascular blood irradiation therapy appears to reduce the time to recurrence in patients with peptic ulcers being treated with antacids, cholinolytics, and antioxidant agents ( 27869 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of control groups.

Pneumonia.

Preliminary clinical research shows that intravascular blood irradiation therapy might enhance drug therapy, reduce disease severity, and decrease the duration of disease and hospital stay in some patients with acute pneumonia ( 27864 ).

Postoperative recovery.

Preliminary clinical research shows that the use of intravascular blood irradiation therapy might reduce thrombotic and hemorrhagic complications in patients with benign prostatic hyperplasia undergoing adenoma removal, when compared with a control group not receiving irradiation ( 27861 ). Additional clinical research shows that intravascular blood irradiation therapy helps maintain adequate perfusion and tissue oxygenation and reduces stress reactions in patients undergoing surgical treatment of esophageal, gastric, and intestinal diseases, when compared with a control group not receiving irradiation ( 27862 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Preliminary clinical research shows that daily intravascular blood irradiation therapy might improve symptoms in patients with RA, when compared with less frequent irradiation. However, in some patients, blood irradiation therapy may actually exacerbate the inflammatory process of RA. Women, patients with low titers of rheumatoid factor, and those in the early stages of the disease appear to achieve the greatest clinical benefit ( 27867 ).

Tuberculosis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that extracorporeal ultraviolet blood irradiation therapy might decrease the mycobacterial load in patients with tuberculosis and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. Clinical symptoms and forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV1) also appear to improve when compared with baseline in these patients ( 27865 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a control group.

More evidence is needed to rate blood irradiation therapy for these uses.

There are various forms of blood irradiation therapy. Regardless of the specific technique used, experts recommend continued patient observation and consumption of oral carbohydrates for 1.5-2 hours following irradiation sessions. Patients are also instructed to avoid intramuscular injections for 1.5 hours after the session to reduce the risk of hematoma ( 27858 ). Transcutaneous blood irradiation : High-power laser light is applied to healthy, unbroken skin, on areas containing large numbers of blood vessels. The advantages of transcutaneous blood irradiation include patient preference and the lack of tissue or skin penetration. Disadvantages include irradiation of nearby tissue, such as muscles, nerves, and skin, and the need for higher irradiation power ( 27498 ). Transcutaneous blood irradiation therapy uses infrared laser irradiation of the skin, typically at a wavelength of 830 nm, with an output of 40 mW and a frequency of 5 Hz. The procedure is typically conducted daily or every other day for 6-10 sessions ( 27870 ). Intranasal blood irradiation : A small light diode is placed into the nasal cavity for the irradiation of the nasal cavity walls. The light diode is usually a low-intensity red color laser or normal red light. The nasal cavity contains a large number of microvascular blood vessels. Intravenous (intravascular) laser blood irradiation : Intravenous blood irradiation typically involves feeding a low-power laser light (632.8 nm of a helium-neon laser) into the forearm vein. A typical energy output is 1-4 mW, with an exposure 10-60 minutes daily or on alternate days, for 3-10 sessions ( 27498 , 27870 ). Ultraviolet blood irradiation : Approximately 200 mL of blood is withdrawn from a vein, and subjected to ultraviolet irradiation outside of the body in a quartz cuvette. The blood is then reinfused into the vein. For treatment of acute poisoning, one study suggests the use of ultraviolet irradiation of 180-6000 mL blood with a MD73M "Izolda" apparatus and a UV irradiation dose of 100-150 J ( 27871 ).

#### ANTICOAGULANT/ANTIPLATELET DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Preliminary clinical research suggests that blood irradiation therapy might reduce platelet aggregation. Case reports also suggest that blood irradiation therapy may be associated with an increased risk of nasal bleeding ( 27858 , 27874 , 27876 ). Theoretically, blood irradiation therapy might increase the risk of bleeding when used concomitantly with anticoagulant/antiplatelet drugs.

Some of these drugs include aspirin, clopidogrel (Plavix), nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as diclofenac (Voltaren, Cataflam, others), ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin, others), naproxen (Anaprox, Naprosyn, others), dalteparin (Fragmin), enoxaparin (Lovenox), heparin, warfarin (Coumadin), and others.

---

#### ANTIDIABETES DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity MODERATE Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Preliminary clinical research and one case report suggest that blood irradiation therapy may lower blood glucose levels ( 27857 , 27858 ). Theoretically, concomitant use with antidiabetes drugs might affect glucose control and increase the risk of hypoglycemia; use with caution. Dose adjustments to diabetes medications might be necessary.

Some antidiabetes drugs include glimepiride (Amaryl), glyburide (DiaBeta, Glynase PresTab, Micronase), insulin, metformin (Glucophage), pioglitazone (Actos), rosiglitazone (Avandia), and others.

#### IMMUNOSUPPRESSANTS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Clinical research suggests that blood irradiation therapy may stimulate immune function in some patients ( 27864 , 27896 , 27897 , 27898 , 27899 , 27900 , 27901 ). Theoretically, concurrent use of blood irradiation therapy might interfere with immunosuppressive therapy.

Immunosuppressant drugs include azathioprine (Imuran), basiliximab (Simulect), cyclosporine (Neoral, Sandimmune), daclizumab (Zenapax), muromonab-CD3 (OKT3, Orthoclone OKT3), mycophenolate (CellCept), tacrolimus (FK506, Prograf), sirolimus (Rapamune), prednisone (Deltasone, Orasone), and other corticosteroids (glucocorticoids).

#### ANTICOAGULANT/ANTIPLATELET HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS

Preliminary clinical research suggests that blood irradiation therapy might reduce platelet aggregation. Case reports also suggest that blood irradiation therapy may be associated with an increased risk of nasal bleeding ( 27858 , 27874 , 27876 ). Theoretically, concomitant use of blood irradiation therapy with herbs and supplements that affect platelet aggregation could increase the risk of bleeding in some people. Some of these herbs include angelica, clove, danshen, garlic, ginger, ginkgo, Panax ginseng, and others.

---

#### HERBS AND SUPPLEMENTS WITH HYPOGLYCEMIC POTENTIAL

Preliminary clinical research and one case report suggest that blood irradiation therapy may lower blood glucose levels ( 27857 , 27858 ). Theoretically, blood irradiation therapy might have additive effects with herbs that decrease blood glucose levels. Herbs with hypoglycemic potential include alpha-lipoic acid, devil's claw, fenugreek, garlic, guar gum, horse chestnut, Panax ginseng, psyllium, and Siberian ginseng.

|

#### AUTOIMMUNE DISEASES

Clinical research suggests that blood irradiation therapy may stimulate immune function in some patients ( 27864 , 27896 , 27897 , 27898 , 27899 , 27900 , 27901 ). Theoretically, blood irradiation therapy might exacerbate autoimmune diseases by stimulating disease activity. Advise patients with autoimmune diseases such as multiple sclerosis (MS), systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), rheumatoid arthritis (RA), or others to avoid or use blood irradiation therapy with caution.

#### BLEEDING DISORDERS

Preliminary clinical research suggests that blood irradiation therapy might reduce platelet aggregation. Case reports also suggest that blood irradiation therapy may be associated with an increased risk of nasal bleeding ( 27858 , 27874 , 27876 ). Theoretically, blood irradiation therapy may increase the risk of bleeding when used by patients with bleeding disorders.

#### DIABETES

Preliminary clinical research and one case report suggest that blood irradiation therapy may lower blood glucose levels ( 27857 , 27858 ). Use with caution in patients with diabetes.

#### SURGERY

Blood irradiation therapy may reduce platelet aggregation, increase bleeding risk, and lower blood glucose levels ( 27857 , 27858 , 27874 , 27876 ). Theoretically, blood irradiation therapy might increase bleeding risk and interfere with blood glucose control during and after surgery. Tell patients to discontinue blood irradiation therapy sessions at least 2 weeks before elective surgical procedures.

None known.

Blood irradiation has been proposed for augmenting various bodily processes.

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

Preliminary clinical research suggests that intravascular blood irradiation has anti-inflammatory effects ( 27876 ).

#### Antiasthmatic effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that intravascular blood irradiation results in formation of active oxygen forms in asthma patients with low baseline blood chemiluminescence ( 27853 ).

#### Anticoagulant effects

Clinical research suggests that intravascular blood irradiation therapy, alone or as part of a multimodal treatment, normalizes blood coagulation and increases fibrinolytic potential ( 27859 , 27872 , 27873 , 27877 ). Some clinical research suggests that blood irradiation decreases platelet aggregation and hyperactivation of plasmin ( 27874 ). Other clinical research suggests that, in pregnant women, low-energy laser intranasal irradiation of the blood improves plasma prothrombin time, activated partial thromboplastin time, thrombin time, and fibrinogen levels ( 27875 ). Additional clinical research suggests that intravascular blood irradiation has antiaggregatory effects ( 27876 ).

#### Antidiabetic effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that intravascular laser blood irradiation decreases blood levels of glucose, glucose-6-phosphate, dehydroascorbic acid, R-3-hydroxybutyric acid, L-histidine, and L-alanine, and increases blood levels of L-arginine ( 27857 ).

#### Antineoplastic effects

Animal research suggests that intravascular laser irradiation of blood might reduce tumor growth and inhibit metastasis ( 27879 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

In patients with acute pneumonia, ultraviolet blood irradiation appears to stabilize lipid peroxidation ( 27881 ). Preliminary clinical research suggests that intravascular laser irradiation reduces malondialdehyde levels and increases total antioxidant capacity in adults with spinal cord injury ( 27882 ). Other clinical research also shows that blood irradiation has antioxidant effects ( 27851 , 27876 , 27880 , 27883 ). Animal research suggests that ultraviolet blood irradiation and oxygenation reduces damage due to free radicals. Blood irradiation therapy appears to reduce malondialdehyde levels and increase the activity of superoxide dismutase (SOD) and glutathione peroxidase (GSH-PX) ( 27884 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that intravascular laser irradiation improves myocardial contractility ( 27876 ). Additional clinical research shows that laser blood irradiation promotes a normalization of myocardium cell membrane barrier and receptor function in patients with tachyarrhythmias refractory to antiarrhythmic agents ( 27885 ). Clinical research also shows that intravenous laser blood irradiation decreases Willebrand's factor activity and normalizes the level of nitric oxide (NO) in hypertensive patients ( 27886 ). In patients with peritonitis, clinical research shows that ultraviolet blood irradiation increases pulse and tissue blood flow but does not affect blood pressure ( 27887 ). Animal research shows that laser blood irradiation alters heart rate variability ( 27888 ). Additional animal research shows that intravascular laser blood irradiation may stabilize erythrocyte membranes and improve myocardial function following resuscitation after hemorrhagic shock ( 27890 ). In animals, blood irradiation therapy might also improve cardiovascular measures such as oxygen transport, blood flow, and tissue perfusion, ultimately resulting in increased survival ( 27889 ).

#### Gastric effects

Clinical research in patients with peptic ulcers suggests that intravascular blood irradiation therapy appears to improve cellular and humoral immunity, lipid peroxidation, resistance of gastric and duodenal mucosa, and time to scarring of the ulcer defect in patients being treated with antacids, cholinolytics, and antioxidant agents ( 27869 ).

#### Hematological effects

Preliminary clinical research in patients with peritonitis shows that intravascular laser irradiation improves the morphological characteristics of red cells and peripheral blood, increasing the number of intermediate red cells and decreasing the number of pre-hemolytic and degenerating forms of red cells ( 27891 ). Clinical research in patients with pancreatitis suggests that intravascular laser irradiation increases the recovery of lymphocyte activity as well as the ATP content of erythrocytes ( 27892 ). Intravascular laser blood irradiation might also improve blood viscosity and increase collateral blood flow ( 27872 ).

#### Immunological effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that ultraviolet blood irradiation in patients with asthma normalizes IgG levels, and improves T cell counts and activity ( 27896 ). Additional clinical research shows that intravascular laser irradiation of blood increases IgG, but not IgM, autoantibodies to DNA in some patients ( 27897 ). Intravascular laser irradiation might also increase CD3, CD4, and CD4/CD8 counts ( 27898 ). In patients with acute pneumonias, autotransfusion of laser-irradiated blood may help normalize T-lymphocyte activity ( 27864 ). Clinical research also suggests that intravenous blood irradiation normalizes the proportion of T-helper cells, as well as neutrophil phagocytosis ( 27900 ). Other clinical research shows improved leukocyte count and activity and increased immunoglobulin levels with blood irradiation therapy ( 27883 , 27901 ).

#### Lipid lowering effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that intravascular laser irradiation of the blood reduces LDL cholesterol and increases HDL cholesterol in adult patients with spinal cord injuries ( 27882 ).
