## Overview

Rolfing is a form of deep tissue massage, developed by a biochemist, Ida Rolf, PhD in the 1950s. The massage is applied using a slow moving pressure with knees, elbows, thumbs, fingers, and knuckles ( 15984 , 15985 ). Practitioners of Rolfing can receive training; however, there are no generally accepted standards or licensure.

## People Use This For

Rolfing is used for back pain, chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), pain, cerebral palsy, relaxation, stress, anxiety, headache, neck pain, osteoarthritis, carpal tunnel syndrome, and many other conditions.

Possibly Safe when Rolfing is used appropriately. Preliminary clinical evidence shows that Rolfing is generally well-tolerated ( 15986 , 88735 , 88736 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Rolfing seems to be well tolerated when used appropriately ( 15986 , 88735 , 88736 ).

#### Genitourinary

Displacement of a ureteral stent, implanted for ureteral stenosis and calculi, has occurred while receiving deep massage using the Rolfing method, Symptoms resolved in the 51-year-old female when the stent was restored to the original position. The stent was of a self-retaining coiled design to avoid spontaneous migration which occurred with earlier stent types ( 110687 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Anxiety.

Preliminary clinical research shows that Rolfing might decrease anxiety in college students when used for one-hour, twice weekly for five weeks ( 88735 ). The effect of Rolfing in patients with diagnosed anxiety disorders is unknown.

Cerebral palsy.

In a preliminary case series, Rolfing produced some improvements to movement and functionality in mildly and moderately impaired patients; however, it did not improve severely impaired cerebral palsy patients ( 15986 ).

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

Preliminary retrospective data suggests that Rolfing might improve functional status, posture, breathing, and sleeping patterns in some patients with chronic fatigue syndrome ( 88736 ).

Range of motion.

A retrospective review of 383 patients who had undergone ten Rolfing sessions for shoulder or hip problems shows that Rolfing improves joint flexion, and internal and external rotation when compared with baseline ( 110688 ). Whether these improvements are maintained in the long-term is unknown.

More evidence is needed to rate Rolfing for these uses.
