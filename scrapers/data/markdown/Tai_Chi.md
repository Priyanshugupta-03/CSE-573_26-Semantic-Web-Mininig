# Tai Chi
Other Common Names Art Martial Interne, Internal Martial Art, Méditation en Mouvement, Moving Meditation, Tai Chi Chih, Tai Chi Chuan, Tai Chi Martial Arts, Tai Ji Quan, Tai-Chi, Taichí, Taichi Quan, Taiji, Taijiquan, Tie Chee
|

---

Caution

Tai Chi should not be confused with Qi Gong , of which it is one specific type.

## Overview

Tai chi is one specific type of Qi gong that was originally developed in China as a martial art for self-defense ( 15872 , 96397 ). Tai chi involves controlled breathing and slow, rhythmic circular body movements thought to facilitate the flow of internal energy or 'qi' ( 96395 , 96397 ). Different styles of tai chi include Chen, Yang, Wu, and Sun, and the postures, order of movements, focus, pace, and knee angles are specific to each style ( 96397 ). In North America, practitioners are not considered health professionals; however, in China, tai chi is often practiced along with conventional, modern medicine ( 15908 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Tai chi has been used safely in clinical trials for up to at least one year without reports of serious adverse outcomes ( 15857 , 15868 , 88903 , 88913 , 88922 , 88925 , 88926 , 88928 , 88940 , 93899 , 93901 , 96397 , 96398 , 96400 , 98000 , 102241 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available. Advise patients to consult with their personal healthcare provider before initiating tai chi or any other exercise program during pregnancy or lactation.

#### General

Tai chi is well tolerated. No serious adverse effects have been reported in clinical research.

#### Musculoskeletal

Tai chi has been associated with joint pain and muscle pain ( 113436 ).

#### Other

In a meta-analysis of the available research, there was no difference in frequency of adverse events between tai chi groups and inactive controls ( 102241 ).

Possibly Effective
Athletic performance.

Practicing tai chi improves aerobic exercise capacity, with the greatest benefits noted in initially sedentary, older adults.

A large meta-analysis of observational studies shows that practicing tai chi has a large effect on improving aerobic exercise capacity, and the effect is most pronounced in initially sedentary, older adults. A pooled analysis of experimental studies shows that effects on aerobic exercise capacity were less robust when compared with observational studies, in those participating in other physical activity, and in younger patients ( 88906 ).

Back pain.

Practicing tai chi reduces pain in patients with chronic low back pain.

A network meta-analysis of 2 low-quality clinical studies in patients with chronic low back pain shows that practicing tai chi reduces back pain when compared with no intervention or conventional rehabilitation ( 114862 ).  Another meta-analysis of clinical research shows that practicing tai chi reduces chronic low back pain by a small to moderate amount when compared with no treatment or wait-list control ( 96392 ). Small, low-quality clinical studies in adults with low back pain also show that practicing tai chi for 40-60 minutes 2-5 times weekly for a duration of 6 weeks to 6 months can modestly reduce the intensity of low back pain when compared with control, backward walking, or jogging and similarly to swimming ( 95773 , 113438 ). Guidelines from the American College of Physicians published in 2017 recommend tai chi as a first-line, nonpharmacological treatment for chronic low back pain ( 95773 ). Tai chi has not been evaluated for acute or radicular back pain ( 95773 ).

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi improves 6-minute walking distance and pulmonary function in patients with COPD.

Clinical research in patients with COPD shows that practicing tai chi, alone or along with usual care, daily for 4-52 weeks increases 6-minute walking distance by about 20-30 meters and modestly improves pulmonary function when compared with usual care or no exercise ( 96397 , 99523 , 107776 ). However, tai chi does not seem to consistently improve quality of life, symptoms, or other functional status measures when compared with usual care or experimental control (e.g., breathing exercises, education) ( 96397 , 99523 , 107775 , 107776 ).

Fall prevention.

Practicing tai chi seems to reduce fall risk in some elderly adults, especially when performed more frequently and with the use of certain styles (i.e., Yang-style tai chi or a style specifically tailored for fall prevention).

Some clinical research shows that practicing tai chi can improve balance, stability, and reduce the risk and/or rate of falls in elderly adults ( 18206 , 88907 , 88942 , 88948 , 93900 , 93910 , 96393 , 102246 , 113438 ); however, other clinical research shows no benefit ( 88886 , 88908 , 88921 , 88944 ). Similarly, analyses of clinical research evaluating the effects of tai chi on fall prevention show conflicting results. Meta-analyses show that tai chi modestly reduces the risk of falls in elderly adults ( 93898 , 93904 , 99515 ). However, the effect of tai chi on the rate of falls is inconsistent, with one analysis suggesting that tai chi moderately reduces the rate of falls when compared with other exercises, but not when compared with non-exercise ( 17137 ), while another analysis suggests that tai chi does not reduce the rate of falling in elderly adults when compared with control ( 93904 ). The best evidence to date comes from an analysis of 18 clinical studies that included over 3800 elderly adults, which suggests that practicing tai chi reduces the risk of falls by about 20%. Approximately 10 elderly adults need to practice tai chi to prevent one fall. The effect of tai chi on the risk of falls appears to vary depending on the type of tai chi and exercise frequency. Results from this analysis also suggest that tai chi reduces the rate of falls in elderly adults by about 30%, with a greater benefit seen for those practicing Yang style tai chi when compared with Sun style tai chi and for those practicing more than three times weekly when compared with only once weekly ( 94161 ). A more recent meta-analysis also confirms that tai chi reduces the number of elderly patients experiencing falls and the rate of falls by about 20% ( 99515 ). Results also suggest that tai chi reduces the rate of falls more significantly in elderly adults when follow-up is limited to only 1-3 months when compared with a longer follow-up of 6-12 months. This may indicate that any benefit of tai chi may wane over time ( 94161 ). Tai chi has also been evaluated in elderly adults at high risk of falling. One meta-analysis of 18 clinical studies shows that tai chi reduces the RATE of falling more significantly in elderly adults at low risk of falling when compared with those at high risk. This may indicate that tai chi is less beneficial in patients at high risk of falling. However, tai chi appears to reduce the RISK of falling similarly in both groups of patients ( 94161 ). Furthermore, a large clinical study of over 1100 community-dwelling adults at high risk of falling shows that tai chi tailored for fall prevention (Tai Ji Quan Moving for Better Balance), 60 minutes twice weekly for 6 months, reduces the rate of falling by 58% when compared with stretching, and by 31% when compared with a multimodal exercise program consisting of aerobic, strength, balance, and flexibility exercises ( 98000 ).

Fibromyalgia.

Practicing tai chi improves symptoms of fibromyalgia similarly to other forms of exercise. However, the optimal duration of tai chi is unclear.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients with fibromyalgia shows that practicing tai chi modestly improves pain, sleep quality, fatigue, depression, and quality of life when compared with standard care. However, the overall impact of tai chi on fibromyalgia is unclear, as studies with durations of 12-16 weeks demonstrated greater benefit than those with durations of 24-32 weeks ( 102240 ). A small clinical study in patients with fibromyalgia suggests that practicing tai chi for 1 hour twice weekly for 10 sessions improves symptoms and quality of life when compared with baseline ( 88897 ). The absence of a control group and the high number of dropouts limits the interpretation of these findings. Limited research has compared tai chi to other forms of exercise. One small clinical study in patients with fibromyalgia shows that practicing tai chi once or twice weekly for 12 or 24 weeks seems to be as effective as exercise for improving function and pain. Although there was no difference between those practicing tai chi once or twice weekly, practicing tai chi for 24 weeks improved symptoms to a greater degree when compared with practicing for only 12 weeks ( 99518 ). This study was limited by imbalanced enrollment in the intervention and control groups.

Heart failure.

Practicing tai chi improves exercise capacity, symptoms, and left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) in patients with heart failure.

Several meta-analyses of clinical studies in patients with heart failure show that practicing tai chi 2-7 times weekly for 4-24 weeks improves 6-minute walking distance by approximately 51 meters when compared with control ( 96391 , 106505 ). These meta-analyses, a subgroup analysis of an additional small meta-analysis, and additional preliminary clinical studies in this population also show that practicing tai chi has a benefit on quality of life, depressive symptoms, and symptoms such as shortness of breath, peripheral edema, and fatigue ( 88895 , 88896 , 96391 , 106505 , 106506 ). Practicing tai chi also appears to improve LVEF by a small to moderate amount when compared with not practicing tai chi ( 96391 ).

Hypertension.

Practicing tai chi reduces blood pressure similarly to aerobic exercise.

Individual clinical studies and meta-analyses of clinical studies in patients with hypertension suggest that practicing tai chi for 6 weeks to 18 months reduces systolic blood pressure by about 6-18 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure by about 2-11 mmHg when compared with baseline or no exercise. However, when compared with a group practicing an aerobic exercise, both interventions appear to decrease blood pressure equally ( 69505 , 88910 , 88911 , 96394 , 107764 , 107765 , 107766 , 107767 ).

Insomnia.

Practicing tai chi seems to improve sleep quality and sleep efficiency and reduce wake time after sleep onset in older patients with chronic insomnia and adults with sleep complaints.

A large clinical study in older adults with chronic insomnia who are not regular exercisers shows that practicing tai chi for 60 minutes three times weekly for 12 weeks improves sleep efficiency by about 3%, reduces wake time after sleep onset by 13 minutes, and reduces the number of nightly awakenings by 2, but has no effect on total sleep or awake time, when compared with no intervention. Effects with tai chi are similar to those with other exercise interventions, with some benefits persisting for up to 2 years post-intervention ( 107773 ). Similarly, a meta-analysis of small clinical studies in older patients with sleep complaints, with or without insomnia, shows that practicing tai chi 1-5 times weekly for 8-25 weeks improves subjective sleep quality, as measured by Global Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) scores, by 2 points when compared with no intervention and by 1 point when compared with active controls such as exercise and health education ( 107772 ). The validity of these findings may be affected by potential publication bias. Preliminary clinical research in healthy older adults with moderate sleep complaints that are below the threshold for diagnosis of insomnia suggests that practicing tai chi for 40-60 minutes three times weekly for up to 24 weeks can improve some measures of sleep, such as sleep quality, efficiency, and duration, when compared with health education sessions or light exercise control ( 17138 , 88890 ). Similarly, two meta-analyses, including many of the same clinical studies, in healthy adults or those with insomnia show that practicing tai chi for 30-120 minutes up to 7 times weekly for up to 60 weeks has a moderate effect on improving subjective sleep quality, efficiency, and duration, and also reduces sleep latency, daytime dysfunction, and the use of hypnotic drugs when compared with non-tai chi control (e.g., acupuncture, psychotherapy, education, general exercise, routine daily activity, usual care, wait list) ( 107758 , 113439 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies and challenges with blinding due to the nature of the intervention. Additionally, most study participants were Asian, limiting the generalizability of the results. Tai chi has also been evaluated for sleep disturbances in patients with cancer. A meta-analysis of 2 clinical studies in adults with a history of cancer shows that practicing tai chi for 7 to 24 weeks improves sleep quality when compared with control ( 114867 ).

Obesity.

Practicing tai chi reduces waist circumference and body weight in patients with obesity to a similar extent as conventional exercise.

A large clinical study in middle-aged or older adults with central obesity shows that practicing tai chi for 60 minutes three times weekly for 12 weeks reduces waist circumference by 1.8 cm and body weight by 0.2 kg, when compared with no intervention. Effects of tai chi are similar to those seen with other exercise interventions, with reductions increasing to 4.3 cm and 0.7 kg, respectively, at 38 weeks post-intervention ( 107774 ). A meta-analysis of preliminary low- to moderate-quality clinical research in patients with overweight or obesity shows that practicing tai chi 1-3 times weekly for 10-12 weeks reduces body mass index (BMI) by approximately 1.65 kg/m 2 when compared with other types of exercise ( 96394 ). Additional low-quality research in patients with obesity shows that practicing tai chi for 30-40 minutes at least three times weekly for 2 years reduces BMI by 2.31 kg/m 2 when compared with no intervention ( 102248 ). The validity of this study was limited by the lack of an active control group.

Osteoarthritis.

Practicing tai chi seems to improve function and disability and reduce pain and stiffness in patients with knee and hip osteoarthritis.

Research in adults with knee and hip osteoarthritis shows that practicing tai chi 1-3 times weekly for 6-24 weeks can improve physical function and stiffness and reduce pain when compared with baseline and control groups ( 88913 , 88914 , 88915 , 88916 , 88917 , 88918 , 88919 , 88920 , 107779 ). Also, several meta-analyses of clinical research in patients with osteoarthritis, mainly of the knee, shows that practicing tai chi 1-5 times weekly for 8-52 weeks can moderately reduce pain and stiffness and improve physical function and disability when compared with not practicing tai chi ( 96392 , 107780 ). However, many of these studies are small and could not adequately blind the participants due to the nature of the intervention, limiting the validity of these findings. The American College of Rheumatology (ACR) and the Osteoarthritis Research Society International (OARSI) recommend the use of tai chi in patients with knee and hip osteoarthritis ( 102114 , 102143 ).

Physical performance.

Practicing tai chi improves self-reported physical function in elderly patients, particularly in those with low physical function at baseline. Effects on objective measures of physical function are mixed.

Several clinical studies in inactive, elderly patients show that practicing tai chi twice weekly for 6 months improves self-reported physical function and self-efficacy when compared with waitlist control ( 88878 , 88879 , 88880 ). The effect of tai chi appears to be greatest in patients with low physical function at baseline ( 88878 ). Other clinical research involving transitionally frail elderly females shows that practicing tai chi twice weekly for 48 weeks can improve self-perceived ambulatory status when compared with wellness care ( 88877 ). Other clinical research in elderly patients has assessed the effects of tai chi on objective measures of physical function. One clinical study in sedentary, elderly females shows that practicing tai chi three times weekly for 12 weeks can improve leg strength, balance, and flexibility better than brisk walking ( 88881 ). Another preliminary clinical study in elderly patients in long-term care facilities shows that practicing tai chi three times weekly for 6 months can improve hand grip strength and lower body flexibility when compared with baseline ( 88884 ). Clinical research in elderly fallers transitioning to frailty also shows that practicing tai chi for 48 weeks can improve balance and leg strength when compared with wellness education ( 88882 ). However, not all research has shown benefit. One preliminary clinical study in healthy elderly adults shows that practicing tai chi three times weekly for 8 weeks does not significantly improve flexibility or balance ( 88888 ). Another clinical study in elderly patients also shows that practicing tai chi three times weekly for 12 months does not improve muscle strength, balance, or flexibility when compared with waitlist control ( 88934 ). Reasons for these conflicting results are not entirely clear. It is possible that the lack of benefit in these latter studies relates to inadequate power to detect between-group differences. It is also possible that the patients in these latter studies had high physical function at baseline and were thus less likely to see benefit from practicing tai chi.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Age-related cognitive decline.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi may improve cognitive function, learning, and memory in elderly patients with age-related cognitive decline.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in elderly patients with cognitive decline shows that practicing tai chi 1-4 times weekly for 10 weeks to 1 year improves cognitive function when compared with no treatment. The improvement with tai chi is slightly greater than that of other active interventions such as exercise or cognitive behavioral therapy ( 96400 ). However, many of the studies included in this analysis were of low methodological quality. Another small meta-analysis of clinical studies in older patients shows that practicing tai chi 5 times weekly for 12 weeks improves learning and memory by 23 points, as measured by the Wechsler Memory Scale (WMS), when compared with no intervention or activities of daily living, although it does not improve executive function ( 107770 ). A small meta-analysis of low-quality clinical studies in elderly patients with cognitive decline shows that practicing tai chi for 6 weeks to 1 year improves mini-mental state examination (MMSE) scores by 1.5, Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) scores by 3.5, and Clinical Dementia Rating (CDR) scores by less than 1 when compared with control ( 107768 ). However, the improvement in these scores may not be clinically important. The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies and unclear reporting.

Anxiety.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with anxiety.

A network meta-analysis of mostly low-quality clinical studies in older adults shows that practicing tai chi reduces anxiety symptoms compared with control. Yang-style tai chi appears to be the most effective form of tai chi for anxiety symptoms, followed by 24-form Simplified tai chi ( 116223 ).

Asthma.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in children with persistent asthma.

A small clinical study in children aged 6-12 years with persistent asthma shows that practicing tai chi chuan for 60 minutes once weekly for 12 weeks reduces exacerbations when compared with no intervention. Tai chi also seems to improve quality of life and asthma control when compared with baseline ( 102245 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Autism spectrum disorder.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in children with autism spectrum disorder.

A small clinical study in children 6-12 years old with autism spectrum disorder shows that practicing tai chi chuan for 60 minutes three times weekly for 6 weeks improves balance and movement coordination when compared with no intervention ( 102247 ). Another small clinical study in this same population shows that practicing tai chi chuan for 45 minutes three times weekly for 12 weeks reduces stereotyped behaviors at 3 days, but not 1 month, post-intervention when compared with baseline ( 107781 ). The validity of these findings is limited by a lack of blinding and a lack of statistical comparison to an unspecified control group.

Breast cancer.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi can improve general well-being or quality of life in patients with breast cancer or in patients who have survived breast cancer.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with breast cancer shows that practicing tai chi for 1 hour three times weekly for 12 weeks improves self-esteem when compared with baseline ( 88891 ). Tai chi has also been shown to decrease fatigue, improve flexibility, and improve general quality of life in patients with breast cancer and patients who have survived breast cancer when compared with baseline ( 88892 , 88893 , 88894 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of comparator groups. Small meta-analyses of clinical studies of variable quality in this same population show that practicing tai chi improves quality of life and reduces depressive symptoms, but not fatigue or sleep quality, when compared with conventional supportive care or non-exercise therapy ( 107761 , 107762 , 114860 ). When used as an adjunct to conventional therapy, practicing tai chi improves fatigue and quality of life when compared with conventional therapy alone ( 107761 ). The effects of tai chi on strength and aerobic capacity have been mixed ( 88893 , 88894 ).

Cancer-related fatigue.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi may improve short-term cancer-related fatigue in patients with breast and lung cancer.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with breast and lung cancer shows that practicing tai chi for more than 8 weeks improves cancer-related fatigue in the short-term, but not in the long-term, when compared with control. Tai chi seems to provide more benefit when compared with physical exercise and psychological support ( 99517 ).

Cognitive function.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi may improve executive function, learning, and memory in adults without cognitive impairment.

A small meta-analysis of clinical studies in healthy college students shows that practicing tai chi 3-5 times weekly for 6-10 weeks improves some executive functions, including inhibitory function and conversion function, when compared with no treatment. Refresh function, an executive function, and cognitive function are not improved ( 107770 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of some of the included studies. A meta-analysis of clinical research in elderly adults without cognitive impairment shows that practicing tai chi 1-4 times weekly for 10 weeks to 1 year might improve learning and memory when compared with control or no treatment. Tai chi can also improve executive function by a small to moderate amount in these patients when compared with no treatment. However, the improvement in executive function with tai chi appears to be similar to that achieved with other exercise interventions, such as walking ( 96400 ). Many of the studies included in this analysis were of low methodological quality.

Cognitive impairment.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in middle-aged or older adults with cognitive impairment.

A meta-analysis of clinical trials in older adults with mild cognitive impairment shows that practicing tai chi improves some assessments of cognitive function, including executive function and global cognition, when compared with control. However, tai chi does not appear to improve other aspects of cognition, such as working memory or memory complaints, in this population. Most studies used Yang-style tai chi practiced 1-5 times weekly for 8-52 weeks, with 120 minutes per week being the most common frequency ( 116222 ). The validity of these findings is limited by a high risk of bias and small sample sizes in the included studies  A small meta-analysis of clinical studies in middle-aged and elderly patients with cognitive impairment shows that practicing tai chi 3-5 times weekly for 12-40 weeks improves MMSE score by 2-4 points, Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) score by 3 points , and executive functioning as measured by a reduction of 14 seconds on the Trail Making Test Part B, when compared with no intervention or activities of daily living ( 107770 ). However, the improvement in these scores may not be clinically important, and the validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies. However, other clinical research in older, community-dwelling adults with mild cognitive impairment shows that practicing Peking Yang-style tai chi consisting of 24 movements twice weekly for 16 weeks does not improve the ability to perform activities of daily living or cognition as measured on the mini-mental state examination (MMSE) when compared with no additional treatment ( 99516 ).

Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with a history of CABG surgery.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with a history of CABG surgery shows that practicing tai chi up to 5 times weekly for 1 year may improve exercise capacity when compared with a control group ( 88898 ).

Coronary heart disease (CHD).

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi may modestly improve aerobic endurance in patients with CHD.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with CHD shows that practicing tai chi modestly improves aerobic endurance when compared with both active and non-active control interventions ( 99513 ).

Dementia.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in adults with, or at risk of developing, dementia.

A meta-analysis of heterogenous, low-quality clinical studies in adults with dementia, mild cognitive impairment, or at risk for dementia shows that practicing tai chi for 15-90 minutes 1-3 times weekly for 7-40 weeks modestly improves cognitive function, as measured by the Mini-mental State Examination (MMSE), when compared with control ( 113431 ).

Depression.

Clinical research suggests that tai chi may help reduce depressive symptoms across different age groups.

A network meta-analysis of mostly low-quality clinical studies in older adults shows that tai chi reduces depressive symptoms compared with control. When comparing different styles, specialized tai chi exercise programs showed the greatest benefit, followed by Yang-style tai chi ( 116223 ). A meta-analysis of small, mostly low-quality clinical studies in adolescents and adults with depressive symptoms shows that practicing tai chi for 8 to 24 weeks reduces depressive symptoms when compared with control groups receiving antidepressants or those receiving no other intervention ( 113433 ). Similarly, a meta-analysis of small, mostly low-quality studies shows that middle-aged and older adults practicing tai chi 32-7 times per week for 10-60 minutes for up to 6 months improves depressive symptoms compared with control, especially 24-style tai chi practiced for 24 weeks or longer ( 116227 ). However, a meta-analysis of 4 clinical trials in patients with depression found no reduction in depressive symptoms between tai chi and control interventions. The certainty of evidence was very low due to high risk of bias, inconsistency between studies, and heterogeneity in the interventions used ( 114859 ).  A network meta-analysis of 3 moderate quality clinical trials in patients with breast cancer suggests that tai chi was among the most effective mind-body exercises for reducing depression symptoms compared with control ( 114860 ). A preliminary clinical study in older adults with major depressive disorder who are only partial responders to escitalopram shows that adjunctive tai chi practice 2 hours weekly for 10 weeks modestly improves symptoms when compared with adjunctive health education ( 96396 ). However, a larger follow-on clinical study in this same population has found no difference in depression severity with adjunctive tai chi practice when compared with adjunctive health education ( 107757 ). Another preliminary clinical study in patients with major depressive disorder shows that practicing tai chi for 36 sessions over 3 months improves symptoms when compared with waitlist control. However, the benefits of tai chi in this latter study may have been related to social support that is associated with tai chi rather than tai chi itself ( 88901 ).

Diabetes.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in adults with type 2 diabetes; the available research is conflicting.

Meta-analyses of clinical research in patients with type 2 diabetes show that practicing tai chi, either along with other exercises or in addition to standard diabetes care, can modestly reduce fasting blood glucose when compared with control. However, it is unclear whether the reduction is clinically significant, and tai chi does not appear to lower levels of fasting glucose more than other types of gentle exercise, including walking and dancing. Additionally, the included studies are small and low-quality, and standard diabetes care is inadequately defined ( 99510 , 99520 , 107778 , 113435 , 113437 , 114871 , 116228 ). Evidence for the effect of tai chi on glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels is mixed, with some meta-analyses showing a reduction of up to about 1% when compared with no treatment, gentle exercise, or standard diabetes care ( 99510 , 99520 , 107778 , 113437 , 114871 , 116228 ), while other studies show no benefit ( 88903 , 88905 , 96395 , 106504 ). Tai chi also does not appear to improve insulin sensitivity in these patients ( 88903 ). Studies included in the meta-analysis were small and included different tai chi styles such as Yang Style, Chen-style, Lin-style, Da Yuan Jiang Tang-style, and Tai Chi Ball ( 99520 ). For patients with type 2 diabetes, the optimal effects of Tai Chi for improving glucose management seem to be with use of 24-style Tai Chi; additionally the greatest effects are observed with practice more than 7 times per week and practice for more than 240 minutes per week ( 116228 ). The effects of tai chi on other outcomes in patients with type 2 diabetes has also been evaluated. Meta-analyses show that in adults with type 2 diabetes, practicing tai chi might modestly improve body mass index, quality of life, and some lipid parameters including high-density lipoprotein cholesterol, and triglycerides but not low-density lipoprotein cholesterol and total cholesterol when compared with various controls ( 107777 , 107778 , 113437 , 114781 , 116228 ).  However, many of these measures are not improved when compared with other forms of exercise and some results are conflicting ( 107777 , 107778 , 114871 ).

Frailty.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with frailty.

A meta-analysis of mostly low-quality clinical studies in older adults with sarcopenia or frailty shows that practicing tai chi for 30-90 minutes 2-7 times weekly for 8-48 weeks improves some measures of physical performance as shown by improvement in the 30-second chair stand test (30CST) and the Timed up and go test (TUGT) and also reduces the number of falls and fear of falling when compared with control. However, no improvement was observed in balance, muscle mass, grip strength, or other measures of muscle performance when compared with control. Subgroup analysis suggests that practicing tai chi is beneficial for patients with frailty when compared with a non-exercise control, but is similarly effective when compared to an exercise control ( 113428 ).

HIV/AIDS.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi may improve quality of life and anxiety in patients with HIV/AIDS.

Some preliminary clinical research in patients with HIV/AIDS suggests that practicing tai chi twice weekly for 8 weeks or once weekly for 10 weeks may improve quality of life measures and anxiety when compared with control ( 88924 , 88929 ).

Hyperlipidemia.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with hyperlipidemia.

A small clinical study in middle-aged and older adults with hyperlipidemia and hypertension shows that practicing Wu-style tai chi three times weekly for 6 weeks improves low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol levels by about 17 mg/dL, but does not improve total cholesterol, high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, or triglyceride levels, when compared with practicing a simplified form of tai chi ( 107767 ).

Lung cancer.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with lung cancer.

A clinical trial in patients with advanced lung cancer shows that practicing tai chi for 60 minutes twice weekly for 16 weeks improves sleep quality, compared with usual care or aerobic exercise. Tai chi did not significantly improve other measures of sleep, such as duration or  efficiency, compared to usual care or aerobic exercise ( 116225 ). A secondary analysis of this same trial shows that practicing tai chi improves cancer-related dyspnea compared with usual care but not compared with aerobic exercise. The validity of these effects is limited by a lack of blinding.

Metabolic syndrome.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with metabolic syndrome.

A small clinical study in patients with metabolic syndrome shows that attending a tai chi class for 1 hour twice weekly and practicing tai chi for 30 minutes three times weekly for 12 weeks seems to lower systolic blood pressure when compared with no intervention. However, it does not seem to affect diastolic blood pressure, glycated hemoglobin, or lipid levels ( 102243 ). This study was limited by a large dropout rate and insufficient power to detect smaller differences between groups.

Methamphetamine use disorder.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with methamphetamine use disorder.

A small clinical study in females with methamphetamine use disorder shows that practicing tai chi three times weekly for 12 weeks improves inhibitory control, but not working memory accuracy, working memory response time, or cognitive flexibility response time, when compared with control ( 107771 ).

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi prevents migraine headache.

A small clinical study in adults with episodic migraine headache 15 days or less per month shows that practicing Yang-style tai chi for one hour daily 5 times weekly reduces frequency of migraine by 2 attacks per month and number of migraine days by 1.5 days per month at 24 weeks when compared with control. However, practicing tai chi does not seem to reduce migraine intensity or duration when compared with control ( 113436 ).

Myocardial infarction (MI).

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with MI.

A meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with MI shows that practicing tai chi at least 3-5 times weekly for 12-48 weeks has a large effect on 6-minute walking distance, especially when the Chen style is practiced for at least 6 months, when compared with no or low-intensity physical activity. Practicing tai chi also has a large effect on left ventricular ejection fraction, especially when practiced for at least 6 months, when compared with no or low-intensity physical activity. However, there was no effect on activities of daily living ( 107755 ).

Osteoporosis.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with osteoporosis.

A meta-analysis in adults with or without metabolic bone disease shows that practicing tai chi modestly increases bone mineral density (BMD) by 0.16 g/cm 2 when compared with conventional treatment, and by 0.05 g/cm 2 when compared with no treatment ( 99521 ). However, another meta-analysis shows that practicing tai chi does not improve BMD in postmenopausal adults ( 100341 ). These differing results might be due to differences in patient populations and testing parameters. It is not yet known if tai chi prevents osteoporotic fractures.

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if tai chi is beneficial in patients with chronic pain.

A network meta-analysis of clinical research in older adults with chronic pain shows that tai chi alleviates pain when compared with non-exercise-based interventions, but not more than traditional physical exercise or other mind-body exercises ( 114878 ).

Parkinson disease.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi may improve motor symptoms in some patients with Parkinson disease.

A meta-analysis of 7 clinical studies in patients with mild to moderate Parkinson disease shows that practicing tai chi along with taking antiparkinsonian medications improves motor score on the Unified Parkinson Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS) by approximately 4.3 points when compared with taking medication alone ( 96398 ). However, controversy exists as to whether the minimal clinically important change in UPDRS motor score is 2.5 points or 5 points ( 95686 ). A small clinical study in this same population shows that adding tai chi practice to routine exercise does not improve motor symptoms, mobility, or balance when compared with routine exercise alone ( 102250 ). However, when compared with other exercise or no exercise, some preliminary clinical research in this same population shows that practicing tai chi can improve balance and mobility and might reduce the risk of falls by up to 67% ( 18206 , 88923 , 96398 ). Other meta-analyses have combined results from studies of patients with Parkinson disease, without specifying the severity of disease or current treatments. One of these meta-analyses shows that practicing tai chi 2-5 times weekly for 8-24 weeks modestly increases gait velocity and improves balance by 3 and 5 points as measured by the Berg Balance Scale (BBS) and the Activities-Specific Balance Confidence scale, respectively, when compared with usual care or route exercise control, although it does not improve quality of life ( 107769 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies and unclear reporting. A meta-analysis of 16 primarily low- and moderate-quality systematic reviews and meta-analyses of clinical studies in patients with Parkinson disease shows that practicing tai chi has a moderate effect on balance and functional mobility when compared with no intervention and a moderate effect on balance alone when compared with different interventions (e.g., resistance training, stretching, walking exercises). Tai chi also has a small effect on falls when compared with no intervention or different interventions ( 107756 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the high heterogeneity of the included studies and the inherent flaws of conducting a meta-analysis of systematic reviews and meta-analyses.

Postoperative recovery.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients recovering from total knee arthroplasty.

A small clinical study in patients with osteoarthritis who are recovering after total knee arthroplasty shows that practicing tai chi chuan exercises for 45 minutes five times weekly for 12 weeks modestly improves function and walking, but not pain or flexibility, when compared with traditional exercises ( 102244 ).

Psychological well-being.

Small clinical studies suggest that tai chi may improve anxiety symptoms in healthy adolescents.

In a meta-analysis of 10 clinical studies, four randomized and six nonrandomized, in healthy adolescents without mental health diagnoses, practicing tai chi for 30-60 minutes five times weekly for 1 year has been associated with small improvements in anxiety symptoms, but not depressive symptoms or stress, when compared with waitlist or other control ( 107427 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi may improve symptoms in patients with RA.

Some preliminary clinical research in patients with RA suggests that practicing tai chi twice weekly for 12 weeks may improve pain, joint swelling, depression, fatigue, balance, and disability ( 88925 , 88926 , 88927 ).

Sarcopenia.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with sarcopenia.

A meta-analysis of mostly low-quality clinical studies in older adults shows that practicing tai chi for 30-90 minutes 2-7 times weekly for 8-48 weeks improves some measures of physical performance as shown by improvement in the 30-second chair stand test (30CST) and the Timed up and go test (TUGT) and also reduces the number of falls and fear of falling when compared with control,. However, no improvement was observed in balance, muscle mass, grip strength, or other measures of muscle performance when compared with control. Subgroup analysis suggests that practicing tai chi is beneficial for patients with sarcopenia when compared with a nonexercise control, but is similarly effective when compared to an exercise control ( 113428 ).

Schizophrenia.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with schizophrenia.

A network meta-analysis of 3 low-quality clinical studies in patients with schizophrenia shows that tai chi does not reduce positive or negative symptoms when compared with other interventions, including usual treatment and exercise ( 114875 ).

Scleroderma.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with scleroderma.

A preliminary clinical study in adults with systemic sclerosis of the diffuse cutaneous subtype who have not routinely participated in exercise activities during the previous 3 months shows that practicing Yang style tai chi for 10 weeks improves balance, sleep, fatigue, anxiety, and depression, but not trunk endurance, when compared with a home exercise program ( 107763 ).

Spinal cord injury.

It is unclear if tai chi can improve recovery from a spinal cord injury.

A small clinical study in patients who have survived spinal cord injury shows that practicing wheelchair tai chi in addition to normal rehabilitation improves static sitting balance, left handgrip strength, and quality of life when compared with normal rehabilitation only ( 99514 ).

Stress.

Small clinical studies and observational research suggest that practicing tai chi reduces stress in healthy and stressed adults.

Observational research in young adults has found that practicing Yang-style tai chi for 18 weeks is associated with decreased stress and improved wellbeing ( 88930 ). A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in healthy patients shows that practicing tai chi seems to reduce negative emotions such as anxiety and depression when compared with control ( 102249 ). This analysis was limited by notable heterogeneity. Additionally, a small clinical study in healthy and stressed adults shows that practicing a simplified 24-movement tai chi for at least 2 hours weekly for 12 weeks reduces state and trait anxiety when compared with waitlist control ( 99522 ).

Stroke.

Practicing tai chi seems to improve gait, balance, and overall motor recovery in patients that have had a stroke.

Meta-analyses in patients that have had a stroke show that practicing tai chi improves gait, as measured by the Timed Up and Go (TUG) test and the Short Physical Performance Battery (SPPB), when compared with control ( 99519 , 100340 , 107759 ). Tai chi also improves Fugl-Meyer Assessment (FMA) scores, as well as balance, as measured by the Berg Balance Scale (BBS), the fall rates from standing, and the functional reach test, when compared with control. However, tai chi does not improve balance on the SPPB when compared with control ( 99519 , 100340 , 107759 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies. A moderate-sized clinical study in adults who experienced acute ischemic stroke an average of 1.5 months prior to study initiation shows that practicing a modified sitting tai chi program for 40 minutes daily 3 times weekly for 12 weeks improves balance control, sitting balance function, , and activities of daily living, when compared with control ( 113440 ). Tai chi has also been evaluated for non-motor symptoms in patients that have had a stroke. A small meta-analysis of clinical studies shows that practicing tai chi modestly improves depression, but not global mental health or sleep disorders, when compared with control ( 107760 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the heterogeneity of the included studies.

Substance use disorder.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with substance use disorders.

In a meta-analysis of 3 small clinical studies, one randomized and 2 nonrandomized, in females with substance use disorders, practicing tai chi for 45-60 minutes 3-5 times weekly for 12 or 24 weeks has not been associated with changes in depressive symptoms when compared with usual care ( 103907 ).

Tension headache.

It is unclear if practicing tai chi is beneficial in patients with tension headache.

Some clinical research shows that practicing Yang-style tai chi for 1 hour twice weekly for 15 weeks improves headache pain, fatigue, social function, emotional wellbeing, and general quality of life in patients with tension-type headaches when compared with no intervention ( 88932 ).

Traumatic brain injury (TBI).

It is unclear if tai chi is beneficial in adults who have experienced TBI.

A small clinical study in patients with a history of TBI shows that practicing Chen style tai chi for 45 minutes twice weekly for 6 weeks improves mood, but does not affect medical outcomes or self-esteem, when compared with a waitlist control ( 88933 ).

Vestibulopathy.

Small clinical studies suggest that practicing tai chi may modestly improve symptoms in patients with vestibulopathy.

Small clinical studies in adults with vestibulopathy show that practicing tai chi (Cheng Man-Ch'ing's Yang-style short form) for 40 minutes once weekly for 10 weeks may improve gait and the stability of body and gaze. However, these improvements were no better than those seen in patients undergoing vestibular rehabilitation ( 88899 , 88900 ).

More evidence is needed to rate tai chi for these uses.

Tai chi can be practiced with a wide range of frequencies and for varying durations. In clinical research, tai chi has typically been practiced for one hour daily, 1-2 times weekly for up to 1 year ( 88898 , 88891 , 88892 , 88893 , 88894 , 88904 , 88905 , 94161 , 96400 , 96391 )( 96394 , 96393 , 96395 , 96396 , 96397 ). There are many different styles of tai chi. Yang style is the most common tai chi style ( 88899 , 88900 , 88930 , 88932 ). Other styles include Chen style, Lin style, Da Yuan Jiang Tang style, and Tai Chi Ball ( 88933 , 99520 ). For some conditions, a certain style of tai chi has been shown to be more beneficial than other styles. For example, Yang style tai chi appears to lower the risk of falling more than Sun style tai chi ( 94161 ). Tai chi tailored for fall prevention, called Tai Ji Quan Moving for Better Balance (TJQMBB), has been used 60 minutes twice weekly for 6 months ( 98000 ).

Tai chi is a traditional Chinese martial art that incorporates low-to-moderate intensity exercise and posture, movements, meditation, and breathing patterns. Tai chi attempts to regulate mind, body, and breath. Tai chi is a fairly well-known form of traditional Chinese exercise and is one specific type of Qi gong ( 15872 ). Regular practice of tai chi induces both cardiorespiratory and muscular adaptations, as improved use of oxygen and muscle strength ( 96397 ). In traditional Chinese medicine, it is believed that disease is caused by a blockage or unbalanced energy flow in the body. Like many therapies in traditional Chinese medicine, tai chi is thought to help unblock, balance, or direct energy flow and help the body heal. Tai chi movements are deliberate, slow, and graceful, with each movement flowing into the next. Many of the movements are named after animals such as crane, dragon, or snake. There is interest in tai chi for improving immune function and immune response in older adults. Preliminary research shows that a combination of tai chi and Qi gong exercises in older adults results in an increased antibody response to influenza vaccination ( 15857 ). There is interest in using tai chi to improve sleep in older adults ( 17138 ). Tai chi may help prevent falls by reducing the fear of falling in older individuals ( 17137 , 93898 , 93904 ). Also, tai chi appears to improve balance, mobility, and strength in older adults, which may help prevent falls ( 15868 , 17137 , 18206 , 88907 , 88921 , 88935 , 88936 , 88937 , 88938 , 88939 )( 88941 , 88942 , 88943 , 88947 , 93897 , 93900 , 93902 , 93906 , 93907 , 93908 , 96393 ).
