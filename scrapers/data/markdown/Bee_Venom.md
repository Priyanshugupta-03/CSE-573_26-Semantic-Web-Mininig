# Bee Venom

Caution

Bee venom should not be confused with other bee products including Bee Pollen , Beeswax , Honey , Propolis , or Royal Jelly . It should also not be confused with Apitherapy , which involves the use of various bee products in the treatment of health conditions.

## Overview

Bee venom is a colored liquid produced by glands associated with the sting apparatus of bees ( 96574 ). The use of bee venom for medicinal purposes is popular in parts of Asia, eastern Europe, and South America ( 96576 ). Bee venom is applied topically, by injection or acupuncture, or via a live sting ( 115158 ). Some bee venom products are available as injectable prescription medications for the treatment of bee venom allergy; these are not within the scope of this topic.

Likely Safe when prescription purified bee venom is given by subcutaneous injection by a trained medical professional ( 118538 ).

Possibly Safe when non-prescription bee venom is given by injection under supervision in clinical research. In clinical research, bee venom injections have been given intramuscularly in doses of 0.0025 mg/kg to 0.01 mg/kg daily for up to 15 days ( 118534 ). Bee venom subcutaneous injections have also been used safely in a dose of 100 mcg monthly for 11 months ( 96572 )....when applied using stings from live bees. Live bee stings have been safely administered under medical supervision in doses up to 20 bee stings 3 times weekly for up to 24 weeks ( 13222 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of bee venom when used topically.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available on non-prescription bee venom; avoid using.

#### General

Overall, prescription forms of injectable bee venom are generally well tolerated when used as prescribed. Some non-prescription forms of injectable bee venom seem to be well tolerated in clinical research.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Parenterally : Injection site erythema, itching, swelling, tenderness, urticaria.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Parenterally : Anaphylaxis, asthma, chest pain or tightness, coagulation abnormalities, confusion, edema, hypotension, laryngeal edema, malaise, palpitations, respiratory distress, somnolence.

#### Cardiovascular

Case reports have described stroke and bradycardia associated with parenteral bee venom therapy ( 96576 ). Other reported adverse cardiovascular effects include cardiac dysfunction, palpitations, hypotension, and chest pain or tightness ( 1343 , 6070 , 111472 , 111474 ).

#### Dermatologic

Local adverse effects of parenteral bee venom include edema, erythema, itching, numbness, pain and tenderness at the injection site, and urticaria, ( 1343 , 13222 , 102531 ). Self-administered bee stings have been associated with eosinophilic foreign body granuloma ( 111482 ), and bee venom acupuncture can cause dermatitis, contact urticaria, skin infection with Mycobacterium chelonae ( 11483 ), giant dermatofibroma, or chronic folliculitis ( 96576 ). A case report describes panniculitis associated with bee venom immunotherapy ( 96576 ). In one case report, Grover's disease developed in an 80-year-old male with a history of honeybee venom allergy, following multiple bee stings ( 115170 ).

#### Hematologic

Bee venom therapy has been associated with coagulation abnormalities including intravascular hemolysis and thrombocytopenia ( 6046 , 96576 , 111474 ). Bee venom given with acupuncture can cause disseminated intravascular coagulation ( 6046 , 96576 ).

#### Hepatic

Two case reports describe hepatotoxicity, including acute icteric hepatitis associated with bee venom acupuncture ( 96576 ). Another case report describes hepatitis B reactivation complicated by nephritic syndrome during bee venom immunotherapy ( 96576 ).

#### Immunologic

Immune-mediated reactions associated with bee venom include rash, itching, generalized hypersensitivity reaction, anaphylaxis, serum sickness reaction, and immune thrombocytopenia ( 6074 , 6077 , 95899 , 96576 , 111470 , 118536 ). Estimates of the rate of immunologic reactions to various forms of bee venom range from 0.025% to 29% ( 96576 , 105770 , 111470 , 111475 , 118536 ). They occur most frequently when doses are increased rapidly. Females and people with a c-KIT mutation may have more severe and frequent adverse effects ( 1343 , 6077 , 102533 , 111470 ). Doses of bee venom associated with adverse effects vary from 0.1 to 2 mL, and anaphylaxis has occurred after 1-24 doses ( 111470 ). Bee venom is also associated with autoimmune disorders, including systemic lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis, Guillain-Barre syndrome, and pemphigus vulgaris ( 96574 ). In a case report, a 68-year-old female developed Guillain-Barré syndrome with progressive quadriplegia ten days after receiving acupuncture with honeybee venom for pain ( 96574 ). In another case, a 64-year-old female developed pemphigus foliaceus, involving pruritic, erythematous, erosive skin lesions with crusting and plaques, after bee venom acupuncture twice weekly for 2 months. Immunoglobulin G was detected in the intercellular spaces between keratinocytes. Partial resolution occurred after 2 months of treatment with oral corticosteroids and azathioprine ( 111473 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

A 63-year-old female who received an accidental injection into the psoas muscle of 2 mL of normal saline containing bee venom diluted in a 1:2000 ratio, experienced severe burning pain at the injection site which persisted for 1 month, with imaging showing inflammation of the muscle. The pain gradually resolved over the following 5 months ( 111475 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Bee venom can cause confusion, headache, pain, and somnolence ( 1343 , 6070 , 96576 , 111472 ). It has also been associated with a case of neuralgic amyotrophy ( 96576 ). There is a case report of delayed nerve injury following a bee sting in the face of a 6-year-old male ( 118537 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

Bee venom has been associated with asthma, hyperventilation, pulmonary or laryngeal edema, respiratory distress, paralysis of respiratory muscles ( 1343 , 6070 , 96576 , 111474 ).

#### Renal

Bee venom immunotherapy has been associated with nephrotic syndrome, acute interstitial nephritis, and acute tubular necrosis ( 96576 , 111474 ). There are also case reports of acute kidney injury, including interstitial nephritis and tubular necrosis, following multiple bee stings ( 111474 , 115166 ).

Possibly Effective
Osteoarthritis.

Receiving intradermal bee venom injections at specific acupuncture points seems to improve pain and function in patients with knee osteoarthritis. Whether other routes of administration are beneficial is unclear.

A high-quality clinical trial in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee shows that injecting bee venom 100 mcg intradermally at 15 acupuncture points on the knees and back weekly for 12 weeks reduces knee pain and increases physical function scores when compared with a histamine control injection ( 102531 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Acne.

It is unclear if topical bee venom is beneficial in patients with acne.

A very small clinical study in Korea in adolescent and adult patients (12 to 35 years of age) with mild to moderate acne shows that applying a serum (4 mL daily) containing purified bee venom 0.006% twice daily for 2 weeks reduces acne severity when compared with control ( 117994 ). The validity of these results is limited by short duration and generalizability.

Aging skin.

Topical bee venom has only been used in combination with other ingredients; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A single-center clinical study in females (40-55 years of age) with self-reported skin health concerns shows that applying 2-3 drops of a combination product (Manuka Health Eternal Renewal Regenerating Face Serum, Manuka Health) containing bee venom (concentration not specified), manuka honey, royal jelly, and a blend of botanical extracts twice daily for 8 weeks improves self-reported perception of skin health (e.g., elasticity, fine lines, skin appearance), but not objective skin parameters (e.g., moisture, pigmentation, texture) assessed by high-definition imaging, when compared to baseline ( 117993 ). The validity of these results is limited by the lack of a comparator group. Furthermore, it is unclear if the self-reported benefits are due to bee venom, the other ingredients, or the combination.

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

Although there has been interest in using topical bee venom for eczema, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of bee venom for this purpose.

Back pain.

Although there has been interest in using parenteral bee venom for back pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of bee venom for this purpose.

Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19).

Although there has been interest in using parenteral bee venom for COVID-19, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of bee venom for this purpose.

Diabetic foot ulcers.

It is unclear if topical bee venom is beneficial in patients with diabetic foot ulcers.

A small clinical trial in patients with diabetic foot ulcers shows that applying a gel containing bee venom, using ultrasound to enhance skin permeability, 3 times weekly for 12 weeks modestly reduces ulcer volume and wound surface area by approximately 60% when compared with 10% to 15% in patients given ultrasound with a gel without bee venom. Each treatment provided a total of approximately 0.6 mg diluted bee venom. All patients were also provided with regular ulcer care ( 115158 ).

Fibromyalgia.

Although there has been interest in using parenteral bee venom for fibromyalgia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of bee venom for this purpose.

Frozen shoulder.

It is unclear if bee venom acupuncture is beneficial in patients with frozen shoulder.

One clinical trial in patients with frozen shoulder shows that receiving intramuscular bee venom injections in acupuncture points, at a dose of 0.0025 mg/kg, but not 0.005 mg/kg or 0.01 mg/kg, daily for 15 days modestly improves shoulder pain and function when compared with control injections with thiamine and procaine 3% ( 118534 ). Another small clinical study shows that receiving bee venom diluted 1:10,000 as injections into acupuncture points twice a week for 8 weeks, as an adjunct to other therapy, improves pain and disability but not range of motion when compared with injection with normal saline. Bee venom injections diluted to 1:30,000 did not improve pain and disability when compared with placebo ( 96573 ). One year after receiving bee venom acupuncture, scores on the Shoulder Pain and Disability Index remained improved over placebo in these patients; however, verbal rating scale scores did not differ from placebo ( 105772 ).

Furuncles (boils).

Although there has been interest in using topical bee venom for boils, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of bee venom for this purpose.

Hemiplegic shoulder pain.

It is unclear if bee venom acupuncture is beneficial in patients with hemiplegic shoulder pain.

Pooled analyses of two small clinical studies shows that bee venom acupuncture modestly decreases pain, but does not affect motor function, when compared with control in patients with shoulder pain after stroke ( 96575 , 105771 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if administering live bee stings is beneficial in patients with MS.

A small clinical trial shows that administering live bee stings in graduated doses up to 20 stings given 3 times weekly does not seem to improve MS. Treatment for 24 weeks does not seem to reduce gadolinium-enhancing lesions on MRI or improve fatigue, disability, or quality of life ( 13222 ).

Neck pain.

It is unclear if bee venom acupuncture is beneficial in patients with neck pain.

In patients with chronic neck pain, receiving 6 sessions of bee venom acupuncture over a 3-week period reduces the impact of the neck pain and improves quality of life more than taking the nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug loxoprofen 180 mg orally daily for 3 weeks. Pain intensity and neck disability index decreased similarly with both treatments when compared to baseline ( 111471 ).

Notalgia paresthetica.

Topical bee venom has only been evaluated in combination with other ingredients; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in a small number of patients with notalgia paresthetica shows that applying a product containing bee venom 0.04% and capsaicin 0.025% to the affected skin daily for 2 weeks reduces pruritus when compared to baseline ( 102530 ). A lack of control group limits the validity of these findings. Also, it is unclear if the effects are due to bee venom, capsaicin, or the combination.

Parkinson disease.

It is unclear if bee venom acupuncture is beneficial in patients with Parkinson disease; research is conflicting.

Data on the effect of bee venom in patients with Parkinson disease are conflicting. Preliminary clinical studies in a small number of patients with Parkinson disease, stable on medication for at least 4 weeks, shows that receiving injections of dilute bee venom into 10 acupuncture points twice weekly, with or without traditional acupuncture, for 8-12 weeks improves motor symptoms, walking speed, quality of life, and ability to perform activities of daily living when compared with baseline, placebo, and/or conventional anti-Parkinson medications alone ( 95900 , 96571 , 102532 ). However, it is unclear whether bee venom acupuncture provides long-term benefits. Also, other research shows no benefit of bee venom therapy. One clinical study in patients with Parkinson disease shows that receiving a specific bee venom 100 mcg (Alyostal) injection subcutaneously once monthly for 11 months does not improve symptoms when compared with placebo. The researchers speculate that the monthly dose may have been too low to have an effect ( 96572 ). Higher quality research is needed to determine the benefits of bee venom, if any, in these patients.

Postoperative pain.

It is unclear if topical bee venom is beneficial in patients with postoperative pain.

A small clinical trial in patients post inguinal hernioplasty shows that applying a gel containing bee venom, using ultrasound to enhance skin permeability, 3 times weekly for 3 weeks reduces postoperative pain by approximately 77% compared with 45% in patients given ultrasound with a gel without bee venom. Hip mobility was also improved. The bee venom gel was applied on selected acupuncture points and around incisions. Each treatment provided a total of approximately 0.6 mg diluted bee venom. All patients were also provided with regular care ( 118535 ).

Psoriasis.

Although there has been interest in using topical bee venom for psoriasis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of bee venom for this purpose.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Although there has been interest in using parenteral bee venom for RA, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of bee venom for this purpose.

Tendinopathy.

Although there has been interest in using parenteral bee venom for tendinopathy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of bee venom for this purpose.

Venous leg ulcers.

It is unclear if topical bee venom is beneficial in patients with venous ulcers.

A small clinical trial in patients with venous leg ulcers shows that applying a gel containing bee venom using ultrasound to enhance skin permeability 3 times weekly for 12 weeks reduces ulcer volume and wound surface area by 73% and 64%, respectively, when compared with 18% and 10% in patients given ultrasound with a gel without bee venom. Each treatment provided a total of approximately 0.6 mg diluted bee venom. All patients were also provided with regular ulcer care ( 115158 ).

Wrinkled skin.

It is unclear if topical bee venom is beneficial in patients with wrinkled skin.

A small clinical study in South Korea in generally healthy female patients (30-49 years of age) with mild to moderate photodamage-associated periorbital wrinkles shows that applying a serum containing purified bee venom 0.006% (4 mL) twice daily for 12 weeks modestly reduces physician-rated visual wrinkle scores, and total wrinkle area, total wrinkle count, and wrinkle depth when compared to baseline ( 117992 ). However, clinical significance is unclear. Furthermore, the validity of these results is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

More evidence is needed to rate the effectiveness of bee venom for these uses.

#### General

Bee venom contains several physiologically active components. Melittin, a biogenic amine, is thought to be responsible for some of the therapeutic effects, and the enzyme phospholipase A2 (PLA2) is a major antigen responsible for adverse effects ( 111470 , 111472 ). Bee venom contains 30% to 60% melittin, 10% to 12% PLA2, and minor quantities of serotonin, hyaluronidase, histamine, catecholamines, and kinin ( 6041 , 6045 , 6046 , 111474 ). Melitin is a potent hemolytic ( 6042 , 6045 ) that causes mast cell degranulation and activates PLA2 ( 6045 ). Other constituents in bee venom include apamin, mast cell degranulating peptide (MCD peptide), procamine, secapin, tertiapin, and other small peptides including adolapin ( 6 , 111470 ). Sweet bee venom is processed to remove compounds causing adverse effects, such as PLA2, and to purify the active ingredient melittin ( 111470 ).

#### Antimicrobial effects

Human research suggests that topical purified bee venom reduces adenosine triphosphate-based skin microorganism measures ( 117994 ).

#### Immunologic effects

Bee venom can cause severe adverse effects. Some components in bee venom seem to cause neutrophil degranulation and superoxide production ( 6 , 6044 ), but other actions of bee venom components seem to decrease superoxide production ( 6 , 6041 , 6045 , 6072 ). The formation of superoxide anions can be destructive to tissues. The bee venom constituent, hyaluronidase, hydrolyzes hyaluronic acid, allowing for the cellular penetration of bee venom constituents . For this reason, hyaluronidase is termed a spreading factor ( 6041 , 111475 ). Mast cell degranulating peptide is chemically similar to the neurotoxin apamin. It causes mast cell degranulation and release of histamine at extremely low concentrations. Histamine causes dilation and increased permeability of capillaries ( 6041 , 111475 ). The constituent adolapin inhibits inflammation and the prostaglandin-synthase system ( 6 ). In animals, whole bee venom, melittin, and apamin have also been shown to cause increases in cortisol levels ( 6039 , 6040 ). The mechanism of other peptides is not yet understood ( 6041 ).

#### Inflammatory effects

Bee venom is of interest for the treatment of various painful or inflammatory conditions. In human research, bee venom injections reduce levels of pro-inflammatory compounds, including interleukin (IL)-1 beta, tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), and C-reactive protein ( 118534,118535). High doses of bee venom can cause severe adverse effects. The constituents melittin and phospholipase A2 (PLA2) are thought to cause an increase in the synthesis of the cytokines TNF-alpha and Il-1, and to stimulate arachidonic acid release resulting in an inflammatory cascade response ( 6044 , 6071 ).
