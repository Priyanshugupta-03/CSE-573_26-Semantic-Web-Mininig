## Overview

Hellerwork is a form of deep tissue bodywork and movement therapy that was developed in the 1970s by aerospace engineer Joseph Heller. It is a form of Structural Integration (Rolfing) involving multiple techniques such as deep-tissue bodywork, movement education, and verbal interaction to improve posture and movement patterns. Hellerwork professional certification involves a 1,250-hour program ( 27742 ).

## History

Joseph Heller, a graduate of Cal Tech and former NASA aerospace engineer, left his career to investigate humanistic psychology and became a senior practitioner of Rolfing Structural Integration. He subsequently developed Hellerwork in 1979. There is little literature available about Hellerwork, as Joseph Heller has concentrated on the teaching of this healing art to practitioners and not on formal scientific investigation. He has published two books, however, one is undated and is posted on his web site, and one is available from book distributors ( 27742 ).

## People Use This For

Hellerwork is used for amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS, Lou Gehrig disease), anxiety, asthma, athletic performance, back pain, cancer, carpal tunnel syndrome, fall prevention, fatigue, headache, hyperthyroidism, mental alertness, muscle soreness, neck pain, osteoarthritis, pain (acute), pain (chronic), Parkinson disease, sprains, and stress. It is also used for sports injuries and to improve posture.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of Hellerwork.

Assessment Techniques : Assessment is conducted through dialog with the client about their life history, medical history, and history of injuries or traumas to the body or psyche. Visual observation is also used, such as having the client stand, walk, or demonstrate mobility. Some practitioners use video recordings of the client walking or moving to give feedback to the client. Finally, palpation is used ( 27742 ). Therapeutic Techniques : Hellerwork takes place in a series of 11 sessions. Each session includes about an hour of bodywork and one-half hour of movement education. Each session focuses on a certain area of the body and the emotions that practitioners believe are related to that area. Bodywork is used to realign the muscles and connective tissue and restore balance to the body. Movement re-education uses exercises that teach stress-free methods for performing everyday movements such as standing, walking, bending, sitting, and reaching. Dialog with the practitioner during the process encourages the recipient to explore how thoughts, beliefs, attitudes, and feelings impact the body. The first ten sessions of the standard Hellerwork protocol are structured similarly to the standard ten sessions of Rolfing. However, Hellerwork emphasizes the psychological aspect of the work more than Rolfing. The 11 that sessions occur are as follows ( 27742 ): 1. "Inspiration" is intended to improve breathing and align the rib cage. 2. "Standing on Your Own Two Feet" is intended to align the legs, knees, and ankles, and distribute body weight evenly over the feet. 3. "Reaching Out" is intended to align the torso and release tension in the arms, shoulders, and sides. 4. "Control and Surrender" is intended to release the lower part of the body's core and bring alignment to the inside of the legs. 5. "The Guts" is intended to release pelvic muscles and to lengthen the front of the core. 6. "Holding Back" is intended to lengthen the core's back. 7. "Losing Your Head" is intended to release and lengthen the top of the core, to align the head over the torso, and to release tension in the head, face, and neck. 8. "The Feminine" is intended to release the muscles of the legs, hips, and pelvis. 9. "The Masculine" is intended to release rotations in the upper half of the body - the arms, shoulders, chest, back, head, and neck - and balance the deep and superficial muscles of this area. 10. "Integration" is intended to work on the joints and body integrity. 11. "Coming Out" focuses on accomplishment, self-expression, and empowerment though a variety of methods.

Hellerwork uses the theoretical principles of Rolfing Structural Integration, the basis of which is the belief that the body stores the trauma of a person's life - physical as well as emotional -- within its physical structure, in the form of muscular and functional holding patterns. It is believed that the body accumulates physical and emotional events through the course of life, and each such event leaves an imprint on structure and function ( 27742 ). It is theorized that these imprints are held in the myofascial (connective tissue) network whose function is to support and connect the body. The connective tissue may become "fixed" or "stuck" to compensate for torques and twists that occur; it gradually hardens, and literally begins to stick to itself. Restricted movement and pain ensue, habitual patterns develop, and the body adapts to functioning with limited options in movement. These fixed patterns are believed to be self-reinforcing, thus keeping the person "stuck in the past" in terms of patterns of posture, emotional responses, attitudes, relationships with others, and movement through the world ( 27742 ). The theory holds that when the person releases these holding patterns in the body through deep tissue manipulation (loosening the adhesions of the fascia) and becomes properly aligned with gravity, this leads to physical, emotional, spiritual, and relational improvements ( 27742 ). Hellerwork is aimed at improving or restoring the body's natural balance and posture. Hellerwork augments the practices and principles of Rolfing with movement education and focused dialog between the practitioner and client ( 27742 ).
