Caution

Do not confuse postbiotics, which do not contain live microorganisms, with Probiotics , which are viable, living microorganisms.

## Overview

Postbiotics have been defined in various ways, and a general scientific consensus on the definition of a postbiotic has not yet been reached. In general, postbiotics, unlike probiotics, do not contain live microorganisms. Rather, postbiotics may contain a wide range of ingredients that have beneficial effects on health, including inanimate microorganisms, microorganism cell components or metabolites, and/or soluble metabolic byproduct(s) from microorganisms ( 105433 , 107638 , 107640 , 107644 ). The International Scientific Association of Probiotics and Prebiotics (ISAPP) defines postbiotics as a safe preparation of inanimate microorganisms and/or their cell components, alone or together with cell metabolites, that have beneficial effects on health. In order to fit the definition of a postbiotic, the microorganisms, their matrix, and the method used to inactivate the organisms must be known, and beneficial effects on health must be confirmed ( 105433 ). However, other definitions have also been proposed. Some scientists consider inactivated probiotics to fall into a classification other than postbiotics. In this case, postbiotic is alternatively defined as the soluble metabolic byproducts of probiotics ( 107638 , 107640 , 107644 ). Other definitions of postbiotics have included soluble factors secreted by live bacteria or released after bacterial lysis, microbial constituents produced by microorganisms, non-viable microorganisms, or functional bioactive compounds generated during fermentation. The definitions generally include reference to promotion of beneficial biological and/or health effects but differ with respect to certain features. These points of differentiation include whether the inanimate microorganism must be present in the preparation and whether the original living microorganism must be considered a probiotic ( 107640 , 107644 ). Despite the need for consensus, the term postbiotic is commonly used to mean either or both the inactivated organism and the metabolic byproducts, even as single ingredients ( 107638 ). Therefore, this monograph provides general information related to products referred to as postbiotics in the scientific literature.

In the US, postbiotics as a group are not specifically regulated by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). The regulation of individual postbiotics will likely depend on their proposed use ( 107433 ).

Postbiotics represent a wide range of products. Some products referred to as postbiotics in clinical research include lysates, the inactivated microorganism, or the inactivated microorganism plus the fermented culture medium ( 108583 , 107535 , 107537 , 107640 ). Formulations of heat-killed or inactivated microorganisms are thought to have a longer shelf-life, reduced requirements for refrigeration, and improved standardization when compared with viable microorganism preparations, such as probiotics. However, storage requirements would ultimately depend on the specific constituents in the product ( 105155 , 105433 , 107504 , 107528 , 107638 ). Inactivation technologies might include heat or high pressure; for strict anaerobic microorganisms, inactivation may also occur via oxygen exposure ( 105433 ). Methods of inactivation described in clinical research have included autoclaving at 121°C for 30 minutes ( 108583 ), heat treatment for 1 hour at 70°C on three consecutive days and by gamma irradiation ( 96278 ), heat treatment at 56-90°C for 30 minutes ( 107532 ), and a two-hour exposure to ultraviolet (UV) light with storage at -80°C ( 107643 ). In general, inactivation is dependent on the temperature and time of exposure, as well as the cell type and the matrix composition. Some residual live microorganisms might remain in the product ( 105433 ). Suitable methods for quantification are still needed. These might include flow cytometry to identify total fluorescent units and active fluorescent units (AFUs), cell counts before inactivation (colony-forming units; CFUs), and other methods to quantify microbial biomass, such as enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay-based approaches, scanning electron microscopy, and others ( 105433 ). Some experts suggest that the proper identification of specific postbiotics requires the genome sequence of the microorganism, a description of the procedure and matrix used for confirmed inactivation, the specific postbiotic composition, an assessment of safety related to the intended use, and clinical research providing evidence of a health benefit ( 105433 ). However, this does not mean that products labeled as postbiotics meet each of these requirements.

#### General

Postbiotics have been defined in various ways. Thus, these products may contain a wide range of ingredients, including inanimate microorganisms, microorganism cell components or metabolites, and/or soluble metabolic byproduct(s) from microorganisms. Some definitions require the original microorganism to be classified as a probiotic, whereas others do not ( 105433 , 107638 , 107640 , 107644 ). Also, some experts suggest that certain components should be excluded from the definition of postbiotics, including, but not limited to, viruses, vaccines, purified cellular metabolites, and undefined/mixed organisms derived from fermented foods ( 105433 ). Given the wide range of components considered for inclusion in postbiotic products, a specific mechanism of action cannot be provided. However, some general mechanisms of action have been proposed.

#### Anti-cancer effects

In an animal model, Lactiplantibacillus plantarum L-137 (HK L-137) inhibits tumor growth ( 107530 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

Postbiotics are of interest for their potential anti-inflammatory effects and have demonstrated anti-inflammatory effects in vitro ( 107534 , 107610 ). Compounds such as short-chain fatty acids or polysaccharides, which might be found in postbiotic preparations, are thought to have systemic and/or local anti-inflammatory effects ( 107640 ). In human research, heat-killed Lactiplantibacillus plantarum L-137 (HK L-137) reduced enzymatic markers of liver inflammation ( 107534 ). In animal research, heat-killed Bifidobacterium breve suppressed the production of some pro-inflammatory cytokines from isolated spleen cells ( 107641 ). However, in human research, inactivated Bacillus coagulans does not seem to have beneficial effects on cytokines involved in inflammation ( 107610 ).

#### Antibacterial effects

Non-viable Limosilactobacillus reuteri DSM17648 is of interest for Helicobacter pylori eradication. Although clinical effects appear to be lacking, non-viable L. reuteri was found to co-aggregate H. pylori in vitro ( 107504 ). Heat-killed Lactobacillus acidophilus has also been shown to reduce the viability H. pylori ( 8562 ). Also, some molecules present in postbiotics, such as lactic acid and bacteriocins, have antimicrobial activity ( 105433 ).

#### Gastrointestinal effects

In humans and animal models, postbiotics seem to modulate the composition of gut microbiota. Although the mechanism is unknown, some experts have suggested that postbiotics might carry quorum sensing and quorum quenching molecules. Some compounds found in postbiotics, such as lactic acid, might be consumed by gut microbiota. Also, some postbiotics contain adhesins, which allow for competition with other microbes at adhesion sites. Additionally, some postbiotics contain secreted proteins or exopolysaccharides which help to promote epithelial barrier function by promoting tight junction function ( 7744 , 8562 , 102297 , 105155 , 105433 , 107504 , 107530 , 107565 , 107640 ).

#### Immunological effects

In vitro, non-viable Lacticaseibacillus rhamnosus has been shown to have immunological effects. This product increased the production of specific cytokines after induction of both intestinal epithelial cells and dendritic cells. The mechanism possibly involved a peptidoglycan ( 107643 ). Heat-killed Lactobacillus acidophilus has been shown to stimulate mucosal IgA production in vitro ( 8562 ). At high concentrations, heat-killed Lactobacillus paracasei Shirota increases tumor necrosis factor-alpha production from stimulated monocytes ( 107532 ). In vitro and in animal models, heat-killed Lactiplantibacillus plantarum L-137 (HK L-137) induces the production of cytokines from T-helper-Type 1 cells, suppresses food allergy-related IgE response, and protects against influenza infection. In human research, taking HK L-137 results in the proliferation of isolated T cells ( 107530 ). Some postbiotics are thought to contain specific patterns that are recognized by receptors of immune cells, resulting in immune modulation. Parts of the postbiotic, including lipoteichoic acid, peptidoglycans, fimbriae or pili, or other cell parts or compounds, might have this function ( 105433 ).

#### Metabolic effects

Some postbiotics contain metabolites or other compounds which might modulate systemic metabolism. Bile salt hydrolase, a microbial enzyme, deconjugates bile acids. This increases their availability to the microbiota and alters the metabolism of glucose and lipids in the body. Short-chain fatty acids also affect the metabolism of the gut microbiota, playing a role in systemic metabolism in the body ( 105433 , 107530 ). Laboratory research shows that some heat-killed microbiota, such as Lactiplantibacillus plantarum OLL2712 and Limosilactobacillus reuteri GMNL-263, have beneficial effects on plasma lipids, including levels of triglycerides and low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol ( 107534 ). In human research, heat-killed Lactiplantibacillus plantarum L-137 (HK L-137) modestly suppressed seasonal increases in LDL cholesterol but had no effect on levels of triglycerides or high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol ( 107534 ).

#### Neurological effects

Some postbiotics possibly contain neuroactive compounds, including neurotransmitters, enzymes, and/or metabolites involved in neurotransmitter or hormone synthesis or release ( 105433 ). In animal models, some probiotics have neurological effects. A postbiotic derived from Limosilactobacillus fermentum and Lactobacillus delbrueckii was found to increase sociability and decrease stress ( 105433 ).

#### Nutritional effects

Postbiotics may be a source of B vitamins, such as folate ( 105433 , 107640 ).
