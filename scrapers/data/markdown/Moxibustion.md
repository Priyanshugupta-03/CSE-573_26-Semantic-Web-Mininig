## Overview

Moxibustion is a therapeutic method in traditional Chinese medicine (TCM). The method involves burning an herb, usually mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris), above the skin or on the acupuncture points to introduce heat into an acupuncture point for therapeutic effects. It may be performed by burning a cone, stick, or loose herb a short distance away from the skin. Additionally, the burning herb may be placed on the head of an acupuncture needle, to increase the temperature gradient of the needle ( 29750 , 29751 ).

Possibly Safe when moxibustion is administered appropriately by a qualified practitioner. Moxibustion has been used in clinical research without reports of serious adverse events ( 29858 , 29889 , 29896 , 29897 , 29899 , 88308 , 88309 , 88310 , 88311 , 96051 )( 96054 , 100768 , 106701 , 108114 , 108119 , 108121 , 108123 , 108124 , 108125 , 108127 )( 108129 ).

PREGNANCY: Possibly Unsafe when used during pregnancy. While moxibustion has been used with apparent safety during pregnancy in most clinical studies ( 29883 , 29884 , 29885 , 29886 , 88618 , 108120 ), some clinical evidence suggests that moxibustion may be associated with premature birth, premature membrane rupture, increased rate of contractions, abdominal pain due to contractions, placental bleeding, reduced or increased fetal movement, and reduced fetal heart rate ( 29889 , 29890 , 88311 , 88343 , 88620 ).

LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the use of moxibustion during lactation; however, it is unlikely that maternal use of moxibustion would pose a significant safety risk to breastfeeding infants.

#### General

Moxibustion, when administered appropriately, seems to be generally well tolerated.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Orally : Blisters, burns, dizziness, fainting, fatigue, gastrointestinal upset, headache, hypersomnia, itching, pain, rash, redness, and respiratory discomfort.

#### Dermatologic

Moxibustion administration may cause burns, rash, flushing, pinhole pain, erythema, rubefaction, blistering, and itching sensations. Additionally, moxibustion may leave a temporary discoloration on the skin, which may be washed off or will disappear on its own ( 29754 , 29776 , 29838 , 29858 , 29889 , 88309 , 88310 , 88311 , 96046 , 96047 )( 96049 , 96050 , 96052 , 96055 , 100768 , 103953 , 106688 , 108114 , 108121 , 108122 , 108127 ). Serious cases of adverse events caused by moxibustion are generally related to cases of negligence, such as not removing the moxa from the skin at the appropriate time or accidentally dropping ash onto the skin ( 88308 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Moxibustion administration may cause abdominal pain, diarrhea, constipation, heartburn, nausea, stomach tenderness and pressure, throat problems, and upset stomach ( 29889 , 88310 , 88311 , 88312 , 96051 ).

#### Immunologic

A case of allergic contact dermatitis has been reported with the use of a self-administered moxibustion device. Dermatitis resolved with a course of oral and topical corticosteroid therapy ( 106705 ). Skin allergy has been reported rarely in other publications ( 108127 , 108128 ).

#### Neurologic/CNS

Moxibustion administration may cause dizziness, fainting, fatigue, headaches, and hypersomnia in some patients ( 29754 , 29838 , 29896 , 88310 , 88311 , 96047 , 96049 , 96051 ).

#### Pulmonary/Respiratory

Moxibustion administration may cause respiratory discomfort in some patients due to the smoke involved with moxibustion ( 29890 , 88311 ). Moxibustion smoke may also cause asthma exacerbations in some patients with asthma ( 88313 ). However, some clinical research suggests that moxibustion treatment might reduce symptoms of asthma ( 96053 ).

#### Other

Moxibustion administration has been associated with fever and infections such as cellulitis, hepatitis C, and severe septic shock secondary to necrotizing fasciitis ( 29754 , 29838 , 88311 , 88314 , 106689 ).

Possibly Effective
Allergic rhinitis (hay fever).

Moxibustion administration with or without acupuncture seems to be beneficial for allergic rhinitis.

A pooled analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with allergic rhinitis shows that moxibustion with acupuncture increases the rate of allergic rhinitis improvement and cure when compared with routine Western medicine ( 29791 ). A meta-analysis of low-quality studies in patients with allergic rhinitis shows that thunder-fire moxibustion, with or without other treatments, improves allergic rhinitis by 7% to 18% when compared with other treatments alone, no treatment, or placebo ( 106693 ). Another meta-analysis of moderate-quality studies in patients with allergic rhinitis shows that the total effective rate of indirect moxibustion, based on cure and evidence of improvement, with or without other treatments, is 16% higher than control groups, which usually included other treatments. Overall symptoms and quality of life were also modestly improved; however, there was no effect on rhinitis severity based on a visual analog scale (VAS) ( 108127 ). These analyses are limited by their overall risk of bias and heterogeneity. Moxibustion has also been compared with acupuncture. One small clinical study in patients with perennial allergic rhinitis shows that 8 sessions of electric heating moxibustion over 4 weeks via a specific electrical device (Cettum, K-medical) is as effective as acupuncture for reducing overall nasal symptoms and improving quality of life. However, beneficial effects associated with moxibustion took longer and it was less effective than acupuncture for reducing the specific symptoms of itching and sneezing ( 108125 ).

Back pain.

Most clinical research shows that moxibustion, when used alone or in conjunction with other treatments, may modestly reduce back pain.

A meta-analysis of small, low-quality clinical studies in people with chronic low back pain shows that moxibustion reduces pain when compared with acupuncture, massage, or taking ibuprofen 300 mg daily or celecoxib 200 mg daily. Furthermore, adding moxibustion to other treatments such as massage, acupuncture, core stability training, or the medicines celecoxib or meloxicam, further reduces back pain when compared with the active treatment alone ( 103944 ). Meta-analyses of low- to moderate-quality clinical research in adults with a herniated lumbar disc have also shown benefit for low back pain. Warm needle moxibustion used alone seems to be slightly more effective than acupuncture or manipulation for improving leg and back pain in these patients ( 96048 ), and adding moxibustion to massage or acupuncture modestly improves pain when compared with acupuncture or massage alone ( 103950 ). The validity of these analyses is limited by the low quality and heterogeneity of the available studies. A small clinical study has also evaluated moxibustion for the management of low back pain associated with osteoporosis in postmenopausal adults. This study shows that receiving thunder-fire moxibustion 3 times weekly for 4 weeks, in addition to calcium and vitamin D supplementation, modestly reduces back pain and seems to improve quality of life when compared with calcium and vitamin D alone ( 100766 ).

Dysmenorrhea.

Moxibustion administration, with or without acupuncture, seems to be beneficial for dysmenorrhea.

Some low- to moderate-quality clinical research in patients with dysmenorrhea shows that moxibustion with or without acupuncture reduces pain, usually when compared with no treatment or usual care ( 29848 , 29850 , 29851 , 29852 , 52075 , 96054 , 106699 , 108118 ). One clinical study shows that using moxibustion 5-7 days before menstruation improves pain duration, but not severity, when compared with using moxibustion at the onset of menstruation ( 106699 ). Additional clinical research shows that moxibustion reduces pain intensity similarly to ibuprofen 300 mg twice daily for 3 days, starting the day before menstruation and repeated for 3 cycles. Furthermore, at 3 months after treatment discontinuation, pain intensity remains 13% lower in the patients that received moxibustion ( 96054 ).

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

Moxibustion administration seems to be beneficial for diarrhea-predominant IBS (IBS-D). It is unclear if it is beneficial for constipation-predominant IBS (IBS-C).

Most clinical research shows that moxibustion is beneficial in patients with IBS-D ( 96055 , 108117 , 108122 , 108123 ). Moxibustion with acupuncture appears to be more effective than sham moxibustion and acupuncture for improving abdominal pain, discomfort, gas, bloating, and stool consistency ( 29797 ). A meta-analysis of low- to moderate-quality research shows that moxibustion over 2-4 weeks is modestly more beneficial than various pharmaceutical agents for reducing overall symptoms ( 108123 ). Also, additional clinical research in patients with IBS-D shows that moxibustion three times weekly for 6 weeks produces adequate relief in about 77% to 82% of patients, compared with 37% to 42% of those given sham or placebo moxibustion. There were also overall improvements in stool form and symptom severity. This research suggests that beneficial effects lasted for up to an additional 18 weeks after treatment completion in up to 76% of patients ( 108117 , 108122 ). Conversely, the available research in patients with IBS-C is limited to a small clinical study showing no benefit with the use of warming moxibustion six times weekly for 4 weeks when compared with baseline or electroacupuncture ( 100768 ).

Osteoarthritis.

Moxibustion administration seems to be beneficial for knee osteoarthritis.

Meta-analyses of small clinical studies in patients with knee osteoarthritis show that moxibustion modestly reduces pain and improves function when compared with sham moxibustion or usual care ( 96047 , 103952 ). A subgroup analysis suggests that moxibustion improves osteoarthritis pain when compared with taking diclofenac, but not when compared with taking celecoxib ( 96047 ). The validity of this study is limited by the low quality and heterogeneity of the available research, as well as a high risk for publication bias. Some research has compared different types of moxibustion. A clinical trial in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that receiving Jade moxibustion improves pain to a greater degree when compared with receiving traditional moxibustion. Jade moxibustion involved wearing heated knee pads containing a jade stone for 20 minutes 3 times weekly for 4 weeks ( 103945 ). Another clinical study in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that receiving moxibustion in 12 sessions over 6 weeks via a specific electrical device (Cettum, K-medical) improves pain similarly to traditional indirect moxibustion and to a greater extent than usual care ( 106688 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Cancer.

Moxibustion administration does not seem to be beneficial for cancer treatment.

A meta-analysis of 5 clinical studies shows that moxibustion does not improve treatment response in patients with various cancer types when compared with standard care ( 29839 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Acne.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for acne, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this purpose.

Ankylosing spondylitis.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for ankylosing spondylitis.

A meta-analysis of results from three clinical studies shows that moxibustion might improve symptoms of ankylosing spondylitis similarly to treatment with sulfasalazine ( 29792 ). Another meta-analysis of clinical trials in patients with ankylosing spondylitis shows that adding moxibustion to standard treatment seems to improve clinical effectiveness and increase flexibility when compared with standard treatment alone ( 103946 ). However, the interpretation of these findings is limited, as clinical effectiveness was not clearly defined. The available research was conducted in China; it is unclear if these results are generalizable to all geographic locations.

Arrhythmia.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for arrhythmia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this purpose.

Asthma.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for asthma.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that heat-sensitive moxibustion may be as effective as Western medicine treatments, usually fluticasone/salmeterol, for reducing asthma symptoms and improving measures of pulmonary function in adults ( 96053 ).

Atherosclerosis.

Moxibustion administration has only been evaluated in combination with acupuncture; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with carotid atherosclerosis shows that moxibustion with acupuncture might improve measures of atherosclerosis and blood flow when compared with taking enteric-coated aspirin ( 29758 ).

Athletic performance.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for improving athletic performance, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this purpose.

Bell palsy.

Moxibustion administration has only been evaluated in combination with acupuncture; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Some preliminary clinical research shows that moxibustion, when used in combination with acupuncture, might improve symptoms and reduce facial disability in patients with Bell palsy when compared with various controls; however, not all research has confirmed this finding ( 29760 , 29762 , 108115 ).

Cancer-related fatigue.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for reducing fatigue in patients with cancer; the available research is conflicting.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with cancer shows that infrared laser moxibustion three times weekly for 4 weeks reduces fatigue by 32% when compared with sham moxibustion. This effect persists for at least 4 weeks after treatment ( 96050 ). Conversely, a clinical study in patients with cancer shows that using both moxibustion and sham moxibustion 30 minutes twice weekly for 8 weeks improves fatigue similarly to usual care. Effects persisted during the 4-week follow-up period for those who had received moxibustion, but not those who had received sham moxibustion ( 106698 ).

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for CVD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Chemotherapy-induced leukopenia.

Moxibustion administration in combination with acupuncture might be beneficial for chemotherapy-induced leukopenia; however, the effect of moxibustion alone is unclear.

A meta-analysis of 17 moderate-quality studies in patients with chemotherapy-induced leukopenia shows that receiving acupuncture-moxibustion therapy improves the total effective rate by 24%, as well as the leukocyte count, when compared with medical therapy. Subgroup analyses revealed that the type and duration of acupuncture-moxibustion therapy explained at least some of the heterogeneity observed in total effective rate and leukocyte count, respectively ( 106690 ). Conversely, a clinical trial in postoperative breast cancer patients receiving adjuvant chemotherapy shows that receiving mild moxibustion once daily, beginning on the second day after each 21-day cycle and continuing until the day before the subsequent cycle, does not improve the incidence of grade 0-4 reductions in white blood cell, absolute neutrophil, hemoglobin, or platelet counts throughout the course of chemotherapy when compared with control ( 106691 ).

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV).

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for CINV.

A meta-analysis of two preliminary clinical studies shows that adjuvant treatment with moxibustion along with chemotherapy can reduce the frequency of CINV by 62% when compared with chemotherapy alone ( 29839 ). Moxibustion may also be beneficial when used in conjunction with antiemetic treatment. Another meta-analysis of seven small preliminary clinical studies shows that adding moxibustion to conventional antiemetic treatment reduces CINV when compared with antiemetic treatment alone ( 100767 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the small size and low quality of the included studies.

Child growth.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion to improve growth in children, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this use.

Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS).

Early research suggests that moxibustion administration can improve symptoms of CFS.

A meta-analysis and systematic review of low- to moderate-quality clinical research show that various types of moxibustion may improve symptoms of CFS ( 29796 , 108114 ). A meta-analysis of 15 studies in patients with CFS shows that moxibustion increases the odds of improvement or cure by about 4.6-fold when compared with acupuncture and 6.4-fold when compared with medication or vitamins. There was also a modest improvement in fatigue when compared with controls including acupuncture, medication, and vitamins ( 108114 ). Also, a clinical study in patients with CFS shows that use of 24 sessions of ginger-indirect moxibustion with acupuncture over 24 weeks is modestly more beneficial than acupuncture alone for reducing fatigue. However, it is no more effective for symptoms of depression or anxiety ( 108121 ).

Chronic kidney disease (CKD).

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for CKD.

A meta-analysis of 23 studies in patients with CKD shows that adjuvant use of moxibustion along with basic treatment reduces serum creatinine by 0.2 mg/dL, but does not improve estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), when compared with basic treatment alone. Subgroup analyses suggest greater improvements when moxibustion is used for a longer duration or via indirect methods ( 106697 ). This analysis is limited by high risk of bias, including publication bias, and heterogeneity of the included studies.

Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for COPD.

Preliminary clinical research shows that moxibustion used along with conventional Western medicine modestly reduces symptoms and improves pulmonary function when compared with conventional Western medicine alone in patients with COPD. The overall efficacy rate is about 91% with moxibustion plus conventional Western medicine, compared to 75% with conventional Western medicine alone ( 29770 ).

Chronic prostatitis and chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CP/CPPS).

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for CP/CPPS, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Constipation.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for constipation.

A meta-analysis of 10 clinical studies in patients with constipation shows that use of moxibustion daily or every other day for 1-8 weeks improves the clinical effectiveness rate when compared with a control of either Western or Chinese medicine treatment methods. Moxibustion also improved first defecation time, clinical symptom scores, and stool quality ( 106686 ). This analysis is limited by significant heterogeneity in moxibustion regimen, as well as publication bias.

Crohn disease.

Moxibustion administration has only been evaluated in combination with acupuncture; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Clinical research in patients with Crohn disease shows that combining herb-partitioned moxibustion with acupuncture 3 times weekly for 12 weeks induces remission in 74% of patients, compared with only 36% of patients given sham moxibustion and superficial acupuncture. Overall disease activity was also reduced by 51% in the moxibustion plus acupuncture group, compared with only 17% in the control group ( 96046 ).

Dementia.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for dementia.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with vascular dementia shows that indirect moxibustion for 4 weeks might modestly improve symptoms when compared with piracetam ( 29778 , 29779 ). The validity of these studies is limited by their short duration and the various moxibustion regimens used.

Depression.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration, alone or in combination with acupuncture, is beneficial for depression, including post-stroke depression.

Preliminary clinical research shows that direct moxibustion combined with acupuncture and intradermal needling might improve symptoms of depression in some patients ( 29843 ). However, a small clinical study in adults with mild to moderate depression shows that electroacupuncture in combination with moxibustion for 20 sessions over 8 weeks does not improve depressive symptoms when compared with sham control ( 106701 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the significant difference in depression severity between the two groups at baseline. In patients with post-stroke depression, some preliminary clinical research shows that moxibustion over 3-12 weeks may be modestly beneficial ( 29845 , 29846 , 108119 , 108126 ). A meta-analysis shows with low certainty that adding moxibustion to other conventional and/or traditional treatments modestly reduces symptoms of depression when compared to treatment without moxibustion ( 108119 ). Also, some small studies suggest that, when used in combination with acupuncture, moxibustion may be as effective as fluoxetine for reducing symptoms after 4 weeks ( 29845 , 29846 ). However, the duration of these studies may have been inadequate to identify any benefit from fluoxetine.

Diabetes.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for diabetes, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Diabetic neuropathy.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for diabetic neuropathy.

A meta-analysis of 11 preliminary studies in patients with diabetic neuropathy shows that use of moxibustion improves both motor and sensory nerve conduction velocities of the median and peroneal nerves when compared with control, usually mecobalamin (vitamin B12) or other vitamins ( 106692 ). However, this analysis is limited by heterogeneity of the included studies.

Diarrhea.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for acute diarrhea in children.

Preliminary clinical research shows that both warming and drug-separated moxibustion decrease time to diarrhea resolution in children with acute viral diarrhea. Moxibustion appears to be at least as effective as use of an intestinal adsorbent ( 29783 , 29784 ).

Dry eye.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for dry eye.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with dry eye shows that the combination of acupuncture and moxibustion improves tear production when compared with sodium hyaluronate eye drops ( 88306 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that moxibustion with thunder-fire herbal moxa stick, as well as massage of acupoints and lacrimal glands around the eye, might improve symptoms of dry eye, such as tear secretion and dryness sensation, when compared to control treatment with "leiran diyanye" ( 88307 ).

Epilepsy.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for epilepsy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for fibromyalgia.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with fibromyalgia shows that herb-partitioned moxibustion combined with standard drug therapy modestly improves fibromyalgia symptoms, including pain and depression, when compared with an observation group treated with standard drug therapy alone ( 29795 ).

Frozen shoulder.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for frozen shoulder.

Preliminary clinical research shows that salt-moxibustion in bamboo circle has similar efficacy to electroacupuncture for reducing pain and improving function in patients with frozen shoulder ( 29757 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that moxibustion with acupuncture significantly improves symptoms of frozen shoulder when compared with acupuncture alone in some patients ( 29785 ). It is unclear how moxibustion compares with control or other standard therapies.

Gastritis.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for gastritis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Gout.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for gout, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Hearing loss.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for hearing loss, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Hemiplegic shoulder pain.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for hemiplegic shoulder pain.

A small clinical study in patients with post-stroke shoulder pain given standard treatment shows that moxibustion with acupuncture over 4 weeks modestly reduces pain when compared with acupuncture alone ( 108126 ).

Herpes zoster (shingles).

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for shingles, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

HIV/AIDS-related peripheral neuropathy.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for HIV/AIDS-related peripheral neuropathy, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Hypercholesterolemia.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for hypercholesterolemia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Hypertension.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for hypertension.

Preliminary clinical research shows that moxibustion does not improve blood pressure in patients with modestly elevated blood pressure ( 29859 ). The effects of moxibustion on blood pressure in patients with hypertension are conflicting and unclear ( 29858 ).

Infertility.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for infertility, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Insomnia.

Early research suggests that moxibustion administration is beneficial for insomnia.

Most low-quality clinical research shows that moxibustion may be beneficial for insomnia ( 29801 , 29803 , 96051 , 108128 ). A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research shows that moxibustion, administered for up to one month, is 16% to 22% more effective than Western medicine treatments (mainly estazolam) or Chinese therapies for improving symptoms of insomnia, such as sleep duration and quality ( 96051 ). Individual preliminary clinical trials in a systematic review also show that moxibustion alone or as an add-on with conventional medicine modestly reduces symptoms of insomnia and might reduce the need for medication when compared with conventional medicine alone or usual care ( 108128 ). Preliminary clinical research also shows that moxibustion with acupuncture, auricular plaster therapy, or Chinese tuina improves insomnia in some patients ( 29801 , 29803 ).

Labor pain.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for labor pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that moxibustion applied to acupoints shortens labor duration and reduces pain when compared with sham moxibustion applied to non-acupoints ( 29884 ).

Laryngitis.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for laryngitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Lymphedema.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for lymphedema.

Meta-analyses show that moxibustion, alone or with acupuncture, might have modest beneficial effects on some, but not all, measures of lymphedema related to breast cancer when compared with usual care, diosmin, or pneumatic circulation. However, each meta-analysis included only two clinical trials of generally low- to moderate-quality ( 108116 ).

Malaria.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for malaria, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Menopausal symptoms.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for hot flashes.

Preliminary clinical research in menopausal adults shows that receiving moxibustion in 14 sessions over 4 weeks might reduce the frequency and severity of hot flashes and improve quality of life when compared with a control group receiving no treatment ( 29806 ).

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for treating migraine headache.

Preliminary clinical research shows that medicated thread moxibustion is as effective as ergotamine plus caffeine for treating migraines in some patients ( 29811 ). Some research has also compared different types of moxibustion. Preliminary clinical research shows that pine needle moxibustion might be more effective than medicated thread moxibustion for treating migraine headaches ( 29810 ). Another small clinical study in patients with migraine without aura shows that use of penetrating moxibustion three times weekly for 4 weeks is associated with a total effective rate of 93%, compared with 80% with the use of mild moxibustion ( 106702 ). It is unclear if the improvements from baseline in headache pain and intensity differed between groups.

Neurodermatitis.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for neurodermatitis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Obesity.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for weight loss.

A small clinical study in females 15-18 years of age with overweight or obesity shows that use of moxibustion three times weekly for 8 weeks improves body mass index by 0.8 kg/m2 at 8 weeks, but not at 4 weeks, when compared with control. Waist-to-hip ratio, body fat ratio, and self-esteem also were improved at 8 weeks, but generally not at 4 weeks ( 106703 ).

Osteoporosis.

Moxibustion administration has only been evaluated in combination with acupuncture; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A meta-analysis of 2-3 small clinical studies in patients with osteoporosis shows that warm needle acupuncture and moxibustion seem to be associated with slight increases in bone mineral density and pain when compared with control ( 100763 ). These findings are limited by the poor quality and small size of the included studies.

Overactive bladder.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for overactive bladder.

A small clinical study in adults with overactive bladder shows that adding 8-12 sessions of moxibustion in addition to behavior training for 4 weeks modestly improves symptom scores, but does not improve lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) or overactive bladder awareness, when compared with behavioral training only ( 100762 ).

Pain (acute).

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for acute pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this purpose.

Parotitis (mumps).

Moxibustion administration has only been evaluated in combination with acupuncture; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in children with mumps shows that moxibustion with acupuncture improves recovery, as measured by reduced parotid gland swelling and pain and normalization of body temperature. However, moxibustion with acupuncture also appears to delay time to cure when compared with conventional medicine ( 29867 ).

Parturition.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for reducing the risk of breech birth; the available research is conflicting.

One early meta-analysis found no benefits with the use of moxibustion alone for reducing the risk of breech birth ( 88312 ). However, other meta-analyses and more recent clinical research show that moxibustion alone reduces the risk of breech birth when compared with control ( 29889 , 106694 , 106695 , 108120 ). When combined with acupuncture, moxibustion seems to reduce the risk of breech birth and the need for Cesarean section in some patients when compared with no treatment ( 29889 , 88312 , 106694 , 106695 ).

Pelvic pain.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for pelvic pain, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

Moxibustion administration has only been evaluated in combination with other treatments; its effect when used alone is unclear.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with PCOS shows that using moxibustion combined with herbal medicine seems to result in a higher pregnancy rate when compared with unspecified Western medicine treatments. However, its effect on ovulation rate was not clear ( 100761 ). Another meta-analysis of low- to moderate-quality clinical research in patients with PCOS shows that using moxibustion combined with acupuncture in addition to basic treatment increases pregnancy rate by about 81% and ovulation by 31%, and reduces miscarriage rate by 55%, when compared with basic treatment alone. Moxibustion was also associated with improvements in levels of luteinizing hormone (LH), testosterone, and insulin, but not follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) or estradiol ( 108129 ).

Postherpetic neuralgia.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for postherpetic neuralgia.

A meta-analysis of 13 studies in patients with postherpetic neuralgia shows that use of moxibustion for 14-35 days improves the clinical efficacy rate of treatment when compared with pharmacologic therapies, herbal medicines, or no treatment. Subgroup analyses suggest greater improvements for certain moxibustion methods, when moxibustion is compared with active controls, or when it is used for longer durations ( 106696 ). This analysis is limited by the high risk of bias, including publication bias, of the included studies.

Postoperative ileus.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for preventing postoperative ileus.

A small meta-analysis of studies in patients that have undergone abdominal or gastrointestinal surgery shows that using moxibustion along with usual care improves time to first flatus and time to bowel sound recovery by 15 hours and 13 hours, respectively, when compared with usual care alone ( 106687 ). This analysis is limited by significant heterogeneity in moxibustion regimen, as well as risk of bias and unclear reporting.

Postoperative recovery.

Moxibustion administration has only been evaluated in combination with acupuncture; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research shows that a combination of acupuncture, electroacupuncture, and moxibustion might improve urinary retention and bladder function recovery in patients following a hysterectomy when compared with using any of these modalities alone ( 88303 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a placebo control.

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for PMS.

Preliminary clinical research shows that hand moxibustion might reduce symptoms of PMS, such as abdominal pain, bloating, hot flashes, and mood changes, in some patients when compared with no treatment ( 29822 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a placebo control.

Psoriasis.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for psoriasis.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with psoriasis shows that use of Zhuang medicine medicated thread moxibustion every two days in conjunction with conventional Western medicine for 30 days modestly improves itching, overall area and severity of psoriasis, and quality of life when compared with conventional treatments alone, which included pidotimod, B vitamins, and topical cod liver oil/zinc oxide. A 75% reduction in overall psoriasis area and severity occurred in 43% of patients given moxibustion, compared with only 11% of those in the control group. Benefits lasted for at least one month after treatment ( 108124 ).

Respiratory tract infections.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for treating or preventing respiratory tract infections, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this purpose.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for RA.

A meta-analysis of mainly low-quality clinical studies shows that moxibustion, when used alone or in combination with anti-inflammatory medication or acupuncture, does not improve symptoms or disease markers in patients with RA when compared with conventional treatments ( 29792 , 29896 ). However, another meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients with RA shows that moxibustion might reduce pain and C-reactive protein (CRP) levels when compared with control. There was no effect of moxibustion on rheumatoid factor ( 100764 ).

Schizophrenia.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for schizophrenia, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Stroke.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for stroke.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that moxibustion might improve motor function in patients post-stroke, but it does not appear to improve activities of daily living, when compared with no treatment ( 29899 ). However, due to the small size and low quality of the available studies, the impact of moxibustion with or without acupuncture on urinary function and spastic paralysis is unclear ( 29899 , 29900 ). Additionally, the validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a placebo control.

Tuberculosis.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for tuberculosis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Ulcerative colitis.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for ulcerative colitis.

A meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research in patients with ulcerative colitis shows that moxibustion with or without acupuncture improves response rate by 24% when compared to control treatment. Subgroup analysis shows that moxibustion with or without acupuncture improves response rate by 23% when compared with sulfasalazine and by 33% when compared with sulfasalazine plus metronidazole or prednisone ( 29860 ). These results are limited by the small number, size, and low quality of the included studies.

Urethral syndrome.

Moxibustion administration has only been evaluated in combination with acupuncture; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research shows that moxibustion combined with acupuncture may help reduce urological symptoms and improve urine flow rates in females with urethral syndrome when compared with unspecified Western medicine treatments ( 88301 ).

Urinary incontinence.

It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for post-stroke urinary incontinence.

A small meta-analysis in patients with post-stroke urinary incontinence shows that moxibustion improves symptoms, including the average volume per void, frequency of caregiver assistance at night, and frequency of daytime urination, as well as severity of urinary incontinence, when compared with usual care ( 106700 ). This analysis is limited by the risk of bias and heterogeneity of the included studies.

Vertigo.

Although there is interest in using moxibustion for vertigo, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

Cervical spondylosis . It is unclear if moxibustion administration is beneficial for cervical spondylosis.

A meta-analysis of small, low-quality clinical studies conducted in China in patients with cervical spondylosis shows that thunder-fire moxibustion reduces pain and cervical vertigo and improves patient satisfaction when compared with controls such as usual care, acupuncture, traditional moxibustion, or physical therapy ( 103947 ). The validity of these findings is limited because heterogeneous control groups were pooled together. It is unclear if these results are generalizable to all geographic locations.

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . Although there is interest in using moxibustion for TMD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of moxibustion for this condition.

More evidence is needed to rate moxibustion for these uses.

Moxibustion involves burning moxa, a dried herbal preparation made of the aged, powdered mugwort herb, at prescribed points on the body. These points are determined based on the patients' symptoms and diagnosis ( 88308 , 96055 , 100764 ). There are various techniques used in moxibustion: Indirect moxibustion (interposed moxibustion) : The ignited moxa cone does not contact the skin directly. Instead, it is separated from the skin by ingredients such as ginseng, salt, garlic, ginger, or aconite cake ( 100764 , 108119 , 108127 ). Direct moxibustion : The ignited moxa cone is used directly to warm the skin surface at the acupuncture point. Direct moxibustion can be "scarring," which involves burning moxa directly on the skin, or "warming," which involves burning moxa above the skin ( 100764 ). Thunder-fire moxibustion : Moxa-cigars held by a moxibustion box are ignited over the treatment site. The fire head is held about an inch away from the skin and burned for about 30 minutes. To maintain the heat and reduce smoke in the air, the moxibustion treatment is covered with a thick towel ( 100766 ). Warm needle moxibustion/acupuncture : This is an integration of acupuncture and moxibustion. A 1-inch moxa stick is put on the handle of an acupuncture needle which has been inserted in the body and ignited. This method is purported to warm the meridians and promote the flow of qi and blood ( 100763 ). Electric moxibustion : Electric moxibustion has been developed because there are safety concerns with traditional moxibustion, such as excessive heat or potentially toxic chemical components. Electric moxibustion has adjustable and constant heat stimulation which has been safety tested and shown to increase blood flow in healthy participants ( 100760 , 108125 ).

#### ASTHMA

Theoretically, moxibustion may exacerbate asthma symptoms. Clinical reports of respiratory discomfort and asthma exacerbation due to the smoke associated with moxibustion suggest that moxibustion may not be safe for use in patients with asthma ( 29890 , 88311 , 88313 ). However, a meta-analysis of small clinical studies suggests that moxibustion is as effective as fluticasone/salmeterol for improving asthma symptoms and pulmonary function ( 96053 ).

A practice of Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), moxibustion is considered to augment and prevent the loss of qi, or the total energy or "life force" of the body. In TCM, there are 12 meridians forming a continuous pathway throughout the body. Chi circulates through the body on these meridians. Theoretically, moxibustion opens the 12 meridians and removes cold, dampness, and stagnation. This theoretically improves consciousness and helps avoid the collapse of qi, especially of the yang qi. Moxa is considered to have a warming yang nature and can prevent patients from experiencing a collapse of yang-qi energy ( 29752 ). In China, yin and yang are two forces that control the universe. Virtually, all medical problems are considered to be due to imbalances in one of these forces. Yin is the feminine side of nature and includes tranquility, darkness, cold, wetness, and depth. Yang is masculine and represents light, heat, activity, dryness, and height. Yin and yang are not the same as good and bad. Instead they are considered complementary forces. Chinese therapeutics intends to correct imbalances of these forces to cure disease ( 7126 ). Proponents propose that moxibustion has beneficial effects for immune function by inducing an inflammatory response, improving circulation, and increasing quantity of white blood cells ( 88315 , 88860 ). The following effects have been noted in in vitro and clinical research:

#### Analgesic effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that substance-partitioned moxibustion increases plasma levels of beta-endorphin ( 29851 ). In clinical research, a type of mild moxibustion involving indirect moxibustion has improved levels of cellular mediators involved in pain ( 96054 ).

#### Anti-aging effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that drug-separated moxibustion increases antioxidant activity of the blood. This is thought by some researchers to imply anti-aging effects ( 88317 ). Additional mechanisms for moxibustion's purported anti-aging effects include changes in cell signaling, including increases in Bcl-2 and protein kinase C expression as shown in some preliminary clinical research ( 88318 ).

#### Anti-diabetic effects

Preliminary clinical research suggests that moxibustion might improve fasting blood glucose, HbA1c, plasma endothelin, nitric oxide, urinary glucose, and malondialdehyde levels in diabetic patients ( 29780 , 88319 ). However, the mechanism of action is unknown.

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

Moxibustion is used for various inflammatory disorders, including arthritis, inflammatory bowel diseases, and soft-tissue injuries. In human research, moxibustion decreases levels of C-reactive protein ( 29819 , 96046 ). Also, animal research has shown enhanced activity of several host defense mechanisms in response to the local inflammation caused by moxibustion ( 88337 , 88338 ).

#### Blood flow effects

In humans, various types of moxibustion have increased local, brain, and whole body blood flow ( 88320 , 88321 ).

#### Bone effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that, in individuals with osteoporosis, warm needle moxibustion improves bone mineral density associated with increased estrogen and decreased osteocalcin, alkaline phosphatase, and urinary calcium/creatinine ( 29816 , 29818 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that acupuncture plus moxibustion improves common carotid artery (CCA), intima-media thickness (IMT), peak systolic velocity (PSV), pulsatility index (PI), and resistance index (RI) ( 29758 ).

#### Ergogenic effects

Preliminary clinical research shows that the improvement of fatigue associated with exercise is associated with improvements in serum creatine kinase activity ( 88324 ).

#### Gastrointestinal effects

Moxibustion is commonly used to reduce symptoms of irritable bowel syndrome, such as diarrhea. In animal research, moxibustion treatment reduces intestinal smooth muscle movements, possibly suggesting a role of substance P or other neurotransmitters ( 88325 ). A combination of acupuncture and moxibustion protects the gastric mucosa in preliminary clinical research ( 29786 , 29787 ). Plasma derived from moxibustion-treated subjects seems to protect gastric epithelial cells by preventing cellular messaging leading to apoptosis ( 88326 ).

#### Immune effects

In humans and animal models, various types of moxibustion, alone or as part of acupuncture treatment, enhance the production of cytokines  and the number and activity of cells involved in immune response ( 29842 , 88328 , 88329 , 88339 , 88340 ). The effects of moxibustion on the immune response might depend on the initial state of response. For example, activation of T cells is increased in healthy patients but decreased in patients with systemic lupus erythematosus ( 88333 ). Also, in patients with ulcerative colitis, drug-separated moxibustion possibly regulates the abnormal ratio of T-lymphocyte subgroups and corrects the abnormal state of autoimmunity ( 88332 ).

#### Neurological effects

Preliminary clinical research suggests that moxibustion increases cerebral spinal fluid levels of somatostatin and arginine vasopressin. These changes might play a role in the benefits of moxibustion on symptoms of dementia in some patients ( 29778 , 29779 ). In animal research, moxibustion seems to promote the uptake of L-tryptophan into the brain, as well as the metabolism of L-tryptophan to 5-hydroxytryptamine (5-HT) ( 108119 ).
