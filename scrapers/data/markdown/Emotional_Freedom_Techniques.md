## Overview

Emotional Freedom Techniques (EFT) is a form of psychological therapy created in the 1990s by Gary Craig. It involves tapping specific acupoints on the body and repeating positive phrases. EFT uses the philosophy of traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) and the concept of meridians. EFT is intended to remove emotional blockages and restore balance in the mind and the body ( 108205 , 108206 , 108209 , 108211 , 108213 , 108214 ).

There is insufficient reliable evidence regarding the safety of EFT; however, there is no reason to expect safety issues. EFT should not delay diagnosis or treatment, and it should not be used as the sole approach to treatment of serious conditions.

#### General

A thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted; however, there is no reason to expect safety issues with EFT.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Anxiety.

Small clinical studies suggest that EFT may be beneficial for reducing symptoms of anxiety.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research shows that EFT has a large beneficial effect on anxiety when compared with no treatment or wait list controls. However, it is no more effective than active treatments such as eye movement desensitization and reprocessing (EMDR), diaphragmatic breathing, and supportive interview. The studies in the analysis included patients with anxiety, phobias, or post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), as well as healthy volunteers with school-related anxiety ( 109420 ). Preliminary clinical research in high-ability adolescents shows EFT is more beneficial than wait list control for reducing anxiety but is no more effective than cognitive behavioral therapy ( 108203 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that EFT is modestly beneficial for reducing anxiety and anger in individuals with chronic anger ( 108211 ). In nursing students, preliminary clinical research shows that EFT modestly reduces anxiety and stress when compared with baseline ( 108207 ). EFT has also been used in combination with EMDR as part of a process called Wholistic Hybrid Derived From Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing and EFT (WHEE). A small clinical study in patients with chronic pain shows that using WHEE modestly reduces anxiety when compared with a wait-list control. However, anxiety in patients originally on the wait list was not decreased when WHEE was initiated ( 108210 ).

Depression.

Small clinical studies suggest that EFT may be beneficial for reducing symptoms of depression, although it is unclear how it compares to conventional treatments.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in a variety of populations with or without clinically diagnosed depression shows that EFT has a large effect on symptoms of depression when compared with baseline, treatment as usual, or treatments like diaphragmatic breathing and supportive interview. However, it is no more effective than eye movement desensitization and reprocessing (EMDR) ( 108212 ). Preliminary clinical research in postmenopausal adults with mild to moderate depression shows that EFT over 8 weeks modestly reduces feelings of depression when compared with sham treatment. Approximately 63% of individuals using EFT were free of depression after treatment, compared with 34% of those using sham treatment ( 108208 ). EFT has also been used in combination with EMDR as part of a process called Wholistic Hybrid Derived From Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing and EFT (WHEE). In patients with chronic pain, using this combination treatment modestly reduces depression when compared with either a wait-list control or baseline ( 108210 ).

Fibromyalgia.

It is unclear if EFT is beneficial for fibromyalgia.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with fibromyalgia shows that following an internet-based EFT program over 8 weeks modestly reduces feelings of anxiety and depression and some measures of pain, and improves vitality and social function, when compared with a wait-list control ( 108213 ).

Frozen shoulder.

Although there is interest in using EFT for frozen shoulder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of EFT for this condition.

Migraine headache.

Although there is interest in using EFT for migraine headache, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of EFT for this condition.

Multiple sclerosis-related fatigue.

It is unclear if EFT is beneficial for fatigue in adults with multiple sclerosis.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with fatigue related to MS shows that EFT over 4 weeks modestly reduces the severity of fatigue when compared with sham treatment. This benefit is maintained for at least an additional 4 weeks ( 108202 ).

Pain (chronic).

EFT has only been examined in combination with other therapies; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic pain, most often related to chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS) and/or fibromyalgia, shows that using a combination therapy involving EFT modestly reduces depression when compared with a waitlist control or baseline ( 108210 ). This therapy combines EFT with eye movement desensitization and reprocessing (EMDR) as part of a process called Wholistic Hybrid Derived From Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing and EFT (WHEE).

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Small clinical studies suggest that EFT may reduce some symptoms of PTSD.

Two meta-analyses of small preliminary clinical trials in patients with PTSD shows that undergoing EFT has a large beneficial effect on short-term symptoms of anxiety and depression when compared with no or usual treatment. Although some individual trials showed that EFT was more beneficial than eye movement desensitization and reprocessing (EMDR), this finding was not consistent ( 108214 , 109420 ). The long-term effects of EFT in this population are not clear.

Pre-procedural anxiety.

It is unclear if EFT is beneficial for reducing pre-procedural anxiety.

Preliminary clinical research in patients hospitalized for lumbar disc herniation surgery shows that EFT modestly reduces anxiety and discomfort when compared with no intervention or music listening ( 108205 ).

Psoriasis.

Although there is interest in using EFT for psoriasis, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of EFT for this condition.

Seizures.

Although there is interest in using EFT for seizures, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of EFT for this condition.

Stress.

It is unclear if EFT is beneficial for reducing stress.

Preliminary clinical research in nursing students shows that EFT modestly reduces stress and anxiety when compared with baseline ( 108207 ).

Stroke.

EFT has only been examined in combination with other therapies; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in patients who have had an ischemic stroke and are receiving standard treatment shows that the combination of EFT with a type of religious mantra, Gayatri Mantra, over seven consecutive days moderately improves quality of life when compared with standard treatment alone ( 108204 ).

Tension headache.

It is unclear if EFT is beneficial for tension headache.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with tension-type headaches shows that performing EFT twice daily for two months modestly reduces headache frequency and intensity when compared with standard care alone. Modelling of these results suggests that using EFT 10 times a month may be associated with a monthly reduction in headache episodes by 0.64 ( 108206 ).

Traumatic brain injury (TBI).

Although there is interest in using EFT for TBI, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of EFT for this condition.

More evidence is needed to rate emotional freedom techniques for these uses.

EFT is based on the premise that the cause of negative emotions is due to a disruption in the body's energy system ( 108209 , 108213 ). During EFT, a traumatic memory or trigger is paired with a reframe of self-acceptance. At the same time, specific acupoints are tapped, massaged, or held ( 108213 , 108214 , 109420 , 109421 ). In order to determine whether more work is needed with a specific trigger, the level of anxiety may be determined throughout treatment so that eventually the memory is not accompanied with much distress ( 108214 ). EFT includes the setup phase which involves naming and affirming the problem, the tapping or recall phase which involves tapping the meridian points 7-10 times each, and the gamut phase which involves performing specific actions to stimulate the brain while tapping the gamut point on the back of the hand between the fourth and fifth metacarpal bones. Affirmations are integrated into the tapping. These affirmations are to be repeated with intent, focus, and sincerity and generally include statements such as "Even though I have this problem, I deeply and profoundly accept myself" ( 108202 , 108205 , 108206 , 108213 , 109420 , 109421 ). In clinical research, the following tapping points have been used: eyebrow, side of eye, under eye, under nose, chin, collarbone, under arm, top of head, wrist, thumb, index finger, middle finger, and little finger ( 108202 , 108205 , 108206 , 108208 ). Actions to stimulate the brain include closing and opening eyes, moving the eyes hard down right and then left while holding the head steady, rolling the eyes clockwise and then counterclockwise, humming a song for two seconds, counting rapidly to five, and then humming the song again ( 108202 , 108205 , 108206 ). EFT may be applied by another individual with training or a client can be trained for self-use at home. Also, it may be used alone or in combination with other types of therapy. Training and/or treatment may be in-person alone or with a group, online, on the phone, through the use of video, and/or by providing information with a brochure. Each session lasts from ten minutes to an hour ( 108202 , 108205 , 108208 , 108209 , 108210 , 108213 , 108214 , 109420 ).

EFT is based on the philosophy of traditional Chinese medicine (TCM). According to TCM theory, the human body contains a network of energy pathways, or meridians, through which vital energy, called "qi," circulates. Illness and symptoms are thought to be caused by problems in the circulation of qi through the meridians. Both physical and mental health are thought to be improved following the removal of the obstruction of this network ( 108202 ). Following a traumatic event, the autonomic nervous system may be activated by a trigger reminiscent of the event. EFT is thought to break the cycle of sympathetic nervous system hyperarousal via awareness, exposure, reframing, and desensitization, along with acupoint stimulation ( 108203 , 108205 , 108214 ). Acupoint stimulation may result in the release of serotonin in the amygdala and prefrontal cortex, as well as in the release of opioids and gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA). This reduces the fight/flight/freeze response, resulting in a reduction in pain, a slower heart rate, and reduced blood pressure ( 108205 , 108214 , 109420 ). Some research also suggests that acupoint stimulation reduces levels of cortisol ( 108205 , 108207 , 108214 , 109420 , 109421 ) and increases theta brain waves associated with relaxation ( 108214 ). In one study there was a decrease in salivary immunoglobulin A ( 109421 ).
