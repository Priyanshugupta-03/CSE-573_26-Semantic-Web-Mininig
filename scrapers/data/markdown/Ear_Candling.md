## Overview

Ear candling, also called thermo-auricular therapy or "coning," is a home remedy used for outer-ear hygiene and the elimination of earwax. Earwax, or cerumen, is the soft, brownish yellow, waxy secretion of the ceruminous glands of the external auditory canal. It is a naturally occurring substance that cleans, protects, and lubricates the external auditory canal ( 97510 ). Normally, earwax is eliminated by self-cleaning mechanisms, aided by chewing and other jaw movements. Earwax impaction is a problem in one out of 10 children, one out of 20 adults, and one-third of geriatric or developmentally delayed populations. As a result, every year in the United States some 12 million persons seek medical attention for earwax buildup, with eight million medical procedures performed annually ( 97510 ). Ear candles are legal in the US; however, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) warns that ear candling can result in serious injuries even when used according to the manufacturer's instructions ( 97510 ). Ear candles are not permitted in Canada ( 14609 ). In Europe, ear candles are regulated as medical devices.

## People Use This For

Ear candling is used for clearing earwax and toxins from the ear canal. It is also used for rhinosinusitis, sinus pain, otitis media, tinnitus, difficulty hearing, migraine headache, vertigo, and cognitive function.

Possibly Unsafe when ear candling is used in the ear. There is concern that ear candling can cause ear canal blockage with candle wax, temporary hearing loss, infection, and other serious adverse effects ( 14608 , 16005 , 16006 , 16007 , 97511 ). Furthermore, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) warns that ear candling can result in serious injuries even when used according to the manufacturer's instructions ( 97510 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Ear candling has been linked to several adverse effects in case reports and in a survey of otolaryngologists. The most frequently reported adverse effect is burns from the lighted candle or hot wax. Other reported adverse effects include blockage of the ear canal with candle wax, temporary hearing loss, otitis externa, hair fire, and tympanic membrane perforation ( 16005 , 16006 , 97511 , 97512 ).

#### Dermatologic

Reported adverse effects include blockage of the ear canal with candle wax, temporary conductive hearing loss, otitis externa, and tympanic membrane perforation ( 16005 , 16006 , 97511 , 97512 ).

Cerumen impaction.

Preliminary clinical research shows that ear candling does not remove ear wax from the ear canal. In some cases, ear candling can cause burns, blockage of the ear canal, hearing loss, and infection ( 16006 , 97511 ). In 2017, the American Academy of Otolaryngology (AAO) published guidelines stating that ear candling is an ineffective treatment that can cause harm and should NOT be used to treat or prevent cerumen impaction ( 97510 , 97511 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of ear candling for its other uses.

Ear candling for earwax (cerumen) removal involves the placement of a hollow candle into the ear canal. The candle is lit at the other end and left in the ear for several minutes. The procedure can be self-administered or performed by another person. The candles are usually made with organic linen, beeswax, honey extract, and essential oils. At the end of a session, usually there is a dark brown waxy substance left on the end of the hollow candle. This substance is thought to be ear wax and other debris. However, research shows that this is really debris from the candle itself ( 16005 , 16007 , 97510 ).

Ear candling is primarily used for clearing earwax from the ear and for a variety of other conditions related to the auditory canal and sinuses. The procedure involves insertion of a hollow candle into the ear canal. The candle is lit and left in place for several minutes. It is theorized that the heat and suction from the hollow candle will soften and suck out substances from the ear ( 16005 , 16007 , 97510 ). However, research shows that ear candles do not have these effects ( 16006 , 97510 ). Another theory is that ear candling heats up the impacted earwax which then slowly leaks out of the ear for a period of time following the procedure ( 97510 ). Other proponents theorize that ear candling dries moisture inside the ear, which then helps to reduce the risk of infection ( 16005 ). When the candle is removed, there is a discolored, often brown, waxy substance left on the candle stub. Ear candling practitioners believe that this is earwax, bacteria, or other material extracted from the ear canal ( 16005 ). However, laboratory assessment of the brown waxy substance shows that it is actually composed of chemicals found in candle wax, and there are no constituents of earwax present ( 97510 ). Overall, there is no reliable evidence that ear candling can remove earwax or treat other conditions ( 14608 , 16005 , 16006 , 16007 ).
