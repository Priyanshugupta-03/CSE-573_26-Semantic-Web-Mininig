# Zone Diet
Other Common Names , Dieta de la zona, Régime Zone

## Overview

The Zone diet, which was popularized by Dr. Barry Sears in his 1995 book, The Zone, is a high-protein, calorie-restrictive diet. It is intended to provide adequate protein and moderate levels of carbohydrates, essential fats, and micronutrients spread throughout three meals and two snacks. Overall, the Zone diet plan consists of a dietary intake of 30% protein, 30% fat, and 40% carbohydrates ( 103115 , 103116 ). KEY HIGHLIGHTS

- Some evidence for use in obesity.
- Possibly safe when followed appropriately.

## People Use This For

The Zone diet is used for obesity, general weight loss, and athletic performance.

Likely Safe when followed appropriately. The Zone diet has been used without reports of safety concerns in multiple clinical studies ( 65645 , 103115 , 103116 , 103118 ). Additionally, there is no reason to expect safety issues.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the Zone diet in pregnancy and lactation. However, there is no reason to expect safety issues as long as nutritional needs are met.

#### General

The Zone diet appears to be generally well tolerated. No adverse effects have been reported in clinical trials ( 65645 , 103115 , 103116 , 103118 ).

Possibly Effective
Obesity.

The Zone diet has been studied for obesity, often in comparison with other branded or popular diets. Most clinical research shows that it results in weight loss, although it is unclear if it is more effective than other dieting programs ( 65645 , 103115 , 103116 ).

Clinical research in insulin-resistant women shows that following the Zone diet for 6 months results in similar reductions in body weight, waist circumference, and triglyceride and insulin levels as the Atkins diet. Both of these diets were superior to a high-carbohydrate, high-fiber diet ( 103116 ). However, another clinical study in premenopausal overweight and obese women shows that following the Zone diet for 12 months is less effective than the Atkins diet for improving weight loss and metabolic parameters. The Zone diet was similarly effective to the LEARN and Ornish diets ( 103115 ). One small clinical study in healthy adults shows that following the Zone diet for 35 days resulted in a greater reduction in body fat and skinfold thickness when compared with the diet recommended by the Italian National Research Institute for Nutrition and Foods ( 65645 ).

---

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Athletic performance.

One very small clinical study in healthy male endurance athletes shows that following the Zone diet for 7 days does not improve athletic performance and might actually reduce the time to exhaustion when compared with baseline ( 103118 ). The validity of this finding is limited by the short duration of diet adherence, the small size of the study, and the lack of a comparator group.

More evidence is needed to rate the Zone diet for this use.

The Zone diet is commonly referred to as a calorie-restrictive, high-protein diet. The diet plan also promotes drinking at least eight glasses of water a day, exercising, moderating food consumption, and limiting saturated fat intake ( 103115 , 103116 ). Adherents are advised to consume lean meats (like fish and poultry), soy products, vegetables, and fruit. The diet plan restricts carbohydrates, especially those with high glycemic content. Adherents are advised to eat five times daily, with no more than five hours between meals ( 103115 , 103116 ). The Zone diet provides a series of tables listing food types and serving sizes. Overall, the diet is composed of approximately 30% fat, 30% protein, and 40% carbohydrates. Adherents are advised to consume an appreciable amount of food from the protein table, to fill up with foods from the fruit and vegetable table, and to consume small amounts of items from the fats and oils table. Other tables identify foods that should be restricted to one serving per day ( 103115 , 103116 ). The Zone diet recommends minimization of saturated fats and stresses the intake of monounsaturated fats. The total intake of fat is generally consistent with an American Heart Association step 1 diet (10% saturated fat, 10% monounsaturated fat, 10% polyunsaturated fat) ( 103114 ).

None known.

None known.

The main components of the Zone diet are high protein and low calorie consumption. Proponents believe that the Zone diet promotes optimal metabolic efficiency in the body by balancing insulin and glucagon. Further, the Zone diet is based on the theory that low total caloric intake may be associated with longer life expectancy. This theory derives from animal studies, but has not been validated in long-term human trials ( 65645 ).
