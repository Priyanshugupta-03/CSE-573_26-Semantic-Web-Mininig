## Overview

Interest in organic food has been steadily increasing in part due to consumer concern about the quality and safety of conventional food. Organic food is grown without synthetic chemicals and involves environmentally safe farming techniques. The production of organic food via organic farming increases the use of sustainable agriculture, which has a positive impact on the environment ( 28052 , 28053 , 28054 ). However, there is extensive variability in organic food regulations across different countries, as well as substantial controversy regarding the true health benefits of organic food ( 28052 ).

## People Use This For

People consume organic food for acne, allergies, Alzheimer disease, anemia, arthritis, asthma, atherosclerosis, atopic dermatitis (eczema), atopic disease, cancer, candidiasis, cardiovascular disease (CVD), chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), cognitive function, coronary heart disease (CHD), depression, diabetes, fibromyalgia, food allergies, headache, hypercholesterolemia, hypertension, infertility, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), lead toxicity, leukemia, liver disease, lung cancer, metabolic syndrome, mercury toxicity, migraine headache, multiple myeloma, multiple sclerosis (MS), obesity, osteoporosis, osteopenia, otitis media, pain, Parkinson disease, pre-eclampsia, premenstrual syndrome (PMS), psoriasis, psychological well-being, scleroderma, stroke, systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), and vitamin and nutrient deficiencies.

Likely Safe when used orally in food amounts. Organic food as part of the diet has been used with apparent safety ( 98877 ), and there is no reason to expect safety issues. In fact, one analysis calculated that organic food has 30% lower risk of being contaminated with pesticides compared to conventional food ( 98956 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Likely Safe when used orally in food amounts. Organic food as part of the diet has been used with apparent safety in clinical research ( 95734 ), and there is no reason to expect safety issues. In fact, consuming organic food during pregnancy is associated with a lower incidence of certain birth defects such as hypospadias ( 95734 ).

#### General

Organic food is generally well tolerated ( 98877 ). There is some evidence that eating greater amounts of organic food is associated with an increased chance of developing a food allergy compared to eating nonorganic foods ( 98949 ); however, the validity of this data is limited.

#### Immunologic

Observational research found that regular and occasional consumption of organic food is associated with increased odds of food allergies compared to not consuming organic food ( 98949 ). This finding is limited because the calculation was not adjusted to account for diet-related behaviors or other confounding variables which might have influenced incidence of allergies.

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Asthma.

Two observational studies have found that regularly consuming organic food is not associated with a reduced incidence of asthma when compared with consuming mainly nonorganic food ( 98949 , 109510 ).

Atopic dermatitis (eczema).

Two observational studies have found that regularly consuming organic food until the age of 2-5 years is not associated with a reduced risk of atopic dermatitis in infants when compared with consuming mainly nonorganic food ( 98954 , 109510 ). However, a subgroup analysis found that regularly consuming organic dairy food is associated with a 36% lower odds of eczema in infants when compared with consuming mainly nonorganic dairy food ( 98954 ).

Atopic disease.

Observational research has found that regularly consuming organic food is not associated with a reduced incidence of atopic disease in infants when compared with consuming mainly nonorganic foods ( 98954 ).

Cancer.

Observational research has found that high dietary intake of organic food is associated with a modest reduction in the risk of cancer when compared with intake of mainly nonorganic food. About 167 patients need to consume a high amount of organic food to prevent one case of cancer ( 98947 ). However, conflicting evidence exits. Other observational research in middle-aged women found that consuming organic food is usually or always not associated with reduced risk of cancer overall compared to never consuming organic food. In a subgroup analysis, always or usually consuming organic food is associated with a 21% reduced risk of non-Hodgkin lymphoma in women when compared with never consuming organic food ( 98950 ). The validity of the multiple subgroup analyses were limited because they did not adjust for multiple comparisons or for history of cancer.

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

Observational research has found that regularly consuming organic food is not associated with a reduced incidence of CVD when compared with consuming mainly nonorganic food ( 98949 ).

Diabetes.

Observational research has found that regular consumption of organic food is associated with reduced odds of type 1 and type 2 diabetes when compared with consuming mainly nonorganic food ( 98949 ). This finding is limited because the calculation was not adjusted to account for diet-related behaviors or other confounding variables that might have influenced the incidence of diabetes.

Food allergies.

Observational research in children 1-6 years of age has found that regularly consuming organic food is associated with more than twice the odds of having food allergies when compared with not consuming organic food ( 109510 ).

Hypercholesterolemia.

Observational research has found that regular consumption of organic food is associated with reduced odds of hypercholesterolemia when compared with consuming mainly nonorganic food ( 98949 ). This finding is limited because the calculation was not adjusted to account for diet-related behaviors or other confounding variables that might have influenced the incidence of hypercholesterolemia.

Hypertension.

Observational research has found that regular consumption of organic food is associated with reduced odds of hypertension when compared with consuming mainly nonorganic food ( 98949 ). This finding is limited because the calculation was not adjusted to account for diet-related behaviors or other confounding variables that might have influenced the incidence of hypertension.

Metabolic syndrome.

Low-quality research on the effects of organic food on metabolic syndrome is conflicting. One observational study has found that higher consumption of organic food (38% to 100% of the diet) is associated with 31% reduced odds of metabolic syndrome when compared with lower consumption of organic food (up to only 12% of the diet) ( 98948 ). However, other population research has found that consuming organic food is not associated with reduced odds of metabolic syndrome when compared with not consuming organic food ( 109508 ).

Obesity.

A meta-analysis of observational research has found that more consumption of organic food is associated with 11% lower odds of obesity when compared with less consumption of organic food ( 98953 , 109951 ). The validity of this study is limited by high heterogeneity.

Otitis media.

Observational research has found that the risk of otitis media is lower in children whose mothers report consuming an organic food diet during pregnancy when compared with those whose mothers consume nonorganic food ( 98952 ).

Pre-eclampsia.

Population research in pregnant women has found that a higher intake of organic vegetables is associated with reduced risk of pre-eclampsia when compared with a lower intake of organic vegetables. However, there was no link between organic fruit, cereals, eggs or milk, or an overall organic diet and risk of pre-eclampsia ( 98958 ).

Psychological well-being.

Observational research has found that higher consumption of organic food is associated with increased life satisfaction when compared with lower consumption of organic food ( 98955 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the effectiveness of organic food for these uses.

Standards of organic crop production ban the use of synthetic chemicals to protect crops. They also ban the use of certain mineral fertilizers such as those containing nitrogen, potassium chloride, and superphosphate. This is done to reduce environmental damage and occurrence of pesticides in edible plants. Organic farming involves use of fertilizers like manure and composts, rotation of legume crops and preventative and non-chemical crop protection methods. Notably, some organic standards allow certain plant or microbial extract or mineral (copper) crop protection products ( 98877 ).

None known.

None known.

Many consumers prefer organic food because they believe it to be safer and more nutritious. However, historically there has been some controversy as to whether organic food is more nutritive and healthier than conventional food ( 28050 , 28051 , 28052 , 98877 ). Organic food might be safer to consume than some conventional foods because it contains fewer pesticides. Common pesticides used in conventional but not organic farming include organophosphates and pyrethroids. In a meta-analysis of research, the occurrence of pesticide residues and the toxic metal cadmium was shown to be greater in conventional foods compared to organic foods ( 98877 ). However, a meta-analysis of wheat grains found that organic grains contained a greater excess of heavy metals, especially chromium and arsenic, when compared with conventional grains ( 101183 ). Some other clinical research shows that, when compared to children and adults that eat non-organic diets, children and adults eating organic foods have reduced pesticide metabolites in the urine, suggesting that they ingest less pesticides ( 28057 , 28058 , 28059 , 101182 ). In addition to other risks, pesticides are thought to reduce the quality of human semen, possibly contributing to infertility. Some preliminary clinical evidence shows that farmers eating a high proportion of organic fruits and vegetables have more morphologically normal sperm compared to those eating fruits and vegetables that are not organic. However, there was no difference between groups in sperm motility and quantity ( 28056 ). It is unclear if organic food is more nutritive than conventional food. Some experts believe that consuming a well-balanced diet overshadows the importance of choosing organic over conventional foods ( 28050 ). Although some research has found that the concentrations of antioxidants such as phenolic acids, flavanones, stilbenes, flavones, flavonols, and anthocyanins are substantially higher in organic crop foods, other studies do not consistently support this finding ( 98877 , 101184 , 101185 , 106457 , 109509 ). Some research has also found that levels of select antioxidants are higher in conventionally grown crops when compared with organically grown crops ( 109509 ). Additionally, the clinical benefit of increased antioxidant concentrations is unclear. In a small clinical study in healthy volunteers, intake of an organic diet increased protein oxidation and decreased total plasma antioxidant capacity compared to baseline, but there was no difference in protein oxidation and antioxidant capacity between people that ate organic food and people that ate conventional food ( 70320 ). Also, although consumption of certain organic foods increases plasma levels of alpha-carotene, beta-carotene, lutein, and zeaxanthin to a greater extent than conventional foods, there are no differences in plasma levels of alpha-tocopherol, retinol, or the carotenoids beta-cryptoxanthin and lycopene ( 101181 ). Several large observational studies suggest that consumption of organic food is inversely associated with certain biomarkers of inflammation ( 106484 ). It has also been inversely associated with body mass index (BMI), rate of childhood and adult obesity, and risk of type 2 diabetes ( 106455 , 106485 ). Some research has also found differences in the nutritional composition of organic dairy and meat products when compared to conventional products. Theoretically, these differences might be attributed to different types of agricultural feed. A meta-analysis of research shows that compared to conventional dairy products, organic dairy products contain more protein, cis-9,trans-11 conjugated linoleic acid, trans-11 vaccenic acid, and omega-3 fatty acids such as alpha-linolenic acid, eicosapentanoic acid, and docosapentanoic acid. However there was no difference between conventional and organic dairy products in levels of vitamin E and beta-carotene ( 98878 ). Another meta-analysis of research found that when compared to conventional meat, organic meat contains a higher level of polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA). Increased dietary PUFA is associated with reduced risk of cardiovascular disease ( 98879 ).
