# Wallflower
Scientific Name Erysimum cheiri (synonym: Cheiranthus cheiri) Family Brassicaceae/Cruciferae. Other Common Names Beeflower, Erysimum, Gillyflower, Giroflée, Giroflée des Murailles, Giroflée Jaune, Giroflier, Handflower, Keiri, Ravenelle, Vélar, Violier Jaune, Wallstock-Gillofer
|

Caution

## Overview

Wallflower is an herb that contains cardiac glycosides. Although it has some historical use as a medicine, it is now considered obsolete as a medicinal herb ( 18 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, wallflower is used for cardiac insufficiency, to encourage menstruation, and as a laxative. It is also used orally for liver and gallbladder diseases because of its bitter taste.

Possibly Unsafe when used orally. Wallflower contains cardiac glycosides, which might cause toxic effects ( 18 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Possibly Unsafe when used orally due to its cardiac glycoside content and possible toxic effects ( 18 ); avoid using.

#### General

Wallflower contains cardiac glycosides, which can cause serious adverse effects. However, the extent of absorption after oral administration is unclear ( 18 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of wallflower.

#### Adult

No typical dosage.

#### CORTICOSTEROIDS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Use of corticosteroids may cause electrolyte depletion and increase risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity ( 19 ).

---

#### DIGOXIN (Lanoxin)
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Contraindicated; therapeutic duplication increases risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity ( 2 ).

---

#### DIURETIC DRUGS
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Theoretically, concomitant use of potassium-depleting diuretics and wallflower can increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity due to potassium depletion ( 506 ). Some diuretics that can deplete potassium include chlorothiazide (Diuril), chlorthalidone (Thalitone), furosemide (Lasix), hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ, Hydrodiuril, Microzide), and others.

---

#### QUINIDINE
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Cardiac medications, including quinidine may increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity; contraindicated ( 18 ).

---

#### QUININE
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Taking quinine with wallflower may increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity ( 18 ).

---

#### STIMULANT LAXATIVES
Interaction Rating Moderate Be cautious with this combination.
Severity HIGH Occurrence POSSIBLE Level of Evidence D (Theoretical based on pharmacology)

Overuse or misuse of stimulant laxatives may cause electrolyte depletion and increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity ( 19 ).

|

#### CALCIUM SUPPLEMENTS

Calcium supplements might increase the adverse effects of wallflower ( 18 ).

---

#### CARDIAC GLYCOSIDE-CONTAINING HERBS

Contraindicated, concomitant use may increase the risk of cardiac glycoside toxicity. Cardiac glycoside-containing herbs include black hellebore, Canadian hemp roots, digitalis leaf, hedge mustard, figwort, lily-of-the-valley roots, motherwort, oleander leaf, pheasant's eye plant, pleurisy root, squill bulb leaf scales, and strophanthus seeds ( 19 ).

---

#### CINCHONA

Use with wallflower seed may increase risk of toxicity ( 19 ).

---

#### EPHEDRA (Ma huang)

Use with wallflower seed may increase risk of toxicity ( 19 ).

---

#### HORSETAIL

Theoretically, overuse or misuse of horsetail with cardiac glycoside-containing herbs increases the risk of cardiac toxicity due to potassium depletion ( 19 ).

---

#### LICORICE

Theoretically, overuse or misuse of licorice with cardiac glycoside-containing herbs increases the risk of cardiac toxicity due to potassium depletion ( 19 ).

---

#### STIMULANT LAXATIVE HERBS

Theoretically, overuse or misuse of stimulant laxatives with cardiac glycoside-containing herbs increases the risk of cardiac toxicity due to potassium depletion. Stimulant laxative herbs include aloe, alder buckthorn, black root, blue flag, butternut bark, colocynth, European buckthorn, fo ti, gamboge, gossypol, greater bindweed, jalap, manna, Mexican scammony root, rhubarb, senna, and yellow dock ( 19 ).

#### CARDIAC CONDITIONS

Wallflower may cause arrhythmias due to its cardiac glycoside content ( 18 ); use with caution.

None known.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the toxicology of wallflower.

#### Absorption

When wallflower is taken orally, its cardiac glycosides may be poorly absorbed ( 18 ).

#### General

All parts of wallflower have been used medicinally ( 108937 ). They contain cardiac glycosides, which are also known as cardenolides or cardioactive steroids, and flavonoids, including isorhamnetin, kaempferol, and quercetin ( 18 , 108937 ). The seeds contain cardiac glycosides such as cheiroside A, cheirotoxin, and neouzarin, and several glucosinolates and isothiocyanates such as cheirolin and iberin ( 108937 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

In vitro, extracts of all parts of wallflower plants have anti-inflammatory activity when compared with diclofenac, with the strongest activity being in the root and flower ( 108937 ).
