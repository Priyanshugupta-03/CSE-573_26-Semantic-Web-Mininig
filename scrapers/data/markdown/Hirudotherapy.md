## Overview

Leeches (Hirudo medicinalis) are ectoparasitic annelids (segmented worms) that live in freshwater pools and ditches in temperate regions ( 106753 , 106755 ). Hirudotherapy is the use of leeches to remove pooled or coagulated blood from patients for medical reasons and has been in use since ancient times. A topical gel containing leech saliva has been used more recently to relieve pain ( 99396 ). Hirudotherapy has also traditionally been used for various conditions unrelated to wounds and to correct an imbalance of the humors.

Companies that began marketing leeches after 1976 are required to obtain approval from the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) ( 15855 ).

Likely Safe when used topically and appropriately under the supervision of a healthcare provider. Specially certified leeches for use in hirudotherapy are considered a medical device by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) ( 15855 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid use.

#### General

Hirudotherapy is generally well tolerated when used appropriately.

#### Most Common Adverse Effects

Topically : Itching, bleeding, skin redness, and vesicle formation around the application site.

#### Serious Adverse Effects (Rare)

Topically : Anemia, contact dermatitis, hypotension, necrotic ulcers, pseudolymphoma, skin infections.

#### Cardiovascular

Topically, hirudotherapy has been associated with hypotension and vasovagal attacks ( 106753 ).

#### Dermatologic

Topically, hirudotherapy can cause itching, pain, vesicle formation, and skin redness at the application site. Less often, it can cause necrotic ulcers, keloid formation, allergic or irritant contact dermatitis, and skin infections. Use of leeches can sometimes cause infections with Aeromonas hydrophila, which is found in the leech gut, and also with parasites. Some of these infections can be resistant to antibiotics ( 99392 , 99394 , 99395 , 99397 , 99398 , 106752 , 106753 ). A case report describes a 59-year-old female who presented with redness and swelling on the face and around the eyes, fever, and leukocytosis after using hirudotherapy for chronic headache ( 106756 ). In another case, a 34-year-old male developed a swollen, itchy, purple-colored rash on the legs and back, and white, reticulated plaques in the mouth a week after using hirudotherapy for knee and leg pain. He also had hyperkeratosis, irregular acanthosis, hypergranulosis, and a lymphocyte-dense cell infiltrate in the upper dermis. He was diagnosed with lichen planus and treated with systemic and topical corticosteroids and antihistamine. The condition resolved within 2 weeks ( 106754 ). There have been several case reports of pseudolymphomas associated with hirudotherapy. A pseudolymphoma is a benign proliferation of lymphoid cells in the skin which presents similarly to malignant lymphoma ( 99390 , 99393 ).  Pseudolymphoma typically presents with firm, pruritic, erythematous, brown or purple papules and nodules, from several weeks to several months after leech therapy. On biopsy, the dermal infiltrate consists mainly of lymphocytes, with some histiocytes, eosinophils, and plasma cells. Treatment is with intralesional, topical, or oral corticosteroids. Cryotherapy has also been used ( 106752 , 106753 ).

#### Gastrointestinal

Topically, hirudotherapy has been associated with an infected anal abscess. A 32-year-old male presented with severe anal pain and swelling 3 days after hirudotherapy for hemorrhoids. The abscess required surgical drainage and treatment with antibiotics. The predominant organism identified was Escherichia coli ( 106755 ). A case report describes a 43-year-old healthy male with upper gastrointestinal bleeding 1 week after receiving hirudotherapy on the lower back and suprapubic region. He was admitted to the intensive care unit, transfused blood products, and then discharged after 1 week without complications ( 112898 ).

#### Hematologic

Topically, hirudotherapy can cause prolonged bleeding and anemia ( 99399 , 106752 , 106753 ). In a case report, a young adult male presented with neck bleeding after self-applying leeches for acne. Bleeding was controlled by suturing ( 99399 ). A case series in critically ill pediatric patients describes bleeding that led to hirudotherapy discontinuation in 5 of 7 cases, and 4 of the patients expired secondary to multiorgan failure ( 112900 ). However, it is unclear if hirudotherapy contributed to mortality; the patients were already critically ill.

#### Ocular/Otic

Topically, hirudotherapy applied directly to or around the eyes might lead to ocular complications. One case reports diffuse superficial and deep conjunctival punctures with subconjunctival hemorrhage and hematoma in 50-year-old male who received leeches directly to the eyes for a few minutes ( 112896 ). Another case reports infective keratitis requiring hospitalization after facial and periocular hirudotherapy in an 85-year-old female ( 112897 ). These patients recovered following treatment that included topical antibiotics and topical steroids for 2-3 weeks ( 112896 , 112897 ).

Likely Effective
Skin grafts.

Hirudotherapy is approved as a medical device for the healing of skin grafts in the United States.

Medicinal leeches applied topically, as in hirudotherapy, are approved as a medical device by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as an adjunct to the healing of graft tissue for issues related to venous congestion ( 15855 ).

---

Possibly Effective
Osteoarthritis.

Hirudotherapy seems to be helpful in osteoarthritis, especially in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee.

A meta-analysis of four small clinical trials in patients with knee osteoarthritis shows that hirudotherapy improves pain by about 5.7 points on the Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Osteoarthritis Index (WOMAC) scale after one week and by 4.7 points after 4 weeks when compared with control ( 99398 ). A small clinical study in patients with osteoarthritis of the knee shows that applying 4-6 leeches to the knee once seems to improve function, stiffness, and pain comparably to topical diclofenac applied twice daily for 28 days ( 28996 ). In addition to using live leeches, hirudotherapy can involve applying a gel made from leech saliva. In a small clinical study in patients with knee osteoarthritis, using a gel liposomal extract of leech saliva in addition to exercise reduces pain when compared with exercise alone ( 99396 ). Although most studies have been for osteoarthritis of the knee, hirudotherapy has also been used for osteoarthritis of the hand. A small clinical study in females with osteoarthritis of the first carpometacarpal joint shows that receiving hirudotherapy  once weekly for 30 days improves pain to a greater degree when compared with topical diclofenac ( 99394 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Tendinopathy.

It is unclear if hirudotherapy is beneficial for tendinopathy.

A small clinical study in patients with chronic lateral epicondylitis shows that hirudotherapy applied for about 45 minutes once weekly reduces pain to a greater degree when compared with diclofenac gel 1% twice daily ( 99391 ).

Venous leg ulcers.

It is unclear if hirudotherapy is beneficial for improving symptoms of venous leg ulcers.

Preliminary clinical research suggests that using hirudotherapy might help healing and decrease swelling and hyperpigmentation of venous leg ulcers in patients with varicose veins ( 28994 ).

Wound healing.

Although there has been interest in using hirudotherapy for wound healing unrelated to skin grafts, including for gangrene and abscesses, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of hirudotherapy for this purpose.

More evidence is needed to rate hirudotherapy for these uses.

Leeches used for hirudotherapy should be applied by a qualified healthcare professional. The skin is cleaned before the leech is attached. If the leech does not bite at first, a blood drop may be used to entice it to bite. Hirudotherapy often involves applying 1-6 leeches topically and leaving on the skin until they fall off, which usually occurs after about 40 minutes. The process is then repeated. This application process has been used in clinical studies for pain conditions such as osteoarthritis and tendinopathies, as well as for skin grafts ( 15855 , 99391 , 99398 ). Hirudotherapy can also involve applying a gel made from leech saliva. In a small clinical study, using a gel of a liposomal extract of leech saliva reduced pain in patients with knee osteoarthritis ( 99396 ).

Hirudotherapy is the use of leeches for medicinal purposes. Leeches secrete a variety of anticoagulants in their saliva, primarily hirudin. Hirudin works by inhibiting the conversion of fibrinogen to fibrin. Other constituents of leech saliva include saratin and calin, which bind to collagen and inhibit von Willebrand factor. Leech saliva also contains hyaluronidase, which cleaves hyaluronic acid; collagenase, which cleaves collagen; apyrase, which cleaves adenosine 5'-diphosphate (ADP) and inhibits platelets; and destabilase, which cleaves stabilizing bonds in fibrin. The anticoagulant effects of hirudin typically last for 15 minutes, while the effects of other blood thinning constituents might last from 10 hours to 7 days ( 99399 ). Leech saliva also provides an anesthetic effect that reduces pain throughout the process ( 15855 ). Leeches are approved as a medical device for conditions such as skin grafts. When blood clots or pools around a wound, it can decrease tissue survival. Applying leeches provides a local anticoagulant effect that helps the tissue survive ( 15855 ). Leech saliva also contains anti-inflammatory and bacteriostatic constituents ( 106752 ).
