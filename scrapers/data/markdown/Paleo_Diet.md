# Paleo Diet
Other Common Names Caveman Diet, Dieta paleo (paleolítica), Hunter-Gatherer Diet, Paleolithic Diet, Stone Age Diet
|

---

## Overview

The paleo diet is a popular dietary pattern intended to replicate the human diet during the Paleolithic Age. Foods consumed during this age include meat, fish, shellfish, vegetables, fruits, roots, eggs and nuts. The paleo diet often avoids grains, dairy products, salt, refined fats, and added sugar ( 28421 ).

## History

The paleo diet is thought to follow the nutritional patterns of people from the Paleolithic era which dates back to 2.6 million to 10,000 years ago. This was before industrial agriculture. In theory, the paleo diet varies and greatly depends on the geographical location and climate of Paleolithic ancestors ( 98137 ).

## People Use This For

The Paleo diet is used for acne, anxiety, asthma, athletic performance, autoimmune diseases, cancer, cardiovascular disease (CVD), chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), depression, diabetes, dysmenorrhea, fatigue, gingivitis, hypercholesterolemia, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, kidney stones (nephrolithiasis), metabolic syndrome, multiple sclerosis-related fatigue, myopia, obesity, osteoarthritis, osteoporosis, rheumatoid arthritis (RA), skin conditions, and skin infections (folliculitis).

Possibly Safe when used appropriately. The paleo diet has been followed in clinical trials with apparent safety and without reports of adverse effects ( 28421 , 28422 , 28423 , 28425 , 28426 , 98137 ). Additionally, there is no reason to expect safety issues as long as the proper nutritional needs are being met. There is some concern that the paleo diet does not contain enough calcium and vitamin D, which can be corrected through supplementation ( 28435 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid use.

#### General

The paleo diet seems to be well-tolerated, although long-term safety of the paleo diet is unknown. As long as recommended nutritional intakes are met, there is little reason to expect adverse outcomes. There is some concern that the paleo diet does not provide enough calcium and vitamin D, which might result in nutrient insufficiency or deficiency ( 28435 , 98136 ).

#### Endocrine

In one case report, a breastfeeding woman reported to the emergency room with ketoacidosis after starting to follow the paleo diet. The ketoacidosis was attributed to the weight loss and carbohydrate deficit created by the diet in conjunction with the increased energy demands during lactation ( 98133 ).

Possibly Effective
Obesity.

Although following the paleo diet reduces weight, there is no evidence that it is better than other reduced calorie diets. Two small preliminary studies show that following the paleo diet for 3-5 weeks reduces weight by 2.3 kg in healthy volunteers and 4.5 kg in overweight postmenopausal healthy women when compared to baseline ( 28425 , 28426 ). One small clinical study shows that following the paleo diet for 6 weeks reduces weight by 3.7 kg, reduces waist size, and improves satiety in overweight men with ischemic heart disease when compared to baseline. These outcomes were comparable to following a Mediterranean-like diet ( 28421 ). Other preliminary clinical research in overweight postmenopausal women shows that weight loss with the paleo diet is 4-5 kg greater than with a low-fat diet at 6, 12, and 18 months, but not at 24 months  ( 100973 ).

However, when the paleo diet is compared with a diet that is not calorie restrictive, it seems to be slightly better for reducing weight. Clinical research in healthy women shows that following the paleo diet reduces weight by 2 kg when compared with following the Australian Guide to Healthy Living (AGHL) dietary pattern. The paleo diet is lower in carbohydrate content and greater in fat content when compared with the AGHL dietary pattern ( 98135 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

A meta-analysis of eight small clinical trials shows that following the paleo diet for up to 180 days might improve some risk factors for CVD. In the pooled analysis, the paleo diet was associated with reductions of 1.7 kg in body weight, 1.3% in body fat percentage, 4.8 and 3.2 mmHg in systolic and diastolic blood pressure, respectively, 9 mg/dL in total cholesterol, 5 mg/dL in low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, and 27 mg/dL in triglycerides, as well as an increase of 2 mg/dL in high density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, when compared with other diets ( 104154 ). However, the generalizability of these results is unclear as the patient populations, comparator diets, and study durations varied widely across the included studies.

Diabetes.

A meta-analysis of four small clinical trials shows that following the paleo diet for up to 12 weeks does not seem to improve plasma glucose, glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c), insulin, or insulin resistance when compared with other healthy diets ( 104152 ). However, there are significant limitations with this analysis. Only two of the studies lasted longer than 3 weeks, and the comparator diets varied across studies. Additionally, only two of the studies included patients diagnosed with type 2 diabetes. One of these studies, which enrolled patients with diabetes or glucose intolerance and ischemic heart disease, shows that following the paleo diet for 12 weeks decreases plasma glucose levels when compared with following a Mediterranean-like diet ( 28421 ). The other study, which enrolled patients with well-controlled type 2 diabetes, shows that following the paleo diet for 3 months decreases HbA1c levels, but does not affect response to glucose tolerance tests, when compared to baseline ( 28420 ). The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Gingivitis.

Preliminary clinical research shows that following the paleo diet for 4 weeks increases bacterial count and plaque levels and decreases bleeding on probing in healthy subjects without access to standard oral hygiene ( 28422 ).

Hypercholesterolemia.

A small clinical study in adults with hypercholesterolemia shows that following the paleo diet for 4 months lowers total cholesterol, triglyceride, and low-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels, and increases high-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels, when compared to baseline or to following the traditional heart-healthy diet ( 98139 ). It is unknown if these effects are maintained long-term.

Metabolic syndrome.

A meta-analysis of 4 preliminary clinical studies assessing risk factors of metabolic syndrome shows that following the paleo diet seems to slightly reduce waist circumference and triglycerides, but not blood pressure or fasting blood glucose, when compared with baseline ( 98137 ). This analysis has been criticized for over-exaggerating clinically insignificant results and using incorrect methods to pool data ( 98134 ). Another meta-analysis of 10 clinical studies in patients with metabolic disorders shows that following the paleo diet has variable effects on measures of glycemic control and lipids ( 106545 ).

Multiple sclerosis-related fatigue.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with multiple sclerosis shows that following the paleo diet for 12 weeks reduces fatigue scores when compared to baseline. Control patients following their regular diet saw non-significant increases in fatigue scores during the same time frame ( 104153 ).

More evidence is needed to rate the paleo diet for these uses.

The paleo diet attempts to mimic the diet of people of the Paleolithic period or the "Stone Age". The paleo diet includes lean meat, fish, shellfish, eggs, fruit, nuts, vegetables, vegetable oils, and seeds. Grains, cereals, beans, dairy, refined sugars, processed foods, and added salt are avoided ( 28420 , 28421 , 28423 , 28424 , 98136 ). Certain paleo dietary patterns allow wine while others do not allow alcohol ( 28421 , 28423 ).

None known.

None known.

#### OSTEOPOROSIS

Due to the avoidance of dairy and fortified grains, the paleo dietary pattern is typically low in calcium intake. Some studies found that typical calcium intake for individuals following the paleo diet ranges between 355 to 395 mg daily. This amount is lower than the recommended daily intake of calcium for most age groups. Following the paleo diet might result in a calcium deficiency or insufficiency, which would be detrimental to patients with, or at risk for, osteoporosis ( 98136 ).

The gradual shift from the diet consumed during the Paleolithic age to the current era is thought to be a cause for many of today's health problems. The modern, Western diet is high in processed foods containing sodium and hydrogenated fats and low in fiber, which is associated with an increased risk of obesity and other chronic diseases. Following the paleo diet involves avoiding those aspects of the Western diet and sticking to fruits, vegetables, lean meats, and seafood. These foods are sources of vitamins, minerals, fiber, and healthy fats such as polyunsaturated and monounsaturated fats ( 28435 , 28436 , 28446 , 28448 ). Proponents state that following the paleo diet increases the pH of bodily fluids such as plasma and urine. Theoretically, the resulting alkaline state of the body may result in health benefits such as improved bone and muscle strength, reduced blood pressure, and decreased risk of kidney stones and asthma ( 28444 ). The paleo diet can lead to weight loss possibly because it is generally high in protein. High protein intake can increase satiety, reducing overall calorie consumption and resulting in weight loss ( 28424 ). The paleo diet has also been associated with reduced leptin levels, which also contribute to increased satiety ( 28423 , 100972 ). Increased levels of glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1), a satiety-promoting hormone, have also been reported in patients following the paleo diet ( 100971 ). Weight loss leads to benefits such as decreased risk for thrombosis, possibly due to a decrease and normalization in levels of plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) ( 28425 ). Weight loss can also improve glycemic indices, blood pressure, and lipid levels ( 28421 , 28426 , 28445 , 98138 , 100972 ). However, it is unclear if these benefits will occur in all patients or if they will last long-term. One study found a loss of initial benefit after 2 years of following the paleo diet ( 100973 ).
