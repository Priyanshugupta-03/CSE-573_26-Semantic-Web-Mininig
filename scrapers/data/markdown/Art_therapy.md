# Art therapy
Other Common Names Anthroposophic Art Therapy, Art Stimulation, Arterapia, Art-thérapie, Chinese Calligraphy Therapy, Clay Art Therapy, Creative Arts Intervention, Creative Arts Therapy, Creative Expression, Mindfulness Based Art Therapy, Phenomenological Art Therapy, Psychodynamic Group Art Therapy, Visual Art Therapy

Caution

Do not confuse art therapy with Music Therapy , another form of expressive therapy.

## Overview

Art therapy is a type of psychotherapy ( 103514 , 103517 , 103519 ). It can be practiced in hospitals, clinics, schools, private practices, and many other locations ( 103507 , 103514 ). It involves a variety of art modalities including drawing, painting, clay modeling, and sculpture ( 103511 , 103517 , 103519 , 103521 ). Art therapy enables the expression and communication of inner thoughts or feelings when verbalization is difficult or not possible ( 103511 , 103521 ). The aesthetic aspect of the creation of art is thought to lift mood, boost self-awareness, and improve self-esteem ( 103511 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of art therapy. However, there is no reason to expect safety concerns when used appropriately.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, there is no reason to expect safety concerns when used appropriately.

#### General

In general, art therapy is well tolerated.

|
Possibly Effective
Depression.

Most clinical research shows that various forms of art therapy improve symptoms of depression in adults and adolescents.

Preliminary clinical research in elderly females stabilized on antidepressants shows that weekly art therapy sessions for 20 weeks improve symptoms of depression and anxiety when compared with control. About 32% of patients have at least a 50% improvement in depression scores, compared with only 4% in the control group ( 103508 ). In adults with moderate to severe depression, adding weekly art therapy to usual treatments for 10 weeks modestly improves symptoms of depression when compared with usual treatments alone, but has no effect on suicidal ideation ( 103521 ). Also in adults with depression, weekly clay modeling therapy for 6 weeks modestly reduces symptoms of depression and improves feelings of well-being when compared with control ( 103523 ). In adolescents aged 13-18 years with depression, a meta-analysis of 6 studies shows that group painting therapy improves positive emotion scores, self-esteem, and resilience, while decreasing negative emotion scores, when compared with control ( 106913 ). The studies were generally low-quality and showed substantial heterogeneity. In patients with cancer, a meta-analysis of six studies shows that up to 22 weekly sessions of art therapy has a moderate effect on depression when compared with no therapy, sham art therapy, or usual treatment ( 103517 ). Another meta-analysis in patients with cancer shows that mindfulness-based art therapy, usually over an 8-week period, improves depression when compared with usual treatment, waitlist control, or support groups ( 103518 ). However, a meta-analysis of three studies specifically in patients with breast cancer shows that art therapy has no effect on depression ( 103520 ). Chinese calligraphy therapy also seems to reduce symptoms of depression. A meta-analysis of small studies shows that Chinese calligraphy therapy reduces depression by a moderate amount when compared with control groups ( 103506 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Age-related cognitive decline.

Although there is interest in using art therapy for age-related cognitive decline, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of art therapy for this condition.

Alzheimer disease.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial for reducing cognitive decline associated with Alzheimer disease.

In patients with mild Alzheimer disease, weekly 2-hour painting sessions over a 12-week period reduces chronic pain, anxiety, and depression, and modestly improves quality of life and most measures of cognition when compared with baseline. However, it does not improve verbal memory ( 103509 ). In other research, art therapy once weekly for 12 weeks improves apathy when compared with baseline or learning therapy using calculations, but has no effect on cognitive function, quality of life, or mood ( 103524 ).

Anorexia nervosa.

Although there is interest in using art therapy for anorexia nervosa, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of art therapy for this condition.

Anxiety.

Most preliminary clinical research shows that art therapy is beneficial for reducing various forms of anxiety.

Preliminary clinical research in females with generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), social anxiety disorder, or panic disorder shows that 10-12 art therapy sessions over 3 months (clay modeling, drawing, or painting) reduces anxiety and improves emotional regulation when compared with a waitlist control. Benefits persist for at least 3 months ( 103503 ). In elderly patients, a meta-analysis of four studies shows that art therapy modestly reduces anxiety ( 103519 ). A meta-analysis of small studies shows that Chinese calligraphy therapy moderately reduces anxiety when compared with control groups ( 103506 ). In patients with cancer, a meta-analysis of six studies shows that up to 22 weekly sessions of art therapy produce moderate improvements in anxiety when compared with no therapy, sham art therapy, or usual treatment ( 103517 ). In a mixed population of patients, most of whom have cancer, a meta-analysis shows that mindfulness-based art therapy, usually over an 8-week period, improves anxiety when compared with usual treatment, waitlist control, or support groups ( 103518 ). However, a meta-analysis of two studies specifically in patients with breast cancer shows that art therapy does not reduce anxiety ( 103520 ).

Asthma.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial for reducing asthma exacerbations.

In children with asthma, preliminary clinical research shows that art therapy once weekly for 7 weeks improves anxiety, communication, and quality of life when compared with a waitlist control. However, art therapy does not affect the frequency or severity of asthma exacerbations ( 103514 ).

Autism spectrum disorder.

Although there is interest in using art therapy for autism spectrum disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of art therapy for this condition.

Breast cancer.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial in patients with breast cancer.

A meta-analysis of 18 clinical studies in patients with breast cancer shows that participation in painting, music, or dance therapy for 1-12 weeks reduces anxiety, depression, pain, and fatigue, and improves quality of life when compared with controls ( 106915 ).

Bulimia nervosa.

Although there is interest in using art therapy for bulimia nervosa, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of art therapy for this condition.

Cancer-related fatigue.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial for fatigue in patients with cancer.

In patients with cancer, a meta-analysis of four studies shows that up to 22 weekly sessions of art therapy have a small effect on fatigue when compared with no therapy, sham art therapy, or usual treatment ( 103517 ). In a mixed population of patients, most of whom have cancer, a meta-analysis shows that mindfulness-based art therapy, usually over an 8-week period, improves feelings of fatigue when compared with usual treatment, waitlist control, or support groups ( 103518 ).

Cognitive impairment.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial in patients with cognitive impairment.

A meta-analysis of twelve small clinical studies in elderly patients with mild cognitive impairment shows that art therapy improves global cognitive function and decreases depression and anxiety when compared with controls ( 103519 ).

Diabetes.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial in children with type 1 diabetes.

In a retrospective review of children with poorly controlled type 1 diabetes, participation in art therapy was associated with a decrease in glycated hemoglobin level in 56% of patients, compared with 23% in the control group. Art therapy involved 1 session every 1-2 weeks for 3 months, then every 4-6 weeks for 6 months, then every 8-12 weeks up to a total of 24 months ( 106910 ).

Neurological trauma.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial in patients with neurological trauma.

In patients studied at least 12 months after a traumatic brain injury, participating in art therapy for 4 months followed by group psychotherapy for 4 months, or vice versa, is associated with improvements in social functioning, emotional autoregulation, and level of awareness, as well as decreased depression, when compared with baseline ( 106916 ). This study had less than 10 participants, and there was no control group or intention-to-treat analysis, limiting the validity of the findings.

Postoperative recovery.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial in patients recovering from surgery.

Preliminary clinical research in patients recovering from major oncologic surgery shows that exposure to art in a gallery for 15 minutes per day for one or more days improves mental wellbeing and hope and decreases anxiety, with no effect on pain, when compared with standard of care ( 106917 ).

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Although there is interest in using art therapy for PTSD, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of art therapy for this condition.

Pre-procedural anxiety.

Although there is interest in using art therapy for pre-procedural anxiety, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of art therapy for this condition.

Psychological well-being.

Small clinical studies suggest that art therapy may help to improve feelings of well-being in various populations.

Preliminary clinical research in menopausal or pregnant patients shows that mindfulness-based art therapy reduces symptoms of distress and modestly improves lifestyle choices when compared with a waitlist control group ( 103505 , 103516 ). A meta-analysis of a mixed population of patients with cancer shows that mindfulness-based art therapy, usually over an 8-week period, has a moderate benefit on feelings of well-being when compared with usual treatment, waitlist control, or support groups ( 103518 ). In addition, preliminary clinical research in children with HIV/AIDs shows that use of a community-based art program for 6 months improves some aspects of psychosocial health when compared with baseline ( 103513 ).

Quality of life.

Small clinical studies suggest that various forms of art therapy might improve quality of life in adults and children.

Most preliminary clinical research from meta-analyses and individual studies shows that art therapy modestly improves quality of life in adults and children, with improvements in mood, physical activity, and energy ( 103504 , 103515 , 103517 , 103518 ). Clinical research in children aged 7-10 years with mild emotional and behavioral difficulties shows that participation in arts therapies (art, music, drama, or dance) for 1 hour per week for 8 weeks improves emotional and behavioral difficulties and sleep duration when compared with a waitlist control. However, a small improvement in health-related quality of life was not statistically significant ( 106914 ). A meta-analysis of seven studies in patients with cancer shows that up to 22 weekly sessions of art therapy has a moderate effect on quality of life when compared with no therapy, sham art therapy, or usual treatment ( 103517 ). In a mixed population of patients with cancer, another meta-analysis shows that mindfulness-based art therapy, usually over an 8-week period, has a moderate benefit on quality of life when compared with usual treatment, waitlist control, or support groups ( 103518 ).

Schizophrenia.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial in patients with schizophrenia; the available research is conflicting.

A clinical study in patients with schizophrenia shows that psychodynamic art therapy twice weekly for 6 weeks in addition to standard treatment reduces positive, but not negative, symptoms and modestly improves some measures of psychosocial functioning when compared with standard treatment alone ( 103511 ). In addition, a meta-analysis of small studies shows that Chinese calligraphy therapy modestly reduces psychosis and other symptoms of schizophrenia when compared with control groups ( 103506 ). Another study in inpatients with schizophrenia shows that participating in thirty 90-minute sessions of Chinese arts (calligraphy, painting, embroidery, beading, or making opera masks) over 15 weeks improves social functioning, confidence, coping, and goal achievement when compared with control ( 106912 ). In contrast to these findings, a large trial of patients with schizophrenia shows that participating in weekly group art sessions for 12 months in addition to standard care does not improve symptoms or function when compared with standard care alone, or when compared with a weekly group activity plus standard care ( 103512 ).

Smoking cessation.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial for reducing smoking rates in adults and adolescents.

Preliminary clinical research shows that art therapy over 6 weeks does not improve smoking cessation rates in adolescents when compared with taking a smoking cessation course ( 103522 ). Conversely, clinical research in adult smokers aged 18-65 years shows that art therapy improves smoking cessation rates from about 8% to 18% when compared with baseline. The art therapy included 4 sessions of music, 4 sessions of poetry, and 2 sessions of painting or calligraphy per week, but the total duration of the intervention was not stated ( 106911 ).

Stress.

Although there is interest in using art therapy for stress, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of art therapy for this condition.

Stroke.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial in patients recovering from a stroke.

Preliminary clinical research in patients recovering from a stroke shows that creative art therapy, including drawing, painting, and clay modeling, along with singing and meditation, twice weekly for 4 weeks, in conjunction with physical therapy five days weekly, reduces depression and improves physical function and quality of life by small to moderate amounts when compared with physical therapy alone. However, it does not improve anxiety or cognition ( 103510 ).

Substance use disorder.

Although there is interest in using art therapy for substance use disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of art therapy for this condition.

Tourette syndrome.

It is unclear if art therapy is beneficial in patients with Tourette syndrome or other tic disorders.

Preliminary clinical research in children aged 7-9 years with tic disorders, including Tourette syndrome, shows that exposure to four different art forms (watercolor painting, clay modeling, coloring, and free drawing) for 40 minutes per week in each of four consecutive weeks reduces stress, tic frequency, and tic complexity, and increases physiological relaxation when compared with waitlist controls ( 106918 ). The validity of this study is limited by its small size and lack of an intention-to-treat analysis.

More evidence is needed to rate art therapy for these uses.

In some places, art therapists are credentialed and follow specific regulations ( 103507 , 103512 ). Psychologists, health professionals, and clinical counselors sometimes use art therapy as part of treatment plans ( 103515 ). An art therapist helps a patient use many different types of artistic expression, including drawing, painting, sculpting, collage, crafts, and murals ( 103503 , 103507 , 103515 , 103517 ). Art therapy may take place individually with an art therapist or in a group setting ( 103507 ). It may be conducted as a single session or as a series of sessions. Formal art therapy usually involves discussion and interpretation of the meaning of what the person has created with an art therapist, and possibly with peers in a group situation ( 103507 ). Individual art therapy sessions generally last 60-90 minutes and are conducted weekly or twice weekly for up to 12 weeks. The sessions include both creativity and reflection ( 103508 , 103511 , 103515 , 103521 ). Sometimes visual art therapy is combined with other types of expressive therapy, including dance, drama, or music ( 103508 , 103510 , 103519 , 103525 ) or is used as part of a mindfulness-, guided imagery-, or phenomenological-based program ( 103508 , 103515 , 103516 , 103518 , 103521 ).

None known.

None known.

Art therapy is used as a complementary therapy in integrative medicine programs. It is based on the principle that creative self-expression is inherently healing and life-enhancing. Art therapy can be used in both children and adults. Some art therapy proponents state that children are particularly responsive to art therapy, as it helps them communicate difficult issues, reconcile emotions, and reduce stress. In theory, through creating and discussing art with an art therapist, patients can increase awareness of self, cope with anxiety, stress, or trauma, and improve cognitive abilities and quality of life. Some proponents believe that these improvements might result in positive benefits for the immune, nervous, and cardiovascular systems.
