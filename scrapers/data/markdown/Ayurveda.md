## Overview

Ayurveda means "science of life," and it stems from the ancient body of spiritual teachings known as the Vedas. It is an integrated system of specific theories and techniques that use diet, herbs, exercise, meditation, yoga, and massage or bodywork. The goal of Ayurveda is to achieve optimal health on all levels: physical, psychological, and spiritual. Ayurveda often suggests that individuals make lifestyle and behavior changes ( 100203 , 100204 ). In India, Ayurveda involves the eight main branches of medicine: pediatrics, gynecology, obstetrics, ophthalmology, geriatrics, otolaryngology, general medicine, and surgery. A majority of the Indian population uses Ayurveda, although it is often used in combination with conventional medicine ( 100204 ). Ayurveda made its way to the West mainly through Europe, where it still has a strong presence today. However, in modern times and particularly in western countries, the practice of Ayurveda is less focused on its spiritual roots than on its use as a form of complementary or alternative medicine ( 100205 ). Ayurveda is practiced in the West by healthcare professionals who are licensed in a variety of disciplines, such as MDs, osteopathic physicians (DOs), naturopaths, acupuncturists, nurses, massage therapists, and chiropractors. It is also practiced by lay people who are not licensed, but who function as health counselors, educators, or consultants ( 100205 ). To practice this discipline, the standard length of training in India is five years ( 100204 ). Similar to traditional Chinese medicine herbal products, Ayurvedic herbal products have suffered from poor quality. Some Ayurvedic products have been found to be contaminated with prescription drugs including corticosteroids and NSAIDs. Other products have been found to be contaminated with high concentrations of heavy metals including mercury, arsenic, and lead ( 15313 ).

Ayurveda literally means "the science of life." It was developed in India over 3000 years ago ( 100203 ). The foundations of Ayurveda are thought to have been laid by the originators of Indian meditation and yoga ( 15311 ). Similarly to yoga, Ayurveda teaches that vital energy, referred to as prana, is the basis of all life and healing. Prana is thought to be governed by the five elements: earth, air, fire, water, and ether. When there is balance and harmony among the five elements, the human body is in health; illness occurs due to an imbalance or lack of harmony ( 100203 ). Ayurveda parallels traditional Chinese medicine in many respects ( 15313 ). The focus of treatment is on the whole person, and balancing or harmonizing body, mind, and spirit. Treatments (rasayana) commonly utilized in Ayurveda include meditation with controlled breathing (pranayama), exercise, diet, herbs, detoxification programs, and massage ( 15312 , 15313 ). Many of the herbs commonly used in traditional Chinese medicine are also used in Ayurveda. The five elements that govern prana form three basic humors (dosha) that regulate bodily function. These three humors are: Vata, which governs movement and mind; Pitta, which governs energy; and Kapha, which governs structure, cohesion, and lubrication. Together, these three humors are referred to as Tridoshas; an imbalance in the Tridoshas is thought to cause disease ( 100203 ). A person's nature is referred to as prakriti. This represents a person's physiology or physiological processes in balance. Each person usually has one or two doshas that dominate that person's nature or energetic constitution. Prakriti is thought to predict a person's response to medications, environmental conditions, and dietary factors ( 100203 ). An imbalanced constitution or imbalanced dosha is referred to as vikriti ( 15312 , 15313 ). For example, when a patient has vikriti, assessment of the patient's doshas and prakriti would help the Ayurvedic practitioner determine what diet, treatments, and habit changes to recommend. Treatments are chosen for the purpose of balancing physiological processes ( 15313 ).
