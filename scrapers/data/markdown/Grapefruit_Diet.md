## Overview

The grapefruit diet was first introduced in the early 20 th century. It is a low-carbohydrate, calorie-restrictive diet that requires the intake of grapefruit, either as whole fruit or juice, with every meal. Most forms of the grapefruit diet limit daily caloric intake to less than 800 calories ( 102102 ).

Grapefruit is known to interact with certain drugs, supplements, foods, conditions, and lab tests. See the grapefruit monograph for more information.

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the grapefruit diet. However, the design of the grapefruit diet might result in insufficient daily intake of calories, essential vitamins, minerals, and other nutrients ( 102101 ). Additionally, consuming large quantities of grapefruit can cause adverse effects. See the grapefruit monograph for more information.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the grapefruit diet in pregnancy and lactation; avoid use.

#### General

A thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted. Consuming large quantities of grapefruit can cause adverse effects. See the grapefruit monograph for more information.

#### Other

The design of the grapefruit diet might result in inadequate intake of calories, essential vitamins, minerals, and other nutrients ( 102101 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Obesity.

Although there has been interest in using the grapefruit diet for obesity, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of the grapefruit diet for this condition.

More evidence is needed to rate the grapefruit diet for this use.

The grapefruit diet is a highly restrictive diet of 800 calories or fewer per day that involves the consumption of grapefruit at each meal. A typical breakfast consists of 1/2 grapefruit or 200 mL of unsweetened grapefruit juice, two eggs, two slices of bacon, and a cup of tea or black coffee. Lunch and dinner generally consist of 1/2 grapefruit or 200 mL of unsweetened grapefruit juice, a salad with any type of dressing, 200 grams of meat, unlimited "non-starchy" vegetables, and a cup of tea or black coffee. Snacking in between meals is not permitted ( 102102 ).

None known.

None known.

Grapefruit may interact with certain conditions. See the grapefruit monograph for more information.

Grapefruit may interact with certain lab tests. See the grapefruit monograph for more information.

Any weight loss seen with the grapefruit diet is likely due to severe calorie restriction. However, the grapefruit diet is based on the theory that grapefruits contain an enzyme that helps the body use fat for energy. Proponents of the diet claim that eating grapefruit before a meal will give the consumer the benefit of this fat-burning enzyme. This enzyme has not been identified ( 102102 ). Grapefruit is relatively low in calories and contains high quantities of pectin, an insoluble fiber. Grapefruit also provides large amounts of vitamin C, vitamin A, and the antioxidants lycopene, beta carotene, xanthine, and lutein. As with other citrus fruits, it exhibits high antioxidant activity in vitro ( 102102 ). However, the evidence for the use of grapefruit for weight reduction is limited. Refer to the grapefruit monograph for more information.
