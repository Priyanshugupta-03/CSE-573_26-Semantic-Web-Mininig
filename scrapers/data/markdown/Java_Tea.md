## Overview

Java tea is a perennial, herbaceous shrub. It is native to tropical and subtropical areas of Asia and Australia. It has been used to treat gout by the people of Yunnan Province, China for over 2000 years ( 18 , 94851 , 102764 , 110427 , 110428 , 115754 , 115757 ). Java tea is also traditionally used for bladder, gallbladder, kidney, and liver disorders.

Possibly Safe when used orally and appropriately, short-term. Java tea 100 mg daily has been safely used in a clinical trial for 8 weeks ( 94851 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

Orally, Java tea seems to be well tolerated. No adverse effects have been reported. However, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted.

Hypertension.

Oral Java tea has only been evaluated in combination with other ingredients; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with uncontrolled hypertension shows that taking a specific combination product (OrthoSiphon, Rottapharm-Madaus SpA) containing Java tea leaf extract 100 mg, standardized to sinensetin 0.1%, berberine 500 mg, monakolin 3 mg, and policosanols 10 mg, daily for 8 weeks, reduces systolic and diastolic blood pressure by 5 mmHg and 2 mmHg, respectively, when compared to baseline. However, this supplement is less effective than hydrochlorothiazide 12.5 mg daily ( 94851 ). It is unclear if these effects are due to Java tea, other ingredients, or the combination.

Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis).

Although there has been interest in using oral Java tea for kidney stones, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of Java tea for this condition.

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

Although there has been interest in using oral Java tea for RA, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of Java tea for this condition.

Urinary tract infections (UTIs).

Oral Java tea has only been evaluated in combination with other ingredients; its effect when used alone is unclear.

Clinical research in females with UTIs who are not taking antibiotics shows that taking a specific combination supplement (Aqualibra, Medice Arzneimittel Pütter GmbH & Co. KG), containing extracts of Java tea 180 mg, spiny restharrow root 160 mg, and goldenrod 360 mg, 3 times daily for 7 days modestly improves symptom scores when compared with placebo. Urinary microbial counts decreased by 63% with the supplement, compared with 25% in those taking placebo. Antibiotics were needed in 15% and 49% of patients taking the supplement or placebo, respectively ( 102763 ). It is unclear if the effects are due to Java tea, other ingredients, or the combination.

More evidence is needed to rate Java tea for these uses.

Theoretically, Java tea might interfere with blood pressure control if used perioperatively. Tell patients to discontinue Java tea at least 2 weeks before elective surgical procedures. Preliminary clinical suggests that Java tea has antihypertensive effects ( 94851 ).

#### General

The applicable parts of Java tea are the leaf and stem tip ( 2 , 18 ). The plant contains anthraquinone derivatives, diterpenoids including orthosiphonones, norstaminols, and secoorthosiphols, flavonoids, methoxylated compounds, and other polyphenols, hydroxycinnamic acids, , lignanoids, organic acids, phenolic acids, saponins, sterols, sugar alcohols, tanshinones, triterpenes, volatile oils, and others ( 110427 , 110428 , 115755 , 115756 , 115757 ).

#### Antiarthritic effects

Java tea is of interest for arthritis. In an animal osteoarthritis model, Java tea extract protected against cartilage degradation and inflammation, possibly by altering the production of inflammatory cytokines in the cartilage ( 102764 ).

#### Antidiabetic effects

In animal research, extracts of the aerial parts of Java tea reduce blood glucose levels possibly by scavenging oxidants and increasing insulin-driven glucose uptake into muscle and adipose ( 115757 ).

#### Antihyperuricemic effects

In vitro and animal studies suggest that Java tea or its constituents might reduce uric acid levels and improve pain associated with gout. An in vitro model of hyperuricemia shows that a Java tea aerial extract, and several of its constituents, promote excretion of uric acid. In an animal model of gouty arthritis, several constituents of Java tea appear to reduce joint swelling and improve pain ( 110428 ). In an animal model of hyperuricemic nephropathy, a Java tea extract reduces serum uric acid levels possibly regulating the gut microbiota and altering expression of the renal urate transporter and enzymes involved in hepatic urate synthesis ( 115754 ).

#### Antimicrobial effects

Java tea is stated to have antimicrobial effects ( 2 , 18 ). Laboratory research also suggests that Java tea has anti-adhesive activity against some types of pathogenic bacteria ( 102763 ). Preliminary research in humans shows that drinking Java tea 4 times daily for 7 days inhibits the adhesion of uropathogenic Escherichia coli to bladder cells in vitro. The active constituents of the plant are thought to be polymethoxylated flavones ( 110429 ).

#### Cognitive effects

In animal research, a Java tea extract improves learning and spatial memory in a water maze test. Mechanisms of action may include reduced amyloid-beta-induced cytotoxicity, increased brain-derived neurotrophic factor levels, and decreased levels of inflammatory cytokines and other compounds ( 115756 ).

#### Gastrointestinal effects

Java tea is stated to have weak antispasmodic effects ( 2 , 18 ).

#### Hepatic effects

In a rat model of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), a Java tea extract and its flavonoid fraction reduce weight gain, improve lipid metabolism, reduce hepatic steatosis, and reverse hepatic inflammation, possibly via antioxidant effects ( 115755 ).

#### Renal effects

Animal research shows that Java tea has diuretic effects, possibly due to an interaction with adenosine A1 receptors in the kidneys ( 94851 ). In vitro, a water extract of Java tea reduces the size of human kidney stones, especially at high concentrations and in the presence of acid ( 110427 ). In a mouse model of hyperuricemic nephropathy, a Java tea extract improves renal function, reduces kidney damage and fibrosis, and lowers serum uric acid levels. Possible mechanisms include altered gut microbiota and expression of the renal urate transporter and enzymes involved in hepatic urate synthesis, and reduced inflammation and renal damage ( 115754 ).
