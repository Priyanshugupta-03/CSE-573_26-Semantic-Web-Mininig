## Overview

Weight Watchers (also referred to as WW) is a large provider of commercial weight loss services worldwide. The cost of the program includes a subscription, with an additional cost for meals. Weight Watchers offers weight loss-related programs and products consisting of several diets and meeting structures, as well as consultations and weight loss tools. Social support is built into the regimen through regular meetings and/or online tools ( 15923 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Weight Watchers has been safely used in clinical trials lasting up to 2 years ( 11264 , 15920 , 15921 , 15922 ). There are no known safety concerns with this diet.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of the Weight Watchers diet when pregnant or lactating. However, there is no reason to expect safety concerns as long as nutritional needs are met.

#### General

The Weight Watchers diet is generally well tolerated. It has been safely used in clinical trials lasting up to 2 years.

Possibly Effective
Diabetes.

Clinical research shows that the Weight Watchers diet may increase weight loss and improve glycemic control in adults with type 2 diabetes, although it is unclear if it is superior to other weight loss programs.

Clinical research in adults with type 2 diabetes shows that following a Weight Watchers diet for 12 months, with access to a registered dietitian certified as a diabetes educator, improves glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels in 71% more patients than standard diabetes counseling and education. The improvement in HbA1c with the Weight Watchers diet was approximately twice that seen with the standard counseling program. Also, diabetes medications were reduced in 26% of patients following a Weight Watchers diet compared with 12% given standard counseling. There were no changes in total or low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol levels ( 96822 ). Other research in adults with obesity and type 2 diabetes shows that following a Weight Watchers diet for 12 months with additional counseling by a diabetes educator is associated with greater engagement in weight control behaviors when compared with standard care. However, improvements in weight loss, glycated hemoglobin, and scores on a scale measuring hedonic hunger do not differ between the groups. In both groups, the increase in weight control behaviors and decrease in hedonic hunger correlated with improvements in glycated hemoglobin and weight loss ( 109930 ).

Impaired glucose tolerance (prediabetes).

The Weight Watchers diet seems to improve weight loss in adults with prediabetes.

Clinical research in patients with prediabetes shows that following a Weight Watchers diet for 12 months results in weight loss of 5.5%, compared with 0.2% in patients using a program developed by the National Diabetes Education Program called Your Game Plan to Prevent Type 2 Diabetes. The Weight Watchers diet also improved glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) and high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels ( 96823 ).

Obesity.

Clinical studies show that the Weight Watchers diet can improve weight loss, although its effects may be similar to those seen with other diets.

Some clinical research shows that overweight adults on the Weight Watchers diet can lose modest amounts of weight ( 11264 , 15920 , 15921 , 15922 ). After 6-24 months, weight loss averages about 3-6 kg ( 11264 , 15921 , 15922 , 96822 , 96823 , 96824 , 96825 , 96826 ). In patients who use the Weight Watchers diet for 12 months, up to 3 kg of the weight loss seems to be maintained 4 years later ( 109929 ). Weight loss while on the Weight Watchers diet does not appear to be significantly different than other diets such as Atkins, Ornish, or the Zone diets ( 15922 ). However, it seems to be less effective at 12 months than Slimming World, available in the UK ( 96825 ). Weight Watchers diet programs include online programs with weekly meetings or a less costly option of an online program only. Attrition rates for the online program are low and there is no difference between groups after 12 months for total weight loss. Clinical research suggests that the online program approximately doubles weight loss at 3 months when compared with use of an online newsletter ( 96827 ). Adding the use of an ActiveLink activity tracking device to the online Weight Watchers diet does not increase weight loss after 3 or 12 months ( 96827 ). In overweight or obese adults, combining a Weight Watchers diet with virtual reality sessions for training in behavioral weight-loss skills (Experience Success Program) for 6 months increases weight loss when compared with the diet alone. This increase is proportionate to the time spent on the virtual reality sessions ( 109928 ). In the US, when evaluated by average cost per kilogram of weight lost, the Weight Watchers diet is considered to be the most cost-effective commercial lifestyle program supported by clinical research ( 96824 ). Continuing the Weight Watchers diet for 52 weeks versus 12 weeks does not seem to increase overall weight loss measured after 5 years of follow-up. However, using simulations to predict cost-effectiveness beyond 5 years suggests that 52 weeks on the diet would be associated with lower overall long-term healthcare costs ( 109929 ).  In the UK, Germany, and Australia, referral to a Weight Watchers diet was found to be more cost effective than referral to standard care based on the national guidelines ( 96826 ).

Two key components of the Weight Watchers diets are the diet points, called SmartPoints, and social connection. SmartPoint diet points are counted daily based on the caloric and nutritional value assigned to various foods. The ideal number of daily diet points is specific to each individual and determined based on gender, weight, weight loss goal, and pregnancy status. The SmartPoint system encourages the consumption of foods such as fruit, vegetables, and lean proteins and discourages sugar and some type of fats. Over 200 foods are considered to be Zero Point foods and can be eaten without tracking or worrying about portion size. Some points can be rolled over to the next day for special occasions ( 15923 ). The weekly meeting component is an important part of the Weight Watchers diet. Meetings include guidance from leaders and printed materials. Participants check their change in weight privately. The meeting leader talks about nutrition, physical activity, healthy habits, weight loss strategies, and motivational tools. During the meetings, participants may share recipes and provide emotional support to each other. Other programs are online only (OnLine Plus) which involves the same support and tracking via online tools, without the in-person meetings. A coaching option gives unlimited one-on-one phone sessions with a coach to work on personalized action plans and skills ( 15923 ).

Following the Weight Watchers diet while taking antidiabetes drugs may increase the risk of hypoglycemia.

Hypoglycemia has occurred in a small number of patients with type 2 diabetes who are taking antidiabetes medications and following the Weight Watchers Diet ( 96822 ). Dose adjustments of antidiabetes medications might be necessary for some people.

#### General

The Weight Watchers is a balanced diet. Many diets eliminate or decrease certain foods such as fats or carbohydrates. Instead of eliminating foods, Weight Watchers uses rigorous food and activity planning that includes nutritionally balanced food. The Weight Watchers diet also includes weekly meetings for group support and/or online support. Weight Watchers uses a point system for controlling food intake instead of counting calories. Weight Watchers also provides prepared meals with a pre-assigned point value. The program also provides specific recipes for preparing meals with certain point values ( 15932 ).

#### Antidiabetes effects

Glycemic improvements are thought to occur with weight loss of as low as 2% to 5% of initial body weight. In patients with diabetes or prediabetes, weight loss from the Weight Watchers diet is associated with improved glycemic control ( 96822 , 96823 ).
