# Alexander Technique
Other Common Names Alexander Proprioception, AT, F.M. Alexander Technique, Technique Alexander, Técnica Alexander
|

---

## Overview

The Alexander Technique is a physical technique that teaches a person to gain awareness of unrecognized tension in the body ( 97513 ). Practitioners of this technique are certified by the American Society for the Alexander Technique and must complete 1600 hours of training ( 16129 ).

## History

The Alexander Technique was created by Frederick Matthias Alexander, a Shakespearean orator from Australia, between 1890 and 1900 ( 16128 ).

## People Use This For

The Alexander Technique is used for back pain, neck pain, repetitive strain injury, carpal tunnel syndrome, stress, fatigue, stuttering, voice loss, performance anxiety, physical performance, Parkinson disease, asthma, migraine headache, and other conditions.

Likely Safe when used appropriately. The Alexander Technique has been safely used in clinical studies ( 16126 , 16127 , 16128 , 88471 , 88477 , 88478 , 97514 , 97516 ). There are no known safety concerns.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using.

#### General

The Alexander Technique is generally well tolerated ( 97516 ). However, some patients report muscle soreness and transient increases in musculoskeletal pain ( 97514 ).

#### Musculoskeletal

Some patients report muscle soreness and transient increases in musculoskeletal pain after using the Alexander Technique ( 97514 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Back pain.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with chronic or recurrent back pain shows that taking two Alexander Technique lessons weekly for a total of six or 24 lessons, improves disability and reduces the number of days with pain at 3 months when compared to an exercise control ( 88471 ). Additionally, some preliminary reports show that using the Alexander Technique in combination with rolfing, manual techniques, acupuncture, chiropractic therapy, psychological interventions, or physical therapy might improve pain in patients with back pain ( 88472 , 88473 , 88474 , 88475 , 88476 ). However, high quality clinical research is needed to confirm these anecdotal findings.

Fall prevention.

Preliminary clinical research shows that instruction in the Alexander Technique might improve functional reach performance, a clinical measure of balance in patients greater than 65 years of age, when compared with no treatment ( 88477 ). Theoretically, this may reduce the incidence of falls. However, this has not been confirmed in clinical research. Additionally, the validity of this finding is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

Neck pain.

One clinical study in adults with chronic neck pain shows that receiving twenty 30-minute Alexander Technique lessons along with usual care reduces the pain and disability score after 12 months by about 4% more than usual care alone ( 97516 ). However, it is not likely that this improvement would be considered clinically significant. Another clinical study shows that receiving Alexander Technique lessons once weekly for 5 weeks reduces neck pain and improves function more than a guided imagery intervention, but is not better than applying a heat-pack for up to 20 minutes once weekly, in patients with chronic non-specific neck pain ( 97514 ).

Parkinson disease.

Preliminary clinical research shows that applying the Alexander Technique for 12-24 lessons can improve some measures of disability and depression in adults with Parkinson disease when compared with no intervention ( 16127 , 88478 ).

Physical performance.

Preliminary clinical research in patients 50 years and older with visual impairments shows that receiving Alexander Technique lessons once weekly for 12 weeks is no different than usual care for improving physical performance as measured by standing balance, gait speed, and the chair stand test ( 97513 ).

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) . Preliminary clinical reports show that applying the Alexander Technique as an adjunct to conventional medication, nutritional therapy, and prosthesis additions or adjustments might improve temporomandibular joint pain and dysfunction in some patients ( 88479 ). The effect of the Alexander Technique alone is unknown. High quality clinical research is needed to confirm these anecdotal findings.

More evidence is needed to rate the Alexander Technique for these uses.

The Alexander Technique is usually provided in a series of lessons. Each lesson can include body work and observed activity. Lessons typically last 30-60 minutes ( 16129 , 97513 ). Patients typically attend a series of one-on-one lessons with an Alexander Technique instructor in order to learn to identify improper habitual responses and voluntary movements during specific activities. Alexander Technique lessons can involve bodywork on a table and/or observation and guidance during normal daily activities such as sitting down, standing up, walking, and carrying items. Bodywork on a table often involves the instructor leading parts of the body, such as the head, arms, or legs, through a series of movements intended to enhance movement and sense of movement. The observed activity portion typically includes a series of movements while the instructor provides feedback and observations, often using a mirror, to help identify and address problematic areas ( 16129 , 97513 ). Unlike most physical therapy programs, the Alexander Technique does not involve exercises of strength and balance ( 97513 ).

The Alexander Technique is a method or process used to become aware of tension, habitual movements, and postures that result in unwanted conditions. It also focuses on how certain thought processes impact these movements. The Alexander Technique is often used to identify and address conditions such as back pain or carpal tunnel syndrome which can result from repetitive movements or postures ( 16126 , 88471 ). The basic principles of the Alexander Technique include the following ( 16120 ):

#### Awareness

This is the process of identifying the habits that result in the problematic issue such as pain or tension.

#### Direction

This is the process of visualizing movement and allowing the body to move reflexively and effortlessly rather than forcing compressed movement.

#### Inhibition

This is the process of noticing when the body is moving in a compressed way that can result in a problem. By noticing this, the unwanted movement can be inhibited or stopped.

#### Primary control

This is the relationship between the head, neck, and spine. The relationship can be either free or compressed. When the relationship is free, the body moves optimally.
