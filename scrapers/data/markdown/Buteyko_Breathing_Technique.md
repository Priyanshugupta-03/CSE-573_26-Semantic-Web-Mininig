# Buteyko Breathing Technique
Other Common Names Buteyko Breathing Training, Buteyko Institute Method, Buteyko Method, Buteyko Technique, Eucapnic Breathing Technique, Eucapnic Buteyko Breathing, Método de respiración Buteyko
|

## Overview

The Buteyko breathing technique was developed by Dr. Buteyko, based on the theory that asthma is caused by hyperventilation and resulting hypocapnia. The Buteyko breathing technique trains people with asthma to reduce their breathing speed and volume ( 98995 ).

## History

The Buteyko breathing technique was developed in Russia by Dr. Konstantin Buteyko in the 1950s ( 98995 ).

## People Use This For

The Buteyko breathing technique is used for allergies, asthma, cardiovascular disease (CVD), chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), atopic dermatitis (eczema), hormonal disorders, migraine headache, nervous system disorders, panic attacks, respiratory disease, sleep apnea, and snoring.

Possibly Safe when used appropriately as an adjunctive therapy to conventional treatment under the supervision of a qualified healthcare professional. The Buteyko breathing technique has been used in clinical research with apparent safety and no reports of adverse events ( 27903 , 27905 , 27906 , 27907 , 98995 , 98996 ). Care should be taken when reducing levels of conventional prophylactic therapy, and patients should continue to carry a rescue inhaler even when practicing the Buteyko breathing technique regularly.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available. However, there is no reason to believe that the Buteyko breathing technique would be unsafe in pregnancy and lactation.

#### General

The Buteyko breathing technique is generally well tolerated. Adverse events have not been reported in clinical research ( 27903 , 27905 , 27906 , 27907 , 98995 , 98996 ).

Possibly Effective
Asthma.

Most clinical research, conducted mainly in adults, shows that the Buteyko breathing technique provides some benefit for asthma patients treated with bronchodilators with or without inhaled corticosteroids (ICS). Clinical trials in patients with asthma show that the Buteyko breathing technique reduces the need for short acting bronchodilators and seems to improve symptoms and quality of life when compared to control ( 27903 , 27905 , 27906 , 27907 , 98996 ). However, the Buteyko breathing technique does not improve lung function measures ( 27903 , 27905 , 27906 , 98995 , 98996 ) and does not consistently reduce ICS dose requirements ( 27905 , 27906 , 98995 ) or the risk of asthma exacerbations ( 27905 , 98995 ). Furthermore, the Buteyko breathing technique seems to be no better than pranayama yogic breathing for improving asthma quality of life ( 98996 ).

A small clinical study in children aged 6-15 years with mild to moderate, partially controlled asthma in the outpatient setting shows that using the Buteyko breathing technique along with standard care has no effect on the need for short acting bronchodilators or ICS therapy when compared with standard care alone. However, the Buteyko breathing technique improves or attenuates changes in some lung function measures ( 106570 ). These findings are limited by risk of bias and missing data. Mouth-taping, one aspect of the Buteyko breathing technique, does not seem to help when used alone. Preliminary clinical research in patients with symptomatic asthma shows that using the Buteyko technique of overnight mouth-taping with microporous tape for 4 weeks does not affect morning peak expiratory flow or asthma symptom scores when compared to baseline ( 98993 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Eustachian tube dysfunction.

A small clinical study in treatment-naïve adults with obstructive Eustachian tube dysfunction (ETD) and persistent symptoms for at least 3 months shows that the Buteyko breathing technique along with nasal corticosteroid therapy completely relieves symptoms in 50% of patients, compared with 24% of patients receiving nasal corticosteroid therapy alone. Tympanogram normalization is also increased among those practicing Buteyko breathing technique; however, patients with type B tympanograms were excluded from this study ( 106569 ). It is unclear if Buteyko breathing technique is beneficial when used alone.

Vocal cord dysfunction.

A very small clinical study in patients aged 12 years or older with exercise-induced paradoxical vocal fold motion shows that the Buteyko breathing technique decreases dyspnea severity and respiratory tidal minute volume, but not dyspnea frequency, when compared with baseline ( 106571 ). These patients either had no history of asthma or had well-controlled asthma. The validity of these findings is limited by the lack of a comparator group.

More evidence is needed to rate Buteyko breathing technique for these uses.

Buteyko breathing technique involves various interventions to decrease respiratory rate and volume.  Some interventions include holding the breath at functional residual capacity (FRC), as well as breathing only through the nose. To ensure breathing through the nose at night, taping the mouth with porous surgical tape is sometimes recommended ( 27903 , 98993 , 98995 ). The Eucapnic Buteyko technique is a westernized version of the Russian Buteyko technique. It also involves exercises that reduce breathing frequency and depth, as well as mouth taping at night to encourage breathing through the nose ( 27905 ).

None known.

None known.

Dr. Konstantin Buteyko, founder of the Buteyko breathing technique, theorized that individuals with asthma usually hyperventilate, leading to an excessive loss of carbon dioxide from the lungs. When carbon dioxide levels are too low (hypocapnia), the airways respond by constricting, consequently lowering oxygen levels and causing symptoms of asthma ( 27902 ). He also theorized that breath holding time (BHT) can be used to identify chronic hyperventilation and hypocapnia. The Buteyko Breathing Technique is intended to correct chronic hyperventilation. However, in a small clinical study of healthy patients, BHT was shown to have a negative correlation with the amount of carbon dioxide present in the lungs, a direct contradiction to Dr. Buteyko's theory. However, a correlation between BHT and irregular spirometry was identified. A lower BHT in patients with irregular spirometry correlated with a thoracic-dominant breathing pattern, which is thought to contribute to dyspnea in asthma ( 98994 ). Others theorize that the Buteyko breathing technique's therapeutic effects might be attributed to a placebo effect related to either the extensive professional attention the patient receives and/or a change in the patient's perception of asthma. This suggests that professional attention and education about asthma and the role of conventional therapy is important ( 27902 , 27903 , 27904 , 27905 ).
