## Overview

Glucuronolactone is a chemical. It is made naturally from glucose in small amounts in the body of humans and other mammals. The average diet provides 1-2 mg of glucuronolactone daily. Synthetic glucuronolactone is used in "energy" drinks ( 102617 , 102620 , 102621 ). KEY HIGHLIGHTS

- Commonly used as an ingredient in "energy" drinks to increase attention and athletic performance, but there is no strong evidence to support these or other uses.
- Likely safe when consumed in food amounts. There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of glucuronolactone when used in medicinal amounts.
- No known major interactions

## History

In 1999, the European Scientific Committee on Food (SCF) was unable to draw conclusions related to the safety of glucuronolactone in concentrations used in "energy" drinks. This opinion was reiterated in a 2003 report. The European Food Safety Authority concluded in 2009 that at levels used in "energy" drinks, the use of glucuronolactone is not a safety concern ( 102621 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, glucuronolactone is used for antituberculosis therapy-associated hepatotoxicity. In combination with caffeine, taurine, and other ingredients, glucuronolactone is used in "energy" drinks to increase attention and athletic performance. In Europe, glucuronolactone is used in some cosmetic products.

Likely Safe when used orally in food amounts. The average diet provides glucuronolactone 1-2 mg daily, which has not been associated with any safety concerns ( 102620 , 102621 ). There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of glucuronolactone when used orally in medicinal amounts. While some countries consider glucuronolactone to be safe when used in amounts found in "energy" drinks ( 102620 , 102621 ), conclusive evidence regarding the safety of glucuronolactone supplements is lacking.

CHILDREN: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using in amounts greater than those commonly found in foods.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using in amounts greater than those commonly found in foods.

#### General

Orally, no adverse effects have been reported. However, a thorough evaluation of safety outcomes has not been conducted ( 102617 ). Glucuronolactone is found in "energy" drinks, often in amounts higher than those typically found in the diet. However, adverse effects associated with "energy" drinks are not thought to be related to glucuronolactone but rather to other ingredients including caffeine and taurine ( 102617 , 102619 , 102621 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the effectiveness of glucuronolactone.

In a subchronic 13-week study in rats, the no-observed-adverse-effect-level (NOAEL) of glucuronolactone was 300 mg/kg daily ( 102620 ). At doses of 600 and 1000 mg/kg daily there was vacuolisation and inflammatory changes in the kidney papilla of females ( 102620 , 102621 ). In a later study, kidney changes were determined to be incidental. The NOAEL was 1000 mg/kg, the highest dose tested. For a 60 kg person, this dose is 200-fold higher than the mean intake from "energy" drinks ( 102620 , 102621 ). There is no information on the genotoxic, teratogenic, or carcinogenic effects of glucuronolactone ( 102621 ). Based on these results, the Margin of Exposure (MOE) in children over the age of 3 years was calculated to be at least 588, with an MOE of at least 909 in adults, suggesting no appreciable health risk ( 102620 ).

#### Absorption

Glucuronolactone is readily absorbed ( 102621 ). In rats, peak plasma levels occur 1-2 hours after oral intake ( 102620 ).

#### Metabolism

In humans, glucuronolactone is metabolized to glucuronic acid, glucaric acid, xylitol, and L-xylulose ( 102613 , 102615 , 102620 , 102621 ). The metabolism of glucuronolactone to glucaric acid occurs in hepatic cells ( 102612 , 102614 ). In rodents, a small amount of glucuronolactone is metabolized to vitamin C ( 102621 ). At physiological pH, glucuronolactone is in equilibrium with glucuronic acid ( 102621 ).

#### Excretion

Glucuronolactone and its metabolites glucuronic acid and glucaric acid are excreted in urine within 24 to 48 hours of oral intake ( 102613 ).

#### General

Glucuronolactone is made naturally from glucose in the body. It is also found in small amounts in the diet. For use in "energy" drinks, glucuronolactone is produced from nitric acid and starch ( 102617 , 102620 , 102621 ).

#### Athletic performance effects

Glucuronolactone is a common ingredient in "energy" drinks, along with caffeine and taurine ( 102620 , 102621 ). There is limited evidence supporting the athletic performance effects of glucuronolactone in humans. In animal research, glucuronolactone prolonged swimming time. The mechanism of action is not clear; however, hepatic glycogen and blood glucose levels were increased ( 102615 ).
