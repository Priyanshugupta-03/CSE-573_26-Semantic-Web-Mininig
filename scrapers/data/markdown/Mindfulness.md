## Overview

Mindfulness is a practice derived from Buddhist theory that involves a purposeful attention and awareness of present thoughts, emotions, and sensations without evaluation or judgement of what is occurring. Various mind-body techniques are used to develop this awareness ( 101273 , 107083 , 107089 , 107101 ).

Likely Safe when used appropriately. Mindfulness, especially as mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), has been safely used in clinical trials. There is no known reason to expect any adverse effects ( 101273 , 100298 , 100397 , 100429 , 105414 , 105418 , 105420 , 105428 , 105432 , 107083 , 107099 )( 109368 , 109879 ).

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; however, mindfulness practices have been used in pregnant adults in one study without reports of adverse effects and there is no known reason to expect any adverse effects ( 105413 ).

#### General

Mindfulness is well tolerated and considered generally safe.

#### Psychiatric

Some studies report rare cases of anger, worsening anxiety, and thoughts of self-harm in patients, but these effects could have been caused by the underlying ailment rather than practicing mindfulness ( 100273 , 105414 , 109368 ).

Possibly Effective
Anxiety.

Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) seems to reduce anxiety in most people when compared with an inactive control, and may be similarly effective to standard therapeutics, psychotherapy education, or cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).

Meta-analyses, preliminary clinical research, and observational research in patients with various anxiety types show that practicing MBSR or modified MBSR improves anxiety severity when compared with baseline or no intervention ( 100271 , 100275 , 105423 , 105424 ). A large preliminary clinical trial in adults with a primary anxiety disorder (e.g., agoraphobia, panic, generalized anxiety disorder, or social anxiety) also shows that practicing MBSR for 8 weeks seems to be as effective as taking escitalopram 10 mg or 20 mg daily for 8 weeks in clinician- or patient-reported anxiety measures ( 109368 , 116678 ). However, most research shows that MBSR doesn't reduce the severity of anxiety immediately after or 3-12 months after treatment when compared with CBT, and possibly psychotherapy education ( 100271 , 105424 , 107096 ). Studies have been conducted in various population groups, including veterans, adolescents and young adults, patients with anxiety disorders, and individuals with feelings of anxiety ( 100271 , 105424 ). Some research shows that benefits might last up to 6 months ( 100275 ). However, most research shows that any benefit of MBSR programs appears to have a limited duration ( 105411 , 105424 ). Although some research disagrees, most clinical research in adults with back pain, cancer, diabetes, hypertension, insomnia, migraine headache, and those who are pregnant also shows that practicing mindfulness reduces anxiety scores when compared with control. However, most studies do not clearly report the prevalence of anxiety at baseline and likely include individuals with and without anxiety ( 105411 , 105418 , 105429 , 105430 , 105432 , 107095 , 109879 , 109882 , 109883 , 109884 )( 113845 , 113848 , 113850 , 116677 ).

Attention deficit-hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).

Mindfulness interventions seem to improve ADHD symptoms in adults and children.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical and observational research in adults and children with ADHD shows that mindfulness-based Interventions (MBIs) have a modest to large effect on overall ADHD symptoms, including attention, hyperactivity, executive function, depression, and anxiety, when compared with waitlist controls. Mindfulness programs used in the studies included Mindfulness Awareness Practice (MAP), Mindfulness Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT), and Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) ( 107093 ). Preliminary clinical research in adult students with self-reported ADHD and/or learning disabilities shows that participating in an MBSR program for 8 weeks improves ADHD hyperactivity and inattention scores when compared with daily device-guided breathing exercises for 3 weeks or no intervention. The MBSR program included 8 weekly mindfulness sessions for 2.5 hours each ( 105422 ).

Back pain.

Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) seems to be effective for the short-term improvement of back pain and physical function. The long-term benefits of MBSR are unclear.

A meta-analysis of clinical research shows that MBSR slightly improves back pain and physical function in the short-term when compared with usual care. However, these benefits are not sustained long-term ( 100397 ). A more recent meta-analysis of clinical research shows that MBSR modestly improves physical function when compared with various inactive and active controls, with benefits lasting for up to 6 months. However, pain was not an outcome and only 2-3 studies were included in each analysis ( 109880 ). An additional large clinical trial in patients with chronic low back pain shows that eight sessions of MBSR reduces pain and improves physical function and mood for up to 6 months after treatment when compared with usual care. However, the frequency of a clinically meaningful improvement in pain was not increased when compared with usual care ( 107094 ). One clinical trial in patients with back pain related to degenerative or herniated disc shows that nine sessions of a specific MBSR program for chronic back pain with daily home exercises modestly improves pain, the overall ability to cope with pain, physical limitations, energy, social function, and well-being when compared with usual care alone. However, there was no effect on sleep, disability, or physical function ( 109882 ). While one small clinical study in patients with low back pain shows that MBSR is no better than CBT or usual care for improving chronic low back pain, this study was limited by poor compliance and variability in follow-up rates between groups ( 100398 ). Guidelines published in 2017 by the American College of Physicians recommend the use of non-pharmacologic therapy, such as MBSR, prior to using pharmacologic therapy in patients with chronic low back pain ( 95773 ).

Cancer-related fatigue.

Low quality studies suggest that mindfulness modestly improves symptoms in patients with cancer-related fatigue. Guidelines from multiple oncology associations include mindfulness as a potential treatment option for reducing fatigue.

A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in adults with cancer shows that specifically participating in mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) for 8 days to 8 weeks moderately improves cancer-related fatigue scores when compared with no intervention. MBSR was led by an expert with or without a recording 1-6 times per week for 0.75-3 hours per session.Preliminary subgroup analyses show that programs with a longer duration may be more beneficial than shorter durations. However, the validity of these results is limited by potential publication bias ( 105420 ). Another meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials shows that participating in an average of 7 sessions of MBSR modestly improves cancer-related fatigue when compared with control, with some preliminary evidence suggesting that benefits last at least 3 months ( 109883 ). Meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials in adult females with breast cancer shows that participating in an MBSR or modified MBSR program for 6-8 weeks improves fatigue scores when compared with no intervention. However, the benefits of mindfulness appear to be limited in duration ( 105411 , 107095 ). A more recent meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in females with cancer shows that mindfulness, specifically MBSR, mindfulness awareness  practice, and mindfulness-based art therapy, has a modest effect on cancer-related fatigue for at least 4 weeks when compared with controls such as usual care or a waitlist control ( 109879 ). The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for cancer-related fatigue recommend MBSR as a nonpharmacologic treatment option for patients who have completed cancer treatment, based on high-quality evidence. For patients undergoing active cancer treatment, mindfulness is included within a broader group of psychosocial interventions such as cognitive behavioral therapy and psychoeducational therapies that collectively have high-level evidence for benefit in managing cancer-related fatigue ( 115901 ). Furthermore, the American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) and the Society for Integrative Oncology recommend mindfulness for patients who have finished cancer treatment and for cancer survivors ( 108673 ).

Depression.

Most clinical research shows that mindfulness reduces symptoms of depression.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in patients with depression shows that mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) reduces the risk of depressive relapse within 60 weeks of treatment to 38% of patients, compared with 49% of those receiving usual care such as antidepressants ( 107105 ). Another meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in young adults shows that practicing mindfulness interventions, usually over 2-8 weeks, modestly reduces depression when compared with a control group not receiving a mindfulness intervention. A sub-analysis limited to five studies of patients with clinical depression shows a large benefit. Types of interventions included adapted mindfulness-based intervention (aMBI), as well as MBCT and mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) ( 109871 ). A meta-analysis of four preliminary clinical trials in patients with a past stroke and clinically diagnosed depression also shows that MBSR and MBCT for 6-8 weeks have a large effect on symptoms of depression when compared with usual care ( 109876 ). Additional individual preliminary clinical trials agree with these findings. A small clinical study in patients with major depression or those with persistent self-reported symptoms of depression shows that practicing a mindfulness-based intervention 25 minutes twice daily for 2 weeks reduces depressive symptoms to a greater degree than psycho-education and resting ( 100293 ). Also, a small clinical study in patients with depression and spinal cord injury (SCI) shows that an 8-week online mindfulness intervention reduces depression, anxiety, unpleasantness of pain, and catastrophizing when compared with an educational control ( 100400 ). Mindfulness has also been evaluated in populations with various underlying conditions. Most clinical research in older adults, pregnant adults, or adults with back pain, hypertension, insomnia, diabetes, stroke, or cancer, shows that practicing mindfulness reduces depression scores when compared with control. However, these studies did not clearly report the prevalence of depression at baseline and likely included individuals with and without depression ( 105411 , 105421 , 105426 , 105427 , 105430 , 105432 , 107095 , 109879 , 109882 , 109883 )( 109884 , 113845 , 113848 , 113850 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in adult females with breast cancer shows that participating in a mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) or modified MBSR program for 6-8 weeks improves depression scores when compared with no intervention. However, the benefits of mindfulness appear to be limited in duration ( 105411 ). Conversely, a more recent meta-analysis in patients with breast cancer shows that participating in an MBSR for 6-8 weeks does not improve depression when compared with control groups not receiving MBSR ( 107095 ).

Fibromyalgia.

Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) seems to be effective for the short-term reduction of fibromyalgia symptoms; however, longer term effects are unclear.

A 2013 meta-analysis of low-quality clinical research in patients with fibromyalgia shows that MBSR modestly improves quality of life and pain in the short-term when compared with usual care or active control interventions ( 100298 ). Additionally, a meta-analysis of low-quality clinical trials in patients with fibromyalgia shows that practicing mindfulness meditation improves depression, quality of life, and sleep quality in the short-term compared with control ( 116679 ). However, the long-term benefits of MBSR for fibromyalgia are unclear. One meta-analysis of clinical research suggests that improvements in depression and stress are maintained at 3 months ( 116679 ).  However, a separate meta-analysis suggests that MBSR does not improve pain, depression, sleep, or fatigue with longer-term follow-up of approximately 6 months ( 100298 ).

Insomnia.

Mindfulness appears to improve some sleep measures in most adults; however, it may be no more effective than other therapies, such as education, cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), or exercise.

A meta-analysis in patients with insomnia shows that, although mindfulness meditation seems to improve total wake time and sleep quality, it does not seem to improve other measures such as sleep onset latency, total sleep time, and sleep efficiency when compared with control ( 100427 ). Another meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in adults with insomnia shows that practicing mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) or modified MBSR for 6-8 weeks improves sleep quality when compared with control groups receiving education or CBT. Subjects practicing MBSR also reported improved depression and anxiety scores. However, these results are limited by high heterogeneity ( 105430 ). A secondary analysis of a clinical trial in generally healthy adults also shows that practicing MBSR for 8 weeks improves sleep scores when compared with no intervention. However, MBSR improved sleep scores to a similar or lesser amount when compared with exercise ( 105431 ). Preliminary clinical research suggests that mindfulness is more effective in subjects with more severe sleep disturbances ( 105431 ). Other preliminary clinical research in females using benzodiazepines and/or another hypnotic agent for insomnia shows that participating in a mindfulness-based relapse prevention (MBRP) program for 8 weeks does not immediately reduce the severity of insomnia when compared with weekly phone monitoring alone. Insomnia severity was reduced 6 months after treatment; however, the relationship to MBRP is not clear ( 107092 ). Mindfulness has also been evaluated for improving sleep in patients with cancer. Some meta-analyses have specifically focused on patients with cancer. A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in adults with cancer shows that practicing MBSR or modified MBSR for 6-8 weeks improves sleep quality when compared with no intervention, but not when compared with an active control such as education or CBT. Individuals practicing MBSR did not show improvements in other measures, such as sleep onset latency, total sleep time, or sleep efficiency, when compared with no intervention or active controls ( 105419 ). Another meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in adult females with breast cancer shows that participating in an MBSR or modified MBSR program for 6-8 weeks improves sleep quality scores when compared with no intervention ( 105411 ). Other research agrees ( 109884 ). However, other meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials in females with cancer show that participating in an MBSR program or mindful practice does not improve sleep quality ( 107095 , 109879 ). The effect of mindfulness on sleep quality has also been investigated in patients with other conditions. Preliminary clinical research in adults with postmenopausal symptoms shows that MBSR training over 8 weeks modestly improves overall sleep quality, including sleep efficiency, latency, and duration, with benefits lasting for at least one month post-treatment, when compared with weekly menopausal health meetings devoid of information related to insomnia ( 109874 ). Additionally, a clinical trial in patients with spinal cord injury shows that participating in a daily MBSR program for 8 weeks improves sleep quality when compared with music therapy ( 116676 ). Some meta-analyses which have included mixed populations have found no effect of MBSR on sleep quality when compared with active controls. These studies included healthy patients, patients with insomnia, and patients with cancer; it is unclear whether evaluating mixed populations together altered the outcomes of the meta-analyses ( 109877 ).

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Most research suggests that mindfulness is effective for reducing symptoms of PTSD.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research show that mindfulness-based treatment is beneficial for reducing symptoms of PTSD ( 100436 , 107081 , 109878 ). One meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with PTSD shows that mindfulness-based treatments, including mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), modestly improve symptoms when compared to a waitlist control. Interventions with a longer training duration seem to have a greater benefit ( 100436 ). Another meta-analysis in patients with military-related PTSD shows that both group-or individual- based mindfulness meditation of short or moderate duration modestly reduced symptoms of PTSD when compared with control conditions including treatment as usual, present-centered group therapy (PCGT), and PTSD health education. Types of mindfulness included mind-body bridging, mindfulness-based exposure therapy (MBET), MBSR, and breathing-based yoga meditation practices ( 107081 ). Also, one meta-analysis of clinical research in patients with PTSD treated specifically with MBSR shows that PTSD symptoms are reduced by a moderate to large degree when compared with control, including usual care, waitlist, other health care, or PCGT ( 109878 ). However, one individual study suggests that improvement of symptoms may be subjective. One randomized controlled trial shows that weekly 90-minute group sessions of MBSR for 8 weeks improves self-reported symptoms, but not clinician-determined symptoms, when compared with PCGT. Also, there was no difference between groups for the rate of response or rate of remission ( 107087 ).

Schizophrenia.

Mindfulness-based psychoeducation seems to improve symptoms of schizophrenia and improve recovery and functioning.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in Chinese patients with psychosis related to schizophrenia shows that mindfulness-based interventions have small to large effects on rehospitalization duration, recovery rate, functioning, coping, self-esteem, and symptoms of psychosis, depression, and anxiety when compared to control groups which generally consisted of treatment with usual care. However, there was no effect of mindfulness on rates of rehospitalization or remission, or on quality of life. There was some evidence of benefit at follow-up times ranging from 6-24 months; however, data were limited ( 107088 ). One large preliminary clinical trial in Chinese and Taiwanese patients included in this meta-analysis shows that receiving mindfulness-based psychoeducation results in shorter hospitalization duration over 2 years and improves positive and negative symptoms when compared to standard treatment alone. The mindfulness intervention seems to also reduce hospitalizations to a greater degree when compared to cognitive behavioral therapy ( 100423 ). Additionally, a network meta-analysis of clinical research shows that mindfulness ranks as the first and third most effective intervention for positive and negative symptoms, respectively, among 5 different types of mind-body therapies ( 114875 ).

Social anxiety disorder.

Mindfulness-based interventions seem to be effective for improving symptoms of social anxiety disorder.

Meta-analyses and preliminary clinical research in patients with social anxiety disorder shows that mindfulness-based interventions, especially mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), improve symptoms when compared with baseline or no treatment. However, mindfulness appears to be less effective than evidence-based treatments such as cognitive behavioral therapy ( 100272 , 100274 , 107098 ). Meta-analyses also show that symptoms of depression, mindfulness, quality of life, and self-compassion all improved, with benefits lasting up to 12 months ( 107098 ). An individual study included in this meta-analysis examined the effects of a mindfulness-based intervention specific to social anxiety disorder (MBI-SAD) ( 100274 ). It is unclear how mindfulness-based interventions compare with treatments such as SSRIs. Also, most individual studies are limited by small size and patient populations that might not be generalizable to all patients with social anxiety disorder.

Stress.

Mindfulness-based interventions seem to reduce stress in healthy adults and adults with various medical conditions.

Clinical studies in students show that mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), or a different 8-week program called mindfulness skills for students, reduces stress before examinations and a stressful arithmetic activity ( 100432 , 100440 ). Another small clinical trial in healthy working adults shows that completing an 8-week mindfulness training tailored for the workplace improves stress, self-compassion, and burnout when compared with control group ( 100431 ). MBSR has also been shown to reduce stress and depressive symptoms in caregivers of patients with dementia when compared with baseline or control ( 100437 , 100443 ). However, shorter mindfulness training might not be sufficient to reduce stress in caregivers. One clinical study in parents of infants in the neonatal intensive care unit shows that a 30-60-minute training session on mindfulness-based neurodevelopmental care does not reduce parental distress when compared with control ( 100441 ). Mindfulness based therapy has also reduced stress associated with various medical conditions. Research shows that MBSR and other mindfulness-based therapy reduces stress and stress-related symptoms such as anxiety, depression, and quality of life in cancer patients when compared with usual care or a waitlist control ( 100283 , 100284 , 100285 , 109884 , 113845 , 113848 ). A small clinical study in patients after solid organ transplants shows that completing the MBSR program improves anxiety, depression, and sleep quality for up to 1 year when compared with a peer-led health education program ( 100434 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that mindfulness-based programs reduce stress in infertile females and adults with chronic back pain ( 100439 , 109882 ).

Possibly Ineffective
Age-related cognitive decline.

Clinical studies show that mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) does not seem to improve cognitive function in older adults.

A large preliminary clinical trial in older adults with subjective cognitive concerns shows that participating in MBSR, alone or in combination with exercise, does not improve memory, executive function, other cognitive measures, or structural brain changes, when compared with health education as control. MBSR treatment consisted of eight weekly sessions followed by monthly sessions, with up to 60 minutes daily of meditative practice for a total of 18 months ( 109881 ). Also, a smaller clinical study in healthy middle-aged or older adults without anxiety disorders shows that participating in a practice modeled after MBSR for 8 weeks does not improve memory or processing speed when compared to practicing karate ( 100290 ). An additional clinical study in older adults with anxiety and/or depression shows that participating in MBSR does not improve cognitive function, although it may improve memory, when compared with participating in a health education program ( 100442 ).

Obesity.

Clinical research suggests that although practicing mindfulness may reduce poor dietary habits, it does not seem to improve weight loss.

Two meta-analyses of clinical trials in adults with obesity show that mindfulness interventions for 6-24 weeks do not reduce body mass index (BMI) or weight, especially when compared with dietary or lifestyle interventions. However, these interventions may reduce impulsive or binge eating ( 100396 , 107100 ). Individual clinical studies also support this conclusion. A small clinical study in obese adults who had bariatric surgery 1-5 years ago shows that practicing a mindfulness-based intervention for 10 weeks does not improve weight loss but may reduce emotional eating at the 6-month follow-up ( 100393 ). An additional small clinical study in overweight and obese females shows that participating in an 8-week mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) program does not improve blood pressure, weight, or insulin resistance at a 16-week follow-up when compared with a health education control ( 100394 ). Preliminary clinical research in adult students, including normal weight, overweight, and obese students, shows that using a mindfulness mobile application for 11 weeks does not reduce weight when compared with completing a diet and exercise diary, although use of the application moderately reduces self-reported uncontrolled and emotional eating ( 105412 ). Also, observational research in pregnant, low-income adults, including normal weight, overweight, and obese adults, shows that participating in a modified MBSR program that incorporated mindful eating practices for 8 weeks does not affect weight at the end of the program or at follow-up 6 months postpartum when compared with no intervention, although it did reduce stress and depression scores ( 105413 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Alcohol use disorder.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for alcohol use disorder.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with a desire to reduce alcohol consumption shows that participating in a mindfulness-based relapse prevention (MBRP) program in combination with transcranial direct current simulation for 8 weeks does not reduce alcohol cravings or alcohol consumption, including heavy drinking days or drinks per drinking day, when compared with MBRP alone. The MBRP program included 8 weekly 2-hour mindfulness sessions led by an expert ( 105415 ). Also, a small clinical study in adults who recently completed treatment for alcohol use disorder shows that participating in an adapted MBRP (MBRP-A) program for 26 weeks does not reduce self-reported alcohol consumption, including any drinking, drinking days, any heavy drinking, or heavy drinking days, when compared with no intervention. The MBRP-A program included 8 weekly 2-hour mindfulness sessions led by an expert and home MBRP-A practice for 30 minutes 6 times per week. The validity of these results is limited by poor adherence to expert-led and home MBRP-A sessions ( 105414 ). A clinical study in female and male adults who reported drinking more than 14 and 21 alcoholic beverages weekly, respectively, and wished to reduce or cease their drinking shows that participating in an MBRP program 1 hour weekly for 8 weeks decreases the number of heavy drinking days and drinks per drinking day when compared to baseline but not when compared with a standard relapse prevention program control group ( 113849 ). The validity of these findings is limited by a high attrition rate and different rates of cannabis use between the two groups.

Alzheimer disease.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based Alzheimer stimulation (MBAS) is beneficial for Alzheimer disease.

A small clinical study in patients with Alzheimer disease taking donepezil shows that participating in three 90-minute weekly sessions of MBAS for 2 years improves scores on the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) and on Cambridge Cognitive Examination (CAMCOG) when compared with control. While mindfulness was no different than cognitive stimulation therapy for improving MMSE or CAMCOG, patient participating in MBAS had better scores on CAMCOG when compared with progressive muscle relaxation ( 100270 ).

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS, Lou Gehrig's disease).

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for ALS.

A small open-label clinical trial in patients with ALS shows that participating in an 8-week meditation training based on the original MBSR program improves quality of life and psychological well-being when compared with usual care ( 100269 ).

Anorexia nervosa.

Although there is interest in using mindfulness for anorexia nervosa, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mindfulness for this condition.

Asthma.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for asthma.

A small clinical trial in adults with mild to severe persistent asthma shows that completing an MBSR group-based 8-week program improves quality of life and stress, but does not seem to improve lung function or asthma control, when compared with participating in an educational program ( 100277 ). However, a similar-sized clinical trial shows that practicing MBSR over 8 weeks results in a clinically significant improvement in asthma control in 36% of patients, compared with 12% of patients on a waitlist. These improvements were associated with modest benefits in feelings of distress and persisted for at least 4 months ( 109873 ).

Athletic performance.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for improving athletic performance.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in healthy athletes shows that practicing mindfulness for 50-60 minutes once weekly and up to twice daily for 1 month to 2 years improves physiological markers and performance in precision sports such as shooting and dart-throwing when compared with control ( 100278 ). The validity of these results is limited by the lack of clarification related to the biomarkers used to provide non-standard composite outcomes such as "physiological performance surrogates".

Autism spectrum disorder.

It is unclear if practicing a modified mindfulness-based therapy (MBT-AS) is beneficial for autism spectrum disorder.

A small clinical trial in high-functioning adults with autism spectrum disorder shows that practicing a modified mMBT-AS protocol 2.5 hours weekly for 9 weeks improves depression, anxiety, and rumination when compared with a waitlist control. The protocol was modified to provide an extra week and patients were advised to meditate for a longer duration of 40-60 minutes daily for 6 days per week ( 100279 ).

Benzodiazepine withdrawal.

It is unclear if participating in a mindfulness-based relapse prevention (MBRP) program is beneficial for reducing the use of benzodiazepines.

Preliminary clinical research in females using benzodiazepines and/or another hypnotic agent for insomnia shows that participating in an MBRP program for 8 weeks reduces the dose of hypnotics used for up to 2 months when compared with weekly phone monitoring only ( 107092 ).

Binge eating disorder.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for binge eating disorder.

A meta-analysis of three preliminary clinical trials evaluating patients with binge eating disorder or overweight or obese individuals with binge eating symptoms shows that mindfulness based interventions over 6-24 weeks modestly decreases symptoms of binge eating disorders and improves mindfulness when compared with promotion of dietary or exercise-based behavioral changes ( 107100 ). In addition, preliminary clinical research in patients with either bulimia nervosa or binge eating disorder shows that using group mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) for 8 weeks as complementary therapy to usual care improves some measures of eating behavior and mindfulness skills and reduces levels of depression and anxiety when compared to usual care alone ( 107102 ).

Bipolar disorder.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) is beneficial for bipolar disorder.

Meta-analyses of preliminary clinical research in patients with bipolar disorder show that MBCT has a small to moderate effect on symptoms of depression, anxiety, stress, and mindfulness, but not manic symptoms or cognition, when compared with baseline ( 107103 , 107104 ). However, based on three randomized controlled trials, MBCT does not reduce symptoms of depression or anxiety when compared with control groups receiving treatment as usual ( 107103 , 107104 ).

Breast cancer.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for breast cancer.

A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in adult females with breast cancer shows that participating in a mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) or modified MBSR program for 6-8 weeks moderately improves quality of life scores when compared with no intervention. However, the benefits of mindfulness appear to be limited in duration. Most subjects were not actively receiving chemotherapy or other cancer treatment during the MBSR program ( 105411 ). In contrast, a more recent meta-analysis shows that participating in MBSR does not improve quality of life when compared to control groups not receiving MBSR ( 107095 ).

Bulimia nervosa.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) is beneficial for bulimia nervosa.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with either bulimia nervosa or binge eating disorder shows that using group mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) for 8 weeks as complementary therapy to usual care improves some measures of eating behavior and mindfulness skills and reduces levels of depression and anxiety when compared to usual care alone ( 107102 ). However, the effects of MBCT on bulimia nervosa specifically are unclear.

Cancer-related pain.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for reducing cancer-related pain.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in adult females with breast cancer shows that participating in MBSR for 6-8 weeks does not improve pain when compared with control groups not receiving MBSR ( 107095 ). The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for adult cancer pain suggest psychosocial support, including MBSR, as integrative treatment options that can be used alone or in conjunction with pharmacological interventions for pain management, based on lower-level evidence. These interventions may be particularly important for vulnerable populations, such as frail or older individuals who may not tolerate pharmacological treatments, or those who prefer non-drug approaches ( 116041 ). The guideline from the Society for Integrative Oncology-American Society of Clinical Oncology indicates that the quality of evidence for mindfulness for general cancer or surgery- or procedure-related pain is low or inconclusive ( 109161 ).

Cannabis use disorder.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based relapse prevention (MBRP) is beneficial for cannabis use disorder.

Preliminary clinical research in individuals willing to reduce or stop cannabis use shows that MBRP for 8 weeks does not reduce the quantity of cannabis smoked when compared with usual care ( 107083 ). A larger study is needed to confirm these results.

Cardiovascular disease (CVD).

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for improving quality of life in patients with CVD.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with CVD shows that participating in an MBSR program for 8 weeks improves quality of life scores by 18% and self-efficacy scores by 20%, with benefits maintained after 3 months of follow up, when compared with no intervention. The MBSR program included 8 weekly sessions led by an expert ( 105417 ).

Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for chronic neuropathic pain in patients with breast cancer.

Preliminary clinical research in survivors of breast cancer with neuropathic pain shows that MBSR does not reduce pain when compared with patients on a waitlist. However, the effect of MBSR on chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy is unclear, as only 41% of patients in this analysis reported neuropathy related to chemotherapy use, with 59% of patients reporting neuropathy primarily related to mastectomy ( 109875 ).

Cocaine dependence.

Although there is interest in using mindfulness for cocaine dependence, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mindfulness for this use.

Cognitive function.

It is unclear if group-based mindfulness meditation is beneficial for improving cognitive function.

A meta-analysis of clinical research in adults shows that when compared with a control group, group-based mindfulness intervention has a small beneficial effect on overall executive functioning, as well as specific executive functions such as inhibition, working memory, and verbal fluency, but not attention shifting ( 107089 ). Most studies included in the analysis were described as non-clinical adult populations; however, some studies included community dwelling older adults, as well as individuals with attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), pain, depression, or cancer. Also, studies included in the analysis vary widely with respect to methods and controls.

Crohn disease.

It is unclear if cognitive-behavioral and mindfulness-based stress reduction (COBMINDEX) is beneficial for Crohn disease.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with mild to moderate Crohn disease shows that COBMINDEX over 3 months modestly reduces fatigue and disease activity, including levels of C-reactive protein and calprotectin, and improves quality of life, when compared with baseline. These benefits did not occur in a group of patients on a waitlist; however, between-group comparisons were not conducted ( 107085 ).

Diabetes.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for type 1 or type 2 diabetes.

A meta-analysis of randomized clinical trials in patients with type 1 or type 2 diabetes shows that participating in a mindfulness program reduces glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) by a mean of 0.28% when compared with an inactive control group. Participating in a mindfulness program also improves quality of life and depression scores when compared with the inactive control. The mindfulness programs included mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) or mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) sessions 1-3 times per week for 2 weeks to 9 months ( 105421 ). Another meta-analysis of small to moderate-sized clinical studies in patients with diabetes shows that 8-week MBSR programs do not improve HbA1c when compared with standard diabetes management or waitlist control ( 113850 ). The reason for the discrepant finding between the 2 meta-analyses for HbA1c is unclear but may be explained by differences in statistical analytical methods used. Additionally, the validity of these findings is limited by substantial heterogeneity of the included studies which enrolled patients with type 1, type 2, and gestational diabetes.

Diabetic neuropathy.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for diabetic neuropathy.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with diabetic neuropathy shows that MBSR over 8 weeks modestly reduces pain and pain catastrophizing and improves function 12 weeks after treatment when compared with usual care while on a waitlist. Response to the intervention, defined as a decrease of at least 1 point on a scale related to pain interference, occurred in 63% of patients practicing MBSR, compared with only 22% of those given usual care. Based on these finding, 2.5 patients needed treatment with MBSR in order for one patient to be much or very much improved ( 109886 ).

Epilepsy.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for epilepsy.

A small clinical study in patients with drug resistant epilepsy shows that practicing mindfulness-based therapy for four 2.5-hour sessions over 2 weeks seems to improve quality of life but does not improve seizure frequency when compared with social support ( 100296 ).

Gaming disorder.

It is unclear if mindfulness meditation is beneficial in patients with gaming disorder.

A clinical trial in adults with gaming disorder shows that participating in a mindfulness meditation program delivered twice weekly for 4 weeks decreases gaming cravings and severity of internet gaming disorder compared with an active control of progressive muscle relaxation, based on a per-protocol analysis ( 116680 ).

Glaucoma.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for glaucoma.

Preliminary clinical research in patients with primary open angle glaucoma and controlled intraocular pressure shows that adding 45 minutes of MBSR daily for 6 weeks to routine treatment modestly reduces intraocular pressure and improves optic disc perfusion when compared with routine treatment alone ( 107091 ). The long-term effects of MBSR on vision are unclear.

Hypertension.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for lowering blood pressure.

A meta-analysis of randomized clinical trials in adults with hypertension shows that participating in an MBSR or modified MBSR program for 8 weeks reduces systolic blood pressure (SBP) by 7 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) by 3 mmHg when compared with control. The MBSR programs included weekly sessions for 1.25-2.5 hours each. The reductions in DBP, but not SBP, were maintained for up to 6 months ( 105432 ). However, another meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials in adults with prehypertension or hypertension shows that participating in an MBSR program for 8 weeks reduces SBP by 11 mmHg, with no change in DBP, when compared with a waitlist control. In contrast, when compared with an active control such as health education or progressive muscle relaxation, DBP, but not SBP, was reduced by 5 mmHg ( 109872 ). These differences may be related to the heterogeneity and low quality of the included studies.

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for IBS symptom reduction.

A small clinical study in females with IBS shows that mindfulness reduces IBS symptom severity by 26%, compared with a 6% reduction in the control support group. This trend was maintained at a 3-month follow-up. The participants in the mindfulness group attended one individual training session followed by 8 weekly group training sessions ( 100388 ).

Labor pain.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for labor pain.

Preliminary clinical research shows that completing a 2.5-day mindfulness-based course to prepare for childbirth improves childbirth self-efficacy and depressive symptoms, but does not reduce labor pain or analgesic use, when compared with a standard childbirth education course ( 100419 ).

Menopausal symptoms.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for menopausal symptoms.

A small clinical study shows that practicing MBSR reduces menopause-related stress, sleep disturbance, and symptom severity when compared with waitlist control. In the MBSR group, those that practiced at home more frequently had greater reductions in symptom severity. However, there was no improvement in pain and physical function ( 100391 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that 8 weekly sessions of MBSR modestly improves quality of life after menopause for up to 3 months after treatment when compared with control. Improvements were noted in psychosocial, physical, and sexual domains ( 107097 ).

Methamphetamine use disorder.

Although there is interest in using mindfulness for methamphetamine use disorder, there is insufficient reliable information about the clinical effects of mindfulness for this use.

Migraine headache.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for migraine headache.

A meta-analysis of small clinical studies in patients with either migraine or tension headaches shows that mindfulness meditation modestly improves pain intensity and headache frequency when compared with control ( 100248 ). However, larger, clinical studies evaluating only adults with migraine headache have yielded conflicting findings with the use of mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) ( 105418 , 105428 , 105429 ). Two clinical trials in adults with a history of migraine headache show that participating in an MBSR program for 8 weeks does not reduce migraine frequency, days with a migraine, or pain intensity when compared with participating in a migraine education program. However, these trials show that participating in an MBSR program improves coping abilities related to migraines, such as increased headache-related quality of life scores and reduced anxiety and disability scores ( 105418 , 105429 ). In contrast, another clinical trial in adults with a history of migraine headache shows that although participating in an MBSR program for 8 weeks does not improve migraine severity, it reduces the average days with a headache by 2.4 over 4 weeks and days with a migraine by 1.7 over 4 weeks when compared with participating in a stress management program ( 105428 ). Each of these MBSR programs included 8 weekly sessions led by an expert for 2-2.5 hours each and encouraged home MBSR practice daily ( 105418 , 105428 , 105429 ).

Multiple sclerosis (MS).

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for MS.

A meta-analysis of 3 small, low quality clinical studies in adults with MS shows that practicing mindfulness-based interventions including mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) and mindfulness-based cognitive therapy daily for 8 weeks reduces pain when compared with waitlist control ( 115558 ). A clinical study in patients with MS with moderate disability shows that practicing a structured mindfulness-based intervention for 8 weeks improves depression, fatigue, and quality of life when compared with usual care. Improvements were reduced but still notable at a 6-month follow-up ( 100392 ).

Opioid withdrawal.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for opioid withdrawal.

Preliminary clinical research in adult males receiving methadone therapy for opiate dependence shows that participating in 8 weekly mindfulness sessions of 2 hours each reduces craving scores by 18% and improves quality of life (QOL) scores by 7% when compared with no intervention. The improved craving and QOL scores were maintained 8 weeks after completing the mindfulness program ( 105416 ).

Pain (chronic).

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for chronic pain reduction.

A meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with chronic pain shows that mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) and other types of mindfulness meditations do not produce statistically significant reductions in pain when compared with control treatments such as education, usual care, support groups, stress management, and waitlist ( 100273 ). However, a small clinical study in patients with HIV and chronic pain shows that MBSR modestly improves pain and stress for at least 3 months when compared with a health education control ( 100399 ).

Parkinson disease.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for Parkinson disease.

A small preliminary clinical study in patients with Parkinson disease shows that participating in a mindfulness-based lifestyle program for 2 hours weekly for 6 weeks does not improve overall symptoms when compared with a waitlist control ( 100416 ). Other preliminary clinical research shows that mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) over a 9-week period improves mindfulness and quality of life in patients with Parkinson disease. Practicing MBSR also improved mindfulness in their caregivers, but did not reduce the feeling of burden on the caregiver ( 107084 ).

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for PCOS.

A small clinical study in patients with PCOS shows that participating in a mindfulness stress management program for 8 weeks improves depression, anxiety, and quality of life (QOL) when compared with no intervention ( 100418 ). A similar small clinical study in patients with PCOS and infertility shows that participating in an 8-week mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) program improves scores on a QOL assessment, but does not improve conception rates, when compared with dry cupping ( 113853 ).

Postherpetic neuralgia.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for postherpetic neuralgia.

Preliminary clinical research in adults with postherpetic neuralgia shows that participating in an MBSR program reduces pain scores, depression scores, and anxiety scores by 37%, 16%, and 13%, respectively, when compared with no intervention. The MBSR program included 8 weekly mindfulness sessions led by an expert for 2.5-3.5 hours each and home MBSR sessions of 0.75-1 hour each, six times per week ( 105425 ). In contrast, a small clinical study in patients with postherpetic neuralgia shows that practicing mindfulness meditation with a recording for 3-15 minutes daily for six weeks does not reduce pain when compared with usual care alone. However, this study may have been inadequately powered to detect a difference between groups ( 100421 ).

Post-stroke fatigue.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for reducing post-stroke fatigue.

A small clinical study in patients after stroke or traumatic brain injury shows that practicing MBSR improves mental fatigue and cognition when compared with waitlist control ( 101270 ).

Pre-eclampsia.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for preventing pre-eclampsia.

A large preliminary clinical trial in individuals at high risk of having a newborn that is below the 10th percentile in size shows that an 8-week MBSR program adapted for pregnancy, including 2.5-hour weekly sessions with a single full-day session, does not reduce the odds of developing pre-eclampsia ( 106879 ).

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS).

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for PMS.

A clinical study in young adults with PMS shows that participating in an MBSR program reduces PMS symptoms, including anxiety, fatigue, and irritability, when compared with a control group receiving no intervention. The MBSR was delivered online and consisted of 2.5-hour sessions weekly for 8 weeks with a 6-hour silent meditation retreat during the 6 th week ( 113847 ). The sample was derived from university students in a single midwifery program in Turkey, limiting the generalizability of findings.

Preterm labor.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for preventing preterm labor.

A large preliminary clinical trial in individuals at high risk of having a newborn that is below the 10th percentile in size shows that an 8-week MBSR program adapted for pregnancy, including 2.5-hour weekly sessions with a single full-day session, does not reduce the odds of a preterm birth ( 106879 ).

Psychological well-being.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for psychological well-being.

A small clinical study in patients after percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) shows that participating in 4 sessions of mindfulness interventions seems to improve psychological and social well-being, but not physical quality of life, when compared with using a self-help booklet ( 100289 ). Mindfulness has also been evaluated for psychological benefits in other special populations. A large clinical study in adults with self-reported maladaptive daydreaming shows that participating in an 8-week program consisting of internet-based mindfulness meditation, psychoeducation, and motivational enhancement with and without additional self-monitoring training improves scores on patient assessments of maladaptive daydreaming after 8 weeks and at  6 months follow-up, when compared with a control group receiving no intervention ( 113851 ). It is unclear if these effects are due to mindfulness, other program components, or the combination. Study findings are limited by reliance on patient-reported outcomes instead of clinician-administered assessments.

Respiratory tract infections.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for preventing or reducing the severity of respiratory tract infections.

Preliminary clinical research in healthy adults shows that completing an 8-week MBSR slightly reduces incidence and severity of respiratory infections when compared with waitlist control ( 100267 ).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for RA.

A small clinical study in patients with RA shows that completing an MBSR 8-week program seems to reduce morning stiffness and pain, but not other measures of disease activity such as C-reactive protein levels and swollen joint count, when compared with waitlist control ( 100422 ).

Sexual dysfunction.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for female sexual dysfunction.

A meta-analysis of preliminary clinical research in females with sexual dysfunction shows that mindfulness-based therapy improves symptoms when compared with baseline, but not when compared with a waitlist control ( 100425 ). This analysis is limited by the small number of studies with small sample sizes.

Small for gestational age (SGA).

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for preventing SGA newborns.

A large preliminary clinical trial in individuals at high risk of having a newborn that is below the 10th percentile in size (SGA) shows that an 8-week MBSR program adapted for pregnancy, including 2.5-hour weekly sessions with a single full-day session, reduces the odds of having an SGA infant by 34% when compared with usual care. The odds of having a severe SGA newborn (<3rd percentile) was also reduced by almost 50% ( 106879 ).

Smoking cessation.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based addiction treatment (MBAT) is beneficial for smoking cessation.

Preliminary clinical research shows that MBAT does not affect abstinence from smoking or reduce cigarettes smoked per day ( 100429 , 100430 , 109870 ). However, the findings from these meta-analyses are limited due to the variability of interventions, control groups, and measured outcomes ( 100429 , 109870 ).

Somatic symptom disorder.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for somatic symptom disorder.

A small preliminary clinical trial in patients with somatic symptom disorder shows that the combination of venlafaxine treatment of up to 150 mg daily with an 8-week MBSR treatment program modestly reduces the severity of both physical and psychological symptoms more than venlafaxine alone. However, there was no effect on most measures of quality of life ( 107086 ).

Stroke.

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for reducing fatigue or improving cognition following a stroke.

A small clinical study in patients who have experienced a stroke or traumatic brain injury shows that practicing MBSR improves mental fatigue and cognition when compared with a waitlist control ( 101270 ).

Substance use disorder.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for substance use disorders.

A meta-analysis of generally low- to moderate-quality clinical research in patients with alcohol and/or various drug use disorders shows that mindfulness-based interventions do not affect abstinence rate, craving intensity, or the amount of substance used when compared to no treatment or when compared with treatments such as standard care, cognitive behavioral therapy, or psychoeducation. However, due to the small number and heterogeneity of included studies, any findings are uncertain. When compared with other treatments, mindfulness-based interventions result in a small reduction in the percentage of days with substance use immediately after treatment and after 4-10 months ( 107099 ). Preliminary clinical research in females in the justice system shows that mindfulness-based relapse prevention (MBRP) reduces substance cravings and use when compared with baseline. However, there were no changes in substance dependence, emotions, or other outcomes ( 107090 ). Additionally, a clinical study in military veterans who had completed a treatment program for substance use disorder shows that participating in an 8-week MBRP aftercare program maintains reductions in the use of alcohol and illicit drugs similarly to a 12-step facilitation aftercare control group during the aftercare program and after 10 months of follow-up ( 113846 ). The validity of this study is limited by a high study attrition rate. Participants in the included research had substance use disorders related to single or mixed use of opioids, alcohol, stimulants, cannabis, and others.

Suicidal ideation.

It is unclear if mindfulness reduces suicidal ideation in otherwise healthy adults.

Preliminary clinical research in college students with a high level of suicidal ideation shows that although brief mindfulness meditation over one month reduces suicidal ideation by a large amount from baseline, it does not reduce suicidal ideation or stress, or improve sleep, when compared with a waitlist control group ( 107080 ). However, this study may have been too small to detect differences between groups.

Tension headache.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for tension headache.

A meta-analysis of clinical studies in patients with tension headaches shows that mindfulness meditation modestly improves pain intensity and headache frequency when compared with control. The benefit seems to be more pronounced when standardized mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), rather than a different mindfulness technique, is used. However, the validity of these findings is limited by high heterogeneity ( 100248 ).

Tinnitus.

It is unclear if mindfulness is beneficial for tinnitus.

One preliminary clinical study shows that attending five 40-minute mindfulness meditation sessions over 15 weeks and practicing meditation at home improves severity of tinnitus, distress, and interference with activities when compared with relaxation therapy in patients with intrusive tinnitus ( 96283 ). However, another small clinical trial in patients with chronic tinnitus shows that performing complex intervention involving mindfulness, physical exercise, and massage seems to improve symptoms when compared with baseline, but not when compared with control ( 101272 ).

Traumatic brain injury (TBI).

It is unclear if mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) is beneficial for reducing fatigue or improving cognition following a TBI.

A small clinical study in patients who have experienced a stroke or TBI shows that practicing MBSR improves mental fatigue and cognition when compared with a waitlist control ( 101270 ).

More evidence is needed to rate mindfulness for these uses.

#### General

Mindfulness involves paying attention in the present moment without being judgmental or reactive. It involves being aware of the present experience, but not taking ownership or judging thoughts and emotions that arise from that experience. Mindfulness seems to reduce psychological stress and pain by classical conditioning rather than by in-depth analysis. It involves practicing being less reactive to a perceived threat. This would theoretically reduce the psychological impact of that threat ( 96283 ).

#### Addiction effects

In adults with internet gaming disorder, mindfulness meditation reduces craving-related brain activation and increases functional connectivity in brain regions implicated in craving, suggesting a neural mechanism for the effect of mindfulness on craving and self-control ( 116680 ).

#### Alcohol and substance withdrawal effects

There is interest in using mindfulness programs to aid recovery and prevent relapses in individuals with alcohol and other substance use disorders ( 105414 , 105415 , 105416 , 107099 , 113846 , 113849 ). Some researchers have suggested that mindfulness improves emotional regulation, decreases stress reactivity, and decouples the association between negative affect and cravings ( 105414 , 105415 ), possibly by increasing the ability to recognize experiences rather than distancing from them and increasing the odds of interrupting the mechanisms underlying craving ( 107099 ). However, clinical research in adults with alcohol or other substance use disorders is mixed ( 105414 , 105415 , 105416 , 107099 , 113846 , 113849 ). Although it is possible that a higher pretreatment expectancy increases the likelihood of benefit related to mindfulness interventions for substance use, one study suggests that positive treatment expectancy did not predict favorable posttreatment outcomes and may have even increased future substance dependence ( 107090 ).

#### Analgesic effects

Clinical research in healthy volunteers shows that mindfulness meditation reduces pain intensity and unpleasantness of skin contact to high heat when compared with placebo cream or sham mindfulness meditation. Mindfulness meditation was linked with orbitofrontal, subgenual anterior cingulate, and anterior insular cortex activation. This suggests that pain relief was activated by brain regions of higher order ( 100403 ). Additional clinical research shows that analgesic effects of mindfulness meditation are not reversed by naloxone and therefore do not seem to be due to effects on opioid receptors ( 100402 ).

#### Cardiovascular effects

There is interest in using mindfulness in the management of cardiovascular diseases. Mindfulness may improve the ability to accept and adhere to relevant lifestyle modifications, such as dieting and exercising, which can help treat hypertension and other contributory factors ( 105417 , 105432 ). Mindfulness may also reduce blood pressure by reducing sympathetic activity. However, some evidence suggests that mindfulness may only reduce blood pressure elevations related to the "white coat effect", without an effect on ambulatory blood pressure ( 105432 ).

#### Immunologic effects

There is interest in mindfulness for normalizing the immune system after cancer treatment. A clinical study in recovering breast cancer survivors shows that Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) for 6 weeks might increase tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-a and interleukin (IL)-6, but does not affect IL-1b, IL-10, transforming growth factor-b1, or soluble TNF receptor-1 when compared with usual care ( 101278 ).

#### Neurologic effects

It is thought that mindfulness meditation can reduce stress and reaction to pain by reducing reactivity of the amygdala. Long-term mindfulness practice seems to reduce engagement of prefrontal regulation and reduce amygdala reactivity. A small clinical study shows that completing an 8-week mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) reduces the reaction to positive pictures but not to negative pictures when compared with control. ( 101275 ). Longer term practice might be needed to reduce reactivity to negative pictures. There is a theory that mindfulness can cause neuroplastic changes to brain structures and change their function. For example, changes to the structure of the amygdala can reduce its reactivity to stressors. A clinical trial in healthy stressed adults shows that a 3-day mindfulness meditation training reduces amygdala resting state functional connectivity when compared with a 3-day relaxation training. In other words, mindfulness meditation seems to reverse the effects of stress that increases amygdala resting state functional connectivity ( 101280 ). However, while amygdala connectivity is reduced, mindfulness has also been shown to increase connectivity within the pre-frontal cortex region which can lead to increased stress resilience and greater executive control. A small clinical trial suggests that mindfulness training increases resting state functional connectivity between dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and dorsal network and ventral network regions. ( 101281 ). Furthermore, a small clinical in patients with Parkinson disease study shows that an 8-week mindfulness-based intervention leads to structural brain changes such as increased gray matter density on MRI ( 100417 ). However, it is not known how these changes impact symptoms of Parkinson disease. Mindfulness might also reduce anxiety by affecting various neurotransmitters and biomarkers. A small clinical study in patients with anxiety shows that mindfulness based meditation improves anxiety, depression and also reduces epidermal growth factor (EGF), but inflammatory markers are not affected. EGF has been associated with various neuro-psychiatric disorders. Theoretically mindfulness reduces anxiety by reducing the concentration of the mediator EGF ( 101276 ). There is also interest in using mindfulness to reduce headache frequency, intensity, and duration. Theoretically, decreasing stress reactivity and response could prevent hyperexcitability, which can trigger headaches ( 105418 , 105428 , 105429 ). Some researchers have also suggested that prolonged practices of mindfulness may lead to changes in the insula cortex and increase dorsolateral prefrontal cortex engagement, which may improve pain control. However, clinical research in adults with migraines that practiced mindfulness for 8 weeks did not identify these changes ( 105428 ).

#### Telomerase effects

The enzyme telomerase, through its influence on telomere length, is associated with health and mortality. A meta-analysis of four clinical studies shows that mindfulness meditation increases telomerase activity in peripheral blood mononuclear cells. Shortened telomeres, and especially shortened immune cell telomeres, are associated with ill health and mortality. Telomerase enzyme can increase the addition of DNA sequences onto telomeres, increasing their length. Increased telomerase activity is associated with markers of better immune function ( 91854 ).

#### Weight loss effects

There is interest in using mindfulness for obesity. Chronic stress and dysregulated eating, such as binge eating, are known to increase body weight. Theoretically, mindfulness may reduce the effects of these contributing factors on weight gain ( 105413 , 107101 ). Mindfulness might reduce feelings of impulsiveness leading to a reduction in overeating without thinking ( 107101 ). Some preliminary research in normal, overweight, and obese adults shows that mindfulness reduces uncontrolled eating, but does not improve weight loss ( 100393 , 105412 , 105413 ).
