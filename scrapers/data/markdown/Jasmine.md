## Overview

Jasmine is a plant with strongly scented flowers and oil. It is used in perfumes, essential oils, aromatherapy, and food flavorings ( 11 , 98684 ). Jasmine has been used for centuries to flavor tea, and also in traditional Chinese medicine ( 56970 , 110678 ).

## People Use This For

Orally, jasmine has been used for hepatitis, hepatic pain due to cirrhosis, and abdominal pain due to dysentery. Jasmine is also used to decrease the risk of stroke. Many Jasminum species have been used as a sedative, aphrodisiac, or in cancer treatment. Topically, jasmine flowers are used to suppress lactation. Jasmine is also used for skin diseases and wound healing. By inhalation, jasmine is used for reducing stress, increasing mental alertness, and decreasing food cravings. In foods, jasmine is utilized to flavor beverages, frozen dairy desserts, candy, baked goods, gelatins, and puddings. In manufacturing, jasmine is used to add fragrance to creams, lotions, and perfumes.

Likely Safe when used orally in the amounts commonly found in foods. Jasmine has Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) status in the US ( 4912 ).

There is insufficient reliable information available about the safety of jasmine in medicinal amounts.

PREGNANCY AND LACTATION: Insufficient reliable information available; avoid using in amounts greater than those found in food.

#### General

Topically, jasmine may cause allergic contact dermatitis, delayed-type hypersensitivity, and type 1 hypersensitivity ( 56955 , 57027 , 57032 , 98684 ).

#### Immunologic

Topically, jasmine essential oil and jasmine flower can cause allergic contact dermatitis ( 56955 , 57027 , 57032 ). Among patients who are sensitive to fragrance materials, jasmine may cause delayed-type hypersensitivity. The reported prevalence of this type of reaction ranges from 0.4% to 1.6% ( 89684 ). One case of type 1 hypersensitivity to jasmine has also been reported. The patient experienced chest tightness, wheezing, itchy skin, and macular erythematous eruption within minutes of exposure to jasmine. The eruption subsided within 20 minutes of moving away from the plant. An immunoblotting assay revealed IgE reactivity ( 98684 ).

Insufficient Reliable Evidence to Rate
Mental alertness.

One preliminary clinical study shows that inhaling the aroma of jasmine essential oil while taking a visual vigilance test does not improve reaction time or accuracy when compared with placebo ( 57019 ). Another preliminary study in healthy male students shows that inhaling the aroma of jasmine essential oil for 30 minutes between concentration tests does not reduce mental fatigue when compared with placebo ( 56977 ).

More evidence is needed to rate jasmine for this use.

#### General

The applicable parts of jasmine are the flower and essential oil. Jasmine contains iridoids, such as ligstroside and oleuropein, secoiridoid glucosides, alkaloids, sterols, fatty acids, triterpenes, lignans, flavonoids, and terpenoids ( 56960 , 56997 , 110677 ). The flowers contain ligustin, oleuropein, and hydroxytyrosol ( 110678 ).

#### Anti-inflammatory effects

A methanol extract of the aerial parts of jasmine given in a dose of 400 mg/kg orally has anti-inflammatory activity in rat models of ulcerative colitis and rheumatoid arthritis (RA). The extract reduces ulcerative colitis disease activity similarly to prednisolone 2 mg/kg. It reduces proinflammatory myeloperoxidase, interferon-gamma, tumor necrosis factor alpha, interleukin (IL)-1, and IL-6 in colonic tissues. It also increases levels of the tight junction proteins claudin-5 and occludin in colonic epithelial cells ( 110677 ). In the rat RA model, the extract reduces levels of rheumatoid factor, C-reactive protein, cathepsin-D, nitric oxide, and inducible nitric oxide synthase. It also reduces bone histopathological lesions, articular cartilage damage, and inflammation of synovial membrane and periarticular tissues, when compared with control animals ( 110677 ).

#### Anticancer effects

Jasmine is traditionally used in the prevention and treatment of cancer. In an animal model, jasmine flower extract prevents the formation of tumors, possibly due to antioxidant constituents ( 56978 ).

#### Antioxidant effects

The antioxidant effects of jasmine are due to phenolic and flavonoid compounds, including coumaroylquinic acid ( 110677 ).

#### Gastroprotective effects

In a mouse model of gastric ulcer induced by acid and ethanol, jasmine extracts alleviate ulceration by increasing prostaglandin E2 production and antioxidant enzyme activity, reducing lipid peroxidation, and reducing production of reactive oxygen species, apoptosis-related proteins, proinflammatory cytokines, and nitric oxide. Some extracts and the hydroxytyrosol ingredient reduce ulcer scores and histopathological changes, restore the thickness of the gastric mucous layer, and improve body weight, food and water intake, and flatulence ( 110678 ).

#### Relaxation effects

The smell of jasmine is thought to induce relaxation, slowing heart rate and improving mood ( 56975 ).

#### Spasmolytic effects

Jasmine is used for abdominal pain due to dysentery. It has spasmolytic effects in guinea pig ileum ( 56962 ).

#### Vasodilatory effects

In isolated thoracic aorta, jasmine extract has a vasodilatory effect. The mechanism of action seems to involve the calcium channels ( 57041 ).

#### Wound-healing effects

Jasmine is traditionally used topically for wound healing. Animal research shows that application of jasmine extract or a mucoadhesive containing jasmine leaves accelerates wound-healing, likely by improving epithelialization and collagen formation and reducing inflammatory cell infiltration ( 56994 , 102542 ).
